### Source and Biology

Shellac is a natural resin secreted by the lac bug (*Kerria lacca*), a tiny scale insect (order Hemiptera, family Kerriidae) that feeds on the sap of certain trees in India and Southeast Asia. The insect is roughly 1-2 mm long as an adult. It is not a beetle — the name "lac bug" is a misnomer from older classification systems.

**Host trees**: The lac bug feeds on a range of host trees. Primary hosts in India: *Schleichera oleosa* (kusum), *Butea monosperma* (palas), *Ziziphus mauritiana* (ber). Secondary hosts: *Acacia catechu*, *Ficus religiosa*, and several other species. Different host trees produce lac with slightly different properties — kusum lac is considered the highest quality.

**Life cycle**: Female lac bugs insert their proboscis into the tree bark and remain stationary for life, secreting a resinous cocoon (the "lac") around their bodies. Each insect secretes ~0.5 mg of resin over its lifetime. The resin forms a continuous encrustation on branches, 5-10 mm thick, called "sticklac." The life cycle from egg to adult is approximately 6 months, with two generations per year in most regions — corresponding to two harvest seasons (June-July and October-November in India).

### Cultivation

- **Host tree management**: Plant host trees (or utilize existing forest trees). Maintain spacing of 5-8 m between trees. Prune lower branches to facilitate access and air circulation.
- **Inoculation**: Collect "brood lac" (sticklac containing developing larvae from the previous season). Tie brood lac bundles to fresh branches of host trees at the start of the growing season. Emerging larvae crawl from the brood lac onto the new branches, settle, and begin feeding and secreting resin.
- **Parasites and predators**: Lac cultivation is threatened by several natural enemies. Predatory mites, parasitic wasps (*Tachardiaephagus* spp.), and the lac predator *Eublemma amabilis* (a moth whose larvae eat lac bugs). Ants often protect lac colonies from predators in exchange for honeydew — beneficial relationship to encourage.
- **Yield**: A single tree yields 1-3 kg of raw sticklac per harvest. India historically produced 30,000-50,000 tonnes of raw lac per year at peak production (early 20th century). Yields vary by host species, tree age, climate, and pest pressure.

### Harvesting and Processing

**Step 1 — Sticklac collection**:
- Cut lac-encrusted branches from host trees with a sharp knife or pruning saw. Timing is important — harvest when the lac layer is at maximum thickness and the next generation of insects has not yet emerged. Sticklac contains the resin, plus insect bodies, twigs, and other debris.

**Step 2 — Crushing and washing**:
- Crush sticklac between rollers or in a mortar to break it into granules. Wash repeatedly in water to remove insect bodies, woody debris, and water-soluble dyes. The wash water contains lac dye (a red pigment historically used as a textile dye and food coloring — a valuable by-product). The washed product is called "seed lac" — granular, brown, still containing some impurities.

**Step 3 — Solvent dissolution**:
- Dissolve seed lac in denatured ethanol (ethyl alcohol, ~95% purity). Shellac dissolves readily in ethanol at room temperature — approximately 1 part shellac to 3-4 parts ethanol by weight for a working solution. Stir or agitate until fully dissolved (several hours to overnight for large batches).

**Step 4 — Filtration**:
- Filter the dissolved shellac through cloth or fine mesh to remove remaining insoluble impurities (insect fragments, wax, sand). For high-purity shellac, filter through finer media (felt, paper) or allow to settle and decant the clear supernatant. Heating the solution to ~50°C improves dissolution and filtration rate but accelerates esterification (shellac's progressive reaction with alcohol that causes it to become insoluble over time — see Aging below).

**Step 5 — Flake formation**:
- Spread the filtered solution thinly on a flat surface (marble slab, glass plate, or metal tray). Allow the ethanol to evaporate. As the alcohol evaporates, the shellac solidifies into a thin, brittle sheet. Break or scrape the sheet into flakes. Product: dry shellac flakes, orange-amber to dark brown depending on source and processing.

**Step 6 — Bleaching (optional)**:
- For white or blond shellac: Dissolve seed lac in warm (~50°C) sodium hypochlorite (NaOCl, bleach) solution. The chlorine bleaches the natural pigments. Neutralize with dilute acid, wash, redissolve in ethanol, and re-flake. Bleached shellac (also called "blonde shellac") is used where a light-colored finish is desired. Bleaching reduces shelf life — bleached shellac is more prone to degradation.

### Properties

- **Solubility**: Dissolves readily in ethanol and isopropyl alcohol. Soluble in alkaline water (borax or sodium carbonate solution) — useful for water-based shellac formulations. Insoluble in water, oils, and most organic solvents.
- **Drying**: Dries by solvent evaporation, not chemical reaction. Drying time: 15-30 minutes for thin coats at room temperature. Fastest-drying natural finish. No curing time needed — fully hardened when solvent has evaporated.
- **Hardness and adhesion**: Hard when dry (pencil hardness ~2H-3H). Good adhesion to wood, metal, and many plastics. Can be applied over most other finishes without lifting (except polyurethane and some modern finishes — shellac solvents may attack them).
- **Water resistance**: Moderate. Shellac is not waterproof — standing water leaves white marks (water rings on tabletops). However, shellac provides good resistance to brief moisture exposure and humidity cycling. Alcohol will redissolve it — shellac-finished surfaces are damaged by alcoholic beverages.
- **Electrical properties**: Good electrical insulator. Volume resistivity ~10¹⁴-10¹⁵ Ω·cm. Used historically for coil insulation and early electronics.
- **Thermal properties**: Softens at ~65-75°C. Decomposes above ~200°C. Cannot withstand soldering iron temperatures.

### Applications

- **French polish**: The highest-quality traditional wood finish. Shellac dissolved in ethanol (typically 2 lb cut = 1 lb shellac per gallon alcohol) applied by hand with a rubber — a pad of cloth wrapped around a wad of cotton, charged with shellac and a drop of oil (linseed or mineral oil to lubricate). The rubber is stroked rapidly across the wood surface in overlapping passes, building up thin coats. After 20-40 coats: a deep, glossy, mirror-like finish with extraordinary depth of color. French polishing is labor-intensive (4-8 hours per typical tabletop) but produces results unattainable with brush-applied finishes. Still used for fine furniture restoration and musical instruments.
- **Phonograph records**: Shellac was the primary binder in 78 RPM records from the 1890s through the 1950s. The record material was a composite: ~25% shellac binder, ~75% filler (crushed slate, limestone, carbon black for color, cotton flock for strength), plus small amounts of plasticizer and lubricant. Records were compression-molded under heat and pressure. Shellac records are brittle — they break if dropped — and have limited playing life (~100 plays before groove wear becomes audible). Replaced by vinyl (PVC) records in the late 1940s-1950s.
- **Electrical insulation**: Coil windings on motors, generators, and transformers were varnished with shellac (applied by dipping or brushing). Shellac provides good insulation, adhesion to copper wire, and moisture resistance sufficient for indoor equipment. Its use declined as synthetic varnishes (polyester, epoxy) offered superior heat resistance and dielectric properties.
- **Wood primer and sealer**: Shellac is an excellent universal sealer. Applied as a thin coat before paint or varnish, it seals porous surfaces, prevents tannin bleed-through (especially on oak, mahogany, and cherry), and bonds to almost any substrate. "Knotting" — shellac specifically applied to seal resinous knots in softwood before painting. De-waxed shellac (processed to remove the natural wax content) provides the best adhesion under other finishes.
- **Pharmaceutical and food**: "Confectioner's glaze" — purified shellac used as a coating on candies, pills, and tablets. Provides gloss, moisture barrier, and controlled release. Classified as food-safe (GRAS status in the US). The wax component of shellac must be removed for pharmaceutical use (de-waxed shellac).
- **Sealing wax**: Traditional document seals combine shellac with turpentine, pigments, and filler. When melted and pressed, shellac provides the binding and hardening properties.

### Aging and Shelf Life

Shellac undergoes gradual esterification with its solvent (ethanol). Over months to years, the ester bonds formed during storage make the shellac increasingly resistant to redissolution. Old shellac may leave a cloudy, lumpy film or fail to dry properly.

- **Fresh shellac**: Fully dissolves in ethanol within hours. Dries clear and hard.
- **Aged shellac (1-2 years)**: Slower to dissolve. May leave slightly cloudy film. Still usable for less-critical applications.
- **Old shellac (3+ years)**: May not fully dissolve. Film quality degraded. Discard and purchase fresh.
- **Storage**: Store flakes in a cool, dry, dark place in sealed containers. Shelf life: ~1 year for bleached shellac, ~2-3 years for orange (unbleached) shellac. Minimize storage temperature — heat accelerates esterification. Do not premix large quantities of shellac solution — mix only what will be used within a few months.

### Safety & Hazards

- **Ethanol flammability**: Shellac is dissolved in ethanol, which is highly flammable (flash point ~13°C, auto-ignition ~365°C). All shellac mixing, application, and drying must occur in well-ventilated areas with no open flames, sparks, or hot surfaces. Store ethanol in approved containers. Ground metal containers to prevent static discharge. Fire extinguisher (Class B — CO₂ or dry chemical) must be immediately available in shellac working areas.
- **Ethanol vapor inhalation**: Ethanol vapors cause headache, dizziness, and narcosis at high concentrations. The odor threshold (~100 ppm) is well below hazardous levels, but prolonged exposure in poorly ventilated spaces causes cumulative effects. Ensure adequate ventilation — 10+ air changes per hour for brush application, local exhaust for spraying.
- **Dermatitis**: Shellac resin and ethanol solvent both cause skin drying and irritation with repeated contact. Nitrile or rubber gloves recommended for prolonged handling. Some individuals develop allergic contact dermatitis to specific shellac components.
- **Lac bug allergens**: Workers handling raw sticklac may develop respiratory or dermal sensitization to insect proteins. Dust from crushing sticklac can trigger allergic reactions. Wear dust mask and gloves during initial processing steps.
