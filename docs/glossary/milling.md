# Milling

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, metals, plants

The mechanical reduction of solid materials to smaller particle sizes through crushing, grinding, or cutting. Milling progresses through the tech tree from hand-operated stone mills (saddle quern: 1-2 kg flour/hour) to water-powered mills (overshot wheel driving stone pairs: 50-100 kg flour/hour) to industrial ball mills for cement raw grinding and powder metallurgy feedstock preparation.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Milling serves dual roles in the tech tree: food processing (grinding grain into flour) and industrial material preparation (grinding ores, raw materials, and metal powders). In the plants domain, grain milling is one of the earliest mechanical processes, with the rotary quern (~300 BCE) representing a significant productivity increase over the saddle quern. In the chemistry domain, ball milling grinds raw materials to specified fineness — cement raw meal to <75 μm, for example. In powder metallurgy, ball milling and attritor milling produce metal powders with controlled particle size distributions.

Soap manufacture uses milling to incorporate additives into the soap base — the "milled soap" process passes soap flakes through chilled steel rollers multiple times to achieve uniform additive distribution and consistent texture.

## Technical Details

Ball milling is the workhorse of industrial size reduction. A rotating cylindrical shell filled with grinding media (steel balls, ceramic balls, or flint pebbles) tumbles the material charge. Impact and attrition between balls and between balls and shell liner reduce particle size. Critical speed (the speed at which centrifugal force holds balls against the shell, preventing tumbling) is calculated as Nc = 42.3/√D RPM, where D is mill diameter in meters. Operating at 65-80% of critical speed provides optimal cascading action.

Grinding energy follows Bond's law: the work required is proportional to the square root of the ratio of feed to product particle size. Multiple stages (coarse crushing → fine grinding) are more energy-efficient than single-stage grinding to final size. Classification (sieving or air classification) between stages removes material that has already reached target size, preventing wasted energy on re-grinding.
