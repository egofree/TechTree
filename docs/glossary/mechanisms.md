# Mechanisms

> **Type**: material | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, computing, metals, mining

Assemblies of moving parts that transmit force, motion, or energy in controlled ways — levers, gears, linkages, cams, cranks, and their combinations. Mechanisms are the functional core of every machine from potter's wheels to mechanical computers. In powder metallurgy, hot isostatic pressing works through the mechanism of isostatic pressure: under 100-200 MPa, internal pore surfaces collapse when the metal's yield strength decreases at temperature, and subsequent diffusion bonding permanently eliminates voids.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Mechanical computing depends on mechanisms for all data processing: gear trains for arithmetic, linkages for function generation, escapements for timing, and clutches for conditional logic. The Babbage Difference Engine uses tens of thousands of precisely machined gear mechanisms to perform polynomial evaluation through addition carried by rotating columns of figure wheels.

In the metals domain, powder metallurgy uses pressure-based mechanisms (compaction presses, isostatic presses) and thermal mechanisms (sintering, diffusion bonding) to form components from metal powders. Mining uses crank-and-connecting-rod mechanisms to convert rotary motion from water wheels or steam engines into reciprocating pump motion for mine dewatering.

## Technical Details

Mechanism design follows kinematic principles: degrees of freedom (how many independent motions are possible), constraints (linkages that restrict motion to desired paths), and force transmission (mechanical advantage through levers, gear ratios, and screw threads). A four-bar linkage converts one type of motion to another (rotary to oscillating, for example). A gear train changes speed and torque. A cam converts rotary motion to a prescribed linear displacement pattern.

In mechanical computing, the key mechanisms are the adder (parallel toothed wheels carrying decimal digits, with tens-carry mechanism propagating overflows), the printer (type wheels raised by the computing mechanism to impress ink on paper), and the stereotype output (molding plaster casts from the computed results for printing plate production). Each mechanism must operate with zero backlash and minimal friction to maintain accuracy across thousands of cycles.
