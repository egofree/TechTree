## Overview

South American camelids — alpacas (*Vicugna pacos*) and llamas (*Lama glama*) — are dual-purpose livestock providing luxury fiber (alpacas) and pack transport plus guard duties (llamas). Domesticated in the Andes over 6,000 years ago, they are exceptionally well-adapted to marginal land, require minimal infrastructure compared to other livestock, and have few health problems. Alpacas produce 2-5 kg of fine fleece every 12-18 months in a range of 22 natural colors. Llamas carry 25-30% of their body weight as pack animals and a single llama can serve as an effective guardian for a flock of 200+ sheep against canine and coyote predators.

## Process Description

## 4.1 Fiber Production (Alpaca)

**Principle**: Alpacas produce 2-5 kg of fine fleece (18-30 microns) every 12-18 months in 22 natural colors. The fiber has no lanolin (unlike sheep wool) and can be spun directly. Alpaca fiber has a hollow core, making it lighter and warmer than sheep wool of equivalent diameter.

**Prerequisites**: Alpaca herd (Huacaya or Suri breeds), shearing equipment, carding tools, spinning wheel or drop spindle.

**Materials**: Hand shears or electric clippers, shearing table or restraint ropes, sorting table, carding paddles or drum carder.

**Procedure**:
1. Shear every 12-18 months. Fiber grows 8-12 cm per year. Restrain alpaca on shearing table or ground (CUYA method — stretch with ropes at four legs).
2. A skilled shearer completes one alpaca in 10-15 minutes (electric) or 30-45 minutes (hand shears).
3. Sort at shearing: separate the prime blanket (back and sides) from neck and leg fiber. Prime blanket is finest (18-26 microns); neck and leg fiber is coarser.
4. Skirt the fleece: spread on table, remove vegetation, second cuts, stained or matted sections.
5. Card: align fibers using hand carders or drum carder. Produces rolag (hand) or batt (drum) for spinning.
6. Spin into yarn using spinning wheel or drop spindle. Alpaca can be spun without washing (no lanolin). Blend 20-30% sheep wool for elasticity — 100% alpaca yarn lacks stretch.

**Expected yield**: 2-5 kg fleece per adult alpaca per shearing. Royal baby grade (<20 μm) fetches premium prices. One alpaca's annual yield makes 2-4 blended garments.

**Strengths**:
- 22 natural colors — widest color range of any fiber animal, reduces or eliminates dyeing
- No lanolin — fiber can be processed without washing, reducing water and labor
- Hollow core fiber provides better insulation per unit weight than sheep wool
- Communal dung piles make manure collection trivial compared to other livestock

**Weaknesses**:
- 100% alpaca yarn lacks elasticity — must blend with 20-30% sheep wool for garments
- Heat stress above 30°C in unshorn animals — shearing timing is critical in warm climates
- Fiber diameter increases with age — older animals produce coarser, less valuable fleece
- Low individual yield (2-5 kg/year) requires larger herds for commercial fiber production

## 4.2 Guard Llama Services

**Principle**: A single llama bonded to a sheep flock provides effective predator defense through instinctive alarm-calling, positioning between flock and threat, and charging/kicking/stomping approaching predators. No training required — the behavior is innate.

**Prerequisites**: Gelded male or female llama (18-24 months at introduction), sheep flock of 200-250 head, bonding period of 1-2 weeks in a small pen.

**Materials**: Llama, small pen for initial bonding with sheep flock, standard fencing.

**Procedure**:
1. Select a gelded male (castrated at 6-12 months to prevent breeding behavior with sheep) or female. Intact males may try to breed sheep, causing injury.
2. Introduce llama to sheep flock in a small pen for 1-2 weeks to bond before turning out together.
3. Llama naturally assumes guard role: moves toward threats, positions between flock and predator, gives alarm call (loud, high-pitched whinny).
4. If predator persists, llama charges, kicks, and stomps. Most coyotes and dogs flee immediately.
5. One llama per 200-250 sheep is sufficient. Multiple llamas may bond with each other rather than the sheep, reducing effectiveness.

**Expected yield**: 75-100% reduction in predation losses (coyotes, stray dogs, foxes). Working life 15-20 years with no feeding costs beyond shared pasture.

**Strengths**:
- No training required — instinctive guarding behavior
- No additional feeding costs — llama grazes with the flock
- 15-20 year working life (vs 5-8 for guard dogs)
- No risk to lambs (guard dogs occasionally injure lambs during play)
- More effective than guard dogs against coyotes in open terrain

**Weaknesses**:
- Less effective against large predators (bears, mountain lions) — llama may be injured or killed
- Only one llama per flock — multiple llamas bond to each other instead of sheep
- Initial bonding period of 1-2 weeks requires small pen management
- Llama may interfere with herding dogs — coordinate guardian and herding strategies

## 4.3 Pack Transport (Llama)

**Principle**: Llamas carry 25-30% of body weight (30-60 kg) over rough, mountainous terrain where horses and mules cannot safely travel. Their soft-padded feet minimize trail erosion. Distance: 15-20 km/day on trails.

**Prerequisites**: Trained pack llama (2+ years), packsaddle with panniers, balanced loads (within 1 kg side-to-side).

**Materials**: Packsaddle (wooden or aluminum frame with padding), panniers (soft-sided pack bags), breast collar and rump strap.

**Procedure**:
1. Condition llama to packsaddle gradually — start with empty saddle, add weight over 1-2 weeks.
2. Load panniers to equal weight (within 1 kg). Total load: 25-30% of llama's body weight.
3. Secure packsaddle with breast collar and rump strap. Check for chafing after 30 minutes.
4. Travel 15-20 km/day on trails. Llamas browse on available vegetation along the trail.
5. Unload at camp. Check saddle area for sores. Allow free grazing overnight.

**Expected yield**: One 170 kg llama carries 40-50 kg of cargo 15-20 km/day. String of 5 llamas moves 200-250 kg of supplies.

**Strengths**:
- Soft-padded feet minimize trail erosion — lowest environmental impact of any pack animal
- Browses along the trail — minimal supplemental feed needed for multi-day trips
- Calm temperament — llamas can be led on a string by a walking person
- Sure-footed on steep, rocky terrain where horses cannot safely travel

**Weaknesses**:
- Slower than horses — 15-20 km/day vs 30-40 km/day for mounted travel
- Cannot carry as much absolute weight as mules — 40-50 kg vs 80-120 kg
- Requires balance — unbalanced loads cause saddle sores within hours
- Limited to terrain navigable on foot — cannot cross deep water or very steep rock faces

## 4.4 Reproduction and Cria Care

**Principle**: Camelids are induced ovulators — females do not have a heat cycle, ovulation is triggered by the act of breeding. Gestation is 11.5 months (350 days). Single cria per pregnancy; twins are extremely rare.

**Prerequisites**: Mature female (12-18 months, 60% mature body weight), mature male (2-3 years), clean foaling area.

**Materials**: Halter and lead, cria coat (fleece jacket) for cold climates, iodine for navel treatment, colostrum supplement as backup.

**Procedure**:
1. Introduce female to male for breeding. Ovulation occurs from physical stimulation — breeding can be planned at any time.
2. Confirm pregnancy via ultrasound or progesterone testing at 30-45 days.
3. Gestation: 350 days (335-365). Crias are born during daylight hours (typically mid-morning) — evolutionary adaptation to cold Andean nights.
4. Normal delivery: 15-30 minutes from first feet to delivery. Dystocia is uncommon; call for assistance if no progress within 1 hour.
5. Cria birth weight: 6-10 kg (alpaca), 10-15 kg (llama). Should stand within 30-60 minutes and nurse within 2 hours.
6. Colostrum is critical — crias are born without antibodies. If cria does not nurse within 4 hours, milk dam and tube-feed or bottle-feed colostrum.
7. In cold climates (below 5°C), towel-dry cria immediately and apply cria coat for first 24-48 hours.
8. Wean at 4-6 months (25-30 kg alpaca, 40-60 kg llama).

**Expected yield**: One cria per female per year. Cria reaches breeding age at 12-18 months (females) or 2-3 years (males).

**Strengths**:
- Induced ovulation allows year-round breeding — no heat detection needed
- Daytime birthing reduces hypothermia risk compared to nighttime foaling in horses
- Crias are hardy from birth — stand and nurse within 1 hour in most cases
- Low dystocia rate — camelids have been selected for easy birthing over 6,000 years

**Weaknesses**:
- 11.5-month gestation — as long as horses, slower than cattle (9 months) or sheep (5 months)
- Single offspring per pregnancy — no twins to boost production
- Crias born without passive immunity — 4-hour colostrum window is critical and unforgiving
- First-time mothers may have poor maternal behavior — require monitoring

## Scaling Notes

A founding herd of 5-10 alpacas (4-8 females, 1-2 males) establishes a self-sustaining fiber-producing flock within 3-4 years. First offspring shearing at 12-18 months yields 1.5-2.5 kg of marketable fleece.

**Stocking rates**: 8-12 alpacas or 5-8 llamas per hectare on improved pasture at sea level. On arid rangeland (under 40 cm annual precipitation), 2-4 animals per hectare with supplemental hay for 60-90 days per year.

**Minimum viable scale**: 2-3 alpacas provide 4-15 kg of fine fiber annually — enough for household textiles.

**Scale bottlenecks**: Shearing labor is the primary constraint — each alpaca takes 10-45 minutes depending on method.

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Probable Cause | Solution |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Heat stress (open-mouth breathing, lethargy) | Ambient temperature above 30°C with high humidity | Shear before summer; provide shade and ventilation; sheared alpacas tolerate heat well |
| Matted fleece | Left unshorn beyond 18 months | Shear on schedule; matted fleece causes heat stress and is difficult to process |
| Fighting teeth injuries (males) | Sharp canine teeth erupted at 2-3 years | Cut or file fighting teeth using obstetric wire or Dremel tool — routine maintenance |
| Vitamin D deficiency (crias, winter) | Short winter days at northern latitudes | Supplement with vitamin D injection (1000 IU/kg body weight) in autumn for growing animals under 2 years |
| Parasite load (barber pole worm) | Warm, wet pasture conditions | Fecal egg counts quarterly; treat with ivermectin or fenbendazole at camelid dosages; rotate pastures |
| Failure of passive transfer (cria) | Cria did not nurse colostrum within 4 hours | Tube-feed or bottle-feed colostrum supplement; monitor for infections in first months |

## Safety

**Handling**: Camelids are generally docile and non-aggressive. They do not kick forward like horses (they kick backward and downward, less forceful). They may spit regurgitated stomach contents when stressed — unpleasant but not dangerous (rinse with water).

**Halter fitting**: Use a properly fitted halter that allows the nostrils to flare — camelids are obligate nasal breathers. A tight halter that restricts nostrils can suffocate the animal.

**Catching**: Use a small catch pen. Corner gently — never chase (causes stress and hyperthermia). Place an arm around the neck and hold the tail base to restrain.

**Llama charging**: Llamas may charge if cornered or protecting a cria. A charging llama is intimidating but rarely makes contact — stand your ground and raise your arms. Most charges are bluffs.

**Copper toxicity**: Camelids are sensitive to copper (like sheep). Never use cattle or horse mineral supplements — use sheep or camelid-specific formulations. Copper toxicity causes fatal liver damage.

## Quality Control

**Fiber quality**: Measure fiber diameter with a micron meter or by sending samples to a testing lab. Target under 26 microns for premium grade. Sort into grades at shearing: blanket (finest), neck (medium), legs (coarsest). Store clean, skirted fleece in paper bags (not plastic — moisture causes mildew).

**Body condition scoring**: Assess backbone and ribs. Healthy camelids should have a smooth covering over the backbone — prominent spine indicates underfeeding. Overweight animals develop metabolic problems.

**Breeding records**: Track breeding dates, birth dates, cria weights, and fiber diameter per animal. Cull animals producing fiber above 30 microns or with poor conformation.

## Variations and Alternatives

**Sheep alternative**: Sheep ([`sheep`](sheep.md)) produce more fleece per animal (3-8 kg/year) and are more widely available. Alpaca fiber is finer and softer but sheep are more productive per hectare.

**Angora rabbit alternative**: Angora rabbits produce finer fiber (12-16 microns) at smaller scale but require daily grooming. See [`rabbits`](rabbits.md).

**Cold climate**: Camelids tolerate -20°C without shelter due to Andean origin at 3,500-5,000 m elevation. Oval-shaped red blood cells provide 15-20% greater oxygen-carrying efficiency at altitude. Protect from prolonged rain at temperatures below 5°C — fleece absorbs 30-40% of its weight in water.

**Hot climate**: Heat stress above 30°C with humidity over 75% reduces feed intake 20-30% and increases parasitic load. Shearing before summer reduces heat stress mortality from 5-8% to under 1%.
