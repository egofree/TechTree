### Weaving & Cloth Production

**Frame loom** (simplest):
- **Construction**: Rectangular wooden frame. Warp (lengthwise) threads tied top and bottom. Weft (crosswise) thread passed over and under warp by hand. Heddle bar (flat stick with holes and slots) lifts alternate warp threads to create shed (gap for weft passage). Width: 30-60 cm. Length limited by frame size.
- **Production**: Very slow (~0.5-1 m²/hour), but requires minimal construction.

**Pit loom / treadle loom** (production-scale):
- **Construction**: Heavy wooden frame, 1-1.5 m weaving width. Warp wrapped around cloth beam (front) and warp beam (back). Treadles (foot pedals) raise and lower shafts (frames holding heddles — string or wire loops, one per warp thread). 2-8 shafts for different weave structures. Beater (reed frame) packs weft tight.
- **Operation**: Depress treadle → shaft lifts → shed opens → throw shuttle (boat shuttle carrying weft yarn) through shed → beat weft with beater → change treadle → repeat. Rhythm: 30-60 picks (weft passes) per minute.
- **Production**: 1-3 m²/hour of medium-weight cloth. A weaver can produce ~5-10 m² of cloth per day.

**Weave structures**:
- **Tabby (plain weave)**: Over 1, under 1, alternating. Simplest, strongest, least flexible. For canvas, sheeting, bandages. 2 shafts required.
- **Twill**: Over 2, under 1 (or 2/2 twill). Diagonal rib pattern visible on fabric surface. Drapes better, more flexible. For clothing, work garments. 4 shafts for basic twills; point twills and herringbone use 4-8 shafts. Heddle lifting sequence advances one shaft each pick, creating the diagonal.
- **Satin**: Over 4+, under 1. Smooth face, less durable. Long floats catch and snag. For fine fabrics. Requires 5+ shafts. Floats distributed so no two adjacent in either direction.

### Warping Procedure

Preparing the warp (lengthwise) threads before they go on the loom:

1. **Calculate warp length**: Finished cloth length + 10-15% for take-up (yarn shortens as it interlaces with weft) + 30-60 cm loom waste (tied to beams, cannot be woven). For a 2-meter cloth length, warp ~2.5-2.8 meters.
2. **Measure warp on warping board or pegs**: Wooden board with pegs set at fixed distance (e.g., 1 meter apart). Walk yarn around pegs in figure-8 pattern, counting each circuit. Cross (lease) at one end keeps threads in order — critical for threading heddles correctly.
3. **Threading order**: Transfer warp to loom. Thread each warp end through a heddle (one per shaft) in the pattern sequence — for plain weave: shaft 1, shaft 2, repeat. For twill: shaft 1, 2, 3, 4, repeat. Sley through reed (determine ends per inch / dent spacing) for proper cloth width.

### Shuttle Construction

Tools for passing weft yarn through the shed:

- **Stick shuttle**: Flat piece of hardwood (30-50 cm), notched at both ends to hold weft yarn. Simplest. Wind yarn in figure-8 pattern. Suitable for frame looms and narrow work. Slow to pass — must be threaded through shed by hand.
- **Boat shuttle**: Boat-shaped wooden body (25-35 cm) with hollow center holding a removable bobbin (small cylinder wound with weft yarn). Yarn feeds through a hole in shuttle side. Flies through shed on a treadle loom — much faster than stick shuttle. Requires bobbins wound evenly (use bobbin winder — small hand-cranked spindle).
- **Ski shuttle**: For heavy weft yarns (rag rugs, bulky wool). Flat bottom, upturned front end. Slides across warp.

### Sailcloth Specifications

Canvas weights for maritime applications:

| Weight (oz/yd²) | Thread Count | Application |
|-----------------|-------------|-------------|
| 6-8 oz | 20-24 ends/inch | Light sails, clothing, bags |
| 10-12 oz | 16-20 ends/inch | Medium sails, tents, awnings |
| 14-18 oz | 12-16 ends/inch | Heavy sails, tarpaulins, floor cloths |
| 20+ oz | 10-14 ends/inch | Sail reinforcements, pallet covers, industrial |

Hemp canvas preferred for sails (rot-resistant, strong when wet) until cotton canvas replaced it in the 19th century. Flax canvas was the Mediterranean standard for millennia. All sailcloth must be fulled and proofed (treated with tar, wax, or oil) for water resistance.

**Fulling** (finishing woven cloth):
- Beat cloth in warm water with fuller's earth (clay) or stale urine (ammonia source). Felt the fibers slightly → cloth shrinks 15-25%, becomes denser and warmer. Fuller's earth absorbs oils. Rinse, stretch on tenter frame (hooks on adjustable bars), dry. Napping (raising surface fibers with teasels or wire cards) for fuzzy surface.

For dyeing processes (mordanting, indigo vat, dye plant cultivation, fastness testing), see [Dyeing](dyeing.md).

### Safety & Hazards

- **Loom machinery**: Power looms have numerous pinch points where warp threads, heddles, and shuttle mechanisms move rapidly. Guard moving parts. Keep hands clear during operation. Emergency stop mechanism required.
- **Shuttle injuries**: Flying shuttles move at speed and can injure if loom is improperly adjusted or shuttle escapes track. Maintain shuttle race in good condition.
- **Dust and fiber inhalation**: Cotton and fiber dust generated during weaving causes respiratory irritation (byssinosis — "brown lung" — with chronic cotton dust exposure). Ventilation in enclosed weaving areas.
- **Repetitive strain**: Hand loom weaving involves repetitive motion. Take breaks. Vary posture. Ergonomic bench height reduces back strain.
