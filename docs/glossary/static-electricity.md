# Static Electricity

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, mining

The buildup and discharge of electrical charge on non-conductive surfaces or in low-conductivity liquids. Static discharge sparks are a major ignition hazard in flammable atmospheres — the invisible danger in distillation, fuel handling, and explosives manufacture.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Static electricity hazards appear in [Distillation](../chemistry/distillation.md) where low-conductivity hydrocarbons and solvents generate charge flowing through pipes, in [Explosives](../chemistry/explosives.md) where static can initiate black powder and primary explosives, and in [Black Powder](../mining/black-powder.md) manufacture. Each context requires specific grounding and bonding procedures.

## Technical Details

The mechanism: flow of low-conductivity liquids (hydrocarbons, organic solvents) through pipes and packing generates static charge by triboelectric effect. Accumulated charge discharges as a spark — an ignition source for any flammable vapor present. The energy in a typical static discharge (0.1-10 mJ) exceeds the minimum ignition energy of most hydrocarbon-air mixtures (0.1-0.5 mJ).

Mitigation hierarchy: (1) Ground all metal equipment — clamps and wire to earth. (2) Limit filling velocity below 1 m/s for road/rail loading. (3) Bond containers before transferring flammable liquids (wire connecting source and destination). (4) Use antistatic additives in fuels to increase conductivity. (5) Maintain humidity above 50% in work areas — moist air dissipates charge.

In explosives manufacture: wear cotton clothing (not synthetic fabrics that generate and hold charge), ground mixing equipment, use conductive flooring, and avoid plastic containers. Shock tube (Nonel) initiating systems are immune to static electricity and stray currents, making them safer than electric blasting caps during thunderstorms or near power lines.
