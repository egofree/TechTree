# Harvesting

Harvesting is the collection of mature crops or raw materials from agricultural or natural sources. Timing is critical: for dye plants like madder, roots are dug in autumn (September–November) after 2–3 years of growth, when alizarin concentration peaks as top growth dies back. Roots are washed thoroughly and dried slowly in shade. Dried roots store for 2+ years, with yields of 2,000–4,000 kg dried root per hectare from a 3-year-old planting.

In rubber production, harvesting (tapping) follows a specific frequency—trees are tapped every 2–3 days, rotating to new cuts on different panels of the trunk. Previous cuts heal over weeks. Trees remain productive for 20–30 years with rest periods of 1–2 months per year extending tree life. For natural fiber crops (flax, hemp, cotton), harvest timing affects fiber quality: harvested too early and fibers are underdeveloped, too late and they become brittle.

Harvesting methods range from manual (hand-picking, digging, cutting with sickles) to mechanized (combine harvesters, mechanical cotton pickers). In a bootstrap context, manual harvesting with simple tools is the starting point. The labor requirement for harvesting often determines the viable scale of cultivation and the timing of mechanization investment. Post-harvest handling (washing, drying, storage) is equally important to preserve quality before processing.
