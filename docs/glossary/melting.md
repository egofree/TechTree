# Melting

> **Type**: equipment | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: glass, metals

The phase transition of a solid material to liquid through the application of heat, and the industrial processes built around that transition. Glass melting requires 1500-1600°C for borosilicate glass (12-24 hours for complete homogenization, stirred with fired clay rods). Aluminum melts at 660°C, copper at 1085°C, iron at 1538°C, and tungsten at 3422°C. Melting is the foundational operation of metallurgy and glassmaking — the ability to produce a homogeneous, bubble-free melt determines product quality.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Melting appears throughout the glass and metals domains. Soda-lime glass melts at ~1000°C in simple wood or coal-fired furnaces. Borosilicate glass requires 1500-1600°C from gas or oil-fired furnaces with forced air, or electric furnaces. Fused silica requires 1700-2000°C from electric furnaces or hydrogen-oxygen torches. Each step up in melting temperature demands more sophisticated furnace construction, refractory linings, and fuel or power sources.

In metals, melting is the first step in casting, alloying, and refining. Copper and bronze melt in crucible furnaces at relatively accessible temperatures. Iron requires forced-air furnaces (blast furnaces for smelting, cupolas for remelting). Steel production in electric arc furnaces melts scrap at 1600-1650°C. Aluminum electrolysis produces molten aluminum from dissolved alumina in cryolite at ~960°C.

## Technical Details

Successful melting requires control of three parameters: temperature (must exceed melting point with sufficient superheat for fluidity), atmosphere (oxidizing, reducing, or inert — determines dross formation and alloy oxidation), and time (sufficient for homogenization and degassing). Molten metal and glass both tend to absorb gases from the furnace atmosphere, creating bubbles in the solidified product. Degassing techniques include fluxing (adding chemicals that react with dissolved gases), vacuum degassing (reducing atmospheric pressure above the melt), and stirring to coalesce and release bubbles.

In glass production, fining agents (antimony trioxide, sodium sulfate) are added to the melt to help bubbles rise and escape. In steelmaking, oxygen lancing converts dissolved carbon to CO gas that escapes, simultaneously decarburizing the melt.
