> **Outputs**: meat, angora_wool, manure, pelts
> **Critical**: Yes — fastest path to animal protein with minimal infrastructure


Rabbits (*Oryctolagus cuniculus*) are the highest-efficiency terrestrial livestock for converting plant material to meat. A single doe produces 180-300 kg of dressed meat per year through her offspring — more protein output per unit of feed than any other mammal. Rabbits require minimal space (stackable cages), reproduce rapidly (28-31 day gestation, 6-12 kits per litter, 4-6 litters per year), and reach slaughter weight in 10-12 weeks. Angora rabbits produce one of the finest natural fibers (12-16 microns), and rabbit manure is uniquely "cold" — it can be applied directly to growing crops without composting.

## Meat Production

**Principle**: Rabbits convert forage and pellets to meat at 2.5-3.5:1 feed conversion ratio. A doe produces 4-6 litters per year of 6-12 kits each. Kits reach 2-2.5 kg slaughter weight in 10-12 weeks.

**Prerequisites**: Breeding doe and buck (at 5:1 ratio), wire cages with nest boxes, grass hay and pellets, water supply.

**Materials**: Hay, pellets (16-18% protein), nest box with straw/pine shavings, iodine for kit care.

**Procedure**:
1. Take doe to buck's cage for mating. Observe buck fall-off indicating successful mating.
2. Return doe to her cage. Gestation: 28-31 days.
3. Place nest box in doe's cage 3-5 days before due date (day 25-26). Doe pulls fur to line nest.
4. Kindling occurs at night, taking 10-30 minutes. Litter: 6-12 kits (average 8).
5. Check nest 12-24 hours after kindling. Remove dead kits. Count live kits.
6. Doe nurses 1-2 times daily (3-5 minutes per session). Rabbit milk: 12-13% protein, 13-15% fat.
7. Kits open eyes at 10-12 days. Begin eating pellets at 2-3 weeks.
8. Wean at 4-6 weeks. Rebreed doe 2-4 weeks post-weaning.
9. Feed fryers unlimited pellets + hay until 10-12 weeks (2-2.5 kg live weight).
10. Slaughter: cervical dislocation, skin, eviscerate, chill to 4°C within 1 hour.

**Expected yield**: 40 fryers per doe per year × 1.5 kg dressed = 60 kg dressed meat per doe. A 10-doe rabbitry produces 300 kg dressed meat per year from 15-25 m² of floor space.

**Strengths**:
- Shortest production cycle of any livestock — egg to table in 12-14 weeks
- Feed on hay and forage alone — no grain required for maintenance
- Rabbit manure applied directly to plants without composting (unique among livestock)
- Most space-efficient: 12-20 kg meat per m² of housing per year

**Weaknesses**:
- Fatal heat stress above 35°C — cooling systems mandatory in hot climates
- Enteritis (digestive disease) kills 50-80% in untreated outbreaks — fiber in diet is the primary defense
- Pasteurellosis ("snuffles") is nearly impossible to eradicate once established — cull affected animals
- Does cannibalize kits if underfed or stressed during kindling

## Angora Wool Production

**Principle**: Angora rabbits produce one of the finest natural fibers at 12-16 microns diameter — finer than cashmere. The fiber is 7× warmer than sheep wool by weight due to hollow core. Annual yield: 400-1,200 g per rabbit depending on breed.

**Prerequisites**: Angora rabbits (English, French, or Giant breed), daily grooming time (5-10 minutes per rabbit), blending fiber with sheep wool or alpaca for spinning.

**Materials**: Fine-toothed comb or hand shears, carding tools, blending fiber (sheep wool or alpaca), spinning wheel or drop spindle.

**Procedure**:
1. Pluck (hand-pull) loose mature fiber every 90-100 days (3-4 times/year). Rabbit naturally releases mature fiber.
2. Alternatively, shear with scissors every 90 days — faster but shorter, more variable fiber.
3. Angora must be blended with 50-80% sheep wool for spinning — pure Angora yarn is too fragile.
4. Daily grooming prevents wool block (gastric stasis from ingested fiber) — the leading cause of death in Angoras.

**Expected yield**: English Angora: 400-500 g/year. French Angora: 500-800 g/year. Giant Angora: 800-1,200 g/year.

**Strengths**:
- Finest diameter of any animal fiber (12-14 microns for English Angora)
- 7× warmer than sheep wool by weight — premium insulation
- One rabbit's annual yield makes 2-4 blended garments
- Harvesting by plucking causes no harm — rabbit releases mature fiber naturally

**Weaknesses**:
- Wool block kills Angoras if not groomed daily — fiber ingested during self-grooming blocks gut
- Pure Angora yarn is too fragile for practical use — must blend with other fibers
- English Angora requires daily grooming (5-10 min/rabbit) to prevent matting
- Heat stress risk higher than meat breeds — dense fleece reduces cooling

## Reproduction and Breeding

**Principle**: Does are induced ovulators — no estrus cycle, breeding can be scheduled at any time. Ovulation occurs 10-13 hours after mating. This enables year-round production with proper management.

**Prerequisites**: Mature doe (4-5 months small breeds, 6-7 months large) and buck (5-8 months). Separate cages. Nest box available 3-5 days before kindling.

**Materials**: Nest box, straw or pine shavings, record book for breeding dates.

**Procedure**:
1. Take doe to buck's cage (never reverse — does are territorial). Observe mating.
2. Palpate abdomen at 10-14 days to confirm pregnancy (grape-sized embryos).
3. Place nest box at day 25-26. Line with straw. Doe adds pulled fur.
4. Kindling at 28-31 days. Check nest within 24 hours.
5. Wean at 4-6 weeks. Rest doe 2-4 weeks before rebreeding.
6. Target: 4-6 litters per year (sustainable). 8+ litters shortens doe lifespan to 1-2 years.

**Expected yield**: 4-6 litters × 8 kits = 32-48 fryers per doe per year. Doe productive lifespan: 2-3 years.

**Strengths**:
- Induced ovulation allows breeding on demand — no heat detection needed
- Shortest gestation of any domestic mammal (28-31 days)
- Kits double birth weight in 6 days on concentrated rabbit milk
- Crossbreeding (NZ White × Californian) produces 10-15% faster growth via hybrid vigor

**Weaknesses**:
- Prolific reproduction burns out does quickly — 8+ litters/year shortens lifespan to 1-2 years
- First-time does may have smaller litters and poor nest-building
- Kits born naked and blind — die within hours without nest warmth
- Cannibalism risk if doe is underfed or stressed post-kindling

## Scaling Notes

A 10-doe rabbitry (1 buck) in 15-25 m² produces 300 kg dressed meat per year — 12-20 kg meat per m² of housing. No other livestock achieves this density. Stackable cages (2-3 tiers) fit a complete operation in a 2 × 3 m footprint.

**Minimum economic scale**: 2-3 does provide 6-12 fryers per month — 9-18 kg dressed meat monthly for a household.

**Scale bottlenecks**: Heat management is the primary constraint — temperatures above 30°C reduce fertility and above 35°C kill. Feed costs scale linearly. Slaughter processing takes 10-15 minutes per fryer.

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Probable Cause | Solution |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Doe not kindling | Overweight, too hot, old doe | Reduce pellets to 100 g/day; cool environment; replace does after 3 years |
| Kit mortality in first week | Cold nest, no fur lining, doe not nursing | Ensure nest box with straw; check doe is lactating; foster excess kits |
| Diarrhea (enteritis) | Sudden feed change, insufficient fiber, bacterial infection | Transition feeds over 5-7 days; unlimited grass hay; treat with sulfonamides |
| Snuffles (pasteurellosis) | Poor ventilation, ammonia, overcrowding | Improve airflow; reduce stocking; cull infected animals — nearly impossible to eradicate |
| Sore hocks | Wire floor without resting board | Add solid resting board covering 25-30% of floor; keep dry |
| Wool block (Angora) | Ingested fiber blocking gut | Daily grooming; unlimited hay; papaya enzyme weekly |
| Doe not kindling (no litter) | Overweight, too hot, or immature | Reduce feed 2 weeks before breeding; ensure temperature below 28°C; breed at 5-6 months of age |
| Kit mortality high (first week) | Doe not nursing or nest box inadequate | Check doe has milk (gently squeeze teat); provide clean straw nesting material; protect from drafts |
| Diarrhea in grow-out rabbits | Enteritis from overfeeding grain or coccidiosis | Reduce grain, increase hay; add oregano or thyme to water; ensure wire floors are clean |
| Ear mites (head shaking, crusts) | *Psoroptes cuniculi* infestation | Treat with mineral oil in both ears (suffocates mites); repeat in 10 days; clean cage |
| Heat stress (above 30°C) | Rabbits cannot sweat | Provide frozen water bottles; mist cages; move to underground or shaded area |
| Wool block (Angora, appetite loss) | Ingested fiber blocking gut | Feed papaya enzyme (breaks down fiber); increase roughage; shear rabbits every 3 months |

## Safety

**Handling**: Rabbits have powerful hind legs with sharp claws. An improperly restrained rabbit can kick hard enough to fracture its own spine. Always support the hindquarters when lifting — one hand under chest, the other under rump. Never pick up by ears or scruff alone. Rabbits are prey animals — approach calmly, avoid sudden movements.

**Zoonotic diseases**: Tularemia (*Francisella tularensis*) — transmitted by ticks and contact with infected rabbits. Wear gloves when processing. Cooking kills bacteria. Symptoms: fever, swollen lymph nodes, skin ulcer. Ringworm — fungal skin infection, more common in Angoras. Wear gloves when handling affected animals. Wash hands after handling rabbits or cleaning cages.

**Processing safety**: Dispatch by cervical dislocation requires proper technique to ensure humane death and avoid self-injury from sharp knives during processing.

## Quality Control

**Meat quality**: Dressed carcass should be 55-60% of live weight. Flesh light-colored (like chicken breast), mild flavor. Chill to 4°C within 1 hour of slaughter. Process fryers at 10-12 weeks — older than 14 weeks produces tougher meat.

**Fiber quality (Angora)**: Measure fiber diameter — target under 16 microns for premium grade. Staple length: 4-8 cm for plucked, shorter for sheared. Assess for matting, vegetable matter, and second cuts. Grade into spinning-ready and felting-grade categories.

**Breeding records**: Track breeding date, kindling date, litter size, mortality, weaning weight per doe. Cull does producing below 6 kits per litter or with kit mortality above 30%.

## Variations and Alternatives

**Cold climate**: Rabbits tolerate cold to -10°C with adequate bedding and shelter from wind. Deep straw in nest boxes insulates kits. Outdoor hutches with solid walls on three sides suffice. Freeze water bottles for summer cooling.

**Hot climate (above 30°C)**: Critical — rabbits cannot sweat and die of heat stroke above 35°C. Provide frozen water bottles in cages, misters, underground burrow systems, or move to cooled indoor spaces.

**Vermiculture integration**: Rabbit manure feeds red wiggler worms (*Eisenia fetida*) for premium worm castings. Worms self-harvest from beneath wire cage floors — zero additional labor.

**Guinea pig alternative**: In Andean regions, guinea pigs (*Cavia porcellus*) fill a similar niche at even smaller scale (0.5-1.2 kg adult weight) but with lower individual meat yield.
