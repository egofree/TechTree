Agriculture & Food Production

Agriculture & Food

Grain cultivation:

  • Crops: Wheat, barley, rye (temperate); rice, millet (subtropical); maize (Americas). Choose based on climate. Wheat and barley are most versatile for bread and brewing.
  • Land preparation: Clear fields, break soil with digging sticks → hoes (stone blades on handles) → iron plows (Metallurgy). One hectare feeds ~4-6 people per year with hand tools.
  • Planting: Broadcast sowing (scatter seed by hand, rake in) or drill sowing (poke holes with dibber, drop seed). Seed rate: ~50-100 kg/hectare for wheat.
  • Harvesting: Sickle (stone or iron blade, curved handle) cuts grain stalks. Threshing (beat with flail to separate grain from chaff). Winnowing (toss in wind — chaff blows away).
  • Yield targets: 1:5 to 1:15 seed-to-harvest ratio (ancient averages). Modern with no inputs: ~1-2 tonnes/hectare.

Animal domestication:

  • Cattle: Draft power (plowing, cart pulling), milk, meat, leather. Needs ~2 hectares pasture per animal.
  • Sheep/goats: Milk, meat, wool (sheep), hides. Hardy, browse poor vegetation. ~0.5 hectare each.
  • Pigs: Meat, fat (tallow for lubricants, soap). Eat waste, forage. Very efficient feed-to-meat ratio.
  • Poultry: Eggs, meat, pest control. Minimal feed requirements.
  • Horses/oxen: Primary motive power before steam. Oxen are stronger for plowing; horses are faster for transport. Both require ~5-10 kg forage daily.

Food preservation:

  • Drying: Sun-dry fruit, meat (jerky), grain. Simplest method. Moisture below 15% prevents spoilage.
  • Smoking: Meat/fish over wood fire at 50-80°C for hours to days. Combined with salting for long-term storage.
  • Salting: Salt (from sea water evaporation or salt deposits) draws moisture from food. 10-20% salt concentration preserves meat for months.
  • Fermentation: Vegetables (sauerkraut: 2-3% salt, anaerobic, 1-4 weeks), grain (beer: malted barley + water + yeast, 1-2 weeks), dairy (yogurt, cheese: lactic acid bacteria coagulate milk proteins).
  • Root cellaring: Cool (3-10°C), humid (85-95% RH), dark underground storage for root vegetables, apples, cabbage. Maintained by ventilation control.

Irrigation:

  • Shaduf: Counterweighted lever arm to lift water ~1-2 m. One operator lifts ~2,500 liters/hour.
  • Canal irrigation: Divert river water through channels. Gravity-fed. Requires surveying (leveling) to maintain flow.
  • Bucket chain: Row of people passing buckets. 5,000+ liters/hour with 10 people.

Crop rotation (3-field system):

  • Rotation: Divide arable land into three fields. Year 1: grain (wheat, rye) → Year 2: legume (beans, peas, lentils, clover) → Year 3: fallow (rest or green manure crop plowed under). Cycle repeats.
  • Nitrogen fixation: Legumes host Rhizobium bacteria in root nodules that fix atmospheric nitrogen into soil-available forms. A legume crop adds ~40-80 kg nitrogen/hectare. Eliminates need for synthetic fertilizer in a 3-field cycle.
  • Benefit: Maintains soil fertility indefinitely. Without rotation, continuous grain cropping depletes soil in 3-5 years, yields drop to uneconomic levels.

Soil management:

  • Composting: Pile plant waste, animal manure, and ash in layers (~1 m high). Turn every 2-4 weeks. Finished compost in 3-6 months (dark, crumbly, earthy smell). Apply 2-5 cm layer to fields before planting.
  • Green manure: Grow clover, vetch, or rye on fallow field, then plow under while green. Adds organic matter and nitrogen. Prevents erosion on idle fields.
  • pH testing: Boil red cabbage or certain lichens (Roccella) in water to create litmus solution. Soil mixed with solution turns red (acidic) or green (alkaline). Target pH 6.0-7.0 for most crops. Amend acidic soil with wood ash (raises pH).

Seed saving:

  • Selection criteria: Save seed from the best plants — tallest stalks, largest grain heads, earliest maturing, most disease-resistant. Mark selected plants before harvest with cordage ties.
  • Drying: Thresh and dry grain to 8-12% moisture content (bite test — seed should crack, not dent). Spread thin in sun for 2-3 days or in dry, ventilated area for 1-2 weeks.
  • Storage: Store in sealed clay pots, woven baskets lined with clay, or underground pits sealed with clay. Keep cool, dry, and rodent-proof. Viability: wheat/barley ~2-5 years stored properly.

Pest management:

  • Companion planting: Plant aromatic herbs (garlic, marigold, wormwood) among crops to deter insects. Beans fix nitrogen for neighboring corn (Three Sisters: corn + beans + squash).
  • Crop timing: Plant before or after peak pest emergence. Early planting lets crops establish before insect populations build.
  • Physical barriers: Netting over fruit bushes (woven cordage or split reed mats), moats around grain stores, ash rings around seedlings to deter slugs.

Granary construction:

  • Raised floor: Build on posts or stone pillars 30-50 cm above ground. Provides ventilation and rodent barrier. Floor: poles covered with woven mat and clay.
  • Rodent-proofing: Smooth stone or clay cone caps on support posts (rats cannot climb inverted cones). Tight-fitting lid. Thatched roof overhangs walls by 30+ cm to shed rain.
  • Capacity: 2×3 m granary stores ~1-2 tonnes grain (1-2 years food for a family). Build multiple for larger communities.

Beekeeping:

  • Log hives: Hollow a section of tree trunk (~80 cm long, ~30 cm diameter opening). Seal one end with wood disk, other end with removable cover. Hang in trees or place on platforms. Attract swarms with beeswax rubbed inside.
  • Honey extraction: Smoke hive entrance to calm bees (burn dried dung or rotten wood for cool smoke). Cut honeycomb with knife. Crush comb and strain through woven cloth. Yield: 10-20 kg honey per hive per year.
  • Wax harvesting: Melt crushed comb in water — wax floats to surface. Pour into molds. Beeswax used for candles, waterproofing, bronze casting investment, and sealant.
  • Pollination value: One hive pollinates crops in ~1 km radius. Crop yield increases of 20-30% near hives. Bees are the single most valuable livestock for agriculture per unit of care invested.
