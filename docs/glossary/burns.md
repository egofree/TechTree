# Burns

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: energy, glass, machine-tools

Glassblowing involves 800-2800°C material. Severe burns from brief contact with hot glass, torch flame, or radiant heat. Wear heat-resistant gloves (Kevlar or leather), safety glasses with side shields, long-sleeve cotton clothing. Hot glass looks identical to cold glass — assume ALL glass near the workstation is hot until proven otherwise.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Burns are a pervasive hazard wherever high-temperature processes occur. In [Glassblowing](../glass/glassblowing.md), molten glass at 800-2800°C causes severe deep burns. In [Charcoal Production](../energy/charcoal.md) and [Forming](../machine-tools/forming.md), hot metal and furnace radiation pose burn risks. In [Joining](../machine-tools/joining.md), welding sparks and molten metal create burn hazards. Burns represent the most common industrial injury across the entire tech tree, from fire-making to semiconductor fabrication.

## Technical Details

Burn severity scales with temperature and contact duration. First-degree burns (sunburn-like) involve the epidermis only. Second-degree burns blister and involve the dermis. Third-degree burns destroy both layers and may damage underlying tissue — these require immediate medical attention and risk infection.

In glassblowing, the hazard is compounded because hot glass looks identical to cold glass. A gather of molten glass at 1100°C and a cooled piece on the bench look the same. The standard safety protocol assumes all glass near the workstation is hot until proven otherwise. Heat-resistant gloves (Kevlar or leather), safety glasses with side shields, and long-sleeve cotton clothing provide baseline protection.

Prevention strategies include clear work zone delineation (hot area vs. cold area), tongs and tools long enough to maintain distance from heat sources, and organized workflows that minimize reaching across hot surfaces. First aid: cool burns immediately under running water for 20 minutes, cover with sterile dressing, never apply ice directly (causes tissue damage), and seek medical attention for anything beyond minor first-degree burns.
