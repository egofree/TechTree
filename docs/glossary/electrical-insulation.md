# Electrical Insulation

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: animals, polymers

Coil windings on motors, generators, and transformers were varnished with shellac (applied by dipping or brushing) from the 1880s through the 1940s. Shellac provides good insulation (dielectric strength 300-500 V/mil), adhesion to copper wire, and moisture resistance sufficient for indoor equipment. Wire enameling: early magnet wire was insulated by passing bare copper through a shellac solution bath and baking to evaporate solvent — multiple passes built up insulation thickness to 25-75 μm.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Electrical insulation is the enabling technology for all electromagnetic devices — motors, generators, transformers, solenoids, and wiring. Without reliable insulation between conductors, windings short-circuit and devices fail. The progression from shellac-varnished windings to modern polymer films tracks the development of the polymer industry. Silk paper (from sericulture) served as an early high-performance insulation material for transformer windings before synthetic alternatives.

## Technical Details

Shellac was the standard electrical insulation material from the 1880s through the 1940s. Its dielectric strength of 300-500 V/mil is adequate for most motor and transformer windings. Magnet wire was manufactured by passing bare copper through a shellac solution bath and baking to evaporate the solvent; multiple passes built up the insulation layer to 25-75 μm. The process is straightforward but requires careful control of solution viscosity and bake temperature to avoid pinholes.

Gutta-percha served as the insulation for the first submarine telegraph cables (starting 1850). Its properties were uniquely suited: thermoplastic (softens in hot water for extrusion around the conductor), waterproof, and electrically insulating. However, gutta-percha degrades in air through oxidation, so submarine cables stored the material underwater before deployment.

Natural rubber, after vulcanization with sulfur, provides good dielectric strength and flexibility for wire insulation and cable jacketing. Rubber-insulated wiring was common from the 1890s through the 1960s, when PVC and polyethylene replaced it.

Silk paper, produced by beating silk fibers into thin sheets, has higher dielectric strength than cotton paper and can be made thinner. Used as layer insulation in transformers and motor coils, this application persists in some high-voltage equipment. Silk's triangular fiber cross-section allows dense packing into thin, strong insulating sheets.

Modern insulation (from the 1950s onward) uses polyimide film (Kapton), polyester film (Mylar), and polyurethane or polyesterimide wire enamels — all requiring mature polymer chemistry.
