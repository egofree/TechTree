# Silica Dust Inhalation

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, glass, silicon

The occupational hazard of breathing crystalline SiO₂ particles generated during silica sand handling, glass batching, and silicon production. Causes silicosis — progressive, irreversible scarring of lung tissue.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Silica dust inhalation is a specific hazard in [Pottery](../ceramics/pottery.md) (clay mixing), [Basic Glass](../glass/basic.md) (raw material handling), and [Mg-Si Production](../silicon/mg-si-production.md) (quartz reduction). Raw silica sand (>95% purity SiO₂) produces respirable crystalline silica dust during weighing, sieving (60-100 mesh), mixing, and crushing cullet.

## Technical Details

The danger comes from particles smaller than 10 µm that reach the alveoli deep in the lungs. Macrophages attempt to engulf and remove these particles but are destroyed by the sharp crystalline surfaces. The resulting inflammatory cascade produces fibrotic scar tissue, reducing gas exchange capacity. Symptoms appear years after exposure: shortness of breath, chronic cough, increased susceptibility to tuberculosis.

Prevention hierarchy: (1) substitution — use less hazardous materials where possible; (2) engineering controls — wet methods, local exhaust ventilation, enclosed processes; (3) administrative controls — exposure limits, rotation; (4) personal protective equipment — P100 respirators as last resort.

In glass production, wetting the batch materials before mixing suppresses dust. In ceramics, glaze mixing should be done with wet rather than dry ingredients. In silicon production, quartz crushing and sizing must be done with water spray or enclosed with dust extraction.
