# Prevention

> **Type**: practice | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: animals, health

Exclude cats from direct contact with stored grain (cats patrol the perimeter, not the grain pile). Wear gloves and an N95 mask when cleaning rodent-contaminated areas. Do not use poultry manure from pest-control flocks directly on edible crops without 90-day composting at 55°C+ to kill pathogens.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Prevention — proactive measures to avoid hazards before they cause harm — is a recurring principle across the tech tree. In pest management (documented within [Animal Husbandry](../animals/animal-husbandry.md)), prevention means excluding pests from food stores rather than relying on elimination after infestation. In [Pharmacology](../health/pharmacology.md), prevention of contamination during drug preparation prevents patient harm. In industrial safety, prevention of exposure to hazardous materials (silica dust, chemical fumes, molten metal) through engineering controls and PPE is always preferred over treating injuries after they occur.

## Technical Details

The hierarchy of controls, from most to least effective: elimination (remove the hazard), substitution (replace with something less hazardous), engineering controls (isolate people from the hazard), administrative controls (change how people work), and PPE (protect the worker). Prevention in the tech tree emphasizes the first three — designing hazards out of processes rather than managing their consequences.

For pest management, prevention means physical barriers (sealed grain storage, raised platforms), biological controls (cats for rodent perimeter patrol, owls in barns), and sanitation (remove harborage, deny food sources). These are more effective and sustainable than reactive poisoning. The rule is clear: cats patrol the perimeter but must not enter grain piles (their feces contaminate food), and poultry manure from pest-control flocks requires 90 days of thermophilic composting at 55°C+ before application to edible crops to destroy *Salmonella*, *Campylobacter*, and parasitic ova.
