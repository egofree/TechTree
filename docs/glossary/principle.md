# Principle

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: foundational | **Domains**: computing, transport, silicon, vlsi-scaling

RF plasma (typically 13.56 MHz) generates reactive species (ions, radicals) from feed gases. A DC self-bias develops on the powered electrode, accelerating positive ions into the wafer surface, combining chemical etching with physical sputtering to achieve anisotropic etching with vertical sidewalls.

## Context in the Tech Tree

A principle is the underlying physical, chemical, or mathematical mechanism that makes a technology work. Understanding principles enables adaptation and troubleshooting when standard procedures fail. In [Telegraph Communication](../transport/telegraph.md), the principle is electromagnetic induction — current in a wire produces a magnetic field that actuates a remote receiver. In semiconductor etching ([Advanced Processes](../vlsi-scaling/advanced-processes.md)), the principle of reactive-ion etching combines chemical and physical mechanisms for directional material removal. In [Silicon Purification](../silicon/purification.md), the principle of fractional distillation exploits boiling point differences to separate compounds.

## Technical Details

Principles in the tech tree span several categories. Electromagnetic principles govern telegraphy (Ohm's law, electromagnetic induction), electrical generation (Faraday's law), and motor operation (Lorentz force). Chemical principles include reduction-oxidation (smelting), fractional distillation (chlorosilane purification), and catalysis (steam reforming, Haber process). Thermal principles drive blast furnaces (counter-current heat exchange), kilns (radiative and convective heat transfer), and heat treatment (phase transformations in steel).

The reactive-ion etching principle illustrates how understanding physics enables engineering: chemical etching alone is isotropic (etches equally in all directions), producing undercut features unsuitable for fine patterns. By adding directional ion bombardment (the DC self-bias accelerates ions vertically), the chemical reaction is enhanced in the vertical direction and suppressed on sidewalls, producing the anisotropic profiles required for sub-micron circuit features.
