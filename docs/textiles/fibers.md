### Fiber Sources

**Flax (linen)**:
- **Cultivation**: Annual plant, ~1 m tall. Sow in spring, harvest ~100 days later when stalks yellow at bottom third. Pull entire plant (don't cut — longer fibers from root end).
- **Retting** (separating fiber from stem): Submerge flax stalks in water (pond, stream, or dew-retting on grass) for 5-14 days. Bacteria decompose pectin binding fibers to woody core (shives). Test: when fibers separate easily from stem, retting is complete. Over-retting weakens fibers.
- **Breaking**: Crush dried retted stalks with wooden breaker (flax brake — hinged wooden blades that snap the woody core). Shives break into short pieces, fibers remain long.
- **Scutching**: Beat broken stalks with flat wooden knife against vertical board. Knock away remaining woody fragments.
- **Hackling (combing)**: Draw fibers through graduated iron or thorn combs (hackles). Coarse hackle removes remaining shives. Fine hackle separates long line fibers (30-90 cm) from short tow (5-15 cm). Line fibers become high-quality linen; tow becomes coarse cloth or rope.
- **Yield**: ~200-400 kg fiber per hectare. Long line fiber ~30% of total; tow ~20%.

**Hemp**:
- **Cultivation**: Annual, 2-4 m tall. Extremely hardy, minimal water. Harvest at pollen shedding (before seeds mature for best fiber). Processing identical to flax: ret (5-10 days in water), break, scutch, hackle.
- **Properties**: Stronger than flax, more rot-resistant, coarser. Best for rope, canvas, and heavy cloth. Historically the primary cordage fiber before synthetic ropes.
- **Yield**: ~1-3 tonnes dry stalk per hectare → ~300-800 kg fiber.

**Cotton**:
- **Cultivation**: Requires 150-180 frost-free days + moderate water. Warm climate crop. Harvest bolls when they open (fibers emerge white and fluffy).
- **Ginning**: Separate seeds from fiber. Simple single-roller gin (wooden roller rotates against fixed blade — fiber passes, seeds are crushed and fall away). Whitney-style saw gin (circular saw teeth pull fiber through grid too narrow for seeds — much faster but damages fibers).
- **Properties**: Soft, comfortable, absorbs moisture. Moderate strength when dry, weaker when wet (opposite of linen and hemp). Poor mildew resistance.
- **Yield**: ~500-1000 kg lint cotton per hectare.

**Wool**:
- **Shearing**: Clip fleece from sheep annually (spring). 2-5 kg fleece per sheep. Sorted by quality — shoulder and sides are finest, breech (hindquarters) coarsest.
- **Scouring**: Remove lanolin (natural wool grease, ~30-50% of greasy fleece weight), dirt, vegetable matter. Wash in hot water (50-60°C) with potash (potassium carbonate) or soap. Multiple washes until water runs clear. Lanolin recovered from wash water by skimming cooled surface — useful for waterproofing, lubricants, and skin protection.
- **Carding vs combing**: Two distinct preparation methods yielding different yarn types. **Carding**: Tease scoured wool between two carding paddles (leather pads covered with bent wire teeth). Disentangles fibers, removes remaining debris, aligns roughly for spinning. Produces rolag (rolled cylinder of fiber) → woolen yarn (fluffy, warm, fuzzy surface, shorter fibers). **Combing**: Draw long-staple wool through metal comb rows (wool combs). Aligns fibers parallel, removes short noils. Produces top (parallel fiber strand) → worsted yarn (smooth, dense, strong, lustrous, longer fibers).

**Silk** (*Bombyx mori*):
- **Cultivation**: Domesticated silkworms fed exclusively on white mulberry leaves. Larvae spin cocoons over 3-4 days. Each cocoon contains a single continuous filament 600-900 m long. Requires controlled temperature (23-25°C) and humidity.
- **Cocoon processing**: Kill pupae by steaming or baking cocoons at 60-80°C (prevents moth from emerging and breaking filament). Sort cocoons by quality — discard stained, pierced, or double cocoons.
- **Degumming**: Boil cocoons in water with sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃, 5-10 g/L) at 80-90°C for 1-2 hours. Removes sericin (gummy protein binding filaments, ~20-30% of raw weight). Fibers become lustrous and soft. Degumming waste (sericin) recoverable as adhesive.
- **Properties**: Extremely fine — individual fiber 0.3-0.5 dtex (decitex, grams per 10,000 m). Tensile strength ~500 MPa. Smooth, triangular cross-section refracts light → characteristic luster. Poor wet strength (loses ~15-20% when damp). Damaged by strong alkalis and prolonged sunlight.

**Nettle fiber** (common stinging nettle, *Urtica dioica*):
- **Harvesting**: Cut stems in late summer when mature but before heavy frost. Strip leaves (wear gloves — stinging hairs contain formic acid and histamine).
- **Retting**: Bundle stems and submerge in water for 2-4 weeks (or dew-ret on grass for 4-6 weeks). Bacteria decompose pectin binding bark fibers to woody core. Test by checking if fibers peel away cleanly from stem.
- **Decortication**: Beat or crush dried retted stems to break woody core. Peel bark strips containing fiber bundles. Scrape away remaining woody fragments with blunt blade.
- **Properties**: Similar to linen — strong, long-staple fiber. Traditionally used for sailcloth, fishing nets, and coarse cloth in northern Europe before flax became dominant. Yields less than flax per hectare but grows wild without cultivation.

**Sinew** (animal tendon):
- **Source**: Leg tendons and long backstrap tendons from deer, elk, cattle. Harvest from fresh carcass — peel away surrounding tissue while tendon is still wet.
- **Processing**: Dry tendons completely (air dry or by low heat). Pound dried tendon with smooth stone or wooden mallet until it separates into individual fibers. Fibers are white, translucent, and extremely strong in tension.
- **Use**: Not spun conventionally — dampen to make flexible, then wrap around bindings (arrow fletching, tool hafting, seam reinforcement). Dries hard and shrinks tight, creating extremely strong join. Primary sewing thread for leather before manufactured thread.

### Fiber Identification Tests

Quick field tests to identify unknown fibers:

- **Burn test**: Hold fiber to flame, observe behavior, smell smoke, examine ash.
  - **Cellulose fibers** (cotton, linen, hemp): Ignite quickly, burn with bright flame, smell like burning paper. Leave light, fluffy gray ash that crumbles.
  - **Protein fibers** (wool, silk): Curl away from flame, burn slowly with sputtering, smell like burning hair or feathers. Leave dark, brittle bead that crushes to powder.
  - **Synthetic fibers** (not applicable at bootstrap stage, but for reference): Melt and bead up. Nylon smells like celery; polyester smells sweet. Hard bead residue.

### Cordage & Rope Making

The domain is "Fiber & Cordage" — rope is the engineered application of prepared fibers. Rope transforms fiber tensile strength into useful mechanical work: lifting, pulling, mooring, lashing, rigging.

**Best rope fibers**:

- **Hemp**: Strongest traditional rope fiber (~50 MPa tensile). Long staple length, naturally resistant to rot and UV. Historically the dominant rope fiber for maritime and industrial use. Becomes stronger when wet (unique among plant fibers).
- **Manila/abaca** (Musa textilis): Strong, flexible, salt-water resistant — the classic maritime rope fiber. Lighter than hemp, doesn't swell significantly when wet. Sourced from leaf fibers of a banana relative, primarily grown in Philippines.
- **Sisal** (Agave sisalana): Moderate strength, widely available in tropical/subtropical regions. Stiffer and less flexible than hemp or manila. Acceptable for general-purpose rope. Shorter fiber length than hemp.
- **Cotton**: Soft but weak — light duty only. Comfortable to handle, good for clothesline and light binding. Poor wet strength, prone to rot.
- **Jute**: Weakest common rope fiber, biodegrades rapidly. Suitable for sacking, twine, and temporary bindings. Cheap and widely available (India, Bangladesh).

**Fiber preparation for ropemaking**: Hackle or comb fibers to align parallel and remove short pieces (same tools as textile fiber preparation). Spin fibers into yarn by twisting — use fingers, drop spindle, or spinning wheel. Z-twist (clockwise when viewed from above) is conventional for single yarn in Western ropemaking tradition. Multiple yarns twisted together form a strand — the intermediate building block between yarn and rope.

**Rope laying (3-strand, most common method)**:

Take 3 strands and twist them together in the opposite direction to the yarn twist. If yarn is Z-twist, lay rope S-twist (counter-clockwise), or vice versa. This counter-twist creates a balanced rope that will not kink, curl, or untwist under load — the fundamental principle of rope mechanics.

**Traditional tool — ropemaking jack**: Wooden frame with 3 rotating hooks on one end (one per strand), driven by a hand crank. Process: Hook 3 strands to the 3 rotating hooks on one jack. Attach other ends to a single hook on a second jack (or fixed anchor point). Walk backward paying out strands while cranking the hooks. Maintain even tension on all three strands. The strands naturally wrap around each other to form a round, balanced rope as they twist. Two-person operation minimum — one cranking, one walking back and guiding.

**Ropewalk method**: Traditional long-ropemaking facility. Yarns are stretched the full length of the ropewalk (up to 300 m, historically up to 370 m at Portsmouth). Strands are laid by walking the length while turning. Produces the longest possible ropes — limited only by building length. Historical naval ropewalks were the longest industrial buildings of their era.

**Properties — breaking strength by diameter** (approximate, for hemp):

- 12 mm diameter → ~10 kN breaking strength
- 24 mm diameter → ~40 kN breaking strength
- 36 mm diameter → ~90 kN breaking strength

Strength scales roughly with cross-sectional area (πr²). Manila ~80% of hemp. Sisal ~60% of hemp.

**Working load**: 1/5 to 1/10 of breaking strength. Safety factor 5:1 for general applications, 10:1 for lifting loads and life-safety situations (hoisting people, critical rigging). Always apply safety factors — fiber degradation, knots, shock loading, and UV exposure all reduce real-world strength.

**Knots reduce strength 30-50%** at the knot location due to sharp bends and fiber compression. Splicing is strongly preferred for permanent connections — a proper splice retains 80-95% of rope strength.

**Splicing techniques**:

- **Eye splice**: Unlay (untwist) the rope end for ~30 cm. Tuck each of the 3 strands over and under through the body of the rope, following the lay. 3-4 full tucks per strand. Creates a permanent loop — stronger than any knot. Essential for attaching rope to fittings (thimbles, hooks, rings).
- **Back splice**: Unlay rope end, form a crown knot with the 3 strands (each strand passes over its neighbor), then tuck each strand back down through the rope body. Prevents rope end from unraveling. Adds bulk — cannot pass through pulleys.
- **Short splice**: Unlay both rope ends, interleave the 6 strands, tuck each through the opposing rope body. Joins two ropes permanently. Doubles diameter at the join — will not pass through pulleys or fairleads.

**Tarring**: Brush hot pine tar (distilled pine resin) onto finished rope. The tar saturates the fibers, making the rope weather-resistant, rot-resistant, and more water-repellent. Essential for maritime ropes exposed to constant moisture. Trade-offs: tarred rope is darker, stiffer, heavier, and harder on hands. Apply by laying rope over a tarring kettle and brushing hot tar along the length.

**Applications**:

- **Lifting** (cranes, hoists, block and tackle): Use highest safety factor (10:1). Splice all attachments — never knot. Inspect regularly for wear, chafe, and UV degradation.
- **Maritime** (rigging, mooring lines, anchor rodes, sails): Saltwater environment demands hemp, manila, or tarred rope. Replace lines showing significant fiber breakage or flexibility loss.
- **Construction** (scaffolding, lifting, lashing, guy wires): Rope lashings for scaffolding — clove hitch and square lashing. Guy wires for temporary structures.
- **Transport** (towing, cargo securing, wagon lashings): Tow ropes need stretch resistance — hemp over cotton. Cargo lashings need reliable knots or splices.
- **Agriculture** (hay lifting, animal restraint, fencing, bindery twine): Sisal acceptable for most agricultural uses. Jute twine for binding sheaves and bales.


### Safety & Hazards

- **Fiber dust (byssinosis)**: Cotton and other plant fiber processing generates fine dust. Chronic inhalation causes byssinosis ("brown lung") — chest tightness, reduced lung function. Ventilation in processing areas. Respirators when dust is visible.
- **Chemical retting hazards**: Retting (decomposing pectin to separate fibers) uses stagnant water — bacterial and fungal exposure risk. Alkaline retting uses NaOH or Ca(OH)₂ — caustic, eye damage. Wear gloves and eye protection.
- **Scutching and hackling injuries**: Beating and combing fibers uses sharp metal tools (hackles have long sharp teeth). Laceration risk. Handle hackles carefully, store with protective covers. Cut-resistant gloves recommended.
- **Chemical processing**: Mercerization uses concentrated NaOH (caustic burns). Fiber bleaching uses chlorine compounds or hydrogen peroxide (chemical burns, oxygen enrichment fire risk). PPE for all chemical treatments.
