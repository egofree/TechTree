# Scale

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, computing

The physical size, capacity, and throughput range of industrial equipment and computing devices. Scale determines whether a process can handle laboratory quantities or production volumes, and in computing, how many operations can be performed simultaneously.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Scale considerations appear in [Kilns](../ceramics/kilns.md), where firing chamber volume determines throughput, and across [Computing](../computing/electronic.md) hardware from single integrators to multi-rack systems. The MIT Differential Analyzer (1931) had 6 integrators and solved 6th-order equations; later versions expanded to 18 integrators. Setup time scales with complexity — hours to days to reconfigure interconnected mechanical computing elements.

## Technical Details

In ceramics, kiln scale ranges from small pit kilns (0.1 m³) for test firings to industrial tunnel kilns (100+ m³) for continuous production. Temperature uniformity becomes harder at larger scales, requiring more sophisticated flue design and burner placement.

In mechanical computing, each additional integrator expands the order of differential equation that can be solved but adds mechanical complexity and alignment requirements. Electronic computing scales differently: transistor count doubles roughly every two years (Moore's Law), and architectural improvements (parallel processing, pipelining) extract more computation per transistor.

In mining and metallurgy, scale determines whether a process is batch or continuous, and whether capital investment in large equipment is justified by throughput needs.
