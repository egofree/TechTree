# Advantages

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: animals, ceramics, chemistry, computing, energy, foundations, gas-handling, health, knowledge, machine-tools, metals, polymers, silicon

Simple, low-cost tooling relative to injection molding (no runner system, lower pressures, simpler mold construction — no side actions needed for parts with straight-pull geometry). Minimal material waste (no runners or gates — only flash at the parting line, typically <1% of part weight). Can mold very large parts (compression presses up to 2000+ tons can mold parts 1-2 m in diameter). Works with both thermoplastics and thermosets. Excellent for fiber-reinforced thermosets.

## Technical Details

In the bootstrap context, evaluating advantages means weighing trade-offs with limited industrial infrastructure. Compression molding is advantageous because it requires simpler tooling than injection molding — no runner system, lower clamping pressures, and mold construction that avoids side actions for straight-pull parts. Material waste stays below 1% of part weight (only flash at the parting line). Presses up to 2000+ tons can produce parts 1-2 m in diameter.

Beyond polymer processing, advantage analysis applies to every fork in the bootstrap path: blast furnace vs. bloomery for iron, Solvay vs. Leblanc for soda ash, absorption vs. vapor-compression for refrigeration. The consistent pattern is selecting the method whose advantages — lower capital cost, fewer dependencies, simpler tooling, broader material compatibility — align with the currently available industrial base. Advantage evaluation also considers energy inputs, labor requirements, and the skill ceiling needed for successful operation.
