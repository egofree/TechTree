# Cultivation

> **Type**: material | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: plants, textiles

Annual plant, ~1 m tall. Sow in spring, harvest ~100 days later when stalks yellow at bottom third. Pull entire plant (don't cut — longer fibers from root end). Plant density affects fiber quality: dense sowing (150-200 kg seed/hectare) produces thin stalks with fine fiber; sparse sowing produces coarse fiber but more seed yield.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Cultivation encompasses the agricultural practices for growing fiber crops, dye plants, and medicinal plants — the biological feedstocks for textiles, colorants, and pharmaceuticals. The specific cultivation parameters (sowing density, harvest timing, climate requirements) directly determine the quality and yield of downstream products. Fiber crop cultivation ranges from zero-effort foraging (nettle) to intensive agriculture (cotton).

## Technical Details

Flax cultivation illustrates the precision required: plants must be pulled entire (not cut) because the longest fibers run from root to tip. Harvest timing is critical — stalks should yellow at the bottom third before pulling. Sowing density is a deliberate trade-off: dense planting (150-200 kg seed/ha) forces plants to grow tall and thin with fine fibers suitable for fine linen; sparse planting produces thicker stalks with coarser fiber but higher seed yield for linseed oil.

Hemp cultivation differs markedly: plants reach 2-4 m, are extremely hardy with minimal water requirements, and are harvested at pollen shedding before seed maturity for best fiber quality. Processing is identical to flax (ret, break, scutch, hackle).

Dye plant cultivation integrates into existing agricultural patterns. Woad (blue dye) is a hardy biennial sown at 2-3 kg/ha, yielding 3-5 tonnes fresh leaves per hectare from first-year rosettes. Madder (red dye) is a perennial requiring 2-3 years before roots accumulate sufficient dye content, then productive for 5-10 years. Weld (yellow dye) tolerates poor, chalky marginal land unsuitable for food crops.

Medicinal plant cultivation requires attention to harvest timing for maximum active compound concentration, and purity assessment for contamination from soil heavy metals, processing equipment, or nearby industrial activity.
