# Oxidation

> **Type**: process | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, health, metals, polymers

A chemical reaction in which a substance loses electrons, typically through combination with oxygen. In the tech tree, oxidation is both a useful process (steel decarburization in the Bessemer converter, copper refining, glass batch oxidation of impurities) and a degradation mechanism (gutta-percha insulation oxidizes in air, becoming brittle; iron rusts; pharmaceutical compounds decompose). Atmospheric oxygen slowly oxidizes gutta-percha surfaces — but submerged in seawater, degradation is extremely slow, as cables recovered after 50+ years on the ocean floor showed intact insulation.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Oxidation appears in every domain that handles reactive materials. In metals, controlled oxidation removes carbon from pig iron to produce steel (the Bessemer process blows air through molten iron, oxidizing carbon to CO). Copper fire refining oxidizes impurities, which are either volatilized or absorbed into slag. In ceramics, kiln atmosphere (oxidizing or reducing) determines glaze color and clay body properties. In health, pharmaceutical oxidation degrades active compounds, requiring storage in cool, dark, dry conditions and sometimes inert-gas packaging.

The polymers domain addresses oxidation as a degradation pathway for natural rubber, gutta-percha, and synthetic polymers. Antioxidant additives (phenols, amines) are incorporated into polymer formulations to slow oxidative aging. UV exposure accelerates oxidation through free-radical chain reactions.

## Technical Details

Oxidation in metallurgical contexts is controlled through atmosphere management. In an oxidizing atmosphere (excess air), carbon, silicon, and manganese in molten iron are preferentially oxidized, converting them to CO, SiO₂, and MnO — the basis of steelmaking. In a reducing atmosphere (limited air, carbon monoxide dominant), iron oxide ores are reduced to metallic iron — the basis of smelting. The balance between oxidation and reduction at the metal-slag interface determines the final composition of the product.

Corrosion — the gradual oxidation of metals in service — is managed through protective coatings (paint, galvanizing, chrome plating), sacrificial anodes (zinc blocks that corrode preferentially), alloy selection (stainless steels form self-healing chromium oxide layers), and environmental control (dehumidification, inert gas blanketing). The cost of corrosion management is a significant fraction of industrial infrastructure maintenance.
