### Overview

Pigs are the most efficient converter of feed to meat among domesticated livestock. They produce 70-80% of their live weight as usable carcass (compared to 55-60% for cattle), and they consume virtually anything organic — crop residues, kitchen scraps, whey from cheese making, culled fruits and vegetables, and forest mast (acorns, beechnuts). Mature weight ranges from 150-300 kg for modern breeds, 100-180 kg for traditional landraces. Gestation is 114 days ("three months, three weeks, three days"), with litter sizes of 8-12 piglets for improved breeds and 6-8 for traditional types.

Pigs reach market weight (90-110 kg) in just 5-7 months — the fastest turnaround of any major livestock species. This rapid growth, combined with high feed conversion efficiency (2.5-3.5 kg feed per kg of gain), makes pigs the premier meat-producing animal for a bootstrapping settlement. A single sow producing two litters per year (16-24 piglets) yields 1,500-2,500 kg of pork annually — enough protein to sustain a small community.

Beyond meat, pigs produce lard (15-30 kg per animal for fat-type breeds) — a versatile cooking fat, lubricant, and industrial material. Bristles are used for brushes. Pigskin makes durable leather. And pig manure (5-10 kg/day, high in nitrogen and phosphorus) is an excellent fertilizer. The pig's role as a waste recycler is perhaps its most underappreciated function: a pig converts food waste and agricultural byproducts into edible protein, closing nutrient loops in a settlement's food system.

### Breed Types and Selection

**Lard breeds** (historically selected for fat production):
- **Berkshire**: Black with white points (feet, face, tail). Compact, early-maturing. Produces well-marbled meat with superior flavor — the preferred breed for premium pork in Japan (Kurobuta). Moderate fat coverage. Berkshire pork commands a premium for its taste and texture. Mature weight 200-250 kg. Good mothers with 8-10 piglets per litter. Hardy and adaptable to outdoor systems.
- **Gloucestershire Old Spot**: White with black spots, large drooping ears. A traditional orchard pig — developed to clean up fallen fruit in cider orchards. Excellent foragers on pasture. Docile temperament. Produces high-quality pork and good lard yields. Hardy in cold, wet conditions.

**Bacon breeds** (selected for length and lean meat):
- **Tamworth**: Ginger-red, long-bodied, erect ears. The most "primitive" looking of British breeds — long snout, lean build. Exceptional foraging ability — one of the best breeds for woodland and pasture systems. Produces long sides ideal for bacon curing. Hardier and more disease-resistant than most modern breeds. Litter size 6-10. Slower growing than commercial hybrids (market weight at 8-10 months) but thriving on low-quality feed.
- **Large White (Yorkshire)**: All white, erect ears, long body. The most numerous pig breed worldwide. Large litters (10-14 piglets). Fast growth. The foundation of commercial hybrid pig production — Large White × Landrace crosses dominate industrial systems. Good mothers. Less hardy for outdoor systems than traditional breeds.

**Modern commercial breeds**:
- **Landrace**: White, lop-eared, long-bodied. Excellent maternal breed with large litters and good milking ability. Used primarily as a dam line in crossbreeding programs. Docile and easy to manage.
- **Duroc**: Red-brown, drooping ears, muscular. Used as a terminal sire in crossbreeding — Duroc genetics add growth rate, feed efficiency, and meat quality (especially marbling) to commercial crossbred pigs. Hardy and adaptable. Faster-growing than traditional breeds.

**Selection for bootstrapping**:
- Traditional and heritage breeds excel in low-input, pasture-based systems. They are hardier, better foragers, and more disease-resistant than commercial hybrids.
- Berkshire or Tamworth for outdoor/pasture systems with good flavor
- Gloucestershire Old Spot for orchard and woodland integration
- Crossbreeding (e.g., Berkshire boar × Large White sows) captures hybrid vigor (heterosis) — crossbred pigs grow 10-15% faster and have better survival than purebreds
- Avoid extreme commercial genotypes that require intensive management and high-quality feed to perform

### Farrowing Management

Farrowing (giving birth) is the most critical management period. Piglets are born with minimal body fat and are vulnerable to chilling, crushing, and starvation. Mortality in the first 3 days accounts for 60-80% of all pre-weaning losses.

**Farrowing pen design**:
Provide a dedicated farrowing pen 3-4 m². Install guard rails (steel pipes or wooden rails) along the pen walls, 20-25 cm off the ground and 15-20 cm out from the wall. These create a safe zone where piglets can escape if the sow lies down against the wall — the most common cause of piglet death is crushing. A heated creep area (a corner or side area with a heat lamp or heating pad, accessible to piglets but not the sow) attracts piglets away from the sow, further reducing crushing risk. Maintain creep temperature at 30-34°C for newborns (day 1), decreasing to 25-28°C by day 7.

**Pre-farrowing preparation**:
- Move the sow to the farrowing pen 5-7 days before expected farrowing date (day 107-109 of gestation). This allows her to settle and reduces stress.
- Provide clean, dry bedding (straw or shredded paper). Remove soiled bedding.
- Have iodine solution (7%) ready for navel treatment.
- Monitor for signs of impending farrowing: restlessness, nesting behavior (rooting and rearranging bedding), milk letdown (can be expressed from teats 12-24 hours before farrowing), increased respiration.

**During farrowing**:
- Piglets are delivered at 10-20 minute intervals. Total farrowing duration: 2-6 hours (longer intervals may indicate problems — intervene if more than 45-60 minutes pass between piglets and the sow is straining without results).
- Clear the fetal membrane from each piglet's nose immediately to prevent suffocation. Dry the piglet vigorously with a clean cloth or straw to stimulate breathing and circulation.
- Dip the navel in 7% iodine solution to prevent bacterial infection.
- Ensure each piglet nurses colostrum within 2 hours. Colostrum provides essential antibodies and energy. Piglets are born with no brown fat and minimal glycogen reserves — they must nurse quickly or become hypoglycemic and die.
- Clip needle teeth (the eight sharp "wolf teeth" at birth) with side-cutting pliers to prevent injury to the sow's udder and to littermates. This is a routine procedure in managed farrowing.
- If litter size exceeds functional teats, foster excess piglets to another sow that farrowed within 24-48 hours (rub the fostered piglets with the foster sow's birth fluids or urine to mask their scent).

### Creep Feeding and Weaning

**Creep feeding**:
Introduce a high-palatability starter feed in the creep area at 2-3 weeks of age. The creep area is accessible only to piglets (opening too small for the sow). Starter feed should be highly digestible: cooked cereals, milk powder, or soy-based pellets. Piglets explore solid food gradually while still nursing. Creep feeding accelerates gut development and reduces the growth check at weaning. Provide fresh feed daily in small quantities to maintain palatability.

**Weaning**:
Traditional weaning at 6-8 weeks allows natural immune development and gut maturation. Commercial systems wean at 3-4 weeks for production efficiency but require high-quality starter feeds and strict hygiene. At weaning, separate piglets from the sow and move to a clean nursery pen. Expect a temporary growth slowdown (the "weaning check") lasting 5-7 days as piglets adjust from milk to solid feed. Provide palatable, easily digestible feed during this transition.

### Pasture-Based and Forest Systems

**Pasture-based systems**:
Pigs on pasture need less purchased feed, produce healthier meat with higher omega-3 fatty acids, and distribute manure directly onto the land. They root (dig) naturally — this can be beneficial (turning and aerating soil, clearing roots and grubs) or destructive (destroying established pasture if left too long in one area).

**Management**:
- Use portable electric fencing (2-3 strands at 30-50 cm height, energized to 3,000-5,000 V) to create paddocks. Rotate pigs to fresh ground every 1-2 weeks.
- Stocking rate: 10-15 growing pigs per hectare on good pasture, fewer on marginal land.
- Provide wallows (mud baths) in temperatures above 25°C — pigs cannot sweat and rely on evaporative cooling from wet skin. A wallow prevents heat stress.
- Supplement pasture with grain or protein feed (pigs cannot thrive on grass alone — they need concentrated energy and protein sources). Typical supplement: 1-2 kg grain per growing pig per day on good pasture.
- Portable shelters (A-frame huts, calf hutches, or simple tarp structures) provide shade and weather protection in each paddock.

**Forest pannage** (traditional woodland grazing):
Pannage is the practice of releasing domestic pigs into oak and beech forests in autumn to feed on fallen acorns and beechnuts (mast). This tradition dates to antiquity and persists in parts of Europe (notably the New Forest in England and the dehesa system in Spain for Iberian ham production).

Benefits of pannage:
- Pigs convert forest mast (which is toxic to cattle and horses in quantity due to tannins) into high-quality pork
- Clearing acorns protects other livestock from poisoning
- The meat from mast-fed pigs develops superior flavor and fat quality — acorn-fed pork produces the world's most prized hams (jamón ibérico de bellota)
- Pigs root and clear underbrush, reducing wildfire fuel
- No supplemental feed required during the mast season (2-3 months in autumn)

Stocking rate for pannage: 3-5 pigs per hectare of productive oak/beech forest, depending on mast yield. A single mature oak tree can drop 20-50 kg of acorns per year.

### Waste Feeding and Nutrient Cycling

Pigs are nature's recyclers. They consume food waste and agricultural byproducts that would otherwise be discarded:

- **Kitchen scraps**: Vegetable peelings, fruit waste, plate scrapings, stale bread. Avoid meat scraps from other pigs (disease transmission risk, especially African swine fever and classical swine fever) and excessive salt.
- **Whey**: The liquid byproduct of cheese making. Feeding whey to pigs is one of the oldest integrated farming practices — dairy and pig production are naturally complementary. Whey contains protein (0.6-0.8%), lactose (4-5%), and minerals. Pigs on whey require less grain supplement.
- **Crop residues**: Cull fruits and vegetables from gardens and orchards, crop harvest byproducts (pea vines, bean pods, corn stalks chopped, sugar beet pulp). Not all crop residues are suitable — some are too fibrous or low in digestible nutrients without processing.
- **Brewery and distillery spent grain**: Moderate protein (18-26% on a dry matter basis), high fiber. Useful supplementary feed, especially for gestating sows.

**Feeding guidelines**:
Growing pigs need 14-18% crude protein and 13-14 MJ metabolizable energy per kg of feed. Gestating sows need 12-14% protein at restricted intake (1.8-2.5 kg/day). Lactating sows need 16-18% protein at ad libitum intake (4-6 kg/day) to support milk production for a large litter.

### Growth Rate and Carcass Quality

**Growth rate targets**:
- Modern commercial breeds: 0.6-0.8 kg/day average daily gain (ADG) from weaning to market
- Traditional/heritage breeds on pasture: 0.35-0.55 kg/day ADG
- Feed conversion ratio (FCR): 2.5-3.5 kg feed per kg live weight gain (modern breeds), 3.5-4.5 kg for traditional breeds on supplemented pasture

**Butcher weights by system**:
- **Conventional (modern breeds)**: 90-110 kg live weight at 5-7 months. Carcass yield 75-80%. Standard commodity pork.
- **Traditional breeds (pasture/forest)**: 70-80 kg at 8-10 months (slower growth, but developing more complex flavor and firmer fat). Carcass yield 70-75%. Superior eating quality.
- **Heavy pigs (for cured products)**: 140-170 kg at 12-18 months. Used for Parma ham, Iberian ham, and other dry-cured products where larger muscles and more fat are desirable. The extended growth period develops intramuscular fat and complex flavor.

**Carcass utilization**:
Pigs have the highest utilization rate of any livestock species:
- **Pork cuts**: loin (chops, roasts), shoulder (Boston butt, picnic), ham (leg), belly (bacon), ribs
- **Lard**: Rendered from fatback (subcutaneous fat), leaf fat (kidney fat — highest grade, best for pastry), and caul fat (used to wrap roasts). Render by cutting fat into small pieces, heating slowly at 100-120°C until fat liquifies and cracklings (crispy residue) float. Strain through cloth. Yield: 70-85% of fat weight. Lard keeps 6-12 months at room temperature, longer refrigerated.
- **Offal**: Liver, kidneys, heart, intestines (sausage casings — clean thoroughly, scrape mucosa, salt and store), blood (blood sausage, black pudding)
- **Bristles**: Boar bristles from the neck and back are used for hairbrushes, paint brushes, and shaving brushes. Harvest at slaughter, clean, and sort by length and stiffness.
- **Pigskin**: Tans into a durable, flexible leather used for garments, gloves, and footballs. Pigskin leather has a distinctive three-hole pore pattern.

### Artificial Insemination

AI in pigs allows the use of superior terminal sires without maintaining boars (which are dangerous and require dedicated housing). Technique:

1. Collect semen from a boar using an artificial vagina or gloved hand method (the boar mounts a dummy sow). A single ejaculate produces 150-300 mL of semen containing 30-60 billion sperm.
2. Dilute semen in extender solution (provides nutrients and protects sperm during storage). Liquid semen remains viable for 3-7 days at 16-18°C.
3. Inseminate the sow 12-24 hours after first detection of standing heat (she stands rigid when pressure is applied to her back — the standing reflex). Deposit 60-80 mL of extended semen into the cervix using a spiral-tipped AI catheter that mimics the boar's corkscrew glans penis. The catheter locks into the cervical rings — wait for the lock before depositing semen.
4. A second insemination 12-24 hours later improves conception rate. Conception rate: 80-90% with good technique and timing.

AI is more practical for pigs than for cattle in a bootstrapping scenario because pig semen can be stored as liquid (no liquid nitrogen required) for several days. However, natural service remains the baseline — a boar covers 15-25 sows per year with minimal equipment.

### Boar Management and Safety

**Safety first**: Boars are large, powerful, and potentially dangerous animals. A mature boar weighs 200-350 kg with tusks that can inflict severe lacerations. Critical safety rules:
- **Never turn your back on a boar.** Always face the boar and maintain awareness of his position.
- Use a boar board (a solid wooden or metal shield, approximately 1 m × 1.2 m) as a physical barrier between you and the boar during handling.
- Boars signal aggression by chomping (clicking their jaws), frothing at the mouth, and sidling up to present a broadside threat display. If a boar charges, get behind a solid barrier — do not attempt to wrestle or restrain a charging boar.
- Boars should have a dedicated, escape-proof pen with a heavy gate and a catch pen or aisle that allows safe access for feeding and cleaning.
- Children and inexperienced handlers should never enter a boar's pen.

### Manure Management

Pig manure is produced at 5-10 kg/day per animal (approximately 2-3 tonnes per growing pig per finishing cycle). It is higher in nitrogen (0.5-0.6% N fresh weight) and phosphorus (0.4-0.5% P₂O₅) than cattle or sheep manure, making it a potent fertilizer. However, it is also high in water content and can be offensive in odor.

**Composting**: Mix pig manure with carbon-rich bedding (straw, sawdust) at a C:N ratio of 25-30:1. Composting at 55-65°C for 3-4 weeks kills pathogens (Salmonella, E. coli) and reduces odor. Finished compost is a high-value soil amendment.

**Direct application**: Pig manure can be applied to cropland, but fresh manure should be incorporated into soil immediately to prevent nitrogen loss (ammonia volatilization) and runoff. Apply at rates that match crop nutrient needs — excess application causes nitrogen leaching into groundwater.

### Common Diseases

- **Swine dysentery** (*Brachyspira hyodysenteriae*): Severe bloody diarrhea, high morbidity. Transmitted by fecal-oral route. Treat with antimicrobials; prevent with hygiene and all-in/all-out management.
- **Porcine reproductive and respiratory syndrome (PRRS)**: Viral disease causing reproductive failure (abortions, stillbirths, weak piglets) and respiratory disease in growing pigs. Highly contagious. No treatment — management focuses on biosecurity, herd immunity, and vaccination where available.
- **African swine fever (ASF)**: Devastating viral disease with near-100% mortality in domestic pigs. No vaccine, no treatment. Transmitted by direct contact, contaminated equipment, and soft ticks. Strict biosecurity (quarantine, no pork product importation, no contact with wild boar) is the only prevention. ASF has caused massive losses in global pig populations.
- **Erysipelas** (*Erysipelothrix rhusiopathiae*): Diamond-shaped skin lesions, fever, arthritis. Survives in soil. Treat with penicillin. Vaccinate sows and boars annually. Can transmit to humans (erysipeloid — painful skin infection from handling infected animals or carcasses).
- **Parvovirus**: Causes reproductive failure in gilts (mummified fetuses, stillbirths). Widespread in pig populations. Vaccinate gilts before first breeding.

### Safety

**Zoonotic diseases**: Pigs carry several diseases transmissible to humans. Erysipeloid (skin infection from handling infected animals), trichinellosis (from eating undercooked pork — Trichinella spiralis larvae encyst in muscle tissue; prevented by cooking pork to 71°C or freezing at -15°C for 20+ days), Salmonella, and influenza (pigs are mixing vessels for avian, swine, and human influenza viruses — they can generate novel pandemic strains). Always cook pork thoroughly. Wear gloves when handling pig manure.

**Boar safety**: See Boar Management section above. A boar is the most dangerous farm animal relative to its size. Treat with constant respect.

**Crushing risk during farrowing**: Sows are large and clumsy with piglets. The guard rails and heated creep area described in Farrowing Management are essential — without them, 20-30% of piglets may be crushed in the first 3 days. Monitor closely during the first 48 hours post-farrowing.

**Handling**: Pigs have poor eyesight but excellent hearing and a strong sense of smell. Move pigs calmly using boards (solid barriers) and gentle persuasion — never use electric prods or excessive force, which causes panic and injuries. Pigs move toward light and away from loud noises. A sorting board (a large, solid panel) allows safe redirection without physical contact.

**Hygiene**: Pigs kept in dirty conditions are more susceptible to disease and parasites. Maintain clean water, fresh bedding, and adequate drainage. The stereotype of pigs as "dirty" animals is a product of poor management — given the choice, pigs eliminate in one area of their pen and keep their sleeping area clean.
