# Condenser

> **Type**: equipment | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, energy

Exhaust steam condensed back to water in a separate vessel (cooled by river water or cooling tower). Creates vacuum at exhaust → larger pressure differential across piston → more work extracted. Also allows feed water recovery (clean, treated water recycled to boiler). Watt's key invention.

## Context in the Tech Tree

The condenser is the component that transformed steam engines from wasteful mine pumps into efficient general-purpose power sources. By separating the condensation step from the cylinder, Watt's separate condenser eliminated the cyclic heating and cooling that wasted most of the Newcomen engine's energy. In distillation, condensers convert vapor back to liquid at every scale — from simple alcohol stills to multi-story industrial fractionation columns.

## Technical Details

In steam power, the condenser maintains a vacuum at the engine exhaust, increasing the pressure differential across the piston and extracting more work from each unit of steam. Water-cooled shell-and-tube designs are standard: cooling water flows on the shell side while steam condenses inside the tubes. The condensed water is recovered as clean boiler feed water, reducing the need for fresh water treatment.

In refrigeration systems, the condenser rejects heat from high-pressure refrigerant vapor, converting it back to liquid. For ammonia-based absorption or compression cycles, condensing pressures of 10-15 bar correspond to saturation temperatures of 25-40°C, rejected via water jackets or air-cooled finned tubes.

In distillation, shell-and-tube condensers at the column overhead achieve total condensation of the vapor stream. Cooling water enters at 20-30°C and exits at 35-45°C. Heat transfer coefficients of 300-800 W/m²·K are typical for condensing organic vapors. The condenser area is sized for the combined reflux and product vapor load.
