# Hemp

> **Type**: material | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: plants, textiles

The strongest traditional rope fiber (~50 MPa tensile strength). Long staple length from bast (stem) fibers of *Cannabis sativa*, an extremely hardy annual reaching 2-4 m tall. Hemp produces rope, canvas, sackcloth, and paper with minimal water and cultivation effort, making it the most widely adapted fiber crop for civilization bootstrapping.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Hemp is a primary fiber plant in the plants domain and feeds directly into the textiles chain for rope-making, spinning, and weaving. It requires retting (5-10 days water immersion), breaking, scutching, and hackling — identical processing to flax but yielding a coarser, stronger fiber. Yield runs ~1-3 tonnes dry stalk per hectare, producing ~300-800 kg of processed fiber. Hemp rope was the standard cordage for maritime and construction use before synthetic fibers.

Hemp cultivation supports the earliest rope-making and textile capabilities. Dense sowing (like flax) produces tall, unbranched stalks with long fibers. Harvest at pollen shedding, before seed maturation, for best fiber quality.

## Technical Details

Hemp bast fibers are extracted from the phloem of the stem. After retting, the woody core (shives) is broken away mechanically, leaving long fiber bundles. Hackling separates line fibers (suitable for rope and canvas) from short tow (suitable for coarse cloth and papermaking).

Hemp fiber is stronger than flax and more rot-resistant, especially in wet conditions. Its primary limitation is coarseness — hemp cloth is rougher than linen and unsuitable for fine garments. As a rope fiber, hemp excels: high tensile strength, good knot-holding, moderate elasticity, and natural resistance to saltwater degradation. Hemp canvas provided sailcloth, tent fabric, and the original denim (de Nîmes) work cloth.
