# Water Procurement

> **Node ID**: foundations.water-procurement
> **Domain**: [Foundations](./)
> **Timeline**: Years 0-5
> **Outputs**: fresh_water, stored_water

## Overview

Water procurement is the first and most critical capability in bootstrapping civilization. A person needs 2-5 liters of drinking water per day, plus 20-50 liters for cooking and hygiene. A community of 50 people requires 1,100-2,750 liters daily just for survival, before accounting for livestock, irrigation, or craft work. Without reliable water access, no other development can proceed. This page covers finding, collecting, transporting, and storing water using stone-age and low-technology methods.

The techniques here require no metal tools and minimal crafted materials. They draw on observation skills, basic construction, and an understanding of how water moves through the landscape. Every method described has been used by human communities for thousands of years and requires only materials available from the natural environment.

## Water Finding

### Vegetation Indicators

Certain plants grow only where groundwater is close to the surface. Willow, alder, reed mace, and cattails all signal a water table within 1-3 m of the surface. Cottonwood trees indicate water within 6-10 m. A sudden line of bright green vegetation in an otherwise dry area often marks a subsurface water source. Rushes and sedges growing in a line across a slope suggest a seep line where groundwater emerges. Poplar trees growing in a cluster nearly always indicate shallow groundwater, typically 2-5 m deep.

### Animal Signs

Converging animal trails often lead to water sources. Multiple trails merging into one path and descending toward a low point in the terrain usually point to a spring or stream. Birds, especially swallows and swifts, nest near water and return to the same water source at dawn and dusk. Insect swarms hovering in one area may indicate standing water nearby. Bees and wasps rarely travel more than 2-3 km from water; following their flight path at dusk can lead to a water source. Animal tracks in mud around a depression confirm recent water presence even when the surface is dry.

### Geological Signs

Dry river beds often contain water below the surface. Dig in the lowest point of the outside bend, where water flows last before the bed dries up. Water is often 30-100 cm below the surface in these locations. Rock crevices in limestone terrain trap rainwater; check shaded overhangs and depressions where evaporation is lowest. Valley floors where two slopes meet concentrate groundwater flow. Look for damp soil, darker earth, or slight depressions in the ground. In mountainous terrain, water often emerges at the contact line between permeable rock (sandstone, limestone) and impermeable rock (clay, shale).

### Dew Collection

Spread wool or linen cloth over grass before dawn, weighted with stones to keep contact with the vegetation. Wring out the cloth at sunrise. Yield: 0.5-2 liters per m² of cloth per night in humid climates. This method works best in coastal or high-humidity areas where nighttime condensation is heavy. Multiple cloths increase total yield. A frame of 4-6 m² of cloth spread over grassy ground can produce 3-8 liters per night. The collected water requires no treatment if the cloth is clean. Wool absorbs more moisture than linen but is heavier to wring out.

### Well Digging by Hand

Where no spring or surface water exists, dig a well. A hand-dug well 1-1.5 m in diameter and 5-15 m deep can reach the water table in many landscapes. Dig during the dry season when the water table is at its lowest, ensuring year-round supply. Shore the walls with stone or timber as you dig to prevent collapse. A well 1.2 m in diameter with stone lining requires 3-5 tons of stone per 3 m of depth. When you hit water, continue digging 1-2 m below the water table to create a sump that stores water between draw cycles. A well producing 200-500 liters per day supports a household. Cover the top with a stone or wooden cap to prevent contamination and accidents. A simple windlass (crank and rope) with a bucket raises water from depths up to 15 m.

### Solar Still

Dig a pit 60-100 cm in diameter and 40-60 cm deep. Place a collection container in the center. Cover the pit with a clear plastic sheet, sealing the edges with soil or stones. Weight the center of the sheet with a stone so it forms a cone shape pointing down into the container. Moisture evaporates from the soil, condenses on the plastic, runs to the lowest point, and drips into the container. Yield: 0.5-2 liters per day depending on soil moisture. Adding green vegetation or urine to the pit soil increases output by 30-50%. This technique produces distilled water, which is free of pathogens and salts. For higher yield, dig multiple stills rather than making one larger pit. A still 1 m in diameter works better than a 2 m one because the condensation distance from soil to plastic is shorter.

## Rainwater Harvesting

### Roof Catchment

A roof acts as a collection surface. The math is straightforward: 100 m² of roof area multiplied by 1 mm of rainfall yields 100 liters of water. In a temperate climate receiving 500-800 mm of rain per year, a 100 m² roof collects 50,000-80,000 liters annually. Even in arid regions with 200 mm of annual rainfall, the same roof captures 20,000 liters per year. Guttering directs the flow. A simple wooden or clay half-pipe gutter running along the roof eaves, sloping 1-2 cm per meter toward the cistern, captures 80-95% of roof runoff. Missing gutters loses 50-70% to splash and ground absorption.

Roofing material affects water quality. Clay tile, slate, and metal sheets produce the cleanest runoff. Thatched roofs shed organic matter and discolor the water but it remains usable after settling for 24 hours. Avoid roofs treated with tar or lead-based paints, as these contaminate the water with toxic compounds.

### First-Flush Diverter

The first rain after a dry period carries dust, bird droppings, leaves, and insect debris from the roof surface. Discard the first 1-2 mm of rainfall before directing flow to storage. A simple diverter uses a small tank or pipe segment that fills first. Once full (typically 50-100 liters for a 100 m² roof), a floating ball or manual valve redirects subsequent flow to the main storage cistern. This single step removes 80-90% of rooftop contaminants. In regions with long dry seasons (3-6 months), the first-flush volume is larger because more debris accumulates. A rule of thumb: divert 1 liter per m² of roof per month since last rainfall.

### Cistern Design

Underground cisterns store harvested rainwater for dry periods. Build from stone or brick, 5-20 m³ capacity for a household. Waterproof the interior with lime plaster applied 3-5 mm thick in 2-3 coats, allowing each coat to dry before applying the next. Cover the cistern with a stone or wood lid to prevent mosquito breeding and contamination from falling debris. An overflow pipe near the top prevents overfilling. Position the cistern slightly below the roof gutter outlet to allow gravity feeding. A settlement chamber before the cistern inlet lets sand and silt drop out of the water column before entering storage. A household of 5 people consuming 100 liters per day needs a 10 m³ cistern to bridge a 100-day dry season.

### Ground Catchment

Where no roof is available, a prepared ground surface collects rainfall. Smooth a sloped area with compacted clay or stone paving, directing runoff to a central collection point. A 100 m² catchment surface receiving 500 mm of annual rain yields 50,000 liters of potential collection. Actual recovery is 50-80% due to evaporation, surface absorption, and minor losses. Clay loses less water to absorption than sandy soil. Contouring the catchment into shallow channels increases flow speed and reduces evaporation losses. A stone-paved catchment with 2-3% slope achieves recovery rates of 70-85%, among the highest for ground-level collection.

## Spring Development

### Spring Box Construction

A spring box protects the natural emergence point of groundwater. Build a stone or concrete box 80×80×80 cm around the spring eye (the point where water flows from the ground). The box has three components: a collection chamber, an overflow pipe near the top, and a screened outlet pipe at the base. Use a collection pipe 5-10 cm in diameter to carry water from the spring box to a storage tank. The screen (fine mesh or gravel filter) keeps debris and small animals out of the supply. Set the box foundation 20-30 cm below the spring eye to capture subsurface flow that may not be visible at the surface. Seal the back and sides of the box against the hillside with clay to prevent surface water from entering.

### Yield Measurement

Measure spring output before investing in infrastructure. Fill a container of known volume and time how long it takes. A spring yielding 1 liter per minute produces 1,440 liters per day. At 70-140 liters per person per day (drinking, cooking, hygiene), that single spring supports 10-20 people. Seasonal variation matters. Measure output at the end of the dry season to determine minimum reliable yield. A spring that produces 5 liters per minute in the wet season may drop to 0.5 liters per minute in the dry season. Record measurements over a full year before committing to a distribution system sized for the wet-season peak.

### Spring Protection

Fence a 10-15 m radius around the spring to prevent livestock from contaminating the source with manure. Dig a shallow diversion ditch upslope to redirect surface runoff away from the spring eye. Plant grass or ground cover around the fenced area to filter any residual runoff. Keep the spring box covered and inspect the screen monthly. Remove accumulated sediment from the collection chamber every 3-6 months. A neglected spring box fills with silt and reduces flow by 30-50% within a year.

### Hillside Seep Collection

Where groundwater seeps from a hillside rather than emerging at a single point, collect it with a gravel-filled trench. Dig a trench 30-40 cm wide and 40-60 cm deep along the seep line, running perpendicular to the slope. Fill with clean gravel or crushed stone. Place a perforated clay pipe or stone channel at the bottom of the trench to carry collected water to a storage tank. A seep trench 10 m long can collect 50-200 liters per day depending on the groundwater flow rate. Cover the trench surface with clay to prevent surface water from entering and contaminating the supply.

## Water Transport

### Clay Pots

Clay pots hold 5-20 liters and are carried on the head or hip. They keep water cool through slow evaporation through the porous walls, lowering stored water temperature by 5-8°C below ambient. Breakage rate runs 10-20% per year depending on handling and wall thickness. Thicker walls last longer but increase weight. Fire-hardened pots survive longer than sun-dried ones. A well-made pot with walls 8-12 mm thick weighs 2-4 kg empty and lasts 3-5 years with careful handling.

### Wooden Buckets

Cedar or oak staves bound with iron or wooden bands produce buckets of 8-15 liters capacity. More durable than clay pots with a service life of 5-15 years if kept moist (dry wood shrinks and leaks). Iron bands last 3-5 years before rusting through; wooden bands require replacement every 2-3 years but need no metal. Oak buckets resist rot better than pine or spruce. Soak new buckets in water for 24-48 hours before use to swell the staves and seal gaps.

### Leather Water Bags

Goat or sheep hide sewn with sinew produces lightweight containers of 2-5 liters. Treated hides (scraped, stretched, and smoked) last 1-3 years before leaking. These bags suit travel because they are light, flexible, and collapse when empty. Untreated hides impart a strong flavor to the water but remain functional. A large goatskin bag holds 4-5 liters and weighs under 500 g empty, making it practical for hunting parties or scouting expeditions.

### Aqueduct Basics

Gravity-fed channels move large volumes of water over distance. Clay or stone-lined channels need a minimum slope of 0.1-0.5% (1-5 m drop per km). Steeper slopes increase flow velocity; maximum velocity should stay around 1 m/s to prevent erosion of the channel bed. Open channels allow easy inspection and debris removal. A channel 30 cm wide × 20 cm deep at 0.3% slope delivers approximately 15-25 liters per second. Covered channels reduce contamination and evaporation losses. For a settlement of 200 people at 100 liters per person per day, a channel delivering 0.25 liters per second (15 liters per minute) running 12 hours per day covers the full demand.

### Pipes and Siphons

Sealed pipes move water uphill using siphon action, provided the outlet is below the water source. Clay pipes 5-10 cm in diameter, joined with lime mortar, carry water over distances of 100-500 m. A siphon works only if the pipe is full of water and free of air pockets. Maximum lift height for a water siphon at sea level is approximately 10 m (limited by atmospheric pressure). At 2,000 m elevation the limit drops to 8 m. Bamboo stems with nodes removed make natural pipes 2-5 m long, suitable for short-distance water conveyance over rough terrain.

## Water Storage

### Clay Pots

Large clay pots store 50-200 liters each. Seal with a fitted lid and store in a cool, shaded location or partially bury in earth. Underground storage keeps water at 15-20°C. The porous walls provide passive cooling through evaporation. Replace pots that develop cracks, as contaminated water enters through the fissures. A household storing 500 liters needs 3-5 large pots. Arrange them in a shaded pit 30-50 cm deep to reduce temperature fluctuations.

### Underground Cisterns

Stone or brick cisterns of 5-50 m³ capacity provide long-term bulk storage. Lime-plastered interiors prevent seepage losses to 1-2% per month. Underground placement keeps water cool year-round at 10-15°C, slowing bacterial growth. Include a sealed access hatch with a rope and bucket for drawing water. A well-built cistern lasts 50-100 years with occasional replastering every 10-15 years. A community of 100 people needs 20-30 m³ of storage to bridge a 30-day dry period at reduced consumption rates.

### Ponds and Reservoirs

Earthen dams 2-5 m high create reservoirs of 1,000-10,000 m³ capacity. A clay core provides waterproofing: compact a 30-50 cm layer of clay across the dam base and upstream face. The dam wall needs a width-to-height ratio of at least 3:1 for stability (a 3 m high dam should be at least 9 m wide at the base). An overflow spillway on one side prevents the dam from overtopping during heavy rain. The spillway should handle peak storm flow, typically 5-10 times the average stream flow. Reservoir water requires settling (24-48 hours) before use to reduce turbidity. A 5,000 m³ reservoir supplies 50 people for 100 days at 100 liters per person per day, or irrigates 0.5-1 hectare of crops through a dry season.

### Seasonal Planning

Size water storage to the longest dry period. A climate with a 4-month dry season and 200 people at 70 liters per person per day needs 1,680 m³ of stored water minimum. Add 20-30% margin for evaporation and seepage losses. Divide storage across multiple cisterns and reservoirs rather than relying on a single structure. A single point of failure in water supply is a community-ending risk. In freezing climates, bury storage below the frost line (0.5-1.5 m depth depending on latitude) or insulate with earth berms 50-100 cm thick to prevent ice formation.

## Water Quality Indicators

### Visual Assessment

Clear water is preferred. Cloudiness (turbidity) above 5 NTU indicates suspended particles including silt, organic matter, or microorganisms. Hold a container of water up to light. If you cannot see through 30 cm of it, the turbidity is too high for safe consumption without treatment. Let turbid water settle for 24 hours, then decant the clear upper portion. A simple turbidity test: lower a white object on a string into the water. If it disappears within 20 cm, the water needs settling or filtration before use.

### Smell

Fresh water has no odor. A sulfur smell (rotten eggs) indicates hydrogen sulfide gas. This is not necessarily harmful at low concentrations but makes water unpleasant to drink. Aeration (pouring water between containers 5-10 times) removes most sulfur odors within minutes. Musty or fishy smells suggest organic decay or algal growth. A strong earthy smell after rain indicates the source is picking up organic compounds from surface runoff and should be checked for contamination.

### Taste

Water should taste neutral. Salty taste indicates dissolved minerals, common in coastal or arid regions where groundwater has concentrated salts. Bitter taste may indicate alkaline soils or contamination from industrial residues. Metallic taste suggests dissolved iron or manganese. Any unusual taste warrants further investigation of the source and treatment before consumption. A simple test: let water sit in a covered container for 12 hours. If the taste improves significantly, the issue was volatile compounds that dissipated. If not, the contaminants are dissolved and require filtration or different treatment.

### Biological Indicators

Look for the absence of dead animals, insects, or algae scum in and around the water source. Live fish and amphibians indicate oxygenated water with some biological health. The presence of mosquito larvae indicates standing water that needs covering or treatment. Crayfish, freshwater shrimp, and caddisfly larvae are positive indicators of reasonable water quality. Absence of all animal life is a warning sign. A stream or pond with no visible insects, fish, or amphibians may be contaminated with chemicals or toxins that kill aquatic life.

### Temperature

Water at 10-15°C is preferred for drinking and storage. Cold water tastes better and suppresses bacterial growth. Warm water above 20°C promotes rapid bacterial multiplication, with colony counts doubling every 20-30 minutes in the 20-40°C range. Water drawn from shallow surface sources in summer often reaches 20-25°C and should be consumed within 24 hours or treated. Deep wells and springs maintain 8-12°C year-round regardless of surface temperature.

### Boiling

Bringing water to a rolling boil for 1-3 minutes kills most pathogens (bacteria, viruses, protozoa). At altitudes above 2,000 m, extend boiling time to 5 minutes because water boils at lower temperatures (approximately 93°C at 2,000 m versus 100°C at sea level). One liter of water boils in 8-10 minutes on a wood fire consuming 150-300 g of dry hardwood. Boiling does not remove chemical contaminants or heavy metals. Boiled water tastes flat because dissolved gases escape during heating; restore taste by pouring between two containers 3-4 times to re-aerate. For a family of 5 consuming 20 liters of drinking water per day, boiling requires 2-3 kg of firewood daily.

### Simple Filtration

A sand filter removes particles and many pathogens through mechanical trapping and biological action. Build a filter from a clay pot or wooden box 50-80 cm tall, filled with layers of gravel (10 cm at the bottom), coarse sand (10 cm), and fine sand (20-30 cm on top). Water poured through the top emerges clearer from the bottom. A slow sand filter processing 1-2 liters per hour per 0.1 m² of surface area removes 90-99% of bacteria after a biological layer (schmutzdecke) develops over 2-3 weeks of continuous use. This layer is essential. A new filter without an established biological layer removes only 50-70% of bacteria. Clean the filter by scraping off the top 1-2 cm of sand when flow rate drops below 50% of original, then replace the sand on top.

## Safety

Waterborne diseases kill more people in early-stage communities than almost any other hazard. Dysentery, cholera, and typhoid spread through contaminated water (see [Health → Medicine](../health/medicine.md)). Always assume surface water is contaminated unless proven otherwise. Diarrheal disease from unsafe water kills an estimated 1.5 million people per year globally in the modern era; in a community without medical infrastructure, the death rate is even higher.

Drowning is a risk at water collection points, especially for children. Mark steep drop-offs at riverbanks and reservoir edges with posts or stones. Build simple railings at frequently used access points. Never send children alone to collect water from deep rivers or steep-sided reservoirs.

Contamination from human or animal waste upstream is the most common cause of waterborne illness. Never locate latrines or animal pens within 30 m uphill of a water source. A single latrine contaminating a shallow well can sicken an entire community within days. Check upstream conditions regularly, especially after rain when runoff carries waste into surface water.

Standing water breeds mosquitoes that carry malaria, dengue, and other diseases. Cover all stored water with tight-fitting lids. Drain or fill temporary puddles within 100 m of dwellings. Stock ornamental or utility ponds with small fish (such as Gambusia, which eat 100+ mosquito larvae per day each) to control larvae. Empty and refill open water containers every 5-7 days to interrupt the mosquito breeding cycle (egg to adult takes 8-10 days in warm water).

### Field Water Quality Testing

**Secchi disk clarity**: Lower a white disk 20 cm in diameter into the water on a calibrated cord. The depth at which it disappears from view is the Secchi depth. Readings below 1.5 m indicate turbidity too high for drinking without treatment. Clear water bodies register 4-10 m Secchi depth. This simple test requires only a white-painted disk and a marked rope.

**Litmus pH testing**: Dip litmus paper into the water sample. The color change indicates approximate pH on a range of 4.5-8.5. Drinking water should fall between 6.5-8.5 pH. Water below pH 6.5 (acidic) corrodes metal pipes and may contain dissolved heavy metals. Water above pH 8.5 (alkaline) tastes unpleasant and reduces the effectiveness of chlorine disinfection. Narrow-range pH paper provides 0.5-unit resolution for roughly distinguishing acceptable from marginal sources.

**Conductivity cell for dissolved solids**: Two electrodes immersed in water measure electrical conductivity, which correlates with total dissolved solids (TDS). A handheld conductivity meter reads in microsiemens per cm (μS/cm). Convert to approximate TDS in mg/L by multiplying by 0.5-0.7 (the conversion factor depends on the mix of dissolved ions). Drinking water TDS should be below 1,000 mg/L. Readings above 2,000 mg/L indicate brackish water unsuitable for long-term consumption. Calibration requires a known-concentration potassium chloride solution.

### Cistern Storage Detail

**Construction**: Lime-plastered stone or concrete cisterns of 5-50 m³ capacity provide durable underground storage. Walls minimum 200 mm thick stone or 150 mm reinforced concrete. Apply lime plaster (1:3 lime:sand by volume) in 3-5 mm coats, 2-3 layers, allowing each to cure before the next. The plaster seals the interior against seepage; well-applied plaster limits losses to 1-2% per month. Cover with a stone or reinforced concrete lid to block contamination, mosquito access, and evaporation. Install an access hatch with a tight-fitting cover for inspection and cleaning. A cistern of 20 m³ capacity, filled from a 100 m² roof catchment in a region receiving 600 mm annual rainfall, bridges a 3-4 month dry season for a household of 5 at conservative consumption rates.

### Emergency Disinfection Methods

**Boiling**: Bring water to a rolling boil for 1 minute at sea level (100°C) to kill bacteria, viruses, and protozoan cysts. At altitudes above 2,000 m, extend to 3 minutes because water boils at lower temperatures (~93°C at 2,000 m). Boiling is the most reliable field disinfection method and requires no chemicals, only fuel and a container.

**Solar disinfection (SODIS)**: Fill clear PET plastic bottles with clear (not turbid) water and expose to full sun for 6 hours. UV-A radiation and thermal heating inactivate pathogens. Works best at water temperatures above 50°C and in water clear enough to read text through. Cloudy weather requires 2 consecutive days of exposure. Does not work with glass (blocks UV-A) or tinted plastic. Turbid water must be filtered first.

**Iodine disinfection**: Add 2-4 drops of 2% tincture of iodine per liter of clear water. Stir and wait 30 minutes before drinking. Iodine kills bacteria and viruses but is less effective against Cryptosporidium cysts. Not suitable for pregnant women or individuals with thyroid conditions. Water treated with iodine has a noticeable taste; adding vitamin C (ascorbic acid) after the 30-minute wait neutralizes both the taste and residual iodine.

### Well Maintenance

**Annual shock chlorination**: Pump the well dry or draw down the water level. Add chlorine (sodium hypochlorite or calcium hypochlorite) to achieve approximately 500 mg/L free chlorine in the well water. For a 1 m diameter well with 5 m of water depth (~3,900 L), this requires roughly 2 L of 10% sodium hypochlorite solution. Agitate by surging (raising and lowering a bailer) to distribute chlorine throughout the water column and over the well walls. Let stand 12-24 hours with the well sealed. Pump the well until chlorine odor dissipates before returning to service. This procedure controls iron bacteria slime, coliform contamination, and biofilm buildup on well casings.

### Rainwater Harvesting System Sizing

**Collection efficiency**: A well-maintained roof catchment system with gutters captures 80-90% of incident rainfall. Losses come from evaporation (3-5%), splash and overflow (5-10%), and first-flush diversion (1-2 mm per storm event). A 100 m² roof in a 600 mm/year rainfall zone with 85% collection efficiency yields approximately 51,000 liters annually.

**Tank sizing for dry season**: Calculate storage to bridge the longest expected dry period. For a household of 5 at 50 liters per person per day (conservation mode) through a 4-month dry season: 5 × 50 × 120 days = 30,000 liters (30 m³). Add 25% margin for evaporation and seepage losses: 37.5 m³. A single cistern of this size is feasible in concrete or stone, or split into two 19 m³ cisterns for redundancy. In arid regions with 6-month dry seasons, a 50 m³ tank is the minimum practical size for household supply.

**Catchment-to-storage ratio**: As a rule, the catchment area should be sized to fill the storage tank at least 1.5-2× during the wet season, allowing for below-average rainfall years. A 100 m² roof with 600 mm annual rainfall, of which 450 mm falls in a 4-month wet season, captures roughly 38,000 liters during the wet months. This fills a 30 m³ tank with margin to spare in a normal year but may fall short in a drought year receiving only 60% of average rainfall. Oversizing the catchment or adding supplementary ground catchment provides drought resilience.

**System redundancy**: Never depend on a single water source or single storage structure. A community water plan should include at least two independent sources (well + rainwater catchment, or spring + river with treatment) and distribute storage across multiple cisterns. If one cistern cracks or one source becomes contaminated, the remaining infrastructure covers essential needs at reduced consumption. Pipe storage cisterns in series so that overflow from the first fills the second, maximizing capture from intense storms that might exceed single-cistern capacity.

**Seasonal inspection cycle**: Before the wet season, clean gutters and first-flush diverters, check cistern lids and screens for integrity, and verify overflow pipes are clear. After the wet season, inspect cistern interiors for sediment accumulation (drain and remove silt if depth exceeds 5 cm), check lime plaster for cracks or spalling, and repair before the next refill cycle. Annual maintenance of catchment infrastructure prevents the gradual capacity losses that accumulate when small problems go unaddressed for years.
