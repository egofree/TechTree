Stone & Wood Tools

Knapping (flint/obsidian/chert):

  • Hard hammer percussion: Round cobble hammerstone (quartzite, granite, 200-500 g) struck against core to remove large flakes. Produces crude blanks.
  • Soft hammer percussion: Antler billet or hardwood club for finer flake removal. Thinner, more controlled flakes.
  • Pressure flaking: Antler tine or copper-tipped tool pressed against edge to remove tiny flakes for final shaping and sharpening. Produces edges sharper than surgical steel scalpels (obsidian ~3 nm edge width vs steel ~500 nm).
  • Tool types: Hand axes (bifacial), scrapers (hide processing, wood shaping), projectile points (hunting), drill bits (rotating between hands or with bow drill attachment), burins (engraving, scoring).

Ground stone tools:

  • Pecking: Repeatedly striking granite, basalt, or greenstone with hammerstone to rough out shape.
  • Grinding: Rub against flat abrasive stone (sandstone) with water and sand slurry. Hours of grinding produce polished, sharp edges.
  • Tool types: Axes (grooved for handle attachment), adzes (woodworking), mortars and pestles (grain processing), manos and metates (grain grinding).

Wooden tools and implements:

  • Hafting: Attach stone heads to wooden handles using sinew, rawhide, or plant-fiber cordage, secured with pine pitch or birch tar adhesive.
  • Birch tar adhesive: Heat birch bark in sealed container (similar to char cloth method) at 300-400°C. Black, sticky tar forms. Roll into balls. Soften with heat to apply. Strongest natural adhesive available.
  • Handles: Split green wood, shape with stone tools, dry slowly to prevent checking.
  • Digging sticks: Fire-harden tip of hardwood stick (heat until brown, not black) for soil penetration.

Cordage and rope making:

  • Fiber extraction: Harvest plant stems (hemp, flax, jute, nettle, dogbane). Ret in water for 1-2 weeks to decompose pectin binding fibers. Beat stems against stone or wood to separate bast fibers from woody core (shives). Comb through coarse teeth to separate and align.
  • Reverse-wrap technique (2-strand): Hold two fiber strands side by side. Twist both clockwise until they kink, then fold in half — they naturally wrap counterclockwise around each other. Continue adding fibers by splicing in new strands before reaching the ends. Produces uniform cordage.
  • 3-strand rope: Lay three completed 2-strand cords side by side and twist together. Stronger and more flexible than 2-strand. Rope diameter determined by starting strand thickness.
  • Strength by diameter: 1 cm hemp rope holds ~100 kg; 2 cm holds ~400 kg. Wet natural fiber rope is stronger than dry (fibers swell and grip tighter).
  • Splicing: To join two ropes or repair breaks, unlay strands 10-15 cm, interleave with matching strands from the other end, and wrap tightly with thin cordage. A good splice retains ~80% of rope strength.

Antler and bone tools:

  • Pressure flaker: Antler tine (deer, elk) for pressure flaking flint. Harder than wood, softer than stone — ideal for controlled flake removal.
  • Awls: Sharpened bone splinters (deer rib, bird bone) for piercing leather, basketry weaving, and drilling holes in wood.
  • Fish hooks: Carved from bone or antler with stone flakes. Barbed designs carved with a burin. Gorge hooks (straight doubles-pointed pieces lashed at midpoint) are the simplest form.
  • Needles with eyes: Split bone or antler, grind slender with abrasive stone, drill eye with stone drill bit or punch through with an awl. Essential for tailored clothing.

Woodworking joints (stone/bronze tools):

  • Mortise-and-tenon: Cut rectangular hole (mortise) in one piece with chisel and auger; shape protruding tenon on mating piece. Peg through both for permanent joint. Foundation of timber framing.
  • Dovetail: Splay-cut interlocking pins and tails. Resists pull-out without fasteners. Used for chests, boxes, drawer joints.
  • Lap joint (half-lap): Remove half the thickness from each piece where they overlap. Simple with stone adze. Used for framing, cross-bracing.
  • Pegged joints: Drill holes through overlapping members, drive hardwood pegs (oak, ash). Pegs swell with moisture and lock tight. Replaces nails in timber construction.

Shelter construction:

  • Wattle-and-daub: Weave flexible saplings or split wood between vertical posts (wattle). Pack both sides with clay-soil-straw mixture (daub). Let dry 1-2 weeks. Produces solid, insulated walls. ~10 cm wall thickness.
  • Post-and-beam: Vertical posts (15-20 cm diameter) set in ground holes 60-90 cm deep. Horizontal beams notched into post tops. Stone or bronze tools sufficient for all joinery. Spacing 1.5-2.5 m between posts.
  • Thatched roof: Bundle reed, straw, or long grass (~60 cm bundles). Tie to roof poles with cordage, overlapping from eave to ridge like shingles. 30-40 cm thatch depth sheds water for 20-30 years. Roof pitch minimum 45° for water run-off.
