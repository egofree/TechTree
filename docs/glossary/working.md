# Working

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: glass, metals

Requires hydrogen-oxygen torch (~2800°C flame temperature). Oxy-acetylene produces a hotter flame (~3100°C) but introduces carbon contamination; hydrogen-oxygen is preferred for purity (only combustion product is water vapor). Graphite tools for shaping (won't stick to silica). Patience — silica is very viscous even at working temperature.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Working — the process of shaping and forming materials at elevated temperature — is a core manufacturing concept across the tech tree. In [Advanced Glass](../glass/advanced.md) production, working fused silica requires temperatures of 1800-2100°C, demanding specialized hydrogen-oxygen torches. In [Basic Glass](../glass/basic.md), working temperatures of 800-1100°C are sufficient for soda-lime glass. In [Powder Metallurgy](../metals/powder-metallurgy.md), working involves compaction and sintering of metal powders into solid components.

## Technical Details

Glass working temperature is the range at which the material is sufficiently plastic to be shaped without cracking, yet viscous enough to hold its form. For soda-lime glass, this range is roughly 700-1000°C. For borosilicate glass (Pyrex), 800-1100°C. For fused silica, 1800-2100°C — a range so extreme that only hydrogen-oxygen torches can achieve it.

The choice of torch fuel matters for product purity. Oxy-acetylene reaches ~3100°C, hotter than hydrogen-oxygen (~2800°C), but the acetylene flame introduces carbon contamination into the silica — unacceptable for semiconductor-grade quartz crucibles and optical components. Hydrogen-oxygen is preferred because its only combustion product is water vapor, leaving the silica uncontaminated.

Graphite tools are used for shaping fused silica because graphite does not wet or stick to silica glass at working temperature. Metal tools would contaminate the glass surface. The extreme viscosity of silica at working temperature means that shaping is slow and requires patience — even at 2000°C, silica flows sluggishly compared to soda-lime glass at 900°C.
