# Construction

> **Type**: equipment | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: animals, ceramics, chemistry, computing, energy, foundations, gas-handling, machine-tools, measurement, metals, mining, optics, photolithography, polymers, textiles, transport

A steel casing (1.5-3 mm sheet steel, 0.8-2.0 m diameter) extends from above the furnace roof down into the furnace charge. Paste blocks are loaded into the top of the casing. As the electrode is consumed at the bottom, the casing and paste descend. Heat from the furnace (waste heat from the smelting process, typically 400-800°C at the casing midpoint) bakes the paste in situ. The pitch softens at ~120°C, flows to fill voids, then carbonizes at 400-600°C into a solid carbon electrode body.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Construction is a cross-cutting concern spanning every domain in the tech tree. From aquaculture ponds and draft-animal harnesses to vacuum chambers and photolithography cleanrooms, the methods and materials of construction determine whether a design can actually be built with available capability. The Söderberg electrode example above illustrates self-baking construction — the electrode forms itself from waste heat during operation, eliminating a separate manufacturing step.

## Technical Details

Construction methods evolve through the tech tree alongside available materials and precision. Early construction relies on wood, stone, clay, and hand tools: timber frames, dry-stone walls, wattle-and-daub. The introduction of cast iron and then steel enables riveted and welded pressure vessels, structural frames, and machine bases. Precision machining enables sealed vacuum chambers, boiler shells rated for 10+ atmospheres, and interchangeable parts.

Key construction principles recur across domains: cylindrical shells resist internal and external pressure better than flat plates (used in boilers, vacuum chambers, kilns); stiffening rings prevent collapse under external pressure; welded seams eliminate the leak paths inherent in riveted joints; and material selection matches the chemical and thermal environment — stainless steel for corrosive gases, carbon steel for structural work, copper for electrical conductors, refractory brick for furnace linings.

The gap between a workable design and a buildable design is often the binding constraint in bootstrapping. A design that requires tolerances beyond current machine capability must be redesigned or the machine capability must first be improved.
