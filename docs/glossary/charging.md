# Charging

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: energy, machine-tools, metals

Load scrap steel (40-60% of charge) and limestone (flux, 5-8% of metal weight) through side doors using charging machines. Pour molten pig iron (the "hot metal," 40-60% of charge) onto the hearth.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Charging is the process of loading raw materials into a furnace or reactor. In [Steelmaking](../metals/steelmaking.md), charging loads the open-hearth furnace or basic oxygen furnace with scrap, pig iron, and flux. In [Casting](../machine-tools/casting.md), charging melts metal in crucibles and cupola furnaces. In [Energy Storage](../energy/storage.md), charging refers to applying electrical current to reverse the battery discharge reaction. The term covers both the mechanical loading of materials and the electrochemical process of restoring battery capacity.

## Technical Details

In steelmaking, the open-hearth furnace charge consists of scrap steel (40-60% of the metallic charge) and limestone flux (5-8% of metal weight), loaded through side doors using mechanical charging machines. Molten pig iron (the remaining 40-60% of the charge) is poured onto the hearth from a ladle. The charge ratio determines the heat balance: pig iron's exothermic impurities (Si, C, P) provide heat, while scrap absorbs excess thermal energy as a coolant.

The basic oxygen furnace uses a simpler charging sequence: first scrap (20-30%, acting as coolant), then molten pig iron (70-80%), then fluxes (burned lime and dolomite). The scrap must be placed at the bottom of the vessel with heavier pieces first — light scrap on top prevents bridging and ensures the oxygen lance can reach the metal bath.

In lead-acid battery charging, the process applies a voltage above the cell's equilibrium (2.35-2.45V per cell) to reverse the discharge reaction (PbSO₄ → Pb + PbO₂ + H₂SO₄). Current is initially high and tapers as the battery approaches full charge. Electrolyte density (measured by hydrometer) indicates state of charge: 1.265 g/cm³ = fully charged, 1.120 g/cm³ = discharged. Overcharging (>2.4V per cell) causes electrolysis of water, producing explosive hydrogen gas — battery rooms must be ventilated.
