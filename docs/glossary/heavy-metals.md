# Heavy Metals

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: health, chemistry, mining, transport

Dense metallic elements — lead, arsenic, cadmium, mercury, and others — that are toxic at low concentrations and accumulate in biological tissues. Regulatory limits for medicinal and food-grade materials: lead <20 ppm, arsenic <5 ppm, cadmium <1 ppm, mercury <1 ppm. Tested by colorimetric methods (dithizone for lead and mercury, silver diethyldithiocarbamate for arsenic) or, at higher industrial maturity, by atomic absorption spectroscopy.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Heavy metals appear as contaminants in ores, process streams, and finished products throughout the tech tree. Mining and smelting liberate lead and arsenic from sulfide ores. Glass batches use lead oxide (PbO) as a flux. Pigments historically contain lead, cadmium, and chromium compounds. Pharmaceutical production must control heavy metal residues to strict limits. Water treatment systems must remove heavy metals from discharge to prevent environmental contamination.

The health domain tracks heavy metal exposure through occupational health protocols: air monitoring in smelters and foundries, blood lead testing for workers, and chelation therapy (EDTA, dimercaprol) for acute poisoning. Sanitation systems test source water and treated water for heavy metal content.

## Technical Details

Heavy metal toxicity arises from their ability to displace essential metals in enzymes and proteins. Lead substitutes for calcium in bone and nerve tissue. Mercury binds sulfur in proteins, disrupting cellular metabolism. Arsenic interferes with cellular energy production by substituting for phosphate. All accumulate with chronic exposure — the body excretes them slowly.

In industrial contexts, heavy metals enter process streams through ore impurities (galena/PbS in silver ores, arsenopyrite/FeAsS in gold ores), corrosion of equipment (lead-lined vessels, cadmium-plated fasteners), and intentional use as reagents. Removing them requires precipitation (adjust pH to form insoluble hydroxides or sulfides), ion exchange, or activated carbon adsorption. The choice depends on concentration, flow rate, and target discharge limits.
