## Problem

Civilization runs on string. Rope hauls stone, cordage binds tools, cloth protects workers from molten metal and chemical splashes, canvas makes sails and tents, and drive belts transmit power from water wheels and steam engines. Without a textile supply chain, most mechanical systems have no way to transfer motion or contain materials.

## Technologies & Systems

### Fiber Sources

**Flax (linen)**:
- **Cultivation**: Annual plant, ~1 m tall. Sow in spring, harvest ~100 days later when stalks yellow at bottom third. Pull entire plant (don't cut — longer fibers from root end).
- **Retting** (separating fiber from stem): Submerge flax stalks in water (pond, stream, or dew-retting on grass) for 5-14 days. Bacteria decompose pectin binding fibers to woody core (shives). Test: when fibers separate easily from stem, retting is complete. Over-retting weakens fibers.
- **Breaking**: Crush dried retted stalks with wooden breaker (flax brake — hinged wooden blades that snap the woody core). Shives break into short pieces, fibers remain long.
- **Scutching**: Beat broken stalks with flat wooden knife against vertical board. Knock away remaining woody fragments.
- **Hackling (combing)**: Draw fibers through graduated iron or thorn combs (hackles). Coarse hackle removes remaining shives. Fine hackle separates long line fibers (30-90 cm) from short tow (5-15 cm). Line fibers become high-quality linen; tow becomes coarse cloth or rope.
- **Yield**: ~200-400 kg fiber per hectare. Long line fiber ~30% of total; tow ~20%.

**Hemp**:
- **Cultivation**: Annual, 2-4 m tall. Extremely hardy, minimal water. Harvest at pollen shedding (before seeds mature for best fiber). Processing identical to flax: ret (5-10 days in water), break, scutch, hackle.
- **Properties**: Stronger than flax, more rot-resistant, coarser. Best for rope, canvas, and heavy cloth. Historically the primary cordage fiber before synthetic ropes.
- **Yield**: ~1-3 tonnes dry stalk per hectare → ~300-800 kg fiber.

**Cotton**:
- **Cultivation**: Requires 150-180 frost-free days + moderate water. Warm climate crop. Harvest bolls when they open (fibers emerge white and fluffy).
- **Ginning**: Separate seeds from fiber. Simple single-roller gin (wooden roller rotates against fixed blade — fiber passes, seeds are crushed and fall away). Whitney-style saw gin (circular saw teeth pull fiber through grid too narrow for seeds — much faster but damages fibers).
- **Properties**: Soft, comfortable, absorbs moisture. Moderate strength when dry, weaker when wet (opposite of linen and hemp). Poor mildew resistance.
- **Yield**: ~500-1000 kg lint cotton per hectare.

**Wool**:
- **Shearing**: Clip fleece from sheep annually (spring). 2-5 kg fleece per sheep. Sorted by quality — shoulder and sides are finest, breech (hindquarters) coarsest.
- **Washing**: Remove lanolin (natural wool grease), dirt, vegetable matter. Wash in hot water (~50°C) with soap or alkali. Multiple washes. Lanolin recovered from wash water (useful for waterproofing and cosmetics).
- **Carding**: Tease washed wool between two carding paddles (leather pads covered with bent wire teeth). Disentangles fibers, removes remaining debris, aligns roughly for spinning. Produces rolag (rolled cylinder of aligned fiber).

### Spinning (converting fiber to yarn/thread)

**Drop spindle** (Foundations stage, simplest):
- **Construction**: Round wooden or fired clay whorl (50-100 g, 3-5 cm diameter) on straight wooden shaft (20-30 cm). Notch at top to secure yarn.
- **Process**: Draft (pull thin stream of fiber from rolag), spin spindle (twist inserted by rotation — clockwise for Z-twist, counterclockwise for S-twist), wind spun yarn onto shaft. Speed: ~50-100 meters per hour. Can produce thread from very fine (sewing) to heavy (rope yarn).
- **Twist direction**: Z-twist (clockwise when viewed from above) is standard for single yarns. Plying (combining multiple singles) uses opposite twist (S-twist) to balance the yarn and prevent kinking.

**Spinning wheel** (the Metallurgy-Machine Tools stage transition, 10x throughput):
- **Great wheel (walking wheel)**: Large (1-1.5 m) wheel turned by hand or foot treadle. Drive band connects wheel to spindle. Wheel rotation spins spindle at high speed. Spinner walks backward while drafting fiber, then reverses to wind yarn onto bobbin. Production: 200-500 m/hour.
- **Treadle wheel (Saxony/flax wheel)**: Foot treadle drives wheel, leaving both hands free for fiber control. Flyer and bobbin assembly: flyer adds twist, bobbin winds yarn. Ratios 8:1 to 20:1 (wheel revolutions per spindle revolution). Fine linen requires high ratio. Production: 300-800 m/hour.
- **Charka (book charka)**: Small portable wheel, hand-cranked. For cotton. High speed, fine yarn.

**Plying** (making multi-strand yarn):
- Spin two or more singles together in opposite twist direction. Creates balanced, stronger yarn. 2-ply (most common), 3-ply (rounder, stronger), 4-ply or more for rope and heavy thread.

### Weaving & Cloth Production

**Frame loom** (simplest):
- **Construction**: Rectangular wooden frame. Warp (lengthwise) threads tied top and bottom. Weft (crosswise) thread passed over and under warp by hand. Heddle bar (flat stick with holes and slots) lifts alternate warp threads to create shed (gap for weft passage). Width: 30-60 cm. Length limited by frame size.
- **Production**: Very slow (~0.5-1 m²/hour), but requires minimal construction.

**Pit loom / treadle loom** (production-scale):
- **Construction**: Heavy wooden frame, 1-1.5 m weaving width. Warp wrapped around cloth beam (front) and warp beam (back). Treadles (foot pedals) raise and lower shafts (frames holding heddles — string or wire loops, one per warp thread). 2-8 shafts for different weave structures. Beater (reed frame) packs weft tight.
- **Operation**: Depress treadle → shaft lifts → shed opens → throw shuttle (boat shuttle carrying weft yarn) through shed → beat weft with beater → change treadle → repeat. Rhythm: 30-60 picks (weft passes) per minute.
- **Production**: 1-3 m²/hour of medium-weight cloth. A weaver can produce ~5-10 m² of cloth per day.

**Weave structures**:
- **Tabby (plain weave)**: Over 1, under 1, alternating. Simplest, strongest, least flexible. For canvas, sheeting, bandages.
- **Twill**: Over 2, under 1 (or 2/2 twill). Diagonal rib pattern. Drapes better, more flexible. For clothing, work garments.
- **Satin**: Over 4+, under 1. Smooth face, less durable. For fine fabrics.

**Fulling** (finishing woven cloth):
- Beat cloth in warm water with fuller's earth (clay) or stale urine (ammonia source). Felt the fibers slightly → cloth shrinks 15-25%, becomes denser and warmer. Fuller's earth absorbs oils. Rinse, stretch on tenter frame (hooks on adjustable bars), dry. Napping (raising surface fibers with teasels or wire cards) for fuzzy surface.

**Dyeing**:
- **Plant dyes**: Madder (red — Rubia tinctorum roots), woad/indigo (blue — Isatis tinctoria leaves, fermented), weld (yellow — Reseda luteola), walnut hulls (brown), onion skins (gold/yellow). Mordant (fixative) needed for most: alum (KAl(SO₄)₂) most common, iron (darkens/saddens), copper (brightens green), tin (brightens red/yellow).
- **Process**: Soak cloth in mordant bath (alum 10-20% of fiber weight, 60-80°C, 1 hour). Rinse. Immerse in dye bath (extract dye from plant material by boiling, strain, add cloth, simmer 1-2 hours). Rinse, dry.

### Rope-Making

**Hand-laid rope (simplest, (Foundations stage)**:
- Take 3 yarns (already spun and plied). Attach one end to fixed hook, other to hand-held hook or stick. Walk backward while twisting all three clockwise. The three yarns wrap around each other. Result: 3-strand hawser-laid rope. Length limited by workspace.

**Rope walk** (production-scale):
- **Setup**: Long straight path (50-300 m). "Top" (stationary frame with hooks) at far end. Jack (rotating frame with hooks, powered by hand crank or water wheel) at near end.
- **Process**:
  1. **Forming the strand**: Attach yarns to hooks on jack. Walk to top, threading yarns through register board (maintains spacing). Attach to top hooks. Jack rotates → yarns twist into strand (S-twist). Multiple yarns per strand depending on rope diameter (3 yarns for light rope, 20+ for ship cable).
  2. **Laying the rope**: Take 3 (or 4) completed strands. Attach one end to jack, other to top. Put through central grooved board (laying top). Jack rotates opposite direction (Z-twist). Three strands wrap around each other. Laying top guided by hand to ensure even winding. Result: hawser-laid rope (3-strand, Z-twist over S-twist strands).
- **Tarring** (optional waterproofing): Pass rope through hot pine tar. Prevents rot in wet conditions. Adds ~10-15% weight but greatly extends service life for marine/outdoor rope.

**Rope specifications**:
| Diameter | Break Strength (hemp) | Typical Use |
|----------|----------------------|-------------|
| 6 mm | ~200 kg | Light cordage, tying |
| 12 mm | ~800 kg | General purpose, hoisting |
| 25 mm | ~3,000 kg | Ship rigging, heavy hoist |
| 50 mm | ~12,000 kg | Mooring, ship cable |

### Industrial Applications

- **Canvas and sailcloth**: Heavy plain-weave hemp or cotton cloth, 300-600 g/m². Water-resistant (proof with linseed oil or wax). SQ3 transport sails, tent shelter, windmill sails.
- **Drive belts for power transmission**: Leather (single or multiple thicknesses, riveted or laced at joint) or heavy canvas (rubber-impregnated when available). Width 5-30 cm. Speed: up to 30 m/s for leather. Tension: deflection ~1/64 of span per cm belt width.
- **Filter cloth for chemical filtration**: Tightly woven linen or cotton. Pore size 10-100 μm. For filtering precipitates, catalysts, pigments, acid suspensions in the Chemistry stage. Acid-resistant: use glass fiber or PTFE cloth (Chemistry+).
- **Protective clothing**: Heavy canvas or leather aprons for foundry work (sparks, splash). Wool garments for chemical work (naturally flame-resistant, absorbs acid splashes without melting — unlike synthetics). Treated canvas for furnace proximity (alum-borate fireproofing treatment).
- **Aircraft fabric covering**: Grade A cotton or linen (2-4 oz/yd²) stretched over airframe, treated with nitrocellulose dope (Chemistry stage / [Petrochemicals](../petrochemicals/petroleum-alternatives.md)) for weatherproofing and structural rigidity — see [Aircraft](../aircraft/development.md). Dope shrinks fabric 5-10%, creating taut drum-like surface.
- **Cleanroom garments** (the Silicon-Photolithography stage transition): Woven polyester or Tyvek-like nonwoven polyolefin. Non-linting, washable, covers entire body. See [Specialty Gases](../specialty-gases/gases-packaging-testing.md).
