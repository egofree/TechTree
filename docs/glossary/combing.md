# Combing

> **Type**: material | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: animals, foundations, textiles

Use metal wool combs with long, sharp teeth. Draw long-staple wool through the combs repeatedly. This aligns fibers strictly parallel and removes short fibers (noils). The combed fiber is called top. Combed fiber produces **worsted yarn**: smooth, dense, strong, with a lustrous surface. Worsteds are harder-wearing and take dye more evenly. Worsted yarn yields 400-1200 m per kg.

## Technical Details

Wool combing uses metal combs with long, sharp teeth (10-15 cm). Long-staple wool (staple length 8-25 cm) is drawn through the combs repeatedly. The process aligns fibers strictly parallel and removes short fibers (noils, typically 15-30% of input weight). The combed fiber output is called top — a continuous strand of parallel fibers ready for drafting into roving and then spinning.

Worsted yarn, produced from combed top, is distinctly different from woolen yarn (produced from carded rolag). Worsteds are smooth, dense, strong, and lustrous, with fibers lying parallel along the yarn axis. They are harder-wearing, take dye more evenly, and produce fabric with a crisp hand. Typical worsted yield: 400-1200 m of yarn per kg of combed fiber.

The noils removed during combing are not wasted — they are carded and spun into woolen yarn for blankets and knitted goods. This dual-product output (top for worsteds, noils for woolens) maximizes fiber utilization.

Combing requires long-staple wool (breeds like Lincoln, Romney, and Border Leicester). Short-staple wools are better suited to carding, as the comb would remove too high a proportion as noils. The choice between combing and carding is therefore driven by fiber length and the desired end product.
