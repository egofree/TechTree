# Reduction

> **Type**: process | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: metals, ceramics, silicon

ZnO + CO → Zn(g) + CO₂ at 1000-1100°C. The Imperial Smelting Furnace charges sintered ZnO calcine and preheated coke. Zinc vapor exits the top and is shock-condensed in a lead splash condenser at 450-500°C — molten lead droplets absorb zinc vapor, preventing re-oxidation.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Reduction — the removal of oxygen from metal oxides — is the fundamental chemical process in extractive metallurgy. In [Iron & Steel Production](../metals/iron-steel.md), iron oxides (Fe₂O₃, Fe₃O₄) are reduced by carbon monoxide to produce metallic iron. In [Zinc Production](../metals/zinc.md), ZnO is reduced by CO at 1000-1100°C, but the zinc vapor must be immediately condensed to prevent re-oxidation — a significant engineering challenge. In [Silicon Production](../silicon/mg-si-production.md), SiO₂ is reduced by carbon at 1800-2000°C. In [Kilns](../ceramics/kilns.md), a reduction atmosphere (excess fuel, limited air) produces distinctive ceramic glaze effects and is essential for specific metallurgical operations.

## Technical Details

Reduction in metallurgy uses carbon (as coke or charcoal) or carbon monoxide as the reducing agent. The general pattern is: metal oxide + carbon/CO → metal + CO/CO₂. The thermodynamics follow the Ellingham diagram: below a critical temperature, the oxide is more stable than CO₂; above it, the reverse is true and reduction proceeds spontaneously.

Iron reduction occurs in two stages in the blast furnace: Fe₂O₃ → Fe₃O₄ → FeO → Fe, with CO serving as the primary reductant in the stack and solid carbon providing direct reduction near the tuyeres. The blast furnace is a counter-current reactor: charge descends (ore, coke, limestone) while hot gas ascends (CO, N₂), providing efficient heat and mass transfer.

Zinc reduction presents a unique challenge: zinc boils at 907°C but reduction requires 1000-1100°C, so zinc is produced as a vapor (ZnO + CO → Zn(g) + CO₂). The vapor must be condensed rapidly to prevent the reverse reaction (re-oxidation). The Imperial Smelting Furnace solves this with a lead splash condenser — molten lead droplets at 450-500°C absorb zinc vapor on contact, forming a lead-zinc solution. On cooling to 440°C, immiscible zinc (lighter) separates and is decanted.
