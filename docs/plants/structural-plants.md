# Structural Plants

> **Node ID**: plants.structural-plants
> **Domain**: [Plants & Botanical Resources](./index.md)
> **Dependencies**: [`plants`](./index.md)
> **Enables**: `plants.structural-plants.artemisia-capillaris`
> **Timeline**: Years 0-10
> **Outputs**: structural_timber, building_materials, thatch, wattle, poles
> **Critical**: Yes — all early construction depends on plant-derived structural materials


Plant species harvested for their structural properties — timber, poles, thatch, and woven building materials. Before fired brick, concrete, or steel, all construction depended on plant-derived materials: timber frames for buildings, woven wattle panels plastered with clay, thatched reed or straw roofs, and split-lath fencing. Even after stone and metal become available, wood remains indispensable for scaffolding, formwork, tool handles, and temporary structures.

This capability covers the selection, harvesting, processing, and structural use of woody plants, large grasses, and reeds for construction. All early shelter — from wattle-and-daub huts to timber-framed longhouses — depends on these materials.

## Harvesting

**Tree felling**:
- **Stone axes**: Hard stone (basalt, granite) ground to a cutting edge, mounted on a handle with birch tar or rawhide binding. Slow — 1-4 hours to fell a 30 cm diameter tree. Axe blade must be wider than the handle to prevent binding.

**Strengths**:
- Lowest technology threshold — requires only hard stone and a binding material
- Reusable — a well-made stone axe fells many trees before needing resharpening
- No fuel consumption — unlike fire-assisted felling
- Precise control over cut location and fall direction

**Weaknesses**:
- Extremely slow — 1-4 hours per tree of 30 cm diameter
- Stone edges chip and dull — frequent resharpening required on hard woods
- Limited to smaller trees — felling trees >50 cm diameter is impractical
- Physically exhausting — high labor cost per tree felled

- **Fire-assisted felling**: Char the base of the trunk around the cut line, then scrape away charcoal with stone or shell. Repeat until the tree falls. Used where stone axes are scarce. Produces a characteristic flared stump base.

**Strengths**:
- Works without quality stone tools — fire and a scraping implement suffice
- Can fell larger trees than stone axes — not limited by cutting edge hardness
- Less physical effort — fire does the cutting work rather than muscle power
- Available wherever fire and wood exist — no special materials needed

**Weaknesses**:
- Very slow — many burn-scrape cycles required; a large tree may take a full day
- No control over fall direction — tree falls where the burn-through dictates
- Wastes the butt log — charred base is damaged and unusable for timber
- Fire hazard — dry forest conditions make controlled burning dangerous
- Produces a flared, weakened stump base — limits usable trunk length

- **Coppicing**: Cut deciduous hardwoods (oak, ash, chestnut, acacia) at ground level on a 7-15 year cycle. Stumps regrow multiple straight poles from the stool. Sustainable indefinitely — medieval coppice stools are documented at 500+ years old. Produces straight poles ideal for wattle, fencing, and tool handles.

**Strengths**:
- Sustainable indefinitely — same stool produces poles for centuries without replanting
- Produces perfectly straight poles — multiple stems compete for light, growing straight up
- No replanting required — regrowth is automatic from the established root system
- Short rotation — 7-15 year cycle vs. 30-80 years for timber trees
- Harvested poles need minimal processing — already straight and small-diameter

**Weaknesses**:
- Limited to poles and small roundwood — cannot produce large beams or planks
- Requires established coppice stools — takes 7-15 years from first cutting to first harvest
- Species-specific — only certain hardwoods coppice vigorously (oak, ash, chestnut, hazel)
- Needs ongoing management — stools must be protected from grazing and competitive vegetation
- Not suitable for construction timber — pole diameter limited to 5-15 cm depending on rotation

- **Pollarding**: Like coppicing but cut at 2-3 m height, keeping regrowth above grazing animals. Used for fodder and straight poles in pastoral landscapes.

**Selection criteria**:
- **Straightness**: Minimal knots and curves for beams and posts. Select trees grown in dense stands (self-pruning lowers branch knots).
- **Growth rate**: Fast-growing species (albizia, acacia, poplar) produce usable timber in 5-10 years but are softer. Slow-growing (oak, teak, ironwood) take 30-80 years but produce dense, durable heartwood.
- **Season of harvest**: Fell in dormant season (late autumn to early spring) when sap is down. Timber felled in winter has lower moisture content, seasons faster, and is more resistant to insect attack.

**Extraction**: Drag logs from forest using animal power (oxen, horses) or human crews with rope and rollers. Skid roads follow natural contours. In wet terrain, use log slides (greased timber tracks) or wait for frozen ground.

## Processing

**Seasoning (drying)**:
- **Air drying**: Stack lumber with stickers (thin cross-battens) between layers for airflow. Protect from rain with a roof or tarp. Drying time: 6-12 months for softwood (50 mm boards), 1-3 years for hardwood. Target moisture content: 15-20% for structural use.
- **Accelerated methods**: Stack over a low fire or in a kiln (once ceramics technology provides one). Reduces drying time to weeks but risks checking (surface cracks) and case hardening if too rapid.
- **Why it matters**: Green (unseasoned) wood shrinks 3-8% as it dries. Shrinkage is greatest tangentially (around the ring), moderate radially, and negligible along the grain. Unseasoned beams warp, split joints open, and structures settle unevenly.

**Sawing and splitting**:
- **Riving (splitting)**: Drive wedges (wood or iron) along the grain with a maul. Produces naturally strong pieces — splitting follows the grain, preserving continuous fibers. Best for planks, shingles, and fence posts. Works best with straight-grained species.

**Strengths**:
- Preserves continuous grain fibers — split pieces are naturally stronger than sawn equivalents
- Fastest method for producing planks — wedges and a maul split a log in minutes
- No specialized saws required — wooden wedges work if iron is unavailable
- Wastes less wood — split follows natural grain, producing less sawdust than sawing
- Produces naturally durable shingles and planks — uncut grain resists moisture penetration

**Weaknesses**:
- Only works with straight-grained species — twisted or knotty wood splits unpredictably
- Cannot produce dimensional lumber to precise specifications — split follows the grain, not a straight line
- Limited to radial or tangential splits — cannot cut across the grain at all
- Produces tapered, irregular planks — not suitable for applications requiring uniform thickness
- Requires skill to read the grain — wrong wedge placement produces short, useless pieces

- **Pit sawing**: One sawyer stands above the log on a scaffold, the other below in a pit. Use a two-handled rip saw (2-3 m blade). Produces dimensional lumber but is extremely labor-intensive — 1-2 m² of boards per day.

**Strengths**:
- Produces true dimensional lumber — flat, uniform-thickness boards to any specified dimension
- Works on any species — grain direction and knots do not prevent sawing
- Can produce wide boards from large logs — splits are limited to narrow pieces
- Precise control over board thickness and width — sawyer guides the cut visually

**Weaknesses**:
- Extremely labor-intensive — 1-2 m² of boards per day for a two-person team
- Requires a specialized rip saw (2-3 m blade) — iron or steel blade is a significant investment
- Physically demanding — the pit sawyer works in cramped, dusty conditions below the log
- Wastes wood as sawdust — kerf (blade width) removes material that splitting preserves
- Requires two skilled workers — both sawyers must coordinate rhythm and angle

- **Whipsawing**: Similar to pit saw but with the log elevated on trestles. More common where pits are impractical.

**Shaping and joining**:
- **Hewing**: Flatten round logs into squared beams with a broad axe (side axe, beveled on one face only). Trim to flat surfaces for wall plates, sills, and rafters.
- **Mortise and tenon**: Cut a rectangular hole (mortise) in one timber and a projecting tongue (tenon) on another. Secure with wooden pegs (trenails). The foundational joint of timber framing — no metal fasteners required.
- **Lap joints**: Overlap two timbers, cut half the thickness from each where they cross. Used where beams meet posts at right angles.
- **Notching**: Saddle notches and V-notches lock round logs together in log cabin construction. Simple, strong, requires only an axe.

## Structural Properties

**Load-bearing characteristics**:
- **Compressive strength (parallel to grain)**: 30-60 MPa for common hardwoods. Wood is strongest in compression along the grain — suitable for posts and columns.
- **Tensile strength**: 50-120 MPa along the grain. Wood is excellent in tension — allows suspended beams and tie-beams in roof trusses.
- **Bending strength**: 40-80 MPa for structural hardwoods. Determines beam and rafter sizing. A 150×150 mm oak beam spans ~4 m under typical floor loads.
- **Weakness**: Shear strength across the grain is only 5-15 MPa. Failures occur at joints, not in mid-span. Connection design is more critical than member sizing.

**Wood moisture and shrinkage**: All wood shrinks as it dries. Tangential shrinkage (around growth rings): 6-12%. Radial shrinkage (across rings): 2-6%. Longitudinal (along grain): <0.3%. Differential shrinkage between tangential and radial planes causes warping and checking. Heartwood shrinks less than sapwood.

## Bamboo Construction

Bamboo (*Bambusa*, *Dendrocalamus*, *Phyllostachys* spp.) is a grass, not a tree, but provides structural material rivaling timber:

**Properties**: Tensile strength up to 350 MPa (comparable to mild steel at 400 MPa). Density 600-800 kg/m³. Compressive strength 60-80 MPa. Weight-to-strength ratio exceeds most structural timbers. Hollow culm provides excellent moment of inertia per unit weight — structurally efficient as a column or beam.

**Harvesting**: Cut mature culms (3-5 years old) at ground level during dry season (lower starch content reduces insect attack). Culms grow to full height in a single growing season (60-120 days) and mature over 2-4 subsequent years. A managed bamboo grove yields 10-30 tonnes/ha/year of usable culm.

**Treatment for preservation**: Bamboo is susceptible to powder-post beetles and fungal decay. Treatment methods:
- Soak in water (immersion) for 4-6 weeks to leach starches that attract insects
- Borax/boric acid solution (5-10% concentration, 1-2 weeks immersion) — most effective chemical treatment available with minimal chemistry
- Smoke treatment over slow fire for 1-2 weeks (traditional method in tropical Asia)

**Joinery**: Bamboo cannot be nailed or screwed (splitting). Traditional joints use lashing (rattan or cordage), dowels (bamboo pins through pre-drilled holes), and notching (cutting fish-mouth joints where horizontal members meet verticals). Steel banding or wire ties become possible once metal is available.

## Thatching Detail

**Reed thatch (*Phragmites australis*)**:
- Roof lifespan: 30-40 years for a well-laid reed thatch (up to 50-80 years with maintenance and proper pitch)
- Harvest reeds in winter when dead and dry (December-February). Cut with sickle or scythe at water's edge. Bundle into yealms (tight bundles ~60 cm diameter at the butt). Combed to remove leaves and straighten
- Ridge requires replacement every 10-15 years; main coat lasts 30-40 years. A reed thatch on a 45-55° pitch sheds water without any waterproof membrane
- Reed yield: approximately 500-800 bundles per hectare of managed reed bed; ~4-5 bundles per m² of roof

**Straw thatch (wheat, rye)**:
- Roof lifespan: 15-25 years (shorter than reed because straw decays faster)
- Use long-stem heritage varieties (modern short-straw wheat is unsuitable). Cut with a binder that preserves full straw length
- Straw must be threshed carefully (flailing, not machine-threshing) to avoid breaking stems. Soak briefly before thatching to restore flexibility
- Spar coating: Fix bundles with hazel or willow spars (split rods ~1 m long, twisted into U-shaped staples driven into the coat)

** Heather thatch (*Calluna vulgaris*)**:
- Used in upland Britain and Scandinavia where reeds are scarce. Creates a dense, springy roof. Lifespan: 20-30 years. Harvested in late summer when flowering.

## Willow Withies and Basketry

Willow (*Salix* spp.) provides flexible rods (withies) essential for basketry, wattle panels, and structural ties:

**Coppicing for withies**: Cut willow stools annually to produce long, straight, flexible rods (1.5-3 m). *Salix viminalis* (basket willow) is the preferred species, producing 20-40 rods per stool per year. Plant 40-60 cm cuttings at 30-50 cm spacing in damp ground. First harvestable crop in year 2.

**Sorting by use**: Rods sorted by length and diameter. Long, thin rods (2-3 m, <8 mm) for fine basketry. Medium rods for wattle panels. Thick rods (>15 mm) for structural ties and furniture frames.

**Soaking**: Dry withies are brittle. Soak in water for 3-7 days before working to restore full flexibility. Mellow (wrap in damp cloth) for an additional 12-24 hours after soaking for optimal pliability.

**Basketry structures**: Woven baskets use a simple over-under pattern on a rigid framework (stakes). Randing (single-rod weaving), pairing (two-rod twist), and waling (three-rod weave) produce progressively stronger baskets. A skilled basket-maker produces 2-4 medium baskets per day.

## Wood Seasoning Detail

**Air drying**: Stack lumber with stickers (thin cross-battens, 20-25 mm thick) between each layer for airflow. Protect from rain with a roof or tarp (do not enclose sides — airflow is essential). General rule: **1 year per 25 mm of thickness** for hardwoods; half that for softwoods. A 50 mm oak board air-dries in 2 years. A 100 mm pine beam in 1 year.

**Strengths**:
- No energy input — sun and air do the work, no fuel or kiln required
- Gentle drying produces the highest-quality lumber — minimal internal stress, checking, or case hardening
- No special equipment — stickers, a roof, and time are sufficient
- Works for any wood thickness — thick beams simply take longer
- Zero risk of kiln defects (honeycombing, case hardening)

**Weaknesses**:
- Very slow — 1 year per 25 mm thickness for hardwoods
- Large inventory of drying lumber ties up capital and space for months to years
- Weather-dependent — humid climates extend drying time; rain can stain exposed wood
- Cannot achieve moisture contents below local equilibrium (~12-18% outdoors)
- Requires covered storage area with good airflow — significant space for commercial quantities

**Kiln drying**: Once ceramics technology provides a kiln (or a purpose-built drying kiln from brick), drying accelerates to 3-6 weeks at 60-80°C. Requires careful control — too-fast drying causes:
- Surface checking (cracks on the surface as it dries faster than the core)
- Case hardening (surface dries and seals, trapping moisture in the core)
- Honeycombing (internal checking from trapped moisture stress)
- Solution: start at lower temperature (40-50°C), increase gradually. Monitor weight loss to track moisture content.

**Strengths**:
- 10-20× faster than air drying — 3-6 weeks vs. 1-2 years for equivalent thickness
- Can achieve lower moisture content than air drying — down to 6-8% for interior furniture
- Predictable schedule — not dependent on weather or season
- Kills insects and fungal spores in the wood — heat sterilization during drying
- Enables year-round lumber production — no waiting for favorable drying weather

**Weaknesses**:
- Requires fuel to maintain 60-80°C for weeks — significant energy cost
- Risk of kiln defects if temperature ramped too fast — checking, case hardening, honeycombing
- Requires a brick kiln or purpose-built drying chamber — ceramics technology prerequisite
- Higher labor for monitoring — temperature and moisture must be tracked daily
- Unequal drying in thick stock — exterior dries faster than core unless carefully staged

**Moisture content targets**: Green wood: 30-80% moisture (hardwood higher than softwood). Structural use (framing): 15-20%. Interior furniture: 8-12%. Wood in heated buildings equilibrates to ~8-12% EMC (equilibrium moisture content). Wood exposed to outdoor air: 12-18%.

**Testing dryness without instruments**: Weigh a sample piece, dry it further in an oven or over a fire, re-weigh. When weight stops decreasing, it's at oven-dry. Compare original weight to oven-dry: moisture content = (wet weight - dry weight) / dry weight × 100%.

**Weather resistance and treatment**:
- **Natural durability**: Heartwood of durable species (acacia, oak, chestnut, teak-family) resists decay for 15-50+ years in ground contact. Sapwood of all species rots within 2-5 years.
- **Charring**: Lightly char the surface of posts to be buried in ground. The carbonized layer resists moisture absorption and fungal attack. Extends ground-contact life from 3-5 years to 10-15 years.
- **Tar and pitch coating**: Apply pine tar or birch tar to exposed surfaces. Water-repellent and mildly antifungal. Reapply every 3-5 years.
- **Elevated foundations**: Set posts on flat stones rather than directly in soil. Stone capillary break prevents rot at the critical ground-line zone. Simplest and most effective preservation method.

**Thatch roofing**:
- **Materials**: Reed (*Phragmites*), long straw (wheat, rye), heather, bracken, palm fronds. Reeds last 50-80 years; straw thatch lasts 15-25 years.

**Reed thatch**:

**Strengths**:
- Longest-lasting roofing material available without industrial technology — 30-50 years, up to 80 with maintenance
- Sheds water without any waterproof membrane — 45-55° pitch is sufficient
- Excellent insulation — 300 mm thickness provides thermal performance rivaling modern insulation
- Fire-resistant when properly compacted — dense reed lay resists spark ignition better than straw
- Reed beds are a renewable resource — managed beds yield 500-800 bundles/ha annually

**Weaknesses**:
- Requires access to reed beds — grows only in wetlands, lakeshores, and river margins
- Highly skilled labor required — reed thatching is a specialist trade with years of apprenticeship
- Fire risk remains significant — spark arrestors on chimneys are mandatory
- Vulnerable to bird damage — birds pull reeds for nesting; netting may be needed
- Thick roof reduces usable attic space — 300 mm coat plus structure adds significant depth

**Straw thatch**:

**Strengths**:
- Widely available — a byproduct of grain cultivation, no special harvesting location needed
- Uses heritage grain varieties that would otherwise have no market — long-stem wheat and rye
- Lighter than reed — less structural load on the roof frame
- Easier to work — straw is more flexible and forgiving than stiff reed
- Can be sourced from any grain-producing farm — no wetland dependency

**Weaknesses**:
- Shorter lifespan — 15-25 years vs. 30-50+ for reed
- Decays faster — straw absorbs moisture more readily than reed
- More vulnerable to rodents and birds — grain remnants attract pests
- Requires long-stem heritage varieties — modern short-straw wheat is unsuitable
- Must be threshed carefully — machine-threshing breaks stems; flail threshing is slower
- **Technique**: Bundle stems, lay in courses 300 mm thick, secure with hazel or willow spars (split rods twisted into staples). Layer from eaves to ridge, each course overlapping the one below. Ridge capped with woven turves or a decorative top layer.
- **Pitch**: Minimum 45° slope for water shedding. Steeper is better — 50-55° is ideal.
- **Fire risk**: Thatch is extremely flammable. Spark arrestors on chimneys are mandatory. Lime wash or alum treatment provides marginal fire retardancy. Keep open flames and sparks well away.

## Wattle-and-Daub Construction

Wattle-and-daub is the earliest and most accessible structural building technique — it requires no sawn lumber, only saplings, mud, and labor.

1. **Frame**: Erect a timber frame of posts (100-150 mm diameter) set 300-600 mm apart in a trench or on a sill beam. Horizontal wattles (rods) woven between posts.
2. **Weave**: Weave green saplings (willow, hazel, acacia), reeds, or split bamboo horizontally between vertical posts at 50-100 mm spacing. Keep material green and flexible during weaving.
3. **Daub**: Mix clay soil with straw or animal hair (reinforcement) and enough water to make a stiff mud. Press into both sides of the woven panel, forcing mud through the wattle to key it. Smooth the surface.
4. **Finish**: Lime wash (if [lime](../ceramics/lime.md) is available) or leave as bare earth. Earthen walls are surprisingly durable if kept dry — roof overhang of 300+ mm is essential.

Wall thickness: 100-150 mm. Insulation value: moderate (poorer than solid masonry, better than single-skin timber). Infill panels in timber-framed buildings use this technique worldwide.

## Safety & Hazards

- **Tree felling**: The most dangerous operation in forestry. Always plan the fall direction (wedge cuts on the fall side, back cut on the opposite). Retreat at 45° from the fall line the moment the tree starts to go. Never fell alone. A leaning tree can barber-chair (split vertically and kick back).
- **Saw injuries**: Hand saws and axes cause lacerations. Keep blades sharp (dull tools require more force and are harder to control). Cut away from the body. Secure the workpiece before cutting.
- **Structural collapse**: Green timber settles and joints loosen as wood dries. Design joints with wooden pegs that can be re-driven. Inspect roof trusses seasonally. Never exceed beam spans — a cracked beam under load fails without warning.
- **Rot and decay**: Inspect structural posts at ground line annually. Probe with a knife — soft wood indicates rot. Replace compromised members immediately. A rotted post can collapse the entire frame.
