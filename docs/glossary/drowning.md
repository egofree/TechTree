# Drowning

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: animals, energy, transport

Working in and around water carries inherent risk. Pond banks are slippery with clay and algae. Wading in deep mud can trap legs. Never work alone near deep water. Keep a rope or pole within reach for rescue. Children are at particular risk around ponds; fence or barrier the perimeter in family settlements.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Drowning risk accompanies every technology that involves water: aquaculture ponds, water wheels and turbines, pumped hydro storage reservoirs, and shipping. As the tech tree introduces increasingly large water impoundments and higher flow rates, the drowning hazard scales accordingly. Safety measures — working in pairs, keeping rescue equipment at hand, and fencing perimeter hazards — are essential non-technical requirements that must accompany any water infrastructure.

## Technical Details

In aquaculture, pond construction creates concentrated drowning hazards: steep-sided ponds with slippery clay and algae-covered banks, deep mud that can trap legs, and water depths of 1.5-3 m. Children are at particular risk in family settlements. Mitigation includes perimeter fencing, shallower edge slopes (1:2 or gentler), and designated safe access points with rope or pole rescue equipment.

At water power installations (water wheels, turbines, mill races), the hazard combines deep water with mechanical entrapment. Water intake structures create powerful suction currents. Turbine casings and draft tubes are underwater confined spaces. Mill races channel fast-moving water with smooth, slippery stone or concrete surfaces.

Safety protocols apply across all water installations: never work alone near deep water; wear life jackets when working on or near water installations; maintain safety ropes at all water access points; post depth markers at ponds and reservoirs; fence or barrier all water installations in settled areas; and train all workers in basic rescue techniques including reach-and-throw methods (never enter the water for a rescue without training and a safety line).
