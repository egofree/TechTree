# Sails

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: energy, textiles, transport

Woven fabric panels cut on the bias and rigged to convert wind energy into propulsion or mechanical power. The primary means of marine propulsion and wind-powered machinery before steam engines.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Sails appear at the intersection of [Wind Energy](../energy/wind.md), [Textile Production](../textiles/sewing-tailoring.md), and [Shipping](../transport/shipping.md). Canvas or sailcloth — tightly woven cotton or linen at 200-600 g/m² depending on sail size and position — must be manufactured before wind propulsion is available. Panel layout follows stress lines, with panels cut on the bias (45° to the warp threads) for optimal shape retention under load.

## Technical Details

Sail construction requires understanding of aerodynamics and materials. Panels are sewn with reinforced edges and bolt ropes for attachment to spars. The simplest rigs — square sails — need only basic weaving and sewing capability. More efficient fore-and-aft rigs (lateen, gaff, Bermuda) require more complex cutting and rigging but provide better windward performance.

Windmill sails follow similar principles but are built as framed wooden structures with adjustable canvas or wooden slats. Sail area, camber, and angle of attack determine power output. For marine use, sail area scales with vessel displacement: roughly 1 m² of sail per 50-100 kg of displacement for working vessels.
