# Hydrofluoric Acid (HF)

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, photolithography

An extremely hazardous mineral acid produced by reacting fluorite (CaF₂) with concentrated sulfuric acid at 200-300°C. HF is the only common acid that dissolves silica and silicate minerals, making it indispensable for glass etching, silicon wafer processing, and fluoride chemistry. Commercial concentration: 48-50% aqueous solution.

## Context in the Tech Tree

HF bridges the chemistry and photolithography domains. It is essential for etching silicon wafers (removing SiO₂ to create circuit patterns), producing synthetic cryolite for aluminum smelting, and manufacturing fluorocarbon gases (CF₄, SF₆) used as plasma etchants in semiconductor fabrication. The acid chemistry domain produces HF as one of five mineral acids, alongside sulfuric, nitric, hydrochloric, and phosphoric.

HF is the most dangerous common industrial acid. At 5-49% concentration it causes severe deep-tissue burns that may not be immediately painful — the delay makes them more dangerous. HF penetrates skin and attacks bone, causing systemic fluoride poisoning. A hand-sized splash of 50% HF can be lethal. Calcium gluconate gel must be on-site before any HF handling as the specific antidote.

## Technical Details

Production uses a lead or steel retort (HF attacks glass — never use glass apparatus). Fluorite is heated with concentrated H₂SO₄: CaF₂ + H₂SO₄ → 2HF + CaSO₄. The gaseous HF (bp 19.5°C) is distilled off and absorbed in water using lead or PTFE vessels. Storage requires PTFE or polyethylene containers — never glass.

In semiconductor processing, dilute HF (typically 1-10% buffered with ammonium fluoride as BOE — buffered oxide etch) removes silicon dioxide selectively without attacking silicon or photoresist. This selective etching is fundamental to photolithographic pattern transfer. In glass manufacturing, HF produces frosted or etched glass surfaces by dissolving the outer SiO₂ layer.
