# Noise

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: energy, health, metals, mining, textiles

Unwanted sound — or more precisely, sound at intensity levels that cause hearing damage, communication interference, or physiological stress. Continuous noise above 85 dB(A) as an 8-hour time-weighted average requires a hearing conservation program. Above 90 dB(A), engineering controls or administrative limits on exposure time are mandatory. Every 3 dB increase halves the permissible exposure time: 90 dB = 8 hours, 93 dB = 4 hours, 96 dB = 2 hours, 99 dB = 1 hour.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Noise is a pervasive occupational hazard across the industrial domains. Forge hammers produce ~95 dB, riveting machines ~105 dB, milling machines ~85 dB at 1 meter. Mining drilling operations, steelmaking electric arc furnaces, textile weaving looms, and internal combustion engines all generate noise levels that require hearing protection and exposure-time management.

The health domain's occupational health capability addresses noise through a hierarchy of controls: engineering controls (enclosure, vibration isolation, mufflers, silencers), administrative controls (rotation of workers to limit exposure duration), and personal protective equipment (earplugs rated NRR 15-33 dB, earmuffs rated NRR 20-30 dB). Audiometric testing tracks cumulative hearing loss over time.

## Technical Details

Noise-induced hearing loss (NIHL) is irreversible. It results from damage to the hair cells of the cochlea, first affecting the 4000 Hz frequency range (the "4 kHz notch" visible on audiograms). Damage accumulates with each over-exposure — there is no recovery. The 85 dB(A) threshold is based on the equal-energy principle: a 3 dB increase represents a doubling of sound energy, which halves the safe exposure duration.

Sound level measurement uses frequency-weighted filters: A-weighting (dB(A)) approximates human hearing sensitivity, de-emphasizing very low and very high frequencies. C-weighting (dB(C)) measures peak impulse noise. Octave-band analysis identifies specific frequency contributions for targeted engineering controls. For noise control design, the first priority is reducing noise at the source (quieter machinery, vibration isolation), followed by blocking the transmission path (enclosures, barriers, absorptive treatment), and finally protecting the receiver (PPE).
