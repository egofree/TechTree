Coke Production

Node ID: energy.fuels.coke Domain: Energy Timeline: Years 15-25 Outputs: coke, coal_gas, coal_tar, ammonia_liquor

Coke is to coal what charcoal is to wood — the refined, high-energy, low-impurity fuel that enables high-temperature industrial processes. Coke is the only solid fuel capable of sustaining the 1800-2100°C temperatures required for blast furnace ironmaking and silicon reduction.

Beehive Oven Method

  • Construction: Dome-shaped brick or stone chamber, ~3-4 m diameter, 2 m tall. Opening at top for charging and ignition, side opening for air control and coke removal.
  • Process:
    1. Charge 3-6 tonnes of crushed coal (5-15 cm pieces) through top opening.
    2. Ignite coal at top surface (light wood/coal kindling).
    3. The fire burns downward through the coal bed. Volatile gases (coal tar, ammonia, coal gas) burn at the top, providing heat for carbonization below.
    4. Air supply controlled by covering top opening with loose bricks — restrict to maintain carbonization temperature (~900-1100°C) without full combustion.
    5. Duration: 48-72 hours for full carbonization.
    6. Quench: Spray with water (carefully — massive steam eruption). Or seal oven and let cool inertly.
    7. Remove coke through side door with iron rakes.
  • Yield: ~60-70% by weight from good coking coal.
  • Limitation: Volatile byproducts are burned rather than captured — wasteful but simple.

By-Product Recovery Oven (Advanced)

  • Horizontal silica brick chamber, 12-15 m long, 0.4-0.5 m wide, 4-6 m tall. Heated externally by burning collected coal gas. Coal charge ~15-20 tonnes. Carbonization time ~16-20 hours.
  • All volatile byproducts captured: coal tar, benzol, ammonia, sulfur. Higher quality coke, but requires more construction effort.

CSR and CRI are the critical metrics for blast furnace coke — high CSR means the coke maintains structural strength even while being consumed, supporting the burden weight above.

Quenching Safety

  • Wet quenching (standard): Spray water onto incandescent coke in the quenching car. Produces massive steam plume. Risk: water trapped in porous coke can cause steam explosion if coke is handled too soon. Always allow 30+ minutes cooling after quenching. Never quench in confined spaces — steam displaces oxygen.
  • Dry quenching (advanced): Pass inert gas (nitrogen) over hot coke to cool it. Recovers sensible heat (~1.5 MJ/kg) for steam generation. No water contact, no steam cloud. Requires gas-tight cooling chamber and circulation fans.

Coke Oven Gas Utilization

By-product ovens yield ~300-350 m³ of gas per tonne of coal carbonized. Composition (typical):

Component Volume % Note
Hydrogen (H₂) 50-60% Main energy carrier
Methane (CH₄) 20-30% High heating value
Carbon monoxide (CO) 5-8% Toxic, combustible
Nitrogen (N₂) 3-5% Diluent
Carbon dioxide (CO₂) 2-3% Diluent
Hydrocarbons (C₂H₄, etc.) 2-3% Condensible
  • Heating value: ~16-18 MJ/m³ (richer than producer gas at 5-6 MJ/m³). Sufficient to heat the coke ovens themselves (self-sustaining process) and supply surplus for steelworks heating.
  • Uses: Oven heating (primary), steel reheating furnaces, boiler fuel, town gas supply. Must be cooled and scrubbed of tar and naphthalene before use in burners.

Byproduct Recovery

Coal tar (~3-5% of coal weight) is a valuable chemical feedstock:

  • Distillation fractions: Light oil (<180°C) → benzene, toluene, xylene. Middle oil (180-240°C) → naphthalene, phenol, cresols. Heavy oil (240-300°C) → creosote (wood preservative). Anthracene oil (300-400°C) → anthracene. Pitch residue (>400°C) → road tar, electrode binder.
  • Ammonia: Recovered from gas stream as ammonium sulfate fertilizer (~1% of coal weight).
  • Sulfur: Recovered from gas as elemental sulfur or sulfuric acid feedstock.
