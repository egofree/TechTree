# Fermentation

Fermentation is a metabolic process in which microorganisms (yeasts, bacteria) convert sugars and other organic substrates into alcohols, acids, or gases under anaerobic conditions. Certain yeasts and bacteria produce glycerol during sugar fermentation, and the "neutral sulfite process" (adding Na₂SO₃ to yeast fermentation) diverts ethanol fermentation toward glycerol production, yielding 20–30% of sugar weight as glycerol. This process was historically important during World War I when German glycerol supplies were blockaded.

In food and agriculture, fermentation preserves food (sauerkraut, yogurt, pickles), produces alcoholic beverages, and generates leavening for bread. In industrial chemistry, fermentation produces ethanol (for fuel and solvent use), acetic acid, lactic acid, and a range of organic chemicals from renewable feedstocks. Fermentation requires temperature control (typically 25–35°C for yeast), a sterile or controlled environment to prevent contamination, and a source of fermentable sugars.

In the bootstrap context, fermentation is one of the earliest biotechnological processes available, requiring only basic vessels, temperature control, and a source of sugar or starch. It provides a route to ethanol, organic acids, and other chemicals without petroleum feedstocks, making it a key bridge technology between agricultural production and chemical industry.
