# Inhalation

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: glass, health, chemistry

The breathing in of airborne contaminants — gases, vapors, dusts, fumes, and aerosols — that constitutes the primary occupational exposure route for toxic substances in industrial settings. First aid response: move the exposed person to fresh air immediately. For CO exposure, which binds hemoglobin 200× more strongly than oxygen, administer 100% oxygen and monitor for delayed neurological effects.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Inhalation hazards appear across nearly every industrial domain. Glassblowing exposes workers to metal oxide fumes (lead, cadmium, chromium from colored glass batches). Smelting produces sulfur dioxide, carbon monoxide, and heavy metal fumes. Chemical processing generates acid mists, solvent vapors, and toxic gas leaks (phosgene, arsine, phosphine). Mining generates silica dust (silicosis risk) and diesel exhaust. Semiconductor fabrication involves dopant gases where even ppm-level inhalation is lethal.

The health domain's occupational health capability addresses inhalation through ventilation engineering, respiratory protection, and exposure monitoring. Proper ventilation design — local exhaust at the source, not general room dilution — is the primary engineering control.

## Technical Details

Inhalation exposure depends on contaminant concentration in air, particle size (particles <10 μm reach deep lungs; <2.5 μm enter alveoli), breathing rate (higher during physical labor), and duration. Exposure limits are expressed as TLV-TWA (threshold limit value, time-weighted average over 8 hours), TLV-STEL (short-term exposure limit, 15-minute ceiling), and IDLH (immediately dangerous to life or health).

Control measures follow a hierarchy: elimination (remove the hazard), substitution (less toxic alternative), engineering controls (ventilation, enclosure), administrative controls (work procedures, time limits), and personal protective equipment (respirators as last resort). For glassblowing and chemical work, local exhaust ventilation capturing contaminants at the source provides the most effective protection.
