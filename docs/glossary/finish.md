# Finish

Finish refers to the final surface treatment applied to a component or structure to achieve the desired appearance, smoothness, and protection. In woodworking and composite construction, finishing involves progressive sanding (120 → 220 → 400 grit) followed by a clear varnish or paint topcoat. After finishing, dynamic balance must be verified—for rotating components like propellers, chalk marks on each blade tip should overlap perfectly when observed from the side during a spin test.

In metalworking, finish describes the surface texture produced by machining, grinding, or polishing operations. Surface finish is quantified by roughness average (Ra) in micrometers or microinches. A turned surface might have Ra 1.6–6.3 μm, while a ground surface achieves Ra 0.2–0.8 μm, and lapped surfaces reach Ra 0.05–0.2 μm. The required finish depends on the component's function: bearing surfaces need smooth finishes to reduce friction, while decorative surfaces need visual uniformity.

Finish quality affects both performance and longevity. Rough surfaces corrode faster, wear more rapidly, and create stress concentrations that can lead to fatigue failure. In a bootstrap context, achieving good finishes with limited tooling requires patience and systematic progression through abrasive grades.
