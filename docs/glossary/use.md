# Use

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, computing, energy, machine-tools, measurement, mining, textiles

## Context in the Tech Tree

Use refers to the specific application or manner of employment of a material, tool, or process — the practical deployment of resources toward functional ends. Across the bootstrapping sequence, understanding the proper use of each material is essential: [Fibers](../textiles/fibers.md) like sinew are used for binding rather than weaving; [Fuels](../energy/fuels.md) are used differently depending on their energy density and combustion characteristics; [Bearings & Abrasives](../machine-tools/bearings-abrasives.md) are used according to their hardness and load capacity. In [Precision Metrology](../measurement/precision-metrology.md), instruments must be used within their calibrated range to maintain accuracy.

## Technical Details

The concept of "use" encompasses both the intended application and the conditions under which a material or tool performs optimally. Sinew fiber illustrates how natural materials often have specific use patterns dictated by their physical properties: sinew's collagen fibers shrink and harden as they dry, creating tension that strengthens joints. This property makes it ideal for bindings (arrow fletching, tool hafting) but unsuitable for weaving into cloth — unlike cotton or wool fibers, which are flexible and durable when dry.

In energy systems, the use of fuels follows a hierarchy: charcoal for clean, hot forging; coal for steam generation and smelting; coke for blast furnace ironmaking; and wood for heating and early industrial processes. Each fuel's use is determined by its calorific value, impurity content, and combustion characteristics.

Proper use also extends to safety: using tools and materials within their rated parameters prevents failures and injuries.
