# Structural Failure

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, energy, transport

The catastrophic collapse or destruction of engineered structures due to overload, fatigue, material degradation, or design inadequacy. Understanding failure modes is essential for designing safe industrial equipment and infrastructure.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Structural failure hazards appear in [Kilns](../ceramics/kilns.md) where thermal cycling degrades refractory brickwork, in [Wind Energy](../energy/wind.md) where storm forces destroy towers and rotors, and in [Aviation](../transport/aviation.md) where structural failure in flight is invariably fatal. Each domain has characteristic failure modes and prevention strategies.

## Technical Details

Windmill structural failure: towers and rotors experience extreme forces in high winds. Overspeed in storms can destroy rotors and throw debris hundreds of meters. Furling mechanisms (turning the rotor out of the wind) or mechanical brakes are non-negotiable safety features. If both fail during a storm, the only recourse is to cut the sail frames away with axes before the mill destroys itself. Unbalanced sails cause destructive vibration that can shake a tower apart over time.

Kiln structural failure: repeated heating and cooling cycles cause spalling (surface flaking) of refractory bricks. Differential thermal expansion between bricks and the steel shell creates stress. Moisture trapped in refractory turns to steam during heat-up, causing explosive spalling. Proper dry-out schedules (slow temperature ramp on first firing) prevent this.

Aviation structural failure: exceeded design limits cause wing spar failure, tail empennage separation, or fuselage decompression. Fatigue cracks propagate from stress concentrators (rivet holes, notches) under cyclic loading. Inspection protocols (visual, dye penetrant, X-ray) detect cracks before they reach critical length. Factor of safety in aircraft structures is typically 1.5 — meaning the structure is designed to withstand 1.5× the maximum expected load.
