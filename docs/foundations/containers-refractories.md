### Pottery & Kilns

Pottery provides containers for storage, transport, cooking, and — critically — refractory vessels for metallurgy and chemistry.

**Clay identification and processing**:
- **Source**: River banks, lake shores, floodplains. Clay = earth that becomes plastic and holds shape when wet, hardens permanently when fired.
- **Test**: Roll clay into pencil-thick coil, bend around finger. If it bends without cracking, it has adequate plasticity.
- **Processing steps**:
  1. **Dry and crush**: Break dried clay into powder. Remove stones, roots, organic matter.
  2. **Slake**: Soak in water for 24-48 hours until completely dissolved into slurry.
  3. **Screen**: Push through coarse screen (4-6 mesh) to remove remaining debris.
  4. **Settle and decant**: Let heavy particles settle, pour off fine clay suspension. Repeat for purity.
  5. **Dewater**: Pour onto absorbent surface (plaster slab, dry wood, canvas) until workable consistency.
  6. **Wedging**: Knead clay on flat surface to remove air bubbles and homogenize. 50-100 kneads. Air bubbles cause explosions during firing.

**Tempering (prevents cracking during drying and firing)**:
- **Grog**: Crushed, fired pottery (20-30% by volume). Best all-purpose temper.
- **Sand**: Clean, angular sand (15-25%). Easy to find, works well.
- **Crushed shell**: 10-20%. Calcine shells first (heat to 600°C, crush).
- **Organic**: Chaff, sawdust, shredded dung (burns out during firing, creates porous ware — good for water cooling, bad for holding liquids).

**Forming methods**:
- **Pinch pots**: Start with ball of clay, press thumb into center, pinch walls to even thickness (~6-8 mm). Good for small bowls, cups. Size limited to ~15 cm.
- **Coil building**: Roll clay into ropes (1-2 cm diameter). Build walls by stacking coils, blending each into the one below with smoothing tool. Allows large vessels (30+ cm). Most versatile early method.
- **Slab building**: Roll clay to uniform thickness (6-10 mm) with rolling pin. Cut and join slabs with slip (clay-water paste). Good for rectangular forms.
- **Mold pressing**: Press clay into/over existing form (basket, carved wood, previously fired pot). Ensures consistency.

**Drying**:
- Air dry slowly for 3-7 days (longer for thick walls). Cover loosely to prevent uneven drying. Rapid drying causes cracking. Bone-dry clay is lighter in color and cool to touch.

**Simple updraft kiln construction**:
- **Scove kiln (simplest firing structure)**: Stack dried pots upside-down in pyramid, surround with fuel (wood, dung), cover with sherds/broken pottery and earth. Light from bottom. Reaches ~600-800°C. Unreliable but requires zero construction.
- **Pit kiln**: Dig pit 30-60 cm deep. Line with stones. Load pots, cover with fuel and earth. More insulation = higher temperatures.
- **Simple updraft kiln (first purpose-built kiln)**:
  - **Construction**: Build box from mud bricks or packed earth, ~60 cm cube, with firebox opening at bottom and chimney opening at top. Place grate (clay bars or iron rods later) between firebox and ware chamber.
  - **Firing cycle**:
    1. **Water smoking (100-200°C)**: Gentle heat for 1-2 hours. Steam drives out. Too fast = explosion. Ware turns from dark to lighter.
    2. **Oxidation (350-600°C)**: Increase heat steadily. Organic matter burns out (smoke, smell). Clay minerals begin to transform.
    3. **Sintering (600-900°C)**: Maximum heat. Clay particles fuse (vitrification begins ~800°C for earthenware). Hold 30-60 minutes.
    4. **Cooling**: Seal kiln, cool slowly over 4-12 hours. Rapid cooling = cracking.
  - **Temperature indicators**: Smoke color, color of pots (red-orange glow visible through spy hole ≈ 800°C), color of chimney gases, Seger cones (pyramids of known melting clay placed inside — they slump at rated temperature).
  - **Fuel consumption**: ~2-4 kg wood per kg of pottery.

**Early refractory clay linings**:
- Fire clay (kaolin-rich, high alumina, low iron) withstands 1200-1400°C+ when fired. Found near coal measures, in weathered granite, or as residual clay deposits.
- For furnace linings: mix fire clay with 30-50% grog (crushed fired fire clay), form into shape, dry slowly, fire. Produces linings that survive multiple smelting cycles.
