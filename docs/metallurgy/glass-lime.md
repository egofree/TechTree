### Water &amp; Wind Power

**Water wheel construction (undershot — simplest)**:
- **Site requirements**: Stream or river with reliable flow, ~0.5 m/s+ water velocity. Can be enhanced with dam and sluice to concentrate flow.
- **Wheel construction**: Wooden paddles (oak, 5-10 cm thick) mounted on wooden axle (30-50 cm diameter, 2-4 m long). Wheel diameter 2-4 m, width 0.5-1.5 m. Paddles dip into flowing water, which pushes them.
- **Power output**: 1-5 HP depending on water flow and wheel diameter. Sufficient for grain grinding, bellows operation, wood sawing.
- **Bearing lubrication**: Tallow or animal fat on wooden bearing surfaces. Water lubrication works for some designs.

**Overshot wheel (higher efficiency)**:
- Requires head (vertical drop) of 2-5+ meters. Water flows over top of wheel, filling buckets. Gravity drives wheel. 2-3x more efficient than undershot.
- Construction similar but with buckets instead of flat paddles. Needs sluice/flume to deliver water to top.

**Power Transmission**:
- **Flat belts** (leather, later canvas): Carry rotational power over distance. Leather belts: 5-15 cm wide, 3-6 mm thick, spliced with rawhide lacing. Tension by sliding one pulley on its mount.
- **Line shafts**: Horizontal iron shaft (2-5 cm diameter) running length of workshop, supported by pillow block bearings (iron or babbitt metal in wooden blocks) every 1.5-2 m. Multiple pulleys on shaft drive different machines.
- **Gear trains**: Wooden peg gears (iron pins in wooden disks) for speed/torque conversion. Ratios typically 3:1 to 6:1. Iron gears arrive with the Machine Tools stage machine tools.
- **Counter shafts**: Enable individual machines to be engaged/disengaged via belt shifter (fork that moves belt between tight and loose pulleys).

### Basic Chemistry Precursors

**Glass production**:
- **Raw materials**: Silica sand (SiO₂, >95% purity, washed and sieved to 60-100 mesh) + flux (wood ash potash K₂CO₃, or natron Na₂CO₃) + stabilizer (limestone CaCO₃ or dolomite). Typical mix: 70% sand, 20% flux, 10% stabilizer.
- **Potash production**: Leach wood ashes with water (ash:water = 1:3 by volume). Boil concentrated solution. Crystallized potassium carbonate = potash. ~5 kg potash per 100 kg dry hardwood ash.
- **Melting**: Heat mixture in clay crucible to 1100-1400°C (requires good furnace + forced air). Melt takes 8-24 hours for full homogenization. Stir with iron rod to remove bubbles and mix.
- **Working**: Gather molten glass on iron blowpipe (1-1.5 m, 1-2 cm diameter). Blow, shape with wet wooden tools, anneal in cooling oven (500-600°C initially, cool slowly over 4-12 hours).
- **Early products**: Beads, simple vessels, flat window glass (crown method: spin blown sphere until it flattens into a disk), glass beads for trade.

**Lime production**:
- **Process**: Heat limestone (CaCO₃) in kiln to 900-1000°C for 4-8 hours. CaCO₃ → CaO (quicklime) + CO₂. Kiln construction similar to pottery kiln but larger (1-2 m tall).
- **Slaking**: Add water to quicklime — vigorous exothermic reaction (CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂, temperature reaches ~150°C). Produces slaked lime (hydrated lime), a soft white powder.
- **Uses**: Mortar (lime + sand + water, 1:3 ratio), whitewash (lime + water), plaster, soil amendment (reduces acidity), hide processing (dehairing), sewage treatment.

**Potash/Lye from wood ashes**:
- See glass production potash section. Concentrated lye (KOH) produced by running water through hardwood ash bed, then boiling to desired concentration. Test strength: float an egg — if it bobs with dime-sized area exposed, concentration is ~10% (correct for soap making).

**Early soap making**:
- **Cold process**: Heat animal fat (tallow or lard) to 40-50°C. Slowly add lye solution (KOH or NaOH) with stirring. Ratio: ~1 kg fat to 130-150 g KOH (or 100-120 g NaOH). Stir to trace (mixture thickens, drips leave visible marks). Pour into mold, cure 4-6 weeks. Result: soft soap (KOH) or hard soap (NaOH).
