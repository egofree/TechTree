### Overview

South American camelids — alpacas (*Vicugna pacos*) and llamas (*Lama glama*) — are dual-purpose livestock providing luxury fiber (alpacas) and pack transport plus guard duties (llamas). Domesticated in the Andes over 6000 years ago, they are exceptionally well-adapted to marginal land, require minimal infrastructure compared to other livestock, and have few health problems. Alpacas produce 2-5 kg of fine fleece every 12-18 months in a range of 22 natural colors. Llamas carry 25-30% of their body weight as pack animals and a single llama can serve as an effective guardian for a flock of 200+ sheep against canine and coyote predators.

### Breeds

#### Alpaca Breeds

- **[Huacaya](../glossary/huacaya.html)** (90% of alpacas): Fluffy, crimped fleece that grows perpendicular to the skin. Crimp (wave pattern) gives the fiber natural elasticity and loft, making it ideal for knitting and felting. Fiber diameter 20-30 microns. 22 recognized natural colors from white through fawn, brown, grey, and black. Hardier and easier to manage than Suri.
- **[Suri](../glossary/suri-alpaca.html)** (10% of alpacas): Long, lustrous fleece that hangs in pencil-like locks (dreadlocks), growing parallel to the body. No crimp — the fiber is straight with a silky handle. Fiber diameter 18-28 microns (often finer than Huacaya). More delicate constitution — requires more shelter and attention. Fiber is prized for weaving and produces fabric with exceptional drape.

#### Llama Types

- **Classic (Ccara) llama**: Light fleece coverage, strong pack conformation. The working type — bred for packing and guarding. 130-180 kg. Legs are well-covered with minimal fiber, reducing maintenance.
- **Woolly (Curaca) llama**: Heavy fleece coverage over entire body including legs. 150-200 kg. Dual-purpose: pack and fiber. Fiber is coarser than alpaca but usable for rugs, ropes, and outer garments. More grooming required.
- **Guard llama**: Not a separate breed, but a behavioral selection. A single adult llama (especially a gelding) placed with a sheep flock will bond with the flock and aggressively defend it against coyotes, stray dogs, and foxes. Effectiveness: one llama can reduce sheep predation losses by 75-100% in many operations.

### Reproduction and Cria Care

**Breeding**:
- Females (hembras) reach breeding age at 12-18 months (60% of mature body weight). Males (machos) are fertile at 18-24 months but best breeding starts at 2-3 years.
- Induced ovulators — females do not have a heat cycle. Ovulation is triggered by the act of breeding (physical stimulation). This means breeding can be planned at any time of year.
- Gestation: 11.5 months (350 days, range 335-365). Single cria per pregnancy; twins are extremely rare.

**Birthing (parturition)**:
- Crias are born during daylight hours (typically mid-morning) — an evolutionary adaptation to cold Andean nights. Birth weight: 8-15 kg (alpacas 6-10 kg, llamas 10-15 kg).
- Normal delivery is rapid: 15-30 minutes from first appearance of feet to delivery. Dystocia (difficult birth) is uncommon but requires veterinary assistance if the cria is not delivered within 1 hour of active labor.
- Cria should stand within 30-60 minutes and nurse within 2 hours. Passive immunity comes entirely from colostrum (first milk) — crias are born without antibodies. If the cria does not nurse within 4 hours, milk the dam and tube-feed or bottle-feed the colostrum.

**Cria care in cold weather**:
- In cold climates (below 5°C at birth), the wet cria loses heat rapidly. Towel-dry immediately. A cria coat (fleece jacket sized for newborns) prevents hypothermia in the first 24-48 hours until the cria is dry, standing, and nursing well.
- Monitor for failure of passive transfer: if cria appears weak, lethargic, or does not nurse within 4 hours, provide plasma or colostrum supplement by stomach tube. Failure of passive transfer leads to susceptibility to infections in the first months.
- Wean at 4-6 months, when cria weighs 25-30 kg (alpacas) or 40-60 kg (llamas).

### Shearing and Fiber Production

**Shearing alpacas**:
- Frequency: every 12-18 months. Fiber grows 8-12 cm per year. If left unshorn beyond 18 months, fleece mats, becomes difficult to process, and causes heat stress in warm weather.
- Method: restrain the alpaca on a shearing table or on the ground (stretched out with ropes at four legs — the "CUYA" method). Use electric shears or hand shears. A skilled shearer completes one alpaca in 10-15 minutes with electric shears, 30-45 minutes with hand shears.
- Yield: 2-5 kg fleece per adult alpaca per shearing. Huacaya yields slightly more than Suri by weight.
- Fiber diameter: 18-30 microns depending on genetics and age. Finest fiber from the blanket (back and sides). Neck and leg fiber is coarser and separated at shearing.

**Fiber grading**:
| Grade | Micron Range | Quality | Use |
|-------|-------------|---------|-----|
| Royal baby | < 20 μm | Ultra-fine | Next-to-skin knitwear |
| Baby | 20-23 μm | Fine | Luxury garments, scarves |
| Super fine | 23-26 μm | Medium-fine | Sweaters, blankets |
| Medium | 26-29 μm | Medium | Socks, mittens, hats |
| Strong | 29-32 μm | Coarse | Rugs, rope, felting |
| Adult | > 32 μm | Very coarse | Carpet, insulation |

**Shearing llamas**:
- Woolly llamas are shorn every 12-24 months. Classic (Ccara) llamas may not need shearing — they naturally shed their lighter coat.
- Yield: 1-3 kg per shearing. Fiber is coarser (25-40 microns) but still usable for outer garments, rugs, and rope.

### Fiber Processing

**Skirting**:
- After shearing, spread the fleece on a table and remove contaminants: vegetation (hay, burrs), second cuts (short fibers from the shearer going over the same area twice), and stained or matted sections. Separate the prime blanket from neck and leg fiber.

**Carding**:
- Align fibers for spinning. Hand carders (flat paddles with fine wire teeth) process small batches: load a handful of fiber on one carder, brush with the other in a rolling motion until fibers are aligned into a rolag (rolled cylinder of aligned fiber). Drum carder (hand-cranked cylinder with carding cloth) processes larger quantities faster — produces a batt (flat sheet of aligned fiber) that is divided into roving (long strips) for spinning.

**Spinning**:
- Spinning wheel or drop spindle converts roving into yarn. Alpaca fiber has no lanolin (unlike sheep wool) — it can be spun directly without washing before spinning, though washing after spinning sets the twist. Alpaca yarn is softer, lighter, and warmer than sheep wool of equivalent diameter (hollow core fibers provide better insulation). Lacks sheep wool's crimp and elasticity — 100% alpaca yarn has less stretch. Often blended with 20-30% sheep wool for elasticity in garments.

**Felting**:
- Alpaca fiber felts readily (fibers interlock when agitated in hot soapy water). Used for hats, boots, saddle pads, and insulation. Felting is simpler than spinning — requires no tools beyond hot water, soap, and a flat surface for rolling.

### Guard Llama Behavior

A single llama bonded to a sheep flock is one of the most effective and low-cost predator defenses available.

**Selection for guard behavior**:
- Not all llamas make good guards. Select a gelded male (castrated at 6-12 months to prevent breeding behavior with sheep) or a female. Intact males may try to breed sheep, causing injury. Age: 18-24 months at introduction.
- Raise the llama from youth with sheep if possible, or introduce the llama to the flock in a small pen for 1-2 weeks to bond before turning them out together.

**Guarding behavior**:
- Llamas are naturally curious and alert. When a predator approaches, the llama moves toward the threat, positions itself between the flock and the predator, and gives an alarm call (a loud, high-pitched whinny). If the predator persists, the llama charges, kicks, and stomps. Most coyotes and dogs flee immediately.
- Effectiveness studies show 75-100% reduction in predation losses when a guard llama is present. Particularly effective against coyotes, stray dogs, and foxes. Less effective against large predators (bears, mountain lions) — the llama may be injured or killed.
- One llama per 200-250 sheep is sufficient. Multiple llamas in the same flock may bond with each other rather than the sheep, reducing guarding effectiveness.

**Advantages over guard dogs**:
- No training required (instinctive behavior)
- No feeding costs (llama grazes with the flock)
- Long working life (15-20 years vs 5-8 for guard dogs)
- No risk to lambs (guard dogs occasionally injure lambs during play)

### Packing with Llamas

Llamas are the premier pack animal for mountainous and rough terrain where horses and mules cannot safely travel.

**Packing basics**:
- Load capacity: 25-30% of body weight. A 170 kg llama carries 40-50 kg comfortably.
- Distance: 15-20 km/day on trails. Slower than horses but more sure-footed on steep, rocky ground.
- Panniers (pack bags): two equal-weight bags hung on either side of a packsaddle. Weight must be balanced within 1 kg side-to-side. Soft-sided panniers are preferred — rigid panniers shift and cause saddle sores.
- Packsaddle: wooden or aluminum frame with padding, secured with a breast collar and rump strap. Must be fitted properly to prevent chafing.

**Advantages for trail transport**:
- Low environmental impact: soft-padded feet (no hooves) minimize trail erosion. Browsers rather than grazers — less impact on campsite vegetation.
- Calm temperament: llamas do not panic easily and can be led on a string by a walking person.
- Minimal feed: llamas browse on available vegetation along the trail. Carry supplemental feed (grain) for multi-day trips above treeline.

### Housing and Fencing

**Minimal infrastructure requirements** — one of the great advantages of camelids.

**Fencing**:
- Standard sheep fencing suffices: woven wire or high-tensile wire at 1.0-1.2 m height. Camelids rarely challenge fences and do not test fencing the way goats do. Barbed wire is unnecessary and discouraged (can snag fleece and cause injury).
- Interior fencing: lightweight, movable electric fencing works for temporary paddock divisions.

**Shelter**:
- Three-sided shelter (open to the east or south) provides adequate protection in most climates. Camelids tolerate cold well (originated in the Andes at 4000+ m elevation) but need shelter from prolonged wet conditions — their fleece absorbs water and can lead to hypothermia if soaked for days.
- Alpacas: 2-4 m² per animal in sheltered area.
- Llamas: 3-5 m² per animal.

**Composting manure**:
- Camelids use communal dung piles — they defecate in concentrated areas rather than scattering manure across the pasture. This makes manure collection exceptionally easy (scoop from the pile) and keeps the pasture cleaner than with cattle or horses.
- Manure production: 5-8 kg/day per adult alpaca, 8-12 kg/day per adult llama. Rich in nitrogen and potassium. Can be composted or applied directly to fields (lower nitrogen content than poultry manure, so less risk of burning plants).

### Health and Disease Resistance

Camelids are notably disease-resistant compared to other livestock species, which is one of their strongest advantages for a bootstrapping operation.

**Common health concerns**:
- **Parasites**: Susceptible to the same gastrointestinal nematodes as sheep (barber pole worm *Haemonchus contortus*, brown stomach worm *Ostertagia*). Monitor with fecal egg counts. Treat with standard livestock dewormers (ivermectin, fenbendazole) at camelid-appropriate dosages.
- **Heat stress**: In hot, humid climates (above 30°C with high humidity), alpacas (especially dark-colored and heavily fleeced) are at risk. Shear before summer. Provide shade and ventilation. Sheared alpacas tolerate heat well.
- **Toes and teeth**: Camelids have soft padded feet with two toes (no hooves). Toenails may need trimming every 2-3 months if not wearing naturally on rocky ground. Fighting teeth (sharp canine teeth in males, erupted at 2-3 years) must be cut or filed to prevent injuries to other animals and handlers.
- **Vitamin D deficiency**: In northern latitudes with short winter days, growing crias may develop rickets from insufficient vitamin D. Supplement with vitamin D injection (1000 IU/kg body weight) in autumn for growing animals under 2 years.

**Vaccination**:
- Standard clostridial vaccine (CD-T: protects against *Clostridium perfringens* types C and D, and tetanus). Annual booster for adults, initial series for crias at 2-3 months.
- Rabies vaccine where risk exists.

**Relative disease resistance**:
- Fewer health problems than cattle, sheep, or goats under similar management. Lower veterinary costs. Natural hardiness from 6000+ years of selection in harsh Andean conditions. This makes camelids particularly attractive for small-scale operations where veterinary access is limited.

### Feed Requirements

**Pasture and browse**:
- Alpacas: 1-2% of body weight in dry matter per day (1-2 kg/day for a 70 kg alpaca). Can maintain on poor-quality pasture that would not sustain cattle. Efficient three-chambered stomach (modified ruminant) extracts nutrients from coarse forage.
- Llamas: 1.5-2.5% of body weight in dry matter per day (2-5 kg/day for a 170 kg llama). Slightly higher requirement than alpacas due to larger size and activity level.

**Supplemental feed**:
- Grass hay in winter when pasture is unavailable. Avoid alfalfa (too rich — causes obesity and heat stress from excess protein metabolism). Good-quality grass hay is ideal.
- Grain supplement: rarely needed except for late-pregnant or nursing females, and working pack llamas. 0.25-0.5 kg/day of rolled oats or mixed grain.
- Minerals: free-choice loose mineral supplement formulated for camelids (or sheep minerals without added copper — camelids are sensitive to copper toxicity, similar to sheep). Provide salt separately.

**Water**: 5-10 L/day per alpaca, 10-20 L/day per llama. Clean, fresh water always available.

### Safety

**Handling camelids**:
- Camelids are generally docile and non-aggressive. They do not kick forward like horses (they kick backward and downward, less forceful). They may spit regurgitated stomach contents when stressed or threatened — unpleasant but not dangerous (rinsing with water resolves it).
- Halter training: use a properly fitted halter (must allow the nostrils to flare — camelids are obligate nasal breathers). A tight halter that restricts nostrils can suffocate the animal.
- Catching: use a small catch pen. Corner gently — never chase (causes stress and hyperthermia). Place an arm around the neck and hold the tail base to restrain.
- Llamas may charge if they feel cornered or are protecting a cria. A charging llama is intimidating but rarely makes contact — stand your ground and raise your arms. Most charges are bluffs.
