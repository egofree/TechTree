### Overview

Goats are the most adaptable and resilient domesticated livestock species. They thrive in arid, mountainous, and marginal environments where cattle and sheep struggle, browsing on woody vegetation that other ruminants refuse. Mature weight ranges from 30-80 kg (does 30-55 kg, bucks 50-80 kg), making them manageable without specialized handling equipment. Gestation is 150 days (range 145-155), producing litters of 1-3 kids with twins as the most common outcome. A doe's productive lifespan is 8-12 years.

Goats produce milk that is more digestible than cow milk (smaller fat globules, different protein structure — many people with cow milk intolerance can digest goat milk), meat (chevon) with a dressed yield of 45-50%, and specialty fibers (cashmere, mohair) that are among the most valuable natural textile fibers. Their browsing behavior makes them excellent brush clearers — 8-12 goats per hectare on browse can suppress invasive woody plants and reclaim overgrown land for cultivation.

The primary challenge with goats is containment. They are agile climbers, accomplished escape artists, and curious explorers. Fencing that would hold cattle or sheep is often insufficient for goats. This trait, combined with their tolerance for marginal conditions, makes goats both indispensable and exasperating.

### Breed Types and Selection

**Dairy breeds**:
- **Saanen**: White or cream-colored, the highest-volume dairy goat breed. Does 60-70 kg. Produces 3-5 L milk/day at peak lactation over a 180-300 day lactation period. Milk averages 3.2-3.5% butterfat. Calm temperament, large frame. The "Holstein of goats" — high volume, moderate components. Widely available worldwide.
- **Alpine**: Various color patterns (cou blanc, cou clair, sundgau, chamoisée). Does 55-65 kg. Produces 2.5-4.0 L/day at 3.3-3.6% butterfat. Hardy and adaptable, good for cold and variable climates. Active and curious personality.
- **Nubian**: Long floppy ears, Roman nose, any color pattern. Does 60-80 kg (one of the larger dairy breeds). Lower volume (2-3 L/day) but the highest butterfat of any dairy goat breed at 4.5-5.0%. The "Jersey of goats" — milk is ideal for cheese and butter production. More heat-tolerant than Swiss breeds. Vocal — Nubians are notably loud and communicative.
- **LaMancha**: Very short ears ("gopher ears" or "elf ears" — a distinctive genetic trait). Does 55-65 kg. Produces 2.5-3.5 L/day at 3.8-4.2% butterfat. Calm, sweet temperament. Excellent dairy character in a medium frame.

**Meat breeds**:
- **Boer**: White body with red/brown head, heavy-muscled. Bucks 90-130 kg, does 80-100 kg. The premier meat goat breed. Fast growth — kids gain 200-350 g/day under good management. Reach slaughter weight (30-40 kg) at 3-4 months. High dressing percentage (50-55%). Excellent maternal traits and high fertility (kidding percentage 180-200%). Originated in South Africa, now found globally.
- **Kiko**: New Zealand-developed meat breed selected for hardiness and minimal intervention. Good growth on browse with minimal supplementation. Excellent parasite resistance. Less muscular than Boer but more resilient under harsh conditions.

**Fiber breeds**:
- **Angora**: Produces mohair — a long, lustrous, curly fiber (not wool). Fiber diameter 23-38 microns, staple length 15-25 cm, growing approximately 2.5 cm per month. Shear twice per year. Yield: 4-6 kg mohair per shearing for mature animals. Mohair takes dye beautifully, has a silk-like sheen, and is used for upholstery, knitting yarns, and woven fabrics. Angora goats are smaller (does 35-55 kg) and more delicate than other goat breeds — they require better nutrition and shelter.
- **Cashmere-producing goats**: Cashmere is the fine undercoat (down) shed by certain goat breeds in spring. Fiber diameter must be under 18.5 microns to qualify as cashmere (most is 14-16 microns). Yield per animal is low: 100-300 g of dehaired cashmere per year (the raw fleece contains both coarse guard hairs and fine down — the two must be separated by dehairing). China produces 70%+ of the world's cashmere. Any goat that produces sufficient down can be considered "cashmere" — it is a fiber type, not a distinct breed. Select and breed for higher down yield and finer diameter.

**Selection for bootstrapping**:
- Dairy focus: Saanen or Alpine for volume; Nubian for high-component milk
- Meat focus: Boer for maximum growth rate; Kiko for hardiness on browse
- Dual-purpose: many traditional and landrace breeds serve both functions adequately
- Hardiness and parasite resistance are paramount in low-input systems

### Browsing Behavior and Pasture Management

Goats are natural browsers, not grazers. While sheep and cattle graze close to the ground on grasses, goats prefer to eat at head height or above — woody browse (shrubs, saplings, brambles, tree leaves), forbs (broadleaf weeds), and the tips of woody plants. Their upper lip is prehensile and their narrow mouth allows selective feeding on individual leaves and twigs.

This browsing preference makes goats uniquely valuable for:
- **Brush clearing**: Goats consume invasive species (kudzu, multiflora rose, blackberry brambles, poison ivy/oak — they are less susceptible to the urushiol toxin than humans). Stock at 8-12 goats per hectare on dense browse. They strip bark from saplings, defoliate shrubs, and suppress regrowth through repeated grazing.
- **Forest understory management**: Goats clear fire breaks, reduce ladder fuels (understory vegetation that carries ground fire into tree canopy), and maintain access trails.
- **Mixed grazing with sheep and cattle**: Goats consume plants that other livestock avoid, increasing total pasture utilization without competing directly for the same forage. A typical mixed stocking ratio: 2-3 goats per 5-6 ewes per 1 cow-calf pair per hectare on diverse pasture.

**Grazing precautions**:
Goats will strip bark from valuable trees if given access. Protect orchard trees, timber plantations, and any desirable woody plants with tree guards (wire mesh cylinders 1-1.5 m tall) or temporary electric fencing. Goats will also climb on and damage vehicles, equipment, and structures if bored or underfed.

### Fencing Requirements

Containment is the single greatest management challenge with goats. Standard livestock fencing is often insufficient.

**Fence specifications**:
- **Height**: Minimum 1.2 m (4 feet) for adult goats; 1.5 m (5 feet) for breeds with climbing tendency or for containing bucks. Goats can jump over obstacles up to 1.2 m and will climb leaning fences.
- **Mesh size**: Woven wire mesh with openings under 15 cm (6 inches) — larger openings allow goats to push their heads through and become stuck (goats back up rather than move forward when panicked, and will strangle in fence mesh). "Horse fencing" with 5 × 10 cm or 10 × 10 cm mesh works well.
- **Electric fencing**: 4-5 strands of electrified wire or a single strand of electrified netting (1.0-1.2 m tall). Goats respect electric fence once trained (first exposure should be in a controlled setting where they encounter the fence and receive a shock — they learn quickly). Portable electric netting allows flexible rotational grazing on browse. Minimum 4,000-5,000 V on the wire — goats have thick hair and require a strong shock to deter them.
- **Woven wire with offset electric**: The most secure permanent goat fence. Woven wire fence (1.2 m) with one or two strands of electrified wire on offset brackets on the inside. The physical barrier prevents pushing through; the electric wire prevents climbing and leaning.

**Gate and corner construction**: Goats test every weakness. Gates must close flush with no gaps. Corner posts should be braced (H-braces) to withstand goats pushing and rubbing. A 10 cm gap at the bottom of a fence is an invitation for a goat to dig out or squeeze through.

### Kidding Management

Goats are generally prolific and capable of kidding without assistance. Typical litter: 1-3 kids, with twins most common. Triplets occur in 10-15% of births in improved breeds. Kids weigh 2.5-4.0 kg at birth.

**Pre-kidding preparation**:
- Separate pregnant does into kidding pens (1.5-2.0 m²) 1-2 weeks before expected kidding. Clean bedding.
- Have iodine solution (7%) ready for navel dipping.
- Have colostrum bank available — either frozen goat colostrum from previous kiddings, or be prepared to milk the doe immediately post-kidding.

**Kidding process**:
Stage 1 labor: restlessness, pawing, getting up and down, vocalizing. Duration 2-6 hours. Stage 2: water bag appears, followed by front feet (soles down) and nose in normal presentation. Delivery should progress within 30-60 minutes of active straining. Intervene if: only one leg appears (reach in and find the other), head appears without legs (pull forward and down), or no progress after 1 hour of hard pushing.

**Post-kidding care**:
- Dip navel in 7% iodine solution within 15 minutes of birth to prevent bacterial infection through the umbilical stump.
- Ensure each kid nurses colostrum within 2 hours — kids are born with minimal body fat and no passive immunity. Colostrum provides antibodies and concentrated energy.
- In cold weather, dry kids with towels and provide a heat lamp. Hypothermia kills kids faster than anything else — a newborn kid's temperature drops rapidly in cold, wet conditions.
- Strip-milk the doe to check mastitis (clots, blood) and verify milk letdown.

**Caudal epididymitis and reproductive health**: Monitor bucks for breeding soundness before the breeding season. A buck-to-doe ratio of 1:25-30 is standard for pasture breeding. In a hand-mating system, a buck can service 2-3 does per day during peak season.

### Milking Technique

Goat milking follows the same principles as cow milking but requires adaptation to the udder conformation — goat udders are smaller, and the teats are more laterally placed.

**Hand milking**: Use the same squeeze-and-press technique as for cows. Grasp the teat at the top (teat closure), squeeze progressively with fingers to express milk. A doe produces 2-5 L/day at peak, so milking is faster per animal than for cows. Milk at 12-hour intervals for maximum yield.

**Milk quality**: Goat milk has smaller fat globules (2-3 microns vs 3-4 microns for cow milk) and a different protein structure (αs2-casein vs αs1-casein dominant in cows). This makes it naturally homogenized (cream does not rise as readily) and more digestible for many people. Flavor should be sweet and clean — any "goaty" taste is the result of poor hygiene, improper cooling, or the buck's strong odor contaminating the milk (keep bucks separate from milking does).

**Milk handling**: Cool milk to 4°C within 30-60 minutes of milking. Strain through a fine mesh filter or cloth to remove hair and debris. Use clean, sanitized containers. Goat milk keeps 5-7 days at 4°C.

### CAE Prevention

**Caprine Arthritis Encephalitis (CAE)** is a lentiviral infection of goats, related to the ovine progressive pneumonia virus. It causes chronic arthritis in adults (swollen knees, progressive lameness) and encephalitis in kids (progressive weakness, paralysis, death). Prevalence in unmanaged herds can exceed 70-80%. The virus is transmitted primarily through colostrum and milk from infected does to their kids.

**Prevention protocol**:
- Heat-treat colostrum to 56°C for 60 minutes — this destroys the virus while preserving immunoglobulins. Use a water bath and stir frequently to avoid hot spots that damage antibodies. Do not exceed 57°C — antibodies begin to denature.
- Pasteurize all milk fed to kids (72°C for 15 seconds, or 63°C for 30 minutes). Do not feed raw milk from infected does to kids.
- Separate kids from infected does at birth (before nursing) and raise on heat-treated colostrum + pasteurized milk.
- Test the herd annually using ELISA or AGID blood tests. Cull or isolate positive animals.
- A CAE-negative herd is achievable in 3-5 years through test-and-cull plus kid separation protocols. The effort is worthwhile — CAE causes significant production losses and animal suffering.

### Disbudding Kids

Most dairy goats are naturally horned. Horns on dairy goats are dangerous — to handlers, to other goats, and to the goat itself (horns can get caught in fencing, feeders, and fencing). Disbudding destroys the horn bud before it attaches to the skull.

**Timing**: At 3-7 days of age, when the horn buds are palpable (small bumps on the skull) but not yet attached to the underlying bone. After 2-3 weeks, the buds become more difficult to destroy completely, and scurs (partial horn regrowth) are more likely.

**Method (cautery iron)**:
1. Restrain the kid in a disbudding box or have an assistant hold it firmly.
2. Clip hair around the horn buds to expose the skin.
3. Heat a disbudding iron (a hollow-tipped, electrically heated tool) to a dull red glow.
4. Place the iron over the horn bud and apply firm, steady pressure for 8-15 seconds, rocking slightly in a circular motion. The goal is to burn a copper-colored ring through the skin and into the bone beneath the bud, destroying the horn-producing tissue.
5. A "figure-eight" motion over the bud ensures complete destruction.
6. Repeat on the other side.
7. Apply a topical antiseptic to the burns.

**Pain management**: Disbudding is painful. Provide a local anesthetic (lidocaine nerve block at the cornual nerve, 1-2 mL per side) if available. Administer a non-steroidal anti-inflammatory (meloxicam or ketoprofen) for post-procedure pain relief. Even without pharmaceutical options, perform disbudding quickly and cleanly — prolonged, incomplete attempts cause more suffering than a single thorough procedure.

### Pack Goats and Utility

Goats can be trained as pack animals for rough terrain. A mature, well-conditioned pack goat (typically a large wether — castrated male — of a dairy or meat breed) carries 15-25 kg over mountainous trails where horses and mules cannot go. They are sure-footed, navigate obstacles easily, and browse along the trail (reducing the need to carry feed). Training follows the same principles as ox training: halter break young, accustom to loads gradually, use positive reinforcement.

Goats also serve as companion animals and herd leaders for sheep — a few goats in a sheep flock provide leadership during herding and can alert the flock to predators. Some shepherds use a single large wether goat as a "judas goat" to lead the flock into pens and handling facilities.

### Manure and Soil Building

Goat manure is similar in composition to sheep manure: 1.5-2.5 kg/day, nitrogen-rich (0.6-0.7% N fresh weight). Goat pellets are small, dry, and easy to handle — they can be applied directly to garden beds without composting, though composting is still recommended to kill pathogens and weed seeds. The nitrogen-phosphorus-potassium (N-P-K) ratio of goat manure is approximately 0.7-0.4-0.5, making it a balanced fertilizer for most crops.

When goats are used for brush clearing, they simultaneously clear land and fertilize it. The manure they deposit while browsing adds organic matter and nutrients to soil that may be depleted from years of woody plant dominance.

### Safety

**Buck handling**: Bucks are large, strong, and can be aggressive during breeding season. They produce a powerful musky odor from scent glands behind the horns — this smell transfers to anything they contact (including the milk if bucks are housed near milking does). Handle bucks with respect; never corner one. Use a lead and halter for control. Castrated males (wethers) are docile and make excellent pack animals or companions.

**Butting**: Goats butt each other as play and dominance behavior. A goat butting a human can cause bruising or knock a person down, especially children. Discourage butting behavior in bottle-raised kids — cute play from a 5 kg kid is dangerous from a 60 kg adult. Never play "head-butting" games with young goats.

**Zoonotic diseases**: Q fever (from birthing fluids), leptospirosis (from urine), and orf (contagious ecthyma, painful hand sores). Caseous lymphadenitis (CL) causes abscesses in goats that can transmit to humans through open wounds. Wear gloves when handling abscesses or birthing materials.

**Escape prevention**: Goats on the loose will destroy gardens, orchards, and ornamental plantings in hours. Invest in adequate fencing before acquiring goats — this is the single most common regret of new goat keepers. A bored goat is a destructive goat; provide adequate space, browse, and companionship (goats are social and should never be kept singly).
