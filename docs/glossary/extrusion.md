# Extrusion

Extrusion is a forming process that forces a softened material through a shaped die to produce continuous profiles of constant cross-section. In electrode manufacturing, the hot pitch-coke mix (green paste, 120–140°C) is loaded into a heated hydraulic press chamber. A ram forces the paste through a die of the desired diameter—300–700 mm for large electrodes, 75–250 mm for smaller sizes. The extrusion process preferentially aligns coke particles along the electrode axis, giving extruded electrodes higher thermal conductivity and lower electrical resistivity along their length than across the diameter (anisotropic properties).

In ceramics, extrusion produces tubes, rods, and honeycomb structures by forcing a plastic clay body through a die. The clay must have the right moisture content and plasticity to flow without cracking. In aluminum production, hot extrusion of billets at 400–500°C creates structural shapes (angles, channels, tubes) using steel dies. Extrusion pressures range from 400–1,000 MPa depending on alloy and profile complexity.

The key advantages of extrusion over other forming methods include high production rates for uniform cross-sections, good surface finish, and the ability to produce complex hollow profiles with mandrel-equipped dies. Tooling costs for dies are moderate compared to casting molds, making extrusion economical for medium-to-high volume production.
