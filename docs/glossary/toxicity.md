# Toxicity

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, plants

Lethal at 50-100 ppm. Immediately Dangerous to Life or Health (IDLH) at 50 ppm. Symptoms: headache, nausea, pulmonary edema at low exposure; death at higher concentrations. Detect with electronic PH₃ monitors (electrochemical sensors), colorimetric tubes, or paper impregnated with silver nitrate (turns black in PH₃). Zero tolerance for leaks.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Toxicity — the degree to which a substance can harm living organisms — is a critical safety concern throughout industrial chemistry. In [Ammonia](../chemistry/ammonia.md) production, toxic gases like PH₃ (phosphine, used as a dopant gas in semiconductor manufacturing) require rigorous containment and monitoring. In [Dopant & Etch Gases](../chemistry/dopant-etch-gases.md), substances such as arsine (AsH₃) and phosphine (PH₃) are among the most toxic industrial gases in use. In [Solvents](../chemistry/solvents.md), benzene (carcinogenic) and carbon tetrachloride (hepatotoxic) require substitution where possible. In [Medicinal Plants](../plants/medicinal-plants.md), understanding plant toxicity is essential for safe therapeutic use.

## Technical Details

Toxicity is quantified by LD₅₀ (lethal dose for 50% of test population), TLV-TWA (Threshold Limit Value — Time Weighted Average for 8-hour exposure), and IDLH (Immediately Dangerous to Life or Health). Phosphine (PH₃), used as an n-type dopant in semiconductor manufacturing, has a TLV-TWA of 0.3 ppm and an IDLH of 50 ppm — making it one of the most toxic gases routinely handled in industrial settings.

Detection methods range from simple chemical indicators (silver nitrate paper turns black in PH₃ presence) to electronic monitors with electrochemical sensors providing continuous readout and alarms. Gas cabinets for toxic dopant gases feature continuous monitoring, automatic valve shutdown on leak detection, and exhaust ventilation to abatement systems.

The hierarchy of controls for toxic substances: elimination, substitution, engineering controls (ventilation, containment), administrative controls (procedures, training), and personal protective equipment (respirators, gloves).
