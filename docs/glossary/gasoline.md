# Gasoline

Gasoline is the light liquid fuel fraction distilled from petroleum at 30–200°C, used primarily in spark-ignition internal combustion engines. Octane rating is the critical quality parameter—higher octane fuel resists detonation (knocking) at higher compression ratios, enabling more efficient engine designs. Early gasoline had octane ratings of 40–60, limiting engines to compression ratios around 5:1. Tetraethyl lead (Pb(C₂H₅)₄) additive raises octane rating and the lead deposits lubricate valve seats, but lead is toxic and should be used only when necessary for engine reliability.

Gasoline has a stoichiometric air-fuel ratio of 14.7:1 and an energy density of approximately 44 MJ/kg (34 MJ/L). Its volatility must be matched to climate: too volatile and vapor lock occurs in fuel lines, too low and cold starting becomes difficult. In aviation applications, volatility and freezing point are additional constraints—aviation gasoline (avgas) has tighter specifications than automotive gasoline.

In a bootstrap context, gasoline production requires petroleum distillation capability. Alternatives include ethanol (octane ~100, but 60% energy density of gasoline) and producer gas (lower energy but producible from solid fuels). The availability of gasoline or a suitable alternative determines whether spark-ignition engines can be deployed for transport, power generation, and agricultural equipment.
