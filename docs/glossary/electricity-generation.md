# Electricity Generation

> **Type**: equipment | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: energy, metals

In modern integrated steelworks, BFG fuels gas turbines or steam turbines for on-site power generation. A large blast furnace (producing 10,000 tonnes/day pig iron) generates enough BFG to produce 50-100 MW of electricity — covering much of the steelworks' internal power demand.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Electricity generation converts mechanical, thermal, or chemical energy into electrical power. In the tech tree, generation methods progress from electrochemical (batteries) through electromechanical (generators driven by steam turbines, water turbines, or wind turbines) to thermoelectric and photovoltaic. Blast furnace gas (BFG) cogeneration illustrates how industrial byproducts can serve as fuel for electricity generation, closing energy loops in integrated steelworks.

## Technical Details

Blast furnace gas (BFG) cogeneration is a prime example of waste energy recovery for electricity generation. BFG has low calorific value (3-4 MJ/m³, versus 35-38 MJ/m³ for natural gas) but is produced in enormous volumes — roughly 1500-2000 m³ per tonne of pig iron. A large blast furnace producing 10,000 tonnes/day generates BFG sufficient for 50-100 MW of electricity generation via gas turbines or steam turbines, covering much of the steelworks' internal demand for electric arc furnaces, rolling mills, and compressed air.

Steam turbine electricity generation operates at 30-40% thermal efficiency with superheat and condenser vacuum. Steam turbines scale from a few MW to 1000+ MW. The steam cycle — boiler (water to steam), turbine (steam to mechanical work), condenser (steam to water), feed pump (water to boiler) — is the dominant electricity generation method in the tech tree.

Water turbine generation via Francis turbines achieves 80-94% efficiency at design flow and is the most efficient electricity generation method available. Hydroelectric plants convert the potential energy of falling water directly to electrical power via generator coupled to the turbine shaft. No fuel, no combustion, no thermal losses — only mechanical and electrical conversion losses.

Electric furnaces are both consumers and indirect enablers of electricity generation: they require 30-50 MW per furnace for steel recycling, but they also produce the specialty steels needed for turbine blades, generator laminations, and transformer cores.
