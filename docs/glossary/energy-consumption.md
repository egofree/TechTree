# Energy Consumption

Energy consumption quantifies the thermal and electrical energy required by industrial processes, typically expressed per unit of output (kWh/tonne or GJ/tonne). In electric arc furnace steelmaking, modern UHP (Ultra High Power) furnaces with oxygen lancing and scrap preheating achieve 300–350 kWh/tonne, while older furnaces require 550–700 kWh/tonne. Auxiliary fuel from natural gas burners (3–5 MW) supplements arc heating during cold spots, bringing total energy input to approximately 1.5–2.5 GJ per tonne of steel.

Energy consumption is a primary cost driver and constraint in bootstrap industry. Electrolytic processes are particularly energy-intensive: aluminum smelting requires 13,000–15,000 kWh per tonne, and chlor-alkali electrolysis for chlorine and caustic soda demands roughly 2,500 kWh per tonne of chlorine. Reducing energy consumption through heat recovery, process optimization, and equipment design is essential for economic viability.

Understanding the energy budget of each process informs decisions about scale, location (proximity to power sources), and sequencing. Processes with high energy demands may need to be scheduled during periods of surplus power generation or co-located with generation assets.
