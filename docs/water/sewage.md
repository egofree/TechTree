# Sewage Collection & Treatment

> **Dependencies**: None (root capability)
> **Enables**: None (leaf capability)

> **Timeline**: Years 10-35
> **Outputs**: treated_wastewater, sewage_disposal

Collection, conveyance, and treatment of wastewater and sewage. In any
settled community, the volume of wastewater generated (drinking,
cooking, washing, latrine flush) rapidly contaminates surface and
groundwater sources if not managed. Cholera epidemics in 19th-century
cities demonstrated that sewage management is not optional — it is a
prerequisite for urban civilization at any scale.

## The Sanitation Problem

A community of 1,000 people generates approximately 50-200 m³ of
wastewater per day (50-200 liters per person per day). This wastewater
contains:

- **Pathogens**: Bacteria (cholera, typhoid, E. coli), viruses
  (hepatitis, rotavirus), parasites (giardia, cryptosporidium,
  helminths)
- **Organic matter**: 200-400 mg/L biochemical oxygen demand (BOD)
- **Suspended solids**: 200-500 mg/L
- **Nutrients**: Nitrogen (20-50 mg/L) and phosphorus (5-15 mg/L)

Untreated discharge creates disease cycles: sewage contaminates water
supply → population sickens → more sewage from sick population.

## Sewage Collection Systems

### Simplified Sewerage (Small-Diameter Gravity)

Low-cost sewer networks suitable for dense settlements.

- **Pipe diameter**: 100 mm minimum (vs. 200+ mm conventional)
- **Gradient**: 0.5% minimum. Shallower trenches reduce excavation cost.
- **Materials**: Clay, concrete, or plastic pipes. Joint sealing with
  cement mortar or rubber rings.
- **Manholes**: At junctions, direction changes, and every 50-80 meters
  for inspection and blockage removal.
- **Ventilation**: Vent pipes at building connections and main junctions
  to prevent gas buildup.

### Combined vs. Separate Systems

- **Combined sewers**: One pipe network carries both sewage and
  stormwater. Simpler to build but overflows during heavy rain, dumping
  raw sewage into waterways.
- **Separate systems**: Independent sanitary and stormwater networks.
  Preferred for new construction — avoids overflow contamination.

### Septic Tanks and On-Site Systems

For settlements too small or dispersed for sewer networks.

- **Septic tank design**: 2-3 chamber concrete or brick tank. Volume:
  1,000-3,000 liters for a household (minimum 1 day retention).
- **Settling**: Solids settle to form sludge; lighter materials float as
  scum. Anaerobic digestion reduces sludge volume by 30-50%.
- **Effluent disposal**: Drain to soakaway pit or leach field (gravel-filled
  trench with perforated pipe). Soil provides additional filtration.
- **Maintenance**: Desludge every 2-5 years. Sludge can be composted
  (thermophilic composting kills pathogens in 2-4 weeks at 55°C+).
- **Limitation**: Not suitable for high water tables, impermeable soils,
  or very dense settlements (insufficient land for leach fields).

### Pit Latrines

The most basic sanitation facility.

- **Design**: Excavated pit 2-3 meters deep, 1-1.5 meter diameter.
  Covered with a slab (concrete, wood, or stone) with a drop hole.
- **Ventilated improved pit (VIP) latrine**: Add a vent pipe with fly
  screen. Reduces odor and flies significantly.
- **Lifetime**: 5-15 years depending on pit volume and number of users.
- **When full**: Cover with soil and dig a new pit. Relocate superstructure.
  Alternatively, empty pit with manual or mechanical pump.

## Primary Treatment

Physical removal of solids from raw sewage.

### Screening

- **Coarse screens**: 15-50 mm bar screens remove rags, sticks, debris.
  Manual raking or mechanical rakes.
- **Fine screens**: 3-6 mm mesh. Removes smaller debris. Requires
  regular backwashing or cleaning.

### Grit Removal

- **Aerated grit chambers**: Air injection creates a spiral flow. Grit
  (sand, gravel) settles; organic matter stays suspended.
- **Velocity-controlled channels**: Flow at 0.3 m/s allows grit to
  settle while organics remain in suspension.

### Primary Sedimentation

- **Design**: Rectangular or circular tanks. 1.5-3 hour residence time.
  Surface loading rate: 30-50 m³/m²/day.
- **Performance**: Removes 50-70% of suspended solids, 25-40% of BOD.
- **Sludge**: Primary sludge (2-6% solids) scraped to a sump and pumped
  to sludge treatment.
- **Scum**: Floating grease and oils skimmed from surface.

## Secondary Treatment

Biological treatment that removes dissolved and colloidal organic matter.

### Activated Sludge Process

The most common secondary treatment method.

- **Process**: Aeration tank where microorganisms consume organic matter.
  Air supplied by mechanical aerators or diffused air blowers.
- **Aeration time**: 4-8 hours. Mixed liquor suspended solids: 2,000-5,000
  mg/L.
- **Secondary clarifier**: Settle the biological floc. Return activated
  sludge to aeration tank; waste excess to maintain solids balance.
- **Performance**: Removes 85-95% of BOD, 85-95% of suspended solids.
- **Energy requirement**: Significant — aeration is energy-intensive.
  Roughly 0.5-1.5 kWh per kg BOD removed.

### Trickling Filters

Lower energy alternative using beds of stone or plastic media.

- **Design**: Circular or rectangular bed of coarse aggregate (50-100 mm
  stone) 1.5-3 meters deep. Sewage distributed over the surface by
  rotating arms or fixed nozzles.
- **Mechanism**: Biological film grows on stone surfaces and consumes
  organic matter as sewage trickles through.
- **Performance**: Removes 70-90% of BOD depending on loading rate.
- **Advantage**: Low energy — only pumping to the top of the filter
  required. No aeration blowers.
- **Limitation**: Less controllable than activated sludge. Fly and odor
  nuisance if overloaded.

### Waste Stabilization Ponds

The simplest secondary treatment — large shallow ponds relying on
natural biological processes.

- **Facultative ponds**: 1-2 meters deep. Aerobic surface layer,
  anaerobic bottom layer. 3-15 day residence time.
- **Maturation ponds**: 0.5-1 meter deep. Final polishing. UV
  disinfection from sunlight. 5-15 day residence time.
- **Area required**: 5-10 m² per person served. Large land requirement
  but zero energy and minimal maintenance.
- **Performance**: 80-99% pathogen removal. Nutrient removal moderate.
- **Best for**: Warm climates with available land. Ideal for early-stage
  civilization where simplicity and low cost matter.

## Sludge Treatment and Disposal

- **Thickening**: Gravity or mechanical thickening to 4-8% solids.
- **Anaerobic digestion**: Heated (35°C) enclosed tanks. 15-30 day
  retention. Produces biogas (60-70% methane) usable for heating and
  power.
- **Dewatering**: Drying beds (sand/gravel, 1-2 weeks in warm weather)
  or mechanical presses. Target: 20-40% solids.
- **Disposal**: Composting (mix with carbon source, thermophilic
  temperatures kill pathogens), land application as fertilizer (if heavy
  metals are controlled), or incineration.

## Effluent Reuse and Discharge

- **Irrigation**: Treated effluent for non-food crops. Nutrient value
  reduces fertilizer needs. WHO guidelines for irrigation water quality.
- **Industrial reuse**: Cooling water, process water after appropriate
  treatment level.
- **River discharge**: Must meet minimum standards — BOD <20-30 mg/L,
  suspended solids <30 mg/L, no detectable fecal coliforms per 100 mL.
