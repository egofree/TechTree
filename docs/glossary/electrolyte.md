# Electrolyte

An electrolyte is the ionic conducting solution that enables electrochemical reactions in batteries, electroplating cells, and metal refining processes. In copper refining, the standard electrolyte is CuSO₄·5H₂O (35–50 g/L Cu²⁺) combined with H₂SO₄ (150–220 g/L) dissolved in water. The sulfuric acid increases conductivity and prevents copper sulfate hydrolysis. Operating temperature is maintained at 50–65°C to reduce electrolyte resistance and improve ion migration. Continuous filtration removes suspended solids that would otherwise cause short circuits between anodes and cathodes.

In aluminum production (Hall-Héroult process), the electrolyte is molten cryolite (Na₃AlF₆) at roughly 960°C, which dissolves alumina (Al₂O₃) and allows aluminum ions to be reduced at the carbon cathode. The choice of electrolyte composition directly affects cell voltage, current efficiency, and product purity across all electrochemical processes.

Electrolyte management is a recurring concern in bootstrap industry: impurity buildup degrades product quality, temperature control affects energy consumption, and electrolyte loss through drag-out or evaporation must be replenished. In battery applications, the electrolyte determines the operating voltage window, internal resistance, and cycle life of the cell.
