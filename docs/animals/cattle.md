### Overview

Cattle are the single most productive domesticated species for a bootstrapping civilization. A mature cow converts pasture and roughage into high-value protein (meat and milk), provides draft power for plowing and transport, produces leather and tallow as industrial materials, and generates 30-40 kg of nitrogen-rich manure per day that sustains soil fertility. Mature weight ranges from 400-800 kg (cows at the lower end, bulls and oxen at the upper). Gestation is 283 days (range 279-290), producing a single calf per pregnancy (twins in roughly 1-2% of births).

No other livestock species matches cattle for combined output of food, fiber, power, and fertilizer. Their size makes them capital assets — each animal represents significant investment in feed, housing, and labor, but returns proportionally high value over a productive lifespan of 10-15 years.

### Breed Types and Selection

**Dairy breeds** (selected for milk production):
- **Holstein-Friesian**: The highest-volume dairy breed. Black and white patched, mature weight 550-700 kg. Produces 25-35 L milk/day at peak lactation, though butterfat is relatively low at 3.2-3.6%. Annual yield can exceed 8,000 L under good management. Large body size means higher feed requirements — roughly 3% of body weight in dry matter daily. Best suited where abundant pasture and forage are available.
- **Jersey**: Smaller frame (350-450 kg), fawn-colored. Lower volume (15-20 L/day) but significantly higher butterfat (4.5-5.5%) and protein content. Milk is ideal for butter and cheese production — each liter yields more dairy product than Holstein milk. Feed-efficient relative to output: a Jersey needs roughly 60% of a Holstein's feed while producing 70-80% of the dairy product value. Excellent for smaller operations with limited pasture.
- **Ayrshire**: Red and white, 450-550 kg. Intermediate production (18-22 L/day) at 3.8-4.2% butterfat. Hardy and adaptable to rough terrain and variable feed quality. Good foraging ability on marginal pasture.

**Beef breeds** (selected for meat yield and growth rate):
- **Hereford**: Red body with white face, belly, and switch. Mature weight 550-800 kg. Excellent feed conversion on pasture. Calm temperament — among the easiest cattle to handle. Dressed carcass yield 60-65%. Hardy in cold climates. The white face is a dominant genetic marker useful for crossbreeding programs.
- **Aberdeen Angus**: Black (occasionally red), polled (naturally hornless). Mature weight 500-700 kg. Known for marbling — intramuscular fat that produces high-quality beef. Moderate frame size, efficient on pasture. Polled genetics eliminate the need for dehorning — a significant safety and handling advantage.
- **Shorthorn**: Red, white, or roan. Dual-purpose heritage breed — solid milk (15-18 L/day) and good beef conformation. Valuable for small-scale operations needing both products from one herd.

**Dual-purpose breeds**:
- In a bootstrapping scenario, dual-purpose breeds are often the pragmatic choice. A single herd produces both milk for daily consumption and calves for beef. Milk production is moderate (8-12 L/day), but every calf not needed as a herd replacement becomes a beef animal. Traditional breeds like Shorthorn, Devon, and Dexter (small frame, 300-350 kg, ideal for smallholdings) fill this role well.

**Selection criteria for bootstrapping**:
- Hardiness over maximum production — breeds that thrive on rough pasture and resist disease
- Calving ease — minimize intervention, maximize calf survival
- Temperament — docile cattle are safer and easier to manage without modern handling equipment
- Dual-purpose utility — every animal should produce multiple outputs

### Dairy Management

**Milking procedure and hygiene**:
Milking must occur at consistent times, ideally 12 hours apart for maximum yield. Clean the udder with warm water and a clean cloth before milking. Strip the first two streams from each teat into a strip cup — this checks for mastitis (clots, discoloration, watery appearance) and removes bacteria-laden milk from the teat canal. Apply milking lubricant or dip teats in an antiseptic solution (iodine-based, 0.5-1% concentration) before and after milking to prevent mastitis-causing organisms from entering the teat canal.

Hand-milking technique: grasp the teat at the base with thumb and forefinger (teat closure), then squeeze progressively with the remaining fingers to force milk downward and out the teat orifice. Release grip to allow the teat cistern to refill. A skilled milker extracts 1-2 L/minute. Machine milking (when available) uses vacuum pulsation at 40-60 kPa to mimic the suckling action.

**Pasteurization**:
Raw milk carries pathogens (Brucella, Mycobacterium bovis, Listeria, E. coli O157:H7, Salmonella, Campylobacter). Two standard methods:
- **Low-Temperature Long-Time (LTLT)**: 63°C for 30 minutes. Gentle on flavor and cream line. Suitable for batch processing in a vat or pot with a thermometer. Stir continuously to ensure even heating.
- **High-Temperature Short-Time (HTST)**: 72°C for 15 seconds. Requires a flow-through heat exchanger for continuous processing. Industry standard. Better throughput but more complex equipment.

Both methods achieve a 5-log reduction in pathogen bacteria. After heating, cool rapidly to 4°C or below. Unpasteurized milk for cheese aging over 60 days at less than 4°C is traditionally considered safe because the combined acid, salt, and aging destroy most pathogens — but this requires careful control.

**Milk composition and yield**:
Holstein milk averages 3.2-3.6% butterfat, 3.0-3.3% protein, 4.8% lactose. Jersey milk averages 4.5-5.5% butterfat, 3.5-4.0% protein. A dairy cow peaks in production 4-8 weeks after calving, then gradually declines over a 305-day lactation. She is then dried off (milking stopped) for 60 days before the next calving to allow udder involution and fetal growth.

### Beef Production

A 500 kg cow yields a dressed carcass of 55-60% (275-300 kg). This carcass breaks down roughly into: 45-50% retail cuts (steaks, roasts, ground beef), 15-20% bone, 10-15% fat trim, and the remainder as waste or processing byproducts.

**Growth stages**:
- **Calf** (birth to weaning at 6-8 months): Nurse on cow's milk, begin grazing at 2-3 weeks. Gain 0.8-1.2 kg/day.
- **Stockers/yearlings** (weaning to finishing): Grazing on pasture. Gain 0.5-0.8 kg/day on good forage.
- **Finishing** (last 60-120 days before slaughter): Higher-energy ration (grain supplement or high-quality pasture) to add fat cover and marbling. Gain 1.0-1.5 kg/day.
- Slaughter at 18-24 months for grain-finished, 24-30 months for grass-finished.

### Breeding and Reproduction

**Calving management**:
Heifers reach breeding age at 14-18 months (target weight 60-65% of mature weight). Gestation 283 days. Calving interval should be 365 days — rebreed 60-80 days postpartum.

Signs of approaching calving: udder filling (2-4 weeks prior), relaxation of pelvic ligaments (24-48 hours, visible as a sunken appearance on either side of the tailhead), mucus discharge, restlessness and isolation from the herd. Stage 1 labor (dilation) lasts 2-6 hours. Stage 2 (delivery) should progress within 1-2 hours of water bag appearance — if no progress after 1 hour of active straining, intervention is needed. Normal presentation: front feet first, head resting on fetlocks. Malpresentations (breech, head back, leg back) require skilled correction or veterinary assistance.

Post-calving: ensure the calf nurses colostrum within 2-4 hours — colostrum contains immunoglobulins the calf needs for passive immunity (calves are born with essentially no immune system). Dip the navel in 7% iodine solution to prevent infection. Monitor the cow for retained placenta (should pass within 12 hours — if not, intervention needed to prevent metritis).

**Castration methods**:
- **Elastrator (rubber ring)**: Apply a tight rubber band around the scrotum above the testicles at 1-7 days of age. Blood supply is cut off; testicles atrophy and fall off in 2-4 weeks. Simple, bloodless, low pain when done very young. Must be done before the scrotum grows too large for the band. Risk: tetanus if the necrotic tissue becomes infected.
- **Burdizzo (crush)**: Clamp the Burdizzo emasculatome across each spermatic cord separately (crush for 10-15 seconds per side). Destroys the cord without cutting the scrotum. Bloodless method suitable for calves up to 4-5 months. Both cords must be fully crushed or the animal remains fertile — check by palpating for a distinct gap in the cord.
- **Surgical**: Cut the bottom of the scrotum and remove the testicles. Most reliable method, suitable for any age but requires a sharp knife and creates an open wound. Apply antiseptic and monitor for infection. More painful in older animals — use restraint.

### Ox Training for Draft Power

Oxen are castrated male cattle trained to pull loads. They are slower than horses but stronger pound-for-pound, require less expensive feed (thriving on roughage alone), and have calmer temperaments.

**Training progression**:
- **Year 1-2**: Halter break calves. Teach leading, standing tied, and basic handling. Accustom to voice commands ("gee" for right turn, "haw" for left turn, "whoa" for stop, "get up" for forward). Handle feet and legs for future hoof trimming.
- **Year 2**: Introduce a yoke (wooden beam across the neck, fitted to the individual animal). Begin with an empty drag (log or sled) on flat ground. Short sessions of 15-30 minutes. Pair with an experienced ox if possible — cattle learn by following a leader.
- **Year 3-4**: Gradually increase load weight and working duration. A mature ox team (two animals) can pull 1,500-2,500 kg on a wheeled cart on flat ground, or plow 0.5-1.0 hectares per day with a single-bottom plow. Working speed: 3-4 km/hour, 6-8 hours/day with rest breaks. Never work oxen in extreme heat without rest and water.

Yoke fitting: the bow yoke (U-shaped wooden bow passing under the neck, secured to the yoke beam with a wooden key) is the traditional design. The yoke beam should rest on the neck muscle (not the spine) and be wide enough to span both animals' shoulders with 10-15 cm clearance on each side. A poorly fitted yoke causes sores and reduces pulling efficiency.

See [Draft Power & Harnessing](draft-power.md) for full details on yoke construction, hitching systems, and load calculations.

### Pasture and Winter Feed Management

**Pasture rotation**:
Divide available pasture into 6-12 paddocks using temporary electric fencing or permanent fencing. Rotate cattle to a new paddock every 3-7 days, allowing grazed paddocks 21-35 days of recovery before re-grazing. This prevents overgrazing (which kills desirable grass species and allows weeds to establish), distributes manure evenly, and reduces parasite loads (parasite larvae die on ungrazed pasture after 6-12 months without a host).

Stocking rate: 0.5-1.0 hectares per animal on good grassland. Marginal land may require 2-3 hectares per animal.

**Hay production for winter**:
Cut pasture grasses and legumes at the early heading stage (maximum protein and digestibility). Cure in the field: sun-dry to 15-20% moisture content (typically 2-4 days depending on weather). Bale with a mechanical baler (rectangular or round bales) or, in a low-tech scenario, stack loose hay in a mow (hayloft) by hand.

Winter feed requirement: 1.2-1.8 tonnes of hay per animal per winter (assuming a 5-6 month feeding period in temperate climates). A 500 kg cow consuming 2.5-3.0% of body weight in dry matter eats roughly 12.5-15 kg dry matter per day. Over 180 days, that is 2,250-2,700 kg of hay per animal. Quality matters: good legume-grass hay provides 10-14% crude protein; poor hay may fall below 7%, requiring grain or protein supplementation.

**[Silage](../glossary/silage.md)** (fermented forage):
An alternative to hay that preserves more nutrients. Chop green forage (corn, grass, or legumes) into 1-3 cm lengths. Pack tightly into a silo, pit, or sealed plastic bag, excluding air. Fermentation produces lactic acid, dropping pH to 3.8-4.5, which preserves the feed for months. Requires airtight storage — exposure to air causes spoilage and mold. Higher moisture content than hay (60-70% vs 15-20%), so heavier to handle but more digestible.

### Hoof Care

Cattle hooves grow continuously and require trimming every 6-12 months, more frequently on soft or wet ground where natural wear is insufficient. Overgrown hooves cause lameness, altered gait, and reduced willingness to work or graze.

**Trimming procedure**: Restrain the animal in a crush or hoof-trimming chute. Lift each foot using a rope and pulley or hydraulic jack. Use hoof nippers (large, heavy-duty clippers) to cut excess growth from the toe and walls. Use a hoof knife to clean out the sole and trim the heel. The goal is a flat, level sole with the toe angle at approximately 45-50° (matching the pastern angle). Apply copper sulfate (5-10% solution) to the cleaned hooves to control foot rot.

### Manure Management

A single cow produces 30-40 kg of fresh manure per day (approximately 8-12 tonnes per year). Fresh cattle manure is roughly 85% water, 0.5-0.7% nitrogen (N), 0.3-0.5% phosphorus (P₂O₅), and 0.4-0.6% potassium (K₂O).

**Composting**: Stack manure with carbon-rich bedding (straw, sawdust) at a C:N ratio of roughly 25-30:1. Turn the pile every 2-4 weeks to aerate. Internal temperature reaches 55-65°C, killing pathogens and weed seeds. Compost is ready in 3-6 months when dark, crumbly, and earthy-smelling. Composted manure is a primary soil amendment for crop production — each tonne applied adds roughly 5-6 kg N, 2-3 kg P, and 5-6 kg K to the soil.

**Manure as fuel**: Dried cattle dung (dung cakes) burns steadily with moderate heat output. Calorific value roughly 12-15 MJ/kg (compared to 15-18 MJ/kg for wood). Traditional fuel in tree-scarce regions. Ash from burned dung is a phosphate-rich fertilizer.

### Common Diseases

- **[Brucellosis](../glossary/brucellosis.md)** (*Brucella abortus*): Causes abortion in cattle (typically at 5-7 months gestation) and undulant fever in humans (chronic flu-like illness transmitted through raw milk or contact with birthing fluids). Test with milk ring test or blood serum agglutination. No practical treatment — test and cull infected animals. Vaccination (Strain 19 or RB51) in calves provides protection.
- **[Anthrax](../glossary/anthrax.md)** (*Bacillus anthracis*): Acute fatal septicemia. Sudden death without prior symptoms in peracute cases. Carcass bloats rapidly, dark unclotted blood from orifices. Spores persist in soil for decades. Do not open a suspected anthrax carcass — exposure to air induces massive sporulation. Report immediately. Vaccinate annually in endemic areas with Sterne strain vaccine.
- **Mastitis**: Inflammation of the udder, usually bacterial (*Staphylococcus aureus*, *Streptococcus agalactiae*, *E. coli*). Detected by strip cup testing (clots, watery milk, discoloration) or California Mastitis Test (CMT — reagent reacts with somatic cells, producing gel). Treatment: intramammary antibiotic infusion. Prevention: teat dipping post-milking, clean milking environment, dry cow therapy (long-acting antibiotic infusion at drying off).
- **Foot-and-mouth disease** (FMD): Highly contagious viral disease. Fever, vesicles (blisters) on tongue, lips, feet, and teats. Drooling, lameness, drop in milk production. Spreads rapidly through aerosol, contact, and contaminated equipment. Mortality in adults is low (1-5%) but morbidity approaches 100%. Devastating to productivity. Quarantine and slaughter of infected herds is the standard control measure in countries attempting eradication.
- **Bovine respiratory disease** (shipping fever): Complex of viral and bacterial pathogens (*Mannheimia haemolytica*, *Pasteurella multocida*) triggered by stress (transport, weaning, weather changes). Fever (40-41°C), nasal discharge, coughing, labored breathing. Most common cause of mortality in feedlot cattle. Treatment: broad-spectrum antibiotics. Prevention: stress reduction, adequate nutrition, vaccination.

**Artificial insemination (AI) basics**:
AI allows the use of superior genetics without maintaining a bull (which is dangerous and expensive). Technique: thaw a frozen semen straw (0.5 mL) in 35-37°C water for 30-60 seconds. Load into an AI gun (a long, thin, sterile catheter). Deposit semen into the uterine body via rectal-vaginal technique — one hand in the rectum manipulates the cervix by touch while the AI gun is passed through the vagina and cervix. Timing is critical: inseminate 6-12 hours after first observed standing heat. Conception rate: 50-70% per service with good technique. Requires liquid nitrogen storage for frozen semen (-196°C), which makes AI an advanced technique — natural service is the baseline.

### Safety

**Bull handling**: Never enter a pen with a bull alone. Bulls are unpredictable and can kill instantly — a 800 kg bull charging at 35 km/h delivers massive blunt force. Use a bull staff (a rigid pole with a snap latch attached to the nose ring) to maintain control. Never turn your back on a bull. Signs of aggression: pawing the ground, head rubbing, broadside display (showing his profile to appear larger), vocalizing. Breeding bulls should have a dedicated, escape-proof pen with a heavy gate.

**Crush and restraint**: Cattle handling requires a crush (squeeze chute) — a strong metal or wooden frame that restrains the animal for veterinary procedures, hoof trimming, and AI. The crush should have a head catch and squeeze panels. A properly designed race (curved, solid-sided chute leading to the crush) uses cattle's natural circling behavior to move them forward calmly. Never use dogs on cattle — the stress response raises cortisol and reduces immune function.

**Zoonotic diseases**: Brucellosis, leptospirosis, Q fever, and cryptosporidiosis transmit from cattle to humans. Wear gloves when handling birthing materials or manure. Pasteurize milk. Wash hands after handling cattle.

**Kicking and crushing**: Cows kick forward and to the side (unlike horses, which kick backward). Stand to the side when working near the rear. Never stand behind a cow in a confined space. A 500 kg animal stepping on a foot causes serious injury — wear reinforced boots.

### Production Metrics

**Dairy production benchmarks**:
A well-managed dairy cow produces 6,000-10,000 L of milk per 305-day lactation, peaking at 25-35 L/day during weeks 4-8 post-calving. Milk composition averages 3.5-4.0% butterfat, 3.0-3.5% protein, and 4.8% lactose across breeds. Jersey cows produce 15-20 L/day at 4.5-5.5% butterfat, yielding 700-900 kg of cheese per lactation from 4,500-5,500 L of milk. Holstein-Friesians produce 25-35 L/day at 3.2-3.6% butterfat, yielding 550-700 kg of cheese from 7,000-9,000 L. Daily feed costs per liter of milk produced: approximately 0.4-0.6 kg of dry matter per liter of milk, with total mixed ration costs of $0.15-0.25 per liter in developed economies. A single dairy cow's annual manure production (8-12 tonnes) fertilizes 0.2-0.4 hectares of cropland at agronomic nitrogen rates.

**Beef production benchmarks**:
Slaughter weights of 450-650 kg live weight at 18-24 months yield dressed carcasses of 270-400 kg (60-65% dressing percentage). Retail cut yield averages 65-70% of carcass weight, producing 175-280 kg of saleable meat. Average daily gain on pasture: 0.7-1.0 kg/day for steers, 0.5-0.8 kg/day for heifers. Grain-finishing adds 1.0-1.5 kg/day during the final 100-120 days on feed, with feed conversion of 6-8 kg dry matter per kg of gain during this phase. Breakeven cost of production: $1.50-2.50 per kg of live weight in pasture-based systems, $2.50-3.50 per kg in feedlot systems. A 500 kg steer consumes 12,000-15,000 kg of forage dry matter over its lifetime.

### Regional Adaptations

Cattle breeds have been developed for virtually every climate and production system. In tropical regions (average temperatures 25-35°C), zebu-type breeds (*Bos indicus*) such as Brahman, Nelore, and Gir thrive due to heat tolerance from their sweat glands (3-4× the density of *Bos taurus*), loose skin that increases surface area for cooling, and resistance to ticks and tropical parasites. Brahman crossbreeds (Brangus: 3/8 Brahman × 5/8 Angus; Santa Gertrudis: 5/8 Shorthorn × 3/8 Brahman) combine tropical adaptation with meat quality. In cold northern climates (-30°C winters), Galloway, Highland, and yak-cattle hybrids (dzo) maintain body condition on poor forage. Highland cattle grow a double coat with an outer guard hair layer 20-30 cm long and a dense woolly undercoat providing insulation to -40°C; they require 20-30% less feed energy for thermoregulation than shorthaired breeds at the same temperature. In arid regions with under 40 cm annual precipitation, Afrikaner and Tuli breeds survive on browse and sparse grass that would not sustain European breeds, requiring water only every 2-3 days (vs daily for *Bos taurus*). Mountain breeds (Brown Swiss, Tyrol Grey) navigate slopes up to 30° and produce 4,000-6,000 L milk on alpine pasture at 1,000-2,000 m elevation.

### Historical Development

Cattle were domesticated from the wild aurochs (*Bos primigenius*) in two independent events: *Bos taurus* in the Fertile Crescent approximately 10,500 years ago (Çayönü, southeastern Turkey) and *Bos indicus* in the Indus Valley approximately 8,000 years ago. The aurochs stood 160-180 cm at the shoulder (bulls weighing 700-1,000 kg) and went extinct in 1627 when the last individual died in the Jaktorów Forest, Poland. Early domestic cattle were multipurpose — small, hardy animals providing traction, milk, meat, leather, and bone tools. Selective breeding for dairy specialization began in the Netherlands during the 1600s-1700s, producing the Holstein-Friesian from black-and-white cattle imported to North America in the 1850s. Beef breed specialization intensified in 18th-century England with the development of Shorthorn (1780s), Hereford (1790s), and Aberdeen Angus (1830s). Artificial insemination, developed in Russia by Ivanov in 1899 and refined in the United States through the 1940s, transformed cattle genetics — by 2020, over 90% of dairy cows in developed nations were bred via AI from fewer than 1,000 elite bulls worldwide. For bootstrapping, a founding herd of 10-15 cattle (8-10 cows, 2-3 heifers, 1-2 bulls) can expand to a productive herd of 40-60 head within 8-10 years using natural service at a 1:25 bull-to-cow ratio.

### Processing and Products

**Carcass breakdown**: A 500 kg live steer yields a 300 kg dressed carcass (60% dressing percentage) that breaks down into approximately 180 kg of retail cuts (60% of carcass), 45 kg of bone (15%), 30 kg of fat trim (10%), and 45 kg of waste and byproducts (15%). Prime cuts include the tenderloin (2.5-3.5 kg), ribeye roll (6-8 kg), strip loin (5-7 kg), and top sirloin (8-10 kg). Ground beef constitutes 35-45% of total retail yield.

**Tallow rendering**: Beef tallow from kidney fat (leaf fat) and subcutaneous fatback yields 70-85% of raw fat weight when rendered at 100-120°C for 4-6 hours. Tallow has a melting point of 40-45°C and saponification value of 195-200 mg KOH/g — ideal for soap making at a ratio of 350-400 g tallow to 50-60 g sodium hydroxide (lye) per kg of finished soap. Tallow candles burn at 0.05-0.08 g/minute with a flame temperature of approximately 1,000°C, producing 12-15 lumens — adequate for reading at 50 cm distance. Annual tallow yield from one 500 kg steer: 15-25 kg, sufficient for 30-50 kg of soap or 150-250 candles.

**Leather production**: One cattle hide yields 3-5 m² of leather after tanning (loss of 30-40% from raw hide weight during liming, fleshing, and tanning). Vegetable tanning with oak or chestnut bark extract (8-12% tannin solution) requires 30-60 days for full penetration of a 4-6 mm hide. Chrome tanning (requires industrial chemicals) completes in 24-48 hours. Cattle leather sole bends withstand 8-12 million flex cycles in footwear use. Rawhide (untanned, dehaired hide) stretched and dried produces drum heads, container linings, and thong material — one hide yields 15-25 m of 2 cm wide rawhide strips after soaking and cutting.

**Bone and horn utilization**: Cattle long bones (femur, tibia, metacarpal) are 70-75% mineral hydroxyapatite by weight and produce durable tool handles, awls, and needles after boiling (4-6 hours to remove marrow), sawing to shape, and polishing with pumice. Bone meal (ground to 1-3 mm particle size) contains 12-16% phosphorus and 24-30% calcium — applied at 200-400 g per m² as a slow-release phosphorus fertilizer. Horn sheaths (20-40 cm length) are thermoplastic keratin — softening at 80-100°C for pressing into combs, buttons, and drinking vessels. Each horn yields 80-150 g of worked keratin.

**Dairy products yield**: From 1,000 L of whole cow milk, a settlement produces approximately 100-120 kg of hard cheese (Cheddar, Gouda types aged 3-12 months at 8-12°C and 85-90% humidity), 40-50 kg of butter (from 35-40 L of cream separated at 3,500-4,000 RPM in a mechanical separator or 12-24 hours gravity separation), or 80-100 kg of yogurt (incubated at 42-45°C for 4-6 hours with 2-3% starter culture). Whey byproduct from cheese making (850-900 L per 1,000 L of milk) retains 20% of the milk protein and 90% of the lactose — it is fed to pigs at 4-6 L/day per grower pig, converting waste into 150-200 g of daily weight gain. Fermented dairy (kefir, cultured buttermilk) extends milk shelf life from 5-7 days to 2-4 weeks at 4°C while adding probiotic bacteria that improve gut health.

### Housing and Infrastructure

**Dairy parlor design**: A rudimentary milking parlor for 10-20 cows requires a covered structure of 4 × 8 m with a concrete floor sloped 2-3% toward a drain, 10 milking stalls (1.2 m wide × 1.8 m long) with head catches, a feed manger at chest height, and a pipeline or bucket collection system. Water supply must deliver 40-60 L per cow per milking session for udder washing and floor flushing. A milk cooling tank (insulated stainless steel, 200-500 L capacity) chills milk to 4°C within 30 minutes using well water or ice-bank cooling. Construction cost for a basic parlor: approximately $2,000-5,000 using timber framing and concrete.

**Cattle crush and handling facility**: A safe handling system requires a curved forcing pen (3-4 m diameter), a solid-sided race (6-8 m long, 70-80 cm internal width tapering to 65 cm), and a crush (squeeze chute) with head bail and side-opening panels. Steel construction (50 mm square tubing, 2-3 mm wall thickness) supports 800-1,000 kg of lateral force from a struggling animal. The race uses cattle's natural tendency to follow the animal ahead — solid sides prevent them from seeing handlers and balking. A well-designed facility processes 40-60 cattle per hour with two operators.

**Winter housing**: In climates with 120+ days of freezing temperatures, cattle require indoor housing for 4-6 months. A bedded-pack barn for 20 cattle (10-15 m² per animal plus 2 m of feed bunk frontage per animal) measures approximately 8 × 40 m with 3-4 m ceiling height, ridge ventilation, and concrete feed alley. Deep straw bedding (initial 20-30 cm, topped weekly with 2-3 kg per animal) composts in place, generating heat that raises bed temperature to 20-30°C even when ambient temperature is -20°C. Annual straw requirement: 2-3 tonnes per animal. Manure pack removal in spring yields 40-60 tonnes of composted manure from a 20-cow barn over one winter season.

**Feed storage**: A 20-cow herd consuming 12-15 kg hay per animal per day requires 36-54 tonnes of hay for a 150-day winter (plus 15% buffer = 41-62 tonnes). Stored as small square bales (20-25 kg each), this requires 1,600-3,100 bales occupying a hayloft or hay shed of 80-120 m² floor area stacked 3-4 m high. Hay must be stored under cover at below 18% moisture — above 25% moisture, spontaneous combustion can occur within 3-7 days of baling. Grain storage for supplementation (2-4 kg/day per working oxen or lactating cow) requires an additional 2-4 tonnes stored in rodent-proof bins (metal or tightly sealed wood). A grain bin of 1.5 m diameter × 2.0 m height holds approximately 2,500-3,000 kg of oats or barley.

**Water infrastructure**: A 20-cow dairy herd requires 800-1,600 L of clean water daily (40-80 L per cow, doubled in hot weather above 30°C). A well or spring producing 4-8 L/minute (240-480 L/hour) meets peak demand during morning and evening drinking. Water troughs (150-200 L capacity, 1 per 20 cattle) with float valves maintain constant supply. In freezing climates, trough heaters (electric or wood-fired) or twice-daily manual filling prevents ice formation — cattle cannot meet water needs from snow alone. Pipeline from water source to trough: 25-40 mm diameter PVC or galvanized steel, buried 60-90 cm below frost line. Total water system cost for a 20-cow operation: $500-1,500 depending on distance from water source.
**Young stock facilities**: Calves from birth to weaning (6-8 months) require individual hutches (1.2 × 1.8 m, solid-walled with outdoor access) for the first 8 weeks to prevent disease transmission, followed by group pens (3-4 m² per calf) with shared feed bunks and water access. Calf hutches made from fiberglass, wood, or recycled plastic drums cost $100-300 each and last 10-15 years. Bed with straw (2-3 kg/day per hutch). Group pens for 10-15 weaned calves require 30-45 m² of well-drained space with a 10-15 m² sheltered area. Target average daily gain from birth to weaning: 700-900 g/day, requiring 4-6 L of milk or milk replacer daily plus calf starter grain introduced at 5-7 days, reaching 1.5-2.0 kg/day starter intake by weaning at 8 weeks for intensive systems.
**Manure handling system**: A 20-cow operation generates 600-800 kg of fresh manure daily (220-290 tonnes per year). A concrete-floored manure storage pad (6 × 10 m, 1.5 m walls, holding 90 m³ or approximately 3 months of accumulated manure) prevents groundwater contamination and allows controlled field application. Compost windrows (1.5-2.0 m wide × 1.0-1.5 m high) of manure mixed with straw bedding at 25-30:1 carbon-to-nitrogen ratio reach 55-65°C within 3-5 days, killing pathogens and weed seeds. Turn windrows every 7-14 days with a tractor-mounted loader or pitchfork. Finished compost (dark, crumbly, earthy-smelling) is ready in 3-6 months and applied at 10-20 tonnes per hectare to cropland, contributing 50-120 kg N, 20-60 kg P, and 50-100 kg K per hectare per application.
**Breeding records**: Track each cow's breeding date, expected calving date (breeding date + 283 days), actual calving date, calf birth weight, calving ease score (1-5 scale), and rebreeding date. Target calving interval under 370 days (365 ideal). Record milk yield per cow per milking using a graduated bucket or scale (1 L of milk weighs approximately 1.03 kg). Annual summary calculations: average milk per cow per lactation, average calving interval, percent calf crop weaned, and feed cost per liter of milk or kg of beef produced. A simple ledger with one page per cow, updated daily, provides the data foundation for culling decisions — cows failing to conceive within 90 days postpartum or producing below 70% of herd average milk yield are candidates for culling.
**Pasture productivity benchmarks**: Well-managed temperate pasture (mixed grass-clover) produces 6-12 tonnes of dry matter per hectare per year, supporting 1.5-2.5 cattle per hectare during a 180-220 day grazing season at 2.5-3.0% of body weight in daily dry matter intake. Applying 20-30 tonnes of composted cattle manure per hectare per year maintains soil organic matter above 3.5% and sustains pasture productivity indefinitely without synthetic fertilizer. Legume content of 25-35% in the pasture sward fixes 80-150 kg N per hectare per year through rhizobial nitrogen fixation, replacing $100-200 per hectare in commercial nitrogen fertilizer costs.
**Oxen economics**: A trained ox team (2 animals, 600-800 kg each) replaces 4-6 human laborers for plowing, hauling, and timber extraction. A single ox pulls 10-15% of body weight at 3-4 km/h for 6-8 hours daily — sufficient to plow 0.3-0.5 hectares per day with a single-bottom moldboard plow cutting a 25-30 cm furrow. Yoke construction from a hardwood beam (oak, ash, or hickory, 10 × 12 cm cross-section, 1.2-1.5 m length) with bow-shaped wooden bows (5 cm diameter) fitted to each animal's neck costs approximately $20-50 in materials and requires 8-12 hours of skilled woodworking. Working oxen require 10-15% more feed than idle cattle — supplement with 2-4 kg grain per working day to maintain body condition during the plowing season (typically 40-60 working days per year for a 4-hectare farm).
A well-managed cattle operation of 20 head on 15-20 hectares provides a settlement with 80,000-120,000 L of milk per year (from 10 dairy cows), 2,000-3,000 kg of beef annually (from surplus calves and cull animals), 150-250 tonnes of manure for crop fertilization, and 1,000-1,500 hours of draft power for tillage and transport — a comprehensive contribution to community food security and agricultural productivity from a single integrated species.
The combined outputs make cattle the single most important livestock species for bootstrapping a self-sufficient settlement, justifying the higher upfront investment in housing, feed storage, and handling infrastructure that cattle require compared to smaller livestock.
Dairy herds should be housed within 100 m of the milking parlor to minimize walking distance and stress. Beef herds on extensive range may travel 2-5 km daily between grazing areas and water sources without adverse effects on weight gain or reproduction.
Feedlot finishing requires 7-10 m² per animal in dry lot conditions with concrete or gravel-surfaced feed aprons and 30-45 cm of bunk space per head for grain delivery.
