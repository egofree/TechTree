### Mining & Prospecting

**Surface prospecting**:
- **Visual indicators**: Malachite (green) and azurite (blue) stains on rock = copper ore. Red/rusty soil = iron ore. White/clear crystals = quartz. Yellow crystals = sulfur. Purple/green fluorite = fluorite. Black heavy sand = magnetite (iron).
- **Stream panning**: Pan stream gravels to concentrate heavy minerals. Gold (yellow, malleable), cassiterite (black, heavy = tin), magnetite (black, magnetic = iron), garnet (red = indicator of metamorphic deposits).
- **Streak test**: Rub mineral on unglazed porcelain. Hematite streaks red-brown (iron). Magnetite streaks black (iron). Pyrite streaks greenish-black (sulfur/iron).
- **Magnet test**: Magnetite (Fe₃O₄) is naturally magnetic. Attracted to lodestone or magnet. Iron indicator.

**Surface mining**:
- **Placer mining (stream deposits)**: Pan, sluice, or riffle-box for heavy minerals in stream gravels. No tools beyond shovel and pan.
- **Open-cut mining**: Strip vegetation and soil from exposed ore body. Break rock with fire-setting (heat rock face with fire, dash with water to crack) and stone/iron wedges.
- **Shallow pits**: Dig vertically 2-5 m into exposed ore. Timber shoring at 1-2 m intervals prevents collapse. Width: minimum 1.5 m for one worker.

**Ore identification reference**:

**Geological indicators for ore deposits**:
- **Quartz veins**: White, glassy veins cutting through rock. Often associated with gold, copper, and other hydrothermal minerals. Sample and pan every quartz vein encountered.
- **Gossan (iron cap)**: Rusty, reddish-brown, porous rock at surface above sulfide ore deposits. Sulfide minerals (pyrite, chalcopyrite) weather to iron oxides at surface. Gossan = reliable indicator of sulfide mineralization at depth. Look beneath the iron cap for primary sulfide ore.
- **Malachite staining**: Green staining on rock faces and in stream beds indicates copper mineralization. Follow stain uphill to source. Azurite (blue) serves the same purpose.
- **Vegetation changes**: Stunted, discolored, or absent vegetation in otherwise healthy area may indicate toxic minerals at shallow depth (copper, arsenic, sulfur). "Indicator plants": certain species thrive in metal-rich soils (e.g., Viola calaminaria for zinc). Conversely, dead zones in wet ground may indicate sulfide oxidation producing acid soils.

**Magnetic surveying**:
- **Dip needle (dipping compass)**: Freely pivoting magnetic needle that measures inclination (vertical angle) of Earth's magnetic field. Anomalies (deviations from expected inclination) indicate subsurface magnetic bodies — magnetite ore bodies, buried iron objects. Survey on a grid (10-50 m spacing). Contour the anomaly values. Positive anomaly = magnetic mass below.
- **Fluxgate magnetometer**: Electronic sensor measuring magnetic field intensity directly. Resolution: 1 nT (nanotesla). Faster and more precise than dip needle. Portable, battery-operated. Map field variations over survey area.
 - **Anomaly interpretation**: Sharp, narrow anomaly = shallow source. Broad, low anomaly = deep source. Elongated anomaly = linear ore body (vein). Circular anomaly = pipe-like body. Estimate depth = half-width of anomaly profile. Magnetic surveys are the most cost-effective method for locating iron ore deposits.

### Geological Survey Methods

**Stratigraphic mapping**:
- Map exposed rock layers (strata) in outcrops, road cuts, and stream banks. Record rock type, thickness, color, grain size, and fossil content for each layer. Establish the vertical sequence — ore deposits often occur at specific stratigraphic horizons (e.g., copper in Permian sandstones, iron in Precambrian banded iron formations averaging 200-400 m thick).
- Correlate strata across the survey area using marker beds — distinctive, laterally continuous layers such as a thin volcanic ash bed or a unique limestone horizon. Stratigraphic correlation establishes the three-dimensional geology from two-dimensional surface exposures. A baseline traverse every 200-500 m perpendicular to strike provides systematic coverage.
- Measure strike and dip with a compass clinometer. Strike is the compass direction of a horizontal line on the rock surface. Dip is the angle the layer tilts from horizontal (0-90°). Record as strike/dip (e.g., 045/30SE). Mapping these values reveals folds, faults, and the overall structural framework controlling ore deposit geometry.

**Structural geology for prospectors**:
- **Faults**: Fractures where rock has moved. Fault zones create pathways for hydrothermal fluids and often host vein-type deposits (gold, silver, copper). Map fault traces on surface — linear valleys, offset ridges, aligned springs. Mineralization commonly occurs where faults intersect or change direction. Fault displacement of 1-50 m is common in mining districts.
- **Folds**: Anticlines (arched upward) trap migrating hydrocarbons and can concentrate ore fluids in their crests. Synclines (bowl-shaped) may collect heavy minerals by gravity. Map fold axes by tracking dip reversals across strike. Iron ore deposits in folded banded iron formations reach ore grades of 55-65% Fe in fold hinges where silica has been leached.
- **Joints and fractures**: Systematic fracture sets create permeability for fluid flow and mineral deposition. Count fractures per meter in outcrop — high fracture density (10-30 fractures/m) indicates zones of enhanced fluid flow and potential mineralization. Measure fracture orientation to predict subsurface trends.

### Mineral Identification Techniques

**Hardness test (Mohs scale)**:
- Rank mineral hardness by scratch testing against reference minerals. Talc (1) scratched by fingernail. Gypsum (2) scratched by fingernail (~2.5). Calcite (3) scratched by copper coin. Fluorite (4) scratched by iron nail. Apatite (5) scratched by knife blade (~5.5). Orthoclase (6) scratched by steel file. Quartz (7) scratches glass. Topaz (8), corundum (9), diamond (10). Ore minerals: pyrite 6-6.5, magnetite 5.5-6.5, hematite 5-6, cassiterite 6-7, galena 2.5.

**Cleavage and fracture**:
- **Cleavage**: Flat surfaces along planes of weak atomic bonding. Galena has perfect cubic cleavage (breaks into cubes). Fluorite has perfect octahedral cleavage (breaks into diamond shapes). Mica has perfect basal cleavage (splits into thin sheets). Number of cleavage directions and angles between them are diagnostic.
- **Fracture**: Irregular breakage pattern where no cleavage exists. Quartz shows conchoidal fracture (smooth, curved surfaces like broken glass). Pyrite shows uneven fracture. Native copper shows hackly fracture (jagged, torn surfaces). Fracture appearance helps identify minerals that lack cleavage.

**Color and luster**:
- **Luster categories**: Metallic (pyrite, galena, magnetite), vitreous/glassy (quartz, fluorite), resinous (sphalerite, zinc ore), adamantine (diamond, zircon), earthy/dull (kaolinite, limonite). Metallic luster in opaque minerals is the strongest single indicator of ore potential.
- **Color reliability**: Mineral color is diagnostic for some species (malachite = always green, azurite = always blue, sulfur = always yellow) but variable for others (fluorite ranges from purple to green to colorless, quartz from clear to smoky). Always cross-check color with streak, hardness, and cleavage for positive identification.

**Specific gravity measurement**:
- Specific gravity (SG) = mineral density ÷ water density. Galena (SG 7.4-7.6) and cassiterite (SG 6.8-7.1) feel noticeably heavy for their size. Heft test: compare weight of unknown mineral to a similar-sized rock of known type. For precise measurement, weigh specimen in air (W_air) and suspended in water (W_water). SG = W_air ÷ (W_air - W_water). Gold (SG 19.3) is unmistakably heavy; quartz (SG 2.65) feels normal.

### Geochemical Prospecting

**Soil sampling**:
- Collect soil samples on a regular grid (25-100 m spacing) across the survey area. Sample the B-horizon soil (30-60 cm depth) where metals accumulate by adsorption onto clays and iron oxides. A-horizon (topsoil) is too affected by organic activity; C-horizon (weathered bedrock) is too localized. Sample mass: 200-500 g per site, sieved to <80 mesh (177 μm) for analysis.
- Analyze for pathfinder elements — elements that associate with the target ore but are cheaper to detect. Arsenic, antimony, and mercury are pathfinders for gold deposits (detectable at 5-50 ppm in soil). Molybdenum and zinc pathfinders for copper porphyry deposits. Geochemical anomalies exceeding 3× background levels warrant follow-up trenching or drilling.

**Stream sediment analysis**:
- Sample fine sediment (<150 μm) from active stream channels at 100-500 m intervals along drainage. Heavy minerals concentrate in sediment traps — behind boulders, in meander pools, at stream junctions. Sieve to <80 mesh, analyze for metal content. Anomalies in upstream samples narrow the search area to the contributing catchment.
- The minus-80-mesh fraction captures both clastic mineral grains and metals adsorbed onto iron-manganese oxide coatings on sediment particles. Gold anomalies as low as 0.01-0.1 g/t in stream sediment indicate a gold source within 1-3 km upstream. Copper anomalies above 100 ppm in sediment warrant upstream follow-up.
- **Heavy mineral concentrate (panning) method**: Pan 10-15 kg of stream sediment to a 10-30 g heavy mineral concentrate. Examine under binocular microscope at 10-30× magnification. Identify gold, cassiterite, scheelite, chromite, and other heavy minerals. Count grains per kilogram of original sediment. Gold counts >5 grains/kg concentrate indicate economic potential. Scheelite (CaWO₄, tungsten ore) fluoresces blue-white under shortwave UV — scan the concentrate with a UV lamp.

**Assay methods**:
- **Fire assay for gold**: The most reliable gold determination method, accurate to 0.01 g/t. Fuse 15-50 g of powdered sample with lead oxide (litharge), soda ash, borax, and silica at 1000-1100°C in a crucible. Lead collects precious metals into a metallic button. Cupel the lead button at 950°C — lead oxidizes and is absorbed by the cupel (bone ash dish), leaving a precious metal bead. Weigh the bead. Dissolve silver with nitric acid (parting) and weigh the residual gold. Fire assay is the industry standard for gold and silver analysis.
- **Colorimetric assay for copper**: Dissolve powdered sample in nitric acid. Add ammonia solution — copper produces an intense blue color (tetraamminecopper complex). Compare color intensity against standards of known concentration using a visual comparator or spectrophotometer (absorbance at 620 nm). Detection limit: 10-50 ppm copper. Fast and inexpensive, suitable for field screening of large sample sets.
- **Gravimetric assay**: Dissolve sample, precipitate the target element as an insoluble compound, filter, dry, and weigh. Barium precipitates as BaSO₄ (barium sulfate). Nickel precipitates as NiDMG (nickel dimethylglyoxime, bright red). Accuracy ±0.1% for major element concentrations.
- **Titrimetric assay (volumetric)**: Dissolve sample, react the target element with a standard solution of known concentration until the endpoint is reached (indicated by color change). Iodometric titration for copper: dissolve in acid, add KI, titrate liberated iodine with sodium thiosulfate (Na₂S₂O₃) using starch indicator (blue → colorless endpoint). Each mL of 0.1 N Na₂S₂O₃ = 6.35 mg Cu. Accuracy ±0.2% for copper concentrations >1%.

### Geophysical Methods

**Magnetic survey (for iron ores)**:
- Magnetometer surveys detect magnetic anomalies caused by subsurface magnetite (Fe₃O₄) or pyrrhotite (Fe₁₋ₓS). Total field magnetic intensity measured in nanotesla (nT). Background Earth field: 25,000-65,000 nT depending on latitude. Anomalies of 100-10,000 nT indicate significant magnetite bodies. Ground traverse spacing: 10-50 m, station spacing 5-25 m. Survey lines perpendicular to expected strike of the ore body.
- Airborne magnetic surveys cover 50-200 line-km per hour at 80-120 m terrain clearance. Detect large, deep magnetic bodies to depths of 300-500 m. Grid spacing 100-500 m for regional surveys, 50-100 m for detailed surveys. The ratio of vertical to horizontal gradient helps estimate source depth.

**Electrical resistivity survey (for sulfides)**:
- Massive sulfide ore bodies (pyrite, chalcopyrite, pyrrhotite, galena) are electrical conductors with resistivities of 0.1-10 Ω·m, contrasting sharply with surrounding rock at 100-10,000 Ω·m. Inject current into the ground through two electrodes, measure voltage between two potential electrodes. Calculate apparent resistivity (ρ = 2πa × V/I, where a = electrode spacing for Wenner array).
- Sulfide ore bodies produce low-resistivity anomalies detectable to depths of 50-100 m with electrode spacings of 50-200 m. Induced polarization (IP) survey is even more effective for disseminated sulfides — it measures the ability of sulfide minerals to store electrical charge (chargeability). IP anomalies of 20-50 milliseconds indicate disseminated sulfide mineralization at depths of 100-200 m.
- **Self-potential (SP) method**: Sulfide ore bodies generate natural electrical potentials of 100-1000 mV due to electrochemical reactions between the oxidized upper portion and reduced lower portion of the body. Measure voltage between a fixed reference electrode and a roving electrode along survey lines. Negative SP anomalies centered over the ore body are diagnostic. No external current source needed — the ore body acts as a natural battery. SP surveys detect massive sulfides to 50-100 m depth and cost very little to perform.

**Gravity survey**:
- Measure local variations in Earth's gravitational field with a gravimeter (resolution 0.01 mGal, where 1 mGal = 10⁻⁵ m/s²). Dense ore bodies (galena at SG 7.5, magnetite at SG 5.2) produce positive gravity anomalies overlying lighter host rock (limestone at SG 2.7, sandstone at SG 2.3). Station spacing 10-50 m. Correct for latitude, elevation, terrain, and tidal effects. Residual gravity anomalies of 0.5-5 mGal indicate ore bodies of 10-100 m dimension at 50-200 m depth.

**Seismic refraction survey**:
- Measure the travel time of seismic waves (generated by hammer strike or small explosive charge) through subsurface layers to determine depth to bedrock and locate fault zones. Geophones (velocity sensors) spaced at 5-25 m intervals along a line detect first arrivals. Wave velocity increases with rock density: soil 300-800 m/s, weathered rock 1500-3000 m/s, competent bedrock 4000-6000 m/s. Depth to bedrock calculated from the crossover distance where the direct wave and refracted wave arrive simultaneously. Useful for placer deposit evaluation (depth to bedrock under gravels) and for locating buried fault zones that may host mineralization.

### Panning Technique Detail

**Gravel sampling protocol**:
- Collect 5-10 kg of gravel from each sample site, targeting the coarsest material in the active stream channel. Dig to bedrock or an impermeable clay layer where heavy minerals concentrate — the "false bedrock." Use a shovel or mattock to break up consolidated gravel. Process the entire sample through the pan to avoid bias. Record sample location, depth, and gravel lithology for each site.
- Color-count method: after panning to concentrate, count the number of gold colors (visible particles) per pan. One color per pan indicates trace gold (0.01-0.05 g/t in source). Ten or more colors per pan indicates a significant gold source within 500 m upstream. Record colors per pan on a field map to define the anomaly pattern.

**Riffle box (sluice box) construction**:
- Build a wooden trough 3-5 m long, 30-40 cm wide, with 15-20 cm high sides. Install riffles (wooden cross-bars 2×4 cm) across the bottom at 5-10 cm spacing. Place carpet, burlap, or miner's moss under the riffles to trap fine gold particles. Set the box at 10-15° slope. Feed gravel at the head with water flow of 50-100 L/min. Heavy minerals settle behind riffles; lighter material washes through. Clean riffles every 2-4 hours of operation. A well-built riffle box processes 0.5-2 m³ of gravel per day and recovers 90-95% of gold >100 μm.

**Rocker box (cradle)**:
- A compact alternative to the sluice box for areas with limited water supply. Wooden box 60-80 cm long with a perforated iron or wooden screen (6-10 mm holes) on top. Gravel placed on screen, water poured over by bucket. Fines fall through screen onto an inclined apron with riffles below. One operator processes 1-3 m³ per day with 20-40 liters of water. Suitable for testing small gravel deposits.

**Pan construction and selection**:
- Prospectors' pans are wide, shallow dishes 30-50 cm diameter, 5-8 cm deep, with sloping sides at 30-45°. Steel pans are standard; plastic pans are lighter and do not rust. Dark-colored pans (black or green) show gold colors better against the background. Pan shape matters: a wide, shallow pan processes gravel faster but loses fine gold; a deeper pan retains fine gold but processes slower. A 40 cm steel pan is the standard field tool.
- The panning motion: submerge the loaded pan in water. Agitate with a circular, oscillating motion to stratify material — heavy minerals sink to bottom. Tilt the pan forward, allow light material to wash over the lip. Periodically shake the pan back and forth to settle heavies deeper. Repeat until only a tablespoon of heavy concentrate remains. Inspect the concentrate carefully — tilt the pan in sunlight to make gold flecks glint. Practice with known gold-bearing gravel until recovery exceeds 90%.

### Indicator Minerals

**Diamond indicator minerals**:
- Garnet (G9 and G10 pyrope garnets, deep red to purple), ilmenite (black, metallic), chromite (black, submetallic, SG 4.5-4.8), and diopside (pale green to colorless, vitreous) are carried to the surface from mantle depths (150-200 km) by kimberlite eruptions. Finding these minerals in stream sediment indicates a kimberlite pipe within 5-20 km upstream. Garnet composition (measured by electron microprobe) distinguishes diamondiferous (G10, high-Cr, low-Ca) from barren (G9) kimberlite.
- Kimberlite itself is a distinctive blue-gray to greenish-gray porphyritic rock containing rounded olivine crystals in a fine-grained matrix. It occurs as vertical pipe-shaped bodies 50-500 m in diameter. Surface expression is often a shallow depression or circular swamp due to rapid weathering.
- Kimberlite indicator mineral (KIM) surveys process 10-25 kg samples of stream sediment or glacial till through dense media separation (SG 3.2-3.3 sodium polytungstate) to concentrate heavy minerals. Microscopic examination identifies the indicator minerals among the concentrate. A single G10 garnet grain in a 25 kg sample is sufficient evidence to warrant follow-up exploration. Most kimberlite pipes contain diamonds at grades of 0.5-5 carats per hundred tonnes, but only 10-20% of pipes are economically viable.

**Limonite gossan for sulfides**:
- Gossan (iron hat) is the oxidized, rust-colored cap above weathered sulfide deposits. Primary sulfides (pyrite FeS₂, chalcopyrite CuFeS₂) react with oxygen and groundwater to form iron oxides (limonite, goethite) at the surface. The gossan is typically 2-20 m thick, porous, and red-brown to yellow-brown. Beneath the gossan, a supergene enrichment zone contains secondary copper minerals (chalcocite Cu₂S at 79.8% Cu, covellite CuS at 66.5% Cu) enriched 2-5× over primary ore grades.
- Boxwork texture in gossan — a network of limonite walls forming cellular patterns — preserves the shape of the original sulfide crystal outlines and allows identification of the primary sulfide mineral before mining. Cubic boxwork = pyrite. Tetrahedral boxwork = tetrahedrite (copper-antimony sulfide). Linear boxwork = stibnite (antimony sulfide).

**Pathfinder elements and dispersion halos**:
- Primary dispersion: elements distributed in rock around the ore body by the same hydrothermal fluids that formed the deposit. Mercury (Hg) halos extend 200-500 m from gold deposits, detectable at 10-100 ppb in soil. Arsenic (As) halos extend 100-300 m from gold and base metal deposits, detectable at 20-500 ppm in soil.
- Secondary dispersion: elements released by weathering and transported by groundwater or surface water downslope and downstream. Mechanical dispersion (clastic transport of mineral grains) forms elongate anomalies in the downslope direction, typically 50-200 m from source. Chemical dispersion (dissolved ions in groundwater) forms broader, more diffuse anomalies extending 100-500 m. Sampling the correct dispersion medium (soil, stream sediment, or vegetation) at the correct depth maximizes anomaly contrast.

### Claim Staking & Legal Framework

**Physical claim marking**:
- Mark claim boundaries with posts (10×10 cm wood, 1.2 m above ground) at each corner. Cut blaze marks on adjacent trees facing the claim. Record claim coordinates and dimensions relative to a surveyed reference point. Typical claim size: 150-500 m along strike × 150-300 m across strike (varies by jurisdiction). Post a notice at each corner with claim name, date, and prospector's name.
- Maintain claim by performing annual assessment work — physical labor or expenditure demonstrating active development. Typical minimum: $100-1000 per year of work or expenditure (varies by jurisdiction). File annual report with mining recorder. Failure to perform assessment work results in claim forfeiture. Assessment work includes trenching, drilling, road building, geological mapping, geophysical surveys, and geochemical sampling — all activities that advance the prospect toward production.

**Claim types**:
- **Lode claim**: Covers hard rock mineral deposits in place (veins, lodes, zones). Maximum size typically 450 m along strike × 300 m wide. Must be located on a known mineralized occurrence or within a mining district.
- **Placer claim**: Covers unconsolidated deposits (gravels, sands) containing valuable minerals. Maximum size typically 650 m along stream × 300 m wide. Staked along watercourses where gold, tin, or heavy minerals have been concentrated by water action.
- **Mill site**: Up to 2 hectares for ore processing facilities, waste disposal, or other mining support infrastructure. Need not be adjacent to the lode or placer claim it serves.

**Legal framework basics**:
- Most mining jurisdictions follow the "free entry" system: any person may stake a claim on Crown (public) land, subject to existing reservations and exclusions (national parks, towns, existing claims). The first person to stake a valid claim earns exclusive rights to the minerals within its boundaries. Surface rights (land ownership) are separate from mineral rights — a prospector may hold mineral rights on land they do not own, but must compensate the surface owner for disturbance.
- Environmental assessment is required before development proceeds beyond exploration. Baseline studies of water quality, flora, fauna, and archaeology establish pre-mining conditions. Reclamation bond (financial guarantee) ensures the site will be restored after mining ceases. Water discharge permits set limits on suspended solids (typically <25 mg/L), pH (6.5-8.5), and dissolved metals.

**Royalty and taxation**:
- Most jurisdictions levy a royalty on mineral production, typically 2-5% of gross revenue (ad valorem royalty) or 2-10% of net smelter return (NSR royalty). The NSR is the payment received from the smelter after deducting treatment charges, refining costs, and transport. Income tax on mining profits applies separately, typically 25-35% corporate rate. Some jurisdictions offer accelerated depreciation for mine development expenditure to encourage investment. Tax stabilization agreements fix the fiscal terms for 10-20 years to protect against future tax increases.

### Deposit Modeling & Resource Estimation

**Trenching and test pitting**:
- Excavate trenches (1-2 m wide, 2-5 m deep, 10-50 m long) across the strike of mineralized outcrops to expose fresh bedrock below weathered surface. Space trenches 25-100 m apart along strike. Map and sample the trench walls in detail — record rock type, alteration, mineralization, and structure for each meter of trench length. Channel sample across the full width of mineralization (cut a continuous groove 10 cm wide × 5 cm deep with a chisel) for assay. Trenching costs 10-50× less than drilling per meter of geological information.
- Test pits are hand-dug or machine-dug excavations 1-3 m diameter, 2-10 m deep. Useful for sampling placer deposits (gold, tin) below the active stream channel and for establishing depth to bedrock in alluvial terrain. Log each pit by depth interval — record gravel lithology, clay layers, boulder frequency, and bedrock type. Panner's depth: dig until bedrock is reached, as heavy minerals concentrate on or just above the bedrock surface.

**Resource estimation basics**:
- **Inferred resource**: Estimate based on geological evidence and assumed continuity from outcrops, trenches, and limited drilling. Tonnage and grade are approximate. Insufficient for mine planning but sufficient to justify exploration expenditure.
- **Indicated resource**: Tonnage, grade, and geometry estimated with reasonable confidence from systematic sampling (closely spaced drilling, 25-50 m hole spacing). Sufficient for preliminary mine design and feasibility studies.
- **Measured resource**: Confirmed by detailed sampling at close spacing (10-25 m). Tonnage and grade known within 10-20%. Sufficient for detailed mine planning and investment decisions.
- **Grade-tonnage curve**: Plot cumulative tonnage vs. cutoff grade. Higher cutoff grade = higher average grade but lower tonnage. Lower cutoff = more ore but lower average grade. The economic cutoff grade is the lowest grade at which mining is profitable, determined by metal price, recovery rate, and operating cost. Typical copper cutoff: 0.2-0.5% Cu. Typical gold cutoff: 0.5-1.5 g/t Au.

### Field Mapping Procedures

**Geological mapping at prospect scale**:
- Produce a geological map at 1:5,000 to 1:10,000 scale covering the prospect area and surrounding 500-1000 m. Walk all outcrops, recording rock type, mineralization, alteration, structure (strike/dip of bedding, faults, joints), and sample locations on a topographic base map. Outcrop mapping is the foundation — every geological interpretation starts from observed rock. Color-code rock units on the map; use standard symbols for structural measurements.
- Alteration mapping is particularly important for porphyry copper and epithermal gold deposits. Potassic alteration (pink-orange K-feldspar, secondary biotite) indicates the high-temperature core of a porphyry system. Phyllic alteration (white sericite + quartz + pyrite, "QSP") forms a shell around the potassic zone. Argillic alteration (white clay, kaolinite) occupies the outer zone. Propylitic alteration (green chlorite + epidote + calcite) marks the distal fringe. Map these alteration zones systematically — they define the vector toward the center of mineralization.

**Pacing and compass traverse**:
- Measure distance by pacing (calibrate your pace length: walk 100 m five times, count steps, divide). Typical pace: 1.3-1.5 m per double-step (left foot to left foot). Record bearing with compass to ±2° accuracy. Plot traverse on map as you go. This low-technology method produces acceptable maps at 1:5,000 scale with <5% distance error. Supplement with hand-level elevations (read vertical angle to horizon, correct for eye height of 1.5 m) for a topographic profile along the traverse.

**Sample documentation**:
- Number every sample sequentially (e.g., P-001, P-002). Record location (distance along traverse + bearing, or GPS coordinates), sample type (grab, channel, chip, soil, stream sediment), lithology, mineralization observed, and sample weight in a field notebook. One sample per 5-25 m of exposed mineralization for channel sampling; one per 25-100 m for soil grids. Wrap channel and chip samples in heavy paper bags; soil samples in cloth bags. Store all samples in a secure, dry location before shipping to the assay laboratory.

**Photo documentation**:
- Photograph every outcrop, trench, and sample site with a scale reference (ruler, lens cap, or person) and a north arrow. Record photo number, location, and description in the field notebook. Outcrop photos provide a permanent record of geological relationships that may be destroyed by subsequent excavation. Shoot in consistent lighting — morning and afternoon sun produce shadows that reveal texture and structure better than midday overhead light.

### Safety & Hazards

- **Terrain hazards**: Prospecting involves traversing rugged, unstable terrain. Rockfalls, loose scree, steep slopes. Sturdy boots, careful footing. Never prospect alone in remote areas. Carry a basic first aid kit, map, compass, whistle, and emergency shelter blanket. A personal locator beacon (PLB) provides satellite distress signaling in areas without cell coverage.
- **Water hazards**: River banks and lake shores (where minerals concentrate) are slippery and subject to flash floods. Be aware of upstream weather. Never wade in fast-moving water above knee depth. Wear a personal flotation device when panning from a boat or working along steep stream banks.
- **Toxic minerals**: Some ore minerals contain arsenic (arsenopyrite), mercury (cinnabar), or lead (galena). Handle with gloves. Do not inhale dust from crushing test samples. Wash hands before eating. Arsenic compounds are tasteless and odorless — never eat, drink, or smoke while handling ore samples. Mercury vapor from cinnabar (HgS) accumulates in enclosed spaces — assay mercury ores only in well-ventilated areas with activated carbon traps.
- **Underground hazards**: If test pits or adits are dug, ensure roof support. Bad air (low oxygen, methane, CO₂) in enclosed spaces — ventilate before entering. Never enter alone. Test pits deeper than 1.2 m must be shored or sloped to prevent collapse. Excavated material must be placed at least 1 m from the pit edge. Atmospheric oxygen below 19.5% causes impaired judgment; below 16% is immediately dangerous to life.

### Geochemical Stream Sediment Sampling Technique

Stream sediment sampling is the most cost-effective regional exploration method for base metals and gold. The procedure: collect 10-50 g of the minus-80 mesh (<177 μm) fraction from active stream sediment, typically from behind boulders or in meander pools where fines accumulate. Sieve the bulk sample in the field using nested brass sieves (80 mesh = 177 μm openings). The fine fraction captures trace metals adsorbed onto iron-manganese oxide coatings on sediment grains. Analyze the sample for pathfinder elements: Cu, Pb, Zn by atomic absorption spectroscopy (AAS) with detection limits of 1-5 ppm, or Au by fire assay with a 10-50 g charge accurate to 0.01 g/t. Sample spacing: 100-500 m along drainage, with at least one sample per first-order stream catchment. Anomalies exceeding 3× background concentration warrant upstream follow-up with soil sampling on 50-100 m grid spacing.
