# Wafer Production

> **Node ID**: silicon.wafer-production
> **Domain**: Silicon
> **Timeline**: Years 30-50
> **Outputs**: silicon_wafers, polished_wafers, epitaxial_wafers

## Overview

Wafer production transforms poly-crystalline or single-crystal silicon ingots into thin, flat, ultra-polished discs — the substrates for all integrated circuit fabrication. The process includes ingot cropping, wire-saw slicing, lapping, chemical-mechanical polishing (CMP), and cleanroom packaging. Wafer flatness (TTV < 5 μm) and surface roughness (< 0.5 nm RMS) are critical for subsequent photolithography.

## Ingot Evaluation and Cropping

**Ingot inspection**: After CZ or FZ crystal growth, inspect the ingot (150-300 mm diameter, 1-3 m long, 30-150 kg) for quality criteria: (1) Resistivity mapping: four-point probe measurements every 25-50 mm along the ingot length. Target: 1-50 Ω·cm for most CMOS applications (dopant concentration 10¹⁴-10¹⁶ atoms/cm³). Resistivity variation along the ingot should be <5% for premium wafers. (2) Oxygen content: FTIR (Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy) measures interstitial oxygen (O_i) at 1107 cm⁻¹ absorption peak. CZ silicon contains 5-20 ppma oxygen (from dissolution of the SiO₂ crucible). Oxygen above 15 ppma can form SiO₂ precipitates during thermal processing (useful for internal gettering of metallic impurities, but excessive precipitation causes warpage). (3) Carbon content: FTIR at 607 cm⁻¹, target <0.5 ppma (carbon nucleates defects). (4) Crystallographic orientation: X-ray diffraction or Laue back-reflection to confirm <100> or <111> orientation within ±0.5° of the seed crystal axis.

**Cropping**: Cut off the seed (top 50-100 mm — heavily dislocated from the neck growth process) and tail (bottom 20-50 mm — tapered and heavily doped due to segregation). Also remove any portions with visible defects (grain boundaries, slip lines, or inclusions). Cropping is done with a diamond-impregnated band saw (blade speed 1500-3000 m/min, feed rate 5-20 mm/min). Typical yield loss from cropping: 10-20% of ingot length. The remaining "body" of the ingot is the material that becomes wafers.

**Diameter grinding**: Grind the ingot to precise diameter on a centerless grinder: 150.0 ±0.5 mm (6-inch), 200.0 ±0.5 mm (8-inch), or 300.0 ±0.5 mm (12-inch). The grinding wheel is diamond-impregnated resin bond (80-200 grit). Rotation speed: 50-200 RPM for the ingot, 2000-5000 RPM for the grinding wheel. Material removal: 0.5-3.0 mm from the as-grown diameter. Coolant: deionized water. The ground surface has a matte finish (Ra 0.5-2.0 μm). After grinding, the ingot is perfectly cylindrical — any out-of-roundness or taper must be <0.1 mm over the full length.

## Wire Saw Slicing

**Wire saw mechanics**: A single continuous steel wire (0.08-0.18 mm diameter, high-carbon steel, tensile strength 3500-4500 MPa) is wound on wire guides (grooved ceramic or polyurethane-coated steel drums, 300-600 mm diameter) in a web of 200-800 parallel wires spaced at the desired wafer thickness (775 μm for 200 mm wafers, 925 μm for 300 mm wafers, including kerf loss). The wire web travels at 5-15 m/s through a slurry of abrasive particles (suspended in glycol or oil carrier). The abrasive — typically silicon carbide (SiC, 300-600 grit, 8-25 μm particle size) for standard slicing, or diamond-grit-coated wire for faster cutting — grinds a thin kerf (100-180 μm wide) through the silicon ingot.

**Slicing parameters**: For a 200 mm diameter, 1 m long ingot (slicing into ~250 wafers): wire speed 10 m/s, feed rate 0.3-0.8 mm/min (slower for larger diameters — 300 mm ingots cut at 0.2-0.4 mm/min), slurry flow 5-15 L/min, slurry concentration 30-50% SiC by weight in polyethylene glycol carrier. Total slicing time: 4-12 hours for 200 mm ingots, 8-20 hours for 300 mm ingots. Kerf loss per wafer: 120-180 μm (lost as silicon dust — ~40-50% of the ingot becomes kerf waste). Wire consumption: 20-50 km of wire per ingot (wire is used once — the abrasive slurry degrades the wire surface). The slurry is recycled: centrifuge to separate SiC abrasive from silicon powder and depleted carrier, remix with fresh abrasive.

**Wafer orientation flat or notch**: During grinding (before slicing), a flat or notch is ground into the ingot periphery to indicate crystal orientation. For <100> wafers: one primary flat along {110} plane. For <111> wafers: primary flat plus secondary flat at 45° or 90° offset. For 300 mm wafers: a single semicircular notch replaces the flat (notch depth 1.0-1.5 mm, radius ~1.0 mm). The flat/notch is used for automated wafer handling and alignment in all subsequent process tools.

## Edge Rounding

**Purpose**: As-sawn wafers have sharp, chipped edges (micro-cracks and subsurface damage from the wire saw). Sharp edges chip during handling, producing particles that contaminate the cleanroom. Edge rounding removes the sharp corners and creates a smooth, rounded profile.

**Process**: Grind the wafer perimeter on a CNC edge grinder using a diamond profiling wheel (300-600 grit). The wheel follows a programmed path that creates a bullet-nose or rounded edge profile (typical radius 0.3-0.8 mm for 200 mm wafers, 0.5-1.0 mm for 300 mm wafers). Material removal: 0.1-0.3 mm from the wafer diameter. Rotation: wafer spins at 50-200 RPM while the grinding wheel engages the edge. Coolant: deionized water. After edge rounding, the wafer diameter is reduced to final specification (200.0 ±0.2 mm or 300.0 ±0.2 mm).

## Lapping

**Purpose**: Wire-sawn wafers have saw marks (parallel grooves, 5-20 μm deep, from the wire path), subsurface damage (micro-cracks extending 5-15 μm below the surface), and total thickness variation (TTV) of 20-50 μm. Lapping removes most of this damage and brings the wafer to near-final thickness with improved flatness.

**Process**: Mount wafers on a carrier plate (ceramic or cast iron, 500-800 mm diameter, holds 5-25 wafers). The carrier plate rotates on a large cast iron lapping plate (800-1500 mm diameter, flat to <2 μm over the full surface) while a slurry of abrasive (aluminum oxide Al₂O₃, 9-15 μm particle size, or silicon carbide SiC) in water or glycol is fed between the wafer surface and the lapping plate. Load: 50-200 g/cm² on the wafers. Plate rotation: 20-60 RPM. Lapping removes 20-50 μm per side (both sides are lapped sequentially or simultaneously on double-sided lapping machines). Time: 10-30 minutes per side. Resulting surface: uniform matte gray finish, TTV reduced to 2-10 μm, subsurface damage reduced to 2-5 μm. Lapped wafer thickness: 725 ±10 μm (200 mm) or 925 ±10 μm (300 mm).

## Etching and Cleaning

**Damage etch**: Lapping leaves subsurface damage (micro-cracks and residual stress in a 2-5 μm surface layer). Remove by chemical etching in a mixture of nitric acid (HNO₃) and hydrofluoric acid (HF), typically in a ratio of 10:1 to 20:1 HNO₃:HF (the "acid etch" or "chemical milling" step). HNO₃ oxidizes silicon to SiO₂; HF dissolves the SiO₂. Net reaction: Si + 4HNO₃ + 6HF → H₂SiF₆ + 4NO₂ + 4H₂O. Etch rate: 1-5 μm/min depending on acid concentration and temperature (20-30°C). Remove 10-30 μm total (5-15 μm per side). This eliminates all mechanical damage and produces a smooth, featureless surface. Alternative: alkaline etch with KOH (20-40% at 70-90°C, etch rate 0.5-2.0 μm/min, anisotropic — etches <100> much faster than <111>). Alkaline etch is less hazardous than HF (no fluoride handling risk) and produces less surface pitting on properly oriented wafers.

**RCA clean**: After etching, clean the wafers in the industry-standard RCA sequence to remove organic, metallic, and particulate contamination. RCA-1 (SC-1, Standard Clean 1): NH₄OH:H₂O₂:H₂O = 1:1:5 at 75-85°C for 10-20 minutes — removes organic contaminants and particles (the alkaline peroxide solution oxidizes organics and the high pH creates electrostatic repulsion that prevents particle redeposition). RCA-2 (SC-2): HCl:H₂O₂:H₂O = 1:1:6 at 75-85°C for 10-20 minutes — removes alkali metals (Na⁺, K⁺) and heavy metals (Fe, Cu, Ni, Au) by forming soluble chloride complexes. Between SC-1 and SC-2, a brief HF dip (1-2% HF for 15-60 seconds) strips the native oxide and any oxide-trapped contaminants. Final rinse in ultrapure water (18.2 MΩ·cm resistivity, <1 particle per mL at ≥0.05 μm, total organic carbon <1 ppb) and spin-rinse-dry (3000-5000 RPM, N₂ gas blow).

## Chemical-Mechanical Polishing (CMP)

**CMP principle**: Simultaneously apply chemical dissolution and mechanical abrasion to remove material at an atomic level. The wafer is pressed face-down against a rotating polishing pad (polyurethane, 1-2 mm thick on a sub-pad of felt or compressed foam) while a colloidal silica slurry (SiO₂ particles 20-80 nm diameter, 10-30% solids in alkaline medium, pH 10-11 adjusted with KOH or NH₄OH) is dispensed onto the pad at 50-200 mL/min.

**CMP parameters**: Down force: 200-500 g/cm² (2-5 psi). Platen rotation: 30-80 RPM. Carrier rotation: 20-60 RPM (counter-rotation to platen for uniformity). Slurry flow: 100-300 mL/min. Polishing time: 10-30 minutes per side. Material removal rate: 0.5-2.0 μm/min. Total removal: 10-30 μm per side. The alkaline slurry slightly dissolves the silicon surface SiO₂ layer (present as native oxide ~1.5 nm thick, regrown continuously), while the silica particles mechanically abrade the softened surface layer. This chemical-mechanical synergy produces an atomically smooth, damage-free surface.

**Polishing outcomes**: Surface roughness: <0.5 nm RMS (measured by atomic force microscopy, AFM, over 10 × 10 μm area), typically 0.1-0.3 nm RMS for prime-grade wafers. Flatness: global flatness (GF) < 3 μm for 200 mm, < 5 μm for 300 mm (measured by capacitive scanning or laser interferometry over the entire wafer surface, reported as total indicator reading, TIR, or site flatness, SFQR). Site flatness (local area, 25 × 25 mm): <0.1-0.3 μm (critical for photolithography depth of focus at sub-100 nm nodes). Nanotopography: <5 nm over 2 × 2 mm area (the "waviness" of the surface at intermediate spatial frequencies — affects gate oxide uniformity). Particle specification: <10 particles ≥0.12 μm per wafer (200 mm), <50 particles ≥0.09 μm per wafer (300 mm).

## Double-Side Polishing (DSP)

For 300 mm wafers and advanced applications, both sides are polished simultaneously on a double-side polishing machine. The wafer floats between two polishing platens (upper and lower) in a carrier with oversized windows (the wafer extends slightly beyond the carrier to ensure full edge-to-edge polishing). Both platens rotate — upper clockwise, lower counter-clockwise. This produces wafers with identical surface quality on both sides and eliminates the "mirror-perfect front / hazy back" asymmetry of single-side polished (SSP) wafers. DSP wafers have superior flatness (TTV <2 μm) because the double-sided process is inherently self-correcting for thickness variation. Most 300 mm prime wafers are DSP.

## Epitaxial Wafer Production

**Epitaxy overview**: Epitaxial wafers have a thin layer (1-100 μm) of single-crystal silicon grown on top of a polished substrate wafer. The epitaxial ("epi") layer has precisely controlled doping (type, concentration, and profile) independent of the substrate. This decoupling allows, for example, a lightly doped (high resistivity) epi layer on a heavily doped (low resistivity) substrate — the standard configuration for CMOS logic wafers (p/p+ or n/n+), where the substrate provides low resistance for backside contact and the epi layer provides the high-quality device region.

**CVD epitaxy process**: Load polished wafers into a horizontal or vertical epitaxial reactor (batch: 10-50 wafers per run, or single-wafer for 300 mm). Heat to 1050-1200°C in hydrogen atmosphere (H₂ carrier gas at 30-100 L/min, atmospheric pressure or reduced pressure at 50-200 Torr). Etch the wafer surface with HCl gas (1-5% in H₂, 2-5 minutes at 1150°C) to remove any residual native oxide and subsurface damage — HCl etches silicon at ~1-3 μm/min at this temperature, removing 2-10 μm. Then introduce silicon source gas: trichlorosilane (SiHCl₃, TCS) at 0.1-1.0% in H₂, or silane (SiH₄) at 0.01-0.1% in H₂. Silicon deposits on the hot wafer surface, growing a single-crystal layer that replicates the substrate crystal structure.

**Growth rates and doping**: TCS at 1100°C: growth rate 0.5-2.0 μm/min. Silane at 900°C: growth rate 0.1-0.5 μm/min (lower temperature, but higher defect density if temperature is too low — "haze" from polycrystalline nucleation). Doping: add phosphine (PH₃) for n-type epi or diborane (B₂H₆) for p-type epi, at 0.01-100 ppm in the gas stream. Dopant concentration in the epi layer is proportional to the dopant gas partial pressure (Henry's law). Typical epi layer thickness: 2-20 μm for CMOS, 50-100 μm for power devices. Epi layer resistivity uniformity: ±5-10% across the wafer, ±5% wafer-to-wafer within a batch.

**Autodoping control**: Dopant atoms from the heavily doped substrate evaporate during the high-temperature epi process and incorporate into the growing epi layer, causing a transition region at the substrate-epi interface where the doping level gradually changes from substrate to epi concentration. This "autodoping tail" extends 0.5-3.0 μm into the epi layer. Minimize by: (1) Using reduced pressure (50-150 Torr) — lower pressure increases the mean free path and sweeps evaporated dopant away from the surface faster. (2) Growing a thin undoped "buffer" layer first (0.5-1.0 μm), then growing the doped epi layer on top. (3) Using lower growth temperature (950-1050°C instead of 1100-1200°C) — reduces dopant evaporation from the substrate but also reduces growth rate.

## Final Inspection and Packaging

**Automated defect inspection**: Inspect each wafer on an automated defect inspection system (laser scanning or brightfield/darkfield imaging). Detect and classify defects: particles (light point defects, LPDs), scratches, haze, crystal-originated particles (COPs — octahedral voids 50-200 nm formed during crystal growth by vacancy aggregation), and stacking faults. Mapping resolution: 0.1-1.0 μm minimum detectable defect size. Each wafer is mapped with defect locations (x,y coordinates) for yield prediction. Rejection criteria: >10 LPDs ≥0.16 μm on 200 mm, >30 LPDs ≥0.12 μm on 300 mm.

**Flatness measurement**: Capacitive or optical (Fizeau interferometer) flatness measurement maps the wafer surface topology at 10,000-100,000 points. Report: site flatness (SFQR — maximum front surface deviation within a fixed site area), global flatness (GBIR — maximum deviation over the entire wafer), and total thickness variation (TTV). For advanced nodes (<100 nm): SFQR <0.10 μm over 25 × 8 mm site, GBIR <3 μm over 300 mm wafer.

**Cleanroom packaging**: Wafers are processed, inspected, and packaged in Class 1-10 cleanrooms (ISO 1-3, <1-10 particles ≥0.1 μm per cubic foot). Each wafer is placed in a single-wafer cassette slot (25-wafer capacity, per SEMI E1.9 standard for 200 mm or SEMI E57 for 300 mm) or in a 25-wafer shipping box (fluoropolymer-coated plastic, sealed in two nested bags with nitrogen purge to prevent oxidation and particle contamination during transport). The wafer is handled only by vacuum wands or Bernoulli grippers (non-contact) on the backside — the polished front surface must never be touched. Shelf life: indefinite when stored properly in sealed nitrogen-purged containers.

## Wafer Thickness and Thinning

**Standard thicknesses**: 150 mm wafers: 625 μm. 200 mm wafers: 725 μm. 300 mm wafers: 775 μm. These thicknesses are set by mechanical handling requirements (a 300 mm wafer at 775 μm thick sags only 50-100 μm under gravity — thin enough to sag uncontrollably would jam in automated wafer handlers). The wafer must be thick enough to resist fracture during handling, thermal cycling (warpage from CTE mismatch between the wafer and deposited films), and vacuum chucking (100-500 mbar suction on the backside for lithography and process tools).

**Backgrinding (wafer thinning)**: For power devices, MEMS, and 3D packaging, wafers are thinned from the backside after front-end processing. Mechanical backgrinding: coarse grind with 400-600 grit diamond wheel (removal rate 5-20 μm/min) to within 20-50 μm of target thickness, then fine grind with 2000-4000 grit (removal rate 0.5-2.0 μm/min) to final thickness +5 μm, then CMP polish (removes 3-5 μm of grinding damage). Minimum achievable thickness by mechanical grinding: 50-100 μm for 200 mm, 75-150 μm for 300 mm (thinner wafers fracture under the grinding load). For extreme thinning (<50 μm): use wet chemical etching (TMAH 25% at 80°C, etch rate 10-30 μm/hour) or atmospheric downstream plasma (ADP) etching (etch rate 5-20 μm/min). Ultra-thin wafers (25-50 μm) are handled on tape frames or temporary bonded to carrier wafers during thinning and subsequent processing.

**Stress relief after grinding**: Mechanical grinding leaves subsurface damage (micro-cracks, residual compressive stress in a 1-5 μm surface layer). If not removed, these cracks propagate during subsequent thermal processing, causing wafer fracture. CMP removes 3-10 μm from the ground surface to eliminate all subsurface damage. Dry etch (SF₆/O₂ plasma, 5-20 μm removal) is an alternative for stress relief — plasma etching is isotropic and damage-free. The combination of coarse grinding → fine grinding → CMP or dry etch produces a thin, flat, stress-free wafer ready for backside processing.

## Gettering

**Purpose**: Metallic impurities (Fe, Cu, Ni, Au) dissolved in silicon create deep-level traps that reduce minority carrier lifetime (from >1000 μs in ultra-pure silicon to <1 μs with 10¹²-10¹³ atoms/cm³ of Fe). Gettering captures these impurities at benign locations away from the active device region (the front surface where transistors are built).

**Intrinsic gettering**: CZ silicon containing 8-15 ppma interstitial oxygen can be heat-treated to precipitate SiO₂ particles inside the wafer bulk (not at the surface). Process: (1) High-low-high cycle: 1100°C for 4-16 hours (dissolves any existing nuclei and out-diffuses oxygen from a 10-30 μm surface "denuded zone"), then 650-750°C for 4-32 hours (nucleates oxygen precipitates in the bulk), then 1000°C for 4-16 hours (grows the precipitates to 50-200 nm). The SiO₂ precipitates act as gettering sites — they attract metallic impurities away from the surface denuded zone. Effective for Fe, Ni, Cu (reduces surface metal concentration by 10-100×). The denuded zone (10-30 μm from each surface) remains precipitate-free and provides a clean region for device fabrication.

**Extrinsic gettering**: Create intentional damage or stress on the wafer backside to attract impurities. Methods: (1) Mechanical back-damage: sandblast or scratch the wafer backside (roughness Ra 0.5-5.0 μm) — the damaged region gettering sites are active at temperatures above 800°C. (2) Phosphorus diffusion gettering: diffuse phosphorus into the backside at 900-1000°C from a POCl₃ source — P-Si pairs form and trap metallic impurities. (3) Polysilicon backside: deposit 1-3 μm of polysilicon on the wafer backside — the grain boundaries getter metals. (4) Argon ion implantation: implant Ar⁺ at 100-200 keV into the backside — the implant damage creates gettering sites. All methods work by creating high-energy sites (dislocations, grain boundaries, or precipitates) that are thermodynamically favorable for metallic impurity segregation.

## SOI (Silicon-on-Insulator) Wafers

**SIMOX (Separation by IMplanted Oxygen)**: Implant oxygen ions (O⁺) at 150-200 keV into a standard silicon wafer at a dose of 1-2 × 10¹⁸ O⁺/cm². The oxygen is implanted at a depth of 100-300 nm below the surface. Anneal at 1300-1350°C for 4-6 hours in argon + 0.5% O₂. The implanted oxygen reacts with silicon to form a continuous buried SiO₂ layer (the "BOX" — Buried OXide, 100-400 nm thick). The silicon above the BOX is the device layer (50-200 nm thick). SIMOX produces a uniform, high-quality BOX but the high-dose implant causes crystal damage in the device layer that requires careful annealing. Implantation cost: $200-500 per wafer (200 mm).

**Bonded SOI**: Thermally oxidize a "handle" wafer (0.5-2.0 μm SiO₂) and a "device" wafer. Clean both wafers in RCA chemistry. Bring the two oxide surfaces into contact at room temperature — van der Waals forces bond the wafers instantly (spontaneous bonding when surfaces are atomically clean and flat, roughness <0.5 nm RMS). Anneal at 800-1100°C for 1-4 hours to convert the weak van der Waals bonds to strong Si-O-Si covalent bonds (bond strength >10 MPa, approaching bulk silica fracture strength). Then thin the device wafer from the backside: coarse grind to within 10-20 μm of target, then CMP to final device layer thickness (10 nm to 100 μm depending on application). Bonded SOI produces thicker, more uniform BOX layers than SIMOX (up to 5 μm BOX), with lower defect density in the device layer.

**Smart Cut process**: The most commercially important SOI wafer production method (developed by LETI/Soitec). Start with a "donor" wafer. Implant hydrogen ions (H⁺) at 50-200 keV through the thermal oxide to a dose of 2-8 × 10¹⁶ H⁺/cm². The hydrogen creates a "smart cut" plane at the implant depth (50-500 nm below the surface). Bond the donor wafer (hydrogen-implanted) to a handle wafer (with or without oxide). Anneal at 400-600°C — hydrogen gas bubbles form at the implant plane, exerting pressure that causes the donor wafer to split along the hydrogen-rich plane, transferring a thin silicon layer onto the handle wafer. The result: a SOI wafer with a device layer of precisely controlled thickness (equal to the hydrogen implant depth). The remaining donor wafer is reclaimed, repolished, and reused (95%+ material utilization). Smart Cut SOI wafer production capacity: >5 million wafers/year (2023).

## Wafer Reclaim

**Purpose**: Test wafers (used for equipment qualification, process development, and monitor wafers) can be reclaimed (stripped of all deposited films and polished) and reused 3-10 times before the wafer becomes too thin for handling. Reclaim saves 50-70% compared to buying new prime wafers.

**Reclaim process**: (1) Strip all films: wet etch or plasma etch to remove photoresist, oxides, nitrides, metals, and polysilicon. Sequential stripping: O₂ plasma ash for resist → HF (5%) for oxide → H₃PO₄ at 160°C for Si₃N₄ → HCl:H₂O₂:H₂O for metals. (2) Surface inspection: verify all films removed, measure residual particles and defects. (3) Light CMP polish: remove 3-10 μm from the wafer surface to eliminate any embedded particles or subsurface damage from previous processing. (4) Final clean and inspection to prime wafer specifications. Reclaim yield: 85-95% per cycle (5-15% loss from breakage, excessive thinning, or unrecoverable defects).

## Wafer Quality Metrics

**Oxygen precipitation**: CZ-grown silicon contains 5-20 ppma interstitial oxygen (from dissolution of the SiO₂ crucible during crystal growth). During wafer processing at 900-1200°C, oxygen can precipitate as SiO₂ particles (50-500 nm) in the wafer bulk. These precipitates are beneficial for intrinsic gettering (see below) but harmful if they form in the device active region (within 10-30 μm of the front surface). Oxygen precipitation is controlled by: (1) Adjusting initial oxygen content (lower oxygen → less precipitation; target 12-16 ppma for balanced gettering). (2) Thermal history of the wafer (rapid thermal processing at >1000°C dissolves precipitates; slow cooling through 650-800°C nucleates them). (3) Denuded zone formation (high-temperature out-diffusion step at 1100°C depletes oxygen from the surface region, preventing precipitation there while allowing bulk precipitation for gettering).

**Metallic contamination limits**: Maximum acceptable surface metal concentrations on prime wafers (measured by TXRF — Total Reflection X-ray Fluorescence, or VPD-ICPMS — Vapor Phase Decomposition Inductively Coupled Plasma Mass Spectrometry): Fe <5 × 10⁹ atoms/cm², Cu <5 × 10⁹, Ni <5 × 10⁹, Al <1 × 10¹⁰, Na <1 × 10¹⁰, Cr <1 × 10¹⁰, Zn <1 × 10¹⁰. At the front-end process line (gate oxidation), limits are 10-100× stricter (<10⁸ atoms/cm² for Fe, Cu, Ni) because even trace metals in the gate oxide reduce breakdown voltage and increase leakage current. Iron is the most problematic contaminant in silicon: Fe interstitials act as deep-level recombination centers (energy level Ev + 0.39 eV), reducing minority carrier lifetime from >1000 μs to <10 μs at 10¹² cm⁻³ Fe concentration.

**Particle specifications**: Prime wafer particle limits (per SEMI M1): 200 mm wafers: ≤10 particles ≥0.12 μm LPD (light point defect) per wafer. 300 mm wafers: ≤30 particles ≥0.09 μm LPD per wafer. Particles are measured by laser surface scanning (darkfield or brightfield, 5-50 mW laser at 400-700 nm, scanning speed 100-500 mm/s). Particle sources: human skin flakes (1-20 μm), stainless steel wear particles from process tools (0.5-5 μm), photoresist residues, silica particles from CMP slurry (20-80 nm). Each process step adds 5-50 particles per wafer on average — a 30-step CMOS process can accumulate 500-1500 particles per wafer. Particle kill ratio: approximately 10-30% of particles ≥0.5 μm that land on active device areas cause yield-limiting defects (shorts, opens, or parameter shifts). Particles <0.1 μm are below the detection limit of current in-line inspection tools but become yield limiters at technology nodes below 28 nm (a 20 nm particle can short a 20 nm gate).

## Wafer Handling and Automation

**FOUP (Front-Opening Unified Pod)**: Standard wafer carrier for 300 mm wafers (SEMI E62 standard). Holds 25 wafers in a sealed, nitrogen-purged enclosure with a front-opening door (interfacing with tool load ports, not the operator). Material: polycarbonate or PEEK shell, carbon-filled for ESD protection (surface resistivity 10⁵-10⁹ Ω/sq). Internal environment: <1% O₂, <1% RH (nitrogen purge prevents native oxide growth on bare silicon). Wafer pitch: 10 mm (center-to-center spacing in the cassette slots). FOUP weight with 25 × 300 mm wafers: ~8 kg. FOUP cost: $300-800 each. An automated 300 mm fab has 5000-20,000 FOUPs in circulation. See [Cleanrooms](../vlsi-scaling/cleanrooms.md).

**SMIF (Standard Mechanical InterFace)**: The 200 mm equivalent — a sealed pod (SEMI E19) that mates with a load port on the process tool. The tool opens the pod door mechanically (not the operator), maintaining the clean environment inside. SMIF pods hold 25 × 200 mm wafers. Operator handles the pod by its handle and places it on the tool load port — the tool takes over from there. Each SMIF pod is opened 30-60 times during a typical CMOS process flow (one open per process step). Pod integrity: <1 particle ≥0.12 μm added per open-close cycle. SMIF pods largely eliminated wafer handling with vacuum wands, reducing particle contamination by 5-10× compared to open-cassette handling.

**Wafer orientation in cassette**: Wafers are loaded into the cassette with the notch (or flat) pointing toward the cassette hinge pin (SEMI standard orientation). The notch must be at a specific angular position (bottom-center for notch wafers, or the flat faces the cassette back for flat wafers). This ensures that the process tool always picks up the wafer with the correct orientation for alignment. The cassette slots are tapered to prevent wafer edge contact with the cassette walls (the wafer rests on two thin rails, contacting only the bottom 2-3 mm of the wafer edge).

## Thermal Processing of Wafers

**Rapid Thermal Processing (RTP)**: Heat a single wafer to 600-1200°C in seconds (ramp rate 50-250°C/s) using an array of halogen lamps (1-2 kW each, 20-200 lamps per system, total power 50-300 kW) above and below the wafer. Temperature measurement: pyrometer (optical, 4-5 μm wavelength, reads wafer backside temperature ±1-3°C accuracy) or thermocouple (type K or S, embedded in the susceptor). Uniformity: ±2-5°C across 300 mm wafer at 1000°C (controlled by lamp zone power adjustment — 4-10 independently controlled zones). Process applications: (1) Implant anneal: activate dopants (dissolve implanted dopant atoms into substitutional lattice sites) at 950-1100°C for 10-30 seconds (short time prevents dopant diffusion — critical for shallow junctions at <100 nm nodes). (2) Gate oxidation: grow 1-5 nm SiO₂ gate oxide at 800-1000°C for 5-60 seconds (dry O₂ ambient at 0.5-2.0 atm). (3) Silicide formation: react deposited metal (Ti, Co, Ni) with silicon at 500-800°C to form low-resistance TiSi₂, CoSi₂, or NiSi contacts.

**Furnace processing (batch)**: Horizontal or vertical tube furnace processes 25-200 wafers simultaneously in a quartz or silicon carbide tube (150-350 mm diameter, 1.5-3.0 m long). Heating elements (SiC or MoSi₂) in 3-5 independently controlled zones maintain temperature uniformity of ±0.5-1.0°C across the batch at 600-1200°C. Ramp rate: 5-20°C/min (much slower than RTP, but processes many wafers per run). Process gases: N₂ (inert), O₂ (oxidation), H₂ (reducing), Ar (inert diluent), HCl (cleaning — volatilizes metallic contaminants). Applications: (1) Field oxidation: grow 300-1000 nm SiO₂ "field oxide" at 950-1050°C in wet O₂ (steam) for 2-8 hours. (2) Gate oxidation: grow 5-50 nm SiO₂ at 800-950°C in dry O₂ for 30-120 minutes. (3) LPCVD deposition: deposit Si₃N₄ (from SiH₂Cl₂ + NH₃ at 750-850°C), polysilicon (from SiH₄ at 600-650°C), or SiO₂ (from TEOS at 650-750°C) on all wafers simultaneously. Tube furnace throughput: 10-50 wafers/hour depending on process time.

## Cost Structure of Wafer Production

**Capital equipment costs** (300 mm prime wafer production line, 100,000 wafers/month capacity): Wire saws ($500K-1.5M each, 5-10 units), lapping machines ($300K-800K each, 3-5 units), CMP polishers ($400K-1M each, 5-10 units), cleaning systems ($300K-1M each, 3-5 units), inspection tools ($500K-2M each, 5-10 units), cleanroom facility ($5K-15K per m², 2000-5000 m² Class 10-100). Total capital investment: $50-200 million. Depreciation: 5-7 years straight-line.

**Operating costs per wafer** (300 mm prime, approximate breakdown): Polysilicon feedstock: $5-10. Crystal growth (energy, consumables): $5-10. Wafering (wire saw, slurry, waste): $5-15. Lapping and CMP (abrasives, pads, chemicals): $5-15. Cleaning (chemicals, UPW): $2-5. Inspection and metrology: $2-5. Cleanroom overhead (HVAC, UPW, N₂, facility): $5-15. Labor: $3-8. Overhead and depreciation: $10-25. Total: $42-108 per 300 mm prime wafer (selling price: $30-80 for commodity, $80-200+ for epi or specialty). Margins are thin for commodity prime wafers and depend heavily on scale and yield.

## Wafer Cleaning Technology

**Ultrapure water (UPW) production**: Wafer cleaning requires water of extreme purity: resistivity 18.2 MΩ·cm (theoretical maximum for pure water at 25°C), total organic carbon (TOC) <1 ppb, particles <1/mL at ≥0.05 μm, dissolved oxygen <10 ppb, silica <1 ppb, metals <10 ppt (parts per trillion) each. UPW production system: municipal water → multimedia filter (5-20 μm) → activated carbon (removes chlorine and organics) → water softener (removes Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺) → reverse osmosis (RO, 95-99% rejection of dissolved ions at 10-20 bar, produces 70-80% permeate) → electrodeionization (EDI, continuous ion exchange + electrodialysis, polishing to <0.1 μS/cm) → mixed-bed ion exchange (final polishing to 18.2 MΩ·cm) → UV sterilization (254 nm, kills bacteria and breaks down TOC) → ultrafiltration (0.01 μm membrane, final particle removal) → distribution loop (continuous recirculation at 2-5 m/s velocity through PVDF or PFA piping, maintained at 20-25°C). UPW consumption: 5-20 L per wafer processed (a fab processing 50,000 wafers/month consumes 5,000-30,000 m³/day of UPW).

**Megasonic cleaning**: Conventional ultrasonic cleaning (20-40 kHz) is too aggressive for patterned wafers — cavitation bubbles (1-50 μm) collapse with sufficient force to damage delicate structures (metal lines, low-k dielectrics). Megasonic cleaning (800-2000 kHz) uses much smaller cavitation bubbles (0.1-1.0 μm) that provide gentle but effective particle removal from submicron features. A megasonic transducer (lead zirconate titanate, PZT, piezoelectric ceramic, 50-150 mm diameter) is mounted on the backside of a quartz tank filled with cleaning solution (dilute SC-1 at 50-60°C). Acoustic power density: 5-20 W/cm². Streaming velocity: 1-5 m/s (acoustic streaming creates a thin boundary layer flow that sweeps particles away from the wafer surface). Particle removal efficiency: >95% for 0.1-1.0 μm particles, <1% feature damage rate. Used after CMP (removes slurry particles from wafer surface and patterned features) and before gate oxidation (critical clean — zero particles tolerated on the gate oxide surface).

**Single-wafer spin cleaning**: Modern 300 mm fabs use single-wafer spin cleaners rather than batch immersion tanks (better particle control, less chemical consumption). The wafer spins at 300-1000 RPM on a vacuum chuck while nozzles dispense cleaning solutions (alternating SC-1, DI water rinse, DHF dilute hydrofluoric acid, DI water rinse, SC-2, final DI water rinse and N₂ spin-dry). Chemical flow: 50-200 mL/min per nozzle. Cycle time: 60-180 seconds per wafer. Particle adders: <2 particles ≥0.09 μm per wafer per clean cycle (compared to 5-20 particles for batch immersion cleaning). Chemical consumption: 30-80% less than batch cleaning (the spinning action uses centrifugal force to spread thin liquid films across the wafer, requiring less volume). The final N₂ spin-dry (pure nitrogen at 20-40 L/min, 3000-5000 RPM spin) removes all water droplets without leaving residue or watermarks (dissolved silica in DI water, if allowed to evaporate, leaves ~1 nm SiO₂ spots on the wafer surface — watermarks).

## Wafer-Level Metrology

**Film thickness measurement**: Ellipsometry: measure the change in polarization of reflected light (Ψ and Δ angles) at 150-800 nm wavelength to determine thin film thickness (1 nm to 10 μm) and refractive index. Spectroscopic ellipsometry (SE) scans 200-800 nm wavelengths, fitting the data to an optical model of the film stack (SiO₂ on Si, Si₃N₄ on SiO₂ on Si, etc.). Accuracy: ±0.1 nm for SiO₂ films >10 nm thick. For ultra-thin gate oxides (1-3 nm), the ellipsometric measurement becomes less certain (the film is much thinner than the wavelength of light) — X-ray reflectometry (XRR) provides better accuracy at these thicknesses (XRR measures the intensity of X-rays reflected at grazing incidence, 0-5°, and fits to a model of electron density vs. depth; accuracy ±0.02 nm for films 1-50 nm).

**Dopant profiling**: Secondary Ion Mass Spectrometry (SIMS): sputter the wafer surface with a focused Cs⁺ or O₂⁺ ion beam (1-10 keV, 1-100 nA, spot size 5-50 μm) and analyze the ejected secondary ions by mass spectrometry (magnetic sector or quadrupole). Depth resolution: 1-5 nm per decade of concentration. Detection limits: B, P, As in Si: 10¹⁴-10¹⁶ atoms/cm³ (ppb to sub-ppb). Sputter rate: 0.5-5.0 nm/s (measured by profilometry of the sputter crater after analysis). A SIMS depth profile of an ion-implanted layer takes 10-60 minutes. Used to verify implant dose and junction depth after annealing. Spreading Resistance Profiling (SRP): press two tungsten carbide probe tips (1-5 μm radius) onto a beveled wafer surface (bevel angle 0.01-1°) and measure the local resistivity at each point along the bevel. Depth resolution: 5-20 nm. Carrier concentration derived from resistivity using Irvin's curves. SRP is destructive but provides direct electrical measurement of the active dopant profile.

**Defect inspection**: Brightfield inspection: illuminate the wafer with broadband light (white light halogen lamp or LED, 400-800 nm) and collect reflected/scattered light with a CCD or CMOS camera. Defects appear as bright or dark spots against the patterned background. Minimum detectable defect size: 50-100 nm (limited by the wavelength of light and pixel resolution). Darkfield inspection: illuminate at a low angle (10-30° from the surface) and collect only scattered light (not the specular reflection). Darkfield is more sensitive to small particles and bumps (which scatter light strongly) but less sensitive to pits and scratches (which scatter weakly). Throughput: 5-20 wafers per hour for full-surface inspection at 0.1 μm sensitivity. Electron beam inspection: scan with a focused electron beam (0.5-2.0 keV, 1-10 nA) and detect secondary electrons. Resolution: 3-10 nm — can detect defects invisible to optical inspection. Throughput: 0.5-2 wafers per hour (very slow — used for sample inspection, not production monitoring). Cost per inspection: $0.50-2.00 per wafer (optical), $5-20 per wafer (electron beam).

## Wafer Shipping and Storage

**Nitrogen-purged packaging**: Polished wafers are extremely sensitive to particle contamination and surface oxidation. Packaging: each 300 mm wafer is placed in an individual slot of a 25-wafer FOUP or wafer shipper (SEMI E57 standard). The shipper is sealed with two nested bags — inner bag (ESD-safe polyethylene, heat-sealed) and outer bag (polyethylene, also heat-sealed). Between the bags, a desiccant packet (silica gel, 5-10 g) absorbs residual moisture. The inner bag is purged with nitrogen (99.999% purity, <5 ppm O₂, <5 ppm H₂O) before sealing — the inert atmosphere prevents native oxide growth on the wafer surface (bare silicon grows ~0.1-0.2 nm/day of SiO₂ in clean, dry air at room temperature; in nitrogen, growth rate is <0.02 nm/day). Shipping box: corrugated cardboard outer container with foam cushioning (25-50 mm thick, 15-30 g/cm² shock absorption rated for 1.5 m drop). Shelf life: 12-24 months from date of manufacture when stored in original sealed packaging at 20-25°C, 40-60% relative humidity. After opening, wafers should be used within 24-72 hours (native oxide growth accelerates once exposed to ambient air).

**Edge exclusion**: Wafer specifications define an "edge exclusion zone" — a ring 2-5 mm from the wafer edge where device fabrication is not performed (edge bead removal in lithography removes photoresist from this zone; etch and deposition uniformity degrade near the edge). For a 300 mm wafer with 3 mm edge exclusion, the usable area is π × (147 mm)² = 67,929 mm² out of π × (150 mm)² = 70,686 mm² total — a 3.9% loss. For 200 mm with 3 mm edge exclusion: usable area is π × (97 mm)² = 29,557 mm² out of π × (100 mm)² = 31,416 mm² — a 5.9% loss. Smaller wafers lose proportionally more area to edge exclusion, one of the economic drivers for migrating to larger wafer diameters (a 300 mm wafer has 2.25× the area of a 200 mm wafer but only 1.8× the edge exclusion loss).

## Wafer Sort and Die Preparation

**Wafer sort (probe test)**: Before dicing, each die on the wafer is electrically tested by probing the bond pads with a probe card (100-10,000 fine tungsten or beryllium-copper needles, each 25-50 μm tip diameter, contacting aluminum bond pads 50-100 μm square). The prober steps across the wafer in a serpentine pattern, testing each die in 0.1-5.0 seconds. Tests include: DC parametric (leakage currents, threshold voltages, breakdown voltages), functional (basic logic or memory operation at low speed), and yield mapping (pass/fail map of the entire wafer — a 300 mm wafer with 10 mm × 10 mm die contains ~700 die; typical yield 70-95% for mature products). Failed die are inked (historically, a physical ink dot) or electronically mapped for exclusion during packaging. Wafer sort throughput: 5-20 wafers per hour per prober (limited by the number of test vectors and probe contact time). Cost: $0.01-0.50 per die depending on test complexity. Die that pass wafer sort proceed to assembly and packaging; die that fail are discarded after dicing (no further processing investment wasted on defective die).

**Dicing (wafer → die)**: Mount the wafer (with active circuits on the front side) face-up on a dicing tape (UV-curable adhesive on a stainless steel or plastic ring frame, 200-300 mm diameter). Dice with a diamond-impregnated resin-bond blade (20-35 μm thick, rotating at 30,000-60,000 RPM) on a precision dicing saw. Blade feed rate: 1-10 mm/s. Cut depth: through the wafer plus 20-50 μm into the tape. Water coolant with 1-5% glycol (prevents blade loading and silicon dust dispersal). Kerf width: 25-40 μm (blade thickness + blade runout). Street width (space between adjacent die reserved for dicing): 50-150 μm (set at the design stage — includes kerf plus margin for blade runout). Dicing damages the die sidewall (micro-cracks and chipping extending 2-10 μm from the cut edge) — this is why streets are wider than the kerf (the damaged zone must not encroach on the active die area). Alternative: laser dicing (nanosecond or picosecond laser at 355-1064 nm, ablates silicon at 0.1-1.0 mm/s) — narrower kerf (2-5 μm), no mechanical damage, but slower and more expensive than blade dicing. After dicing, expand the tape (stretch the adhesive film radially to separate the die by 2-5× the street width for easy pickup). Die size examples: microcontroller 3 × 3 mm, smartphone SoC 8 × 12 mm, high-end CPU 15 × 20 mm, GPU 20 × 30 mm.

**Die attach and wire bonding**: After dicing, individual die are picked from the expanded tape by a die bonder (vacuum collet, 5-20 mN pickup force, placement accuracy ±25-50 μm) and bonded to a leadframe or package substrate with die attach adhesive (silver-filled epoxy for moderate-power devices, thermal conductivity 1-5 W/(m·K), cured at 150-180°C for 30-120 minutes; or eutectic Au-Si solder for high-power devices, 80% Au + 20% Si, mp 363°C, thermal conductivity 50-100 W/(m·K)). Wire bonding connects each die pad to the corresponding package lead with 25 μm gold wire (ball bonding: form a ball on the wire tip with an electric flame-off spark, thermosonically bond the ball to the aluminum pad at 200-250°C, 50-150 mN force, 60-120 kHz ultrasonic energy for 10-30 ms, then loop the wire to the lead and stitch-bond with a wedge). A typical IC has 50-500 wire bonds, each taking 30-100 ms. Wire bond pull strength: >40 mN (25 μm Au wire — tested per MIL-STD-883 Method 2011.7). Wire bond failure modes: pad lift (bond too strong — tears the aluminum pad off the die), heel crack (bond too weak — wire fractures at the bond heel during thermal cycling), and intermetallic growth (Au-Al purple plague, AuAl₂, grows at the bond interface at >200°C over time — increases resistance and eventually causes open circuit). See [Electronics Assembly](../electronics/assembly.md).

**Known good die (KGD)**: For multi-chip modules and system-in-package (SiP) applications, bare die must be fully tested to the same quality level as packaged parts. KGD testing includes: full-speed functional test at the wafer level (requires high-speed probe cards with 10+ GHz bandwidth, impedance-matched to 50 Ω, contact resistance <0.5 Ω per probe), burn-in at 125-150°C under bias for 24-168 hours at the wafer level (wafer-level burn-in, WLBI, using temporary contact or full-wafer probe cards), and hot/cold test at -40°C to +125°C. KGD cost premium: 20-50% over standard die (extensive testing on every die vs. sampling). Without KGD, assembly yield of multi-chip modules degrades multiplicatively — a 4-chip module with 95% good die each has only 0.95⁴ = 81.5% module yield; with KGD at 99.5% confidence, module yield improves to 0.995⁴ = 98.0%. The economic value of KGD increases with the number of die per module.

**Die yield economics**: For a 300 mm wafer costing $50-80, containing ~700 die of 10 × 10 mm each at 80% yield, the cost per known-good die is $50 / (700 × 0.80) = $0.089 — the wafer is the cheapest part of semiconductor manufacturing. The subsequent processing (lithography, etch, deposition, CMP, metrology — 30-50 process steps) adds $200-1000 per wafer for a total of $250-1080. The packaged, tested die then costs $250 / (700 × 0.80) = $0.45 to $1080 / (700 × 0.80) = $1.93 — still remarkably cheap per function. This is why semiconductor manufacturing is fundamentally a yield business: a 10% yield improvement (80% → 90%) on a $1000 wafer saves $1000 × (0.90 - 0.80) / 0.80 = $125 per wafer in effective die cost, worth $12.5 million per year on a 100,000 wafers/month fab.

**Wafer reclaim economics**: A 300 mm prime wafer costs $30-80 new. A reclaimed 300 mm wafer costs $10-25 (after 3-10 reclamation cycles). A typical fab uses 5,000-20,000 test/monitor wafers per month — reclaiming at $10-15 per wafer vs. $50-80 new saves $1-3 million per year. Reclaim yield per cycle: 85-95% (5-15% lost to breakage or excessive thinning). After 5 reclamation cycles, the wafer is ~50-80 μm thinner than new — eventually too thin for reliable vacuum chucking in process tools (<700 μm for 300 mm). The reclaimed wafer market is ~$200-400 million/year globally, dominated by 300 mm (65% of volume) and growing as wafer costs increase.

**Future wafer sizes**: The semiconductor industry periodically evaluates larger wafer sizes to reduce per-die cost. The transition from 200 mm to 300 mm (completed ~2005) reduced per-die cost by ~30%. A proposed 450 mm transition has been studied since 2008 but postponed indefinitely — the estimated $15-30 billion investment in new equipment (crystal pullers, wafering, lithography, inspection) cannot be justified by the diminishing returns. Current consensus: 300 mm will remain the standard for the foreseeable future, with innovation focused on 3D stacking and advanced packaging rather than larger wafers.
