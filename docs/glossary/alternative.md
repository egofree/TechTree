# Alternative

> **Type**: equipment | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, health, measurement, metals, silicon, transport

Potash (K₂CO₃) can be extracted by evaporating lye solution to dryness, then heating the residue in a kiln (calcining) to convert carbonate to oxide (K₂O). K₂O + water = KOH (caustic potash).

## Context in the Tech Tree

The "alternative" label marks substitute materials, methods, or pathways when the primary route is unavailable. In [Lime Production](../ceramics/lime.md), potash provides an alternative alkali to soda ash. In [Sanitation](../health/sanitation.md), alternative disinfection or waste treatment methods are documented. In [Non-Ferrous Metals](../metals/non-ferrous.md), alternative ore processing routes address situations where the primary mineral is absent. Alternative paths are essential for bootstrapping — no single location has every resource, so the tech tree must document workable substitutes.

## Technical Details

Alternative pathways serve as fallback routes in the dependency chain. The canonical example is alkali production: where soda ash (Na₂CO₃) from the Solvay process is unavailable, potash (K₂CO₃) from wood ashes provides a viable alternative. The extraction process involves leaching hardwood ash with water, evaporating the lye solution to dryness, then calcining the residue to convert potassium carbonate to potassium oxide (K₂O), which reacts with water to produce caustic potash (KOH).

Alternative sourcing extends to every domain: alternative fuels (wood gas for petroleum), alternative fibers (nettle for flax), alternative metals (arsenical bronze when tin is scarce), alternative energy sources (wind for water power). The bootstrap strategy treats alternatives not as inferior substitutes but as parallel pathways, each with its own trade-offs in quality, yield, labor, and resource requirements. Documenting alternatives is critical because the bootstrap sequence cannot assume access to optimal feedstocks at every stage.
