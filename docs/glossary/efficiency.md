# Efficiency

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: animals, ceramics, computing, energy, metals, silicon

Cathode current efficiency varies: Cu (acid copper) ~100%, Ni (Watts bath) ~95%, Cr (hard chrome) only 12-25% (most current goes to H₂ evolution). Low-efficiency processes generate large volumes of hydrogen gas — requires excellent ventilation.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Efficiency — the ratio of useful output to total input — is the universal metric that determines whether a technology is worth building. From draft animal harness efficiency (3× improvement from breast strap to collar harness) to steam engine thermal efficiency (1% Newcomen to 40% modern steam turbine) to semiconductor device efficiency, improvements in efficiency unlock new applications and reduce resource requirements. The harness example is especially instructive: the same horse delivers 500 kg draw in a breast strap or 1500-2000 kg in a collar harness — a threefold improvement requiring nothing but knowledge and padded leather.

## Technical Details

In electroplating, cathode current efficiency determines how much of the applied current actually deposits metal versus evolving hydrogen gas. Copper acid plating achieves near-100% efficiency — nearly all current goes to Cu²⁺ reduction. Hard chrome plating wastes 75-88% of current on hydrogen evolution, generating large hydrogen volumes that require ventilation and reducing the effective deposition rate. This efficiency variation directly affects process economics and safety requirements.

In hydropower, turbine efficiency varies by type and operating point. Pelton wheels achieve 85-91% at design flow for high-head applications. Francis turbines reach 80-94% at design flow with excellent speed regulation via wicket gates — making them the dominant choice for hydroelectric generation. Kaplan turbines maintain 80-92% efficiency over a wide flow range due to adjustable blades, suited for low-head, high-volume river installations.

In steam power, the efficiency progression is the central narrative: Newcomen atmospheric engines at ~1% thermal efficiency (enormous coal waste but they pumped water from mines); Watt's separate condenser at ~3-5% (3× improvement enabling factory power); triple-expansion engines at ~8-12% (standard for marine propulsion by 1880); and steam turbines at 30-40% (enabled by superheat and condenser vacuum). Each efficiency increment opened new applications.

In animal draft power, the collar harness converts the same animal's pulling capacity from ~500 kg (breast strap) to 1500-2000 kg by redirecting force from the windpipe to the shoulders. This is the single highest-return investment in the draft power chain.
