# Insulation

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, energy, metals, polymers

Material or construction that prevents unwanted heat flow, electrical conduction, or sound transmission. In the tech tree, the term primarily refers to thermal insulation (preventing heat loss from furnaces, boilers, and distillation columns) and electrical insulation (preventing current leakage from wires, transformers, and coils). Early electrical insulation used cotton thread wrapping, shellac varnish, and rubber solution. Thermal insulation used asbestos, mineral wool, firebrick, and later synthetic foams.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Electrical insulation is essential from the energy domain onward — every generator, motor, transformer, and wire requires insulation between conductors and between conductors and ground. Wire insulation methods progress from simple (dip in rubber solution, wrap with cotton thread) to sophisticated (gutta-percha extrusion for submarine cables, XLPE for modern power cables). Gutta-percha, a natural thermoplastic polymer, was the critical insulation for the first transatlantic telegraph cables because it remained stable submerged in seawater for decades.

Thermal insulation governs the efficiency of every high-temperature process: kilns, furnaces, boilers, steam pipes, and distillation columns. Refractory brick linings, ceramic fiber blankets, and insulating castables reduce heat loss and maintain process temperatures.

## Technical Details

Electrical insulation materials must resist the operating voltage, temperature, and chemical environment. Shellac (insect-derived resin) insulates early motor and transformer windings at moderate temperatures (<120°C). Rubber (natural or synthetic) provides flexible insulation for wires. Gutta-percha extrudes around copper conductors through a die, building up 3-5 mm thickness for submarine cables. The key manufacturing challenge is eliminating air bubbles — even microscopic voids create weak points for electrical breakdown.

Thermal insulation performance is measured by thermal conductivity (k-value, W/m·K). Lower is better: refractory brick ~1.0, ceramic fiber blanket ~0.1, still air ~0.025. Thickness and k-value together determine heat loss rate through the insulation layer.
