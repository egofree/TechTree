### Agriculture & Food

**Grain cultivation**:
- **Crops**: Wheat, barley, rye (temperate); rice, millet (subtropical); maize (Americas).
  Choose based on climate. Wheat and barley are most versatile for bread and brewing.
- **Land preparation**: Clear fields, break soil with digging sticks → hoes (stone blades on handles)
  → iron plows (Metallurgy). One hectare feeds ~4-6 people per year with hand tools.
- **Planting**: Broadcast sowing (scatter seed by hand, rake in) or drill sowing
  (poke holes with dibber, drop seed). Seed rate: ~50-100 kg/hectare for wheat.
- **Harvesting**: Sickle (stone or iron blade, curved handle) cuts grain stalks.
  Threshing (beat with flail to separate grain from chaff). Winnowing (toss in wind, chaff blows away).
- **Yield targets**: 1:5 to 1:15 seed-to-harvest ratio (ancient averages). Modern with no inputs: ~1-2 tonnes/hectare.

**Grain milling**:
- **Saddle quern**: Flat stone base 30-50 cm × 20-30 cm with a hand-held rubbing stone 15-20 cm.
  Operator kneels and pushes the upper stone back and forth over grain spread on the base.
  Output: ~1 kg flour/hour. Extremely labor-intensive.
  The earliest milling method, requiring no specialized tools beyond two suitable stones.
  Stone choice matters: coarse granite or sandstone grips grain better than smooth river stones.
- **Rotary quern**: Two circular stones 30-50 cm in diameter.
  The upper stone rotates on the stationary lower stone.
  A central feed hole drops grain between the stones, and grooves cut into the grinding faces
  channel flour outward to the edge. Output: 1-5 kg flour/hour, roughly a 5× improvement over
  the saddle quern. One person can operate it seated.
  Requires dressed (grooved) stone faces that need periodic re-cutting as they wear smooth.
  The rotary quern is a major ergonomic leap and the basis for all later mechanized milling.
- **Watermill** (if water power available): Overshot water wheel 2-4 m diameter drives a
  horizontal millstone 0.8-1.2 m diameter through a gear train.
  Output: 50-100 kg flour/hour, feeding a whole settlement from one mill.
  Requires [Machine Tools](../machine-tools/machine-tools.md)-level gearing and precision construction.
  Water flow of 100-300 liters/second with 2-4 m head drives the wheel.
  The watermill frees enormous human labor and is one of the highest-return investments
  in a growing community.

**Food preservation**:
- **Drying**: Sun-dry fruit, meat (jerky), grain. Simplest method. Moisture below 15% prevents spoilage.
- **Smoking**: Meat/fish over wood fire at 50-80°C for hours to days. Combined with salting for long-term storage.
- **Salting**: Salt (from sea water evaporation or salt deposits) draws moisture from food. 10-20% salt concentration preserves meat for months.
- **Fermentation**: Vegetables (sauerkraut: 2-3% salt, anaerobic, 1-4 weeks),
  grain (beer: malted barley + water + yeast, 1-2 weeks),
  dairy (yogurt, cheese: lactic acid bacteria coagulate milk proteins).
- **Root cellaring**: Cool (3-10°C), humid (85-95% RH), dark underground storage
  for root vegetables, apples, cabbage. Maintained by ventilation control.

**Irrigation**:
- **Shaduf**: Counterweighted lever arm to lift water ~1-2 m.
  One operator lifts ~2,500 liters/hour.
- **Canal irrigation**: Divert river water through channels. Gravity-fed.
  Requires surveying (leveling) to maintain flow.
- **Bucket chain**: Row of people passing buckets. 5,000+ liters/hour with 10 people.

**Crop rotation (3-field system)**:
- **Rotation**: Divide arable land into three fields.
  Year 1: grain (wheat, rye) → Year 2: legume (beans, peas, lentils, clover)
  → Year 3: fallow (rest or green manure crop plowed under). Cycle repeats.
- **Nitrogen fixation**: Legumes host Rhizobium bacteria in root nodules that fix
  atmospheric nitrogen into soil-available forms.
  A legume crop adds ~40-80 kg nitrogen/hectare.
  Eliminates need for synthetic fertilizer in a 3-field cycle.
- **Benefit**: Maintains soil fertility indefinitely.
  Without rotation, continuous grain cropping depletes soil in 3-5 years,
  yields drop to uneconomic levels.

**Soil management**:
- **Composting**: Pile plant waste, animal manure, and ash in layers (~1 m high).
  Turn every 2-4 weeks. Finished compost in 3-6 months (dark, crumbly, earthy smell).
  Apply 2-5 cm layer to fields before planting.
- **Green manure**: Grow clover, vetch, or rye on fallow field, then plow under while green.
  Adds organic matter and nitrogen. Prevents erosion on idle fields.
- **pH testing**: Boil red cabbage or certain lichens (Roccella) in water to create litmus solution.
  Soil mixed with solution turns red (acidic) or green (alkaline).
  Target pH 6.0-7.0 for most crops. Amend acidic soil with wood ash (raises pH).

**Seed saving**:
- **Selection criteria**: Save seed from the best plants, tallest stalks, largest grain heads,
  earliest maturing, most disease-resistant.
  Mark selected plants before harvest with cordage ties.
- **Drying**: Thresh and dry grain to 8-12% moisture content
  (bite test: seed should crack, not dent).
  Spread thin in sun for 2-3 days or in dry, ventilated area for 1-2 weeks.
- **Storage**: Store in sealed clay pots, woven baskets lined with clay,
  or underground pits sealed with clay. Keep cool, dry, and rodent-proof.
  Viability: wheat/barley ~2-5 years stored properly.

**Pest management**:
- **Companion planting**: Plant aromatic herbs (garlic, marigold, wormwood) among crops
  to deter insects. Beans fix nitrogen for neighboring corn (Three Sisters: corn + beans + squash).
- **Crop timing**: Plant before or after peak pest emergence.
  Early planting lets crops establish before insect populations build.
- **Physical barriers**: Netting over fruit bushes (woven cordage or split reed mats),
  moats around grain stores, ash rings around seedlings to deter slugs.

**Granary construction**:
- **Raised floor**: Build on posts or stone pillars 30-50 cm above ground.
  Provides ventilation and rodent barrier.
  Floor: poles covered with woven mat and clay.
- **Rodent-proofing**: Smooth stone or clay cone caps on support posts
  (rats cannot climb inverted cones). Tight-fitting lid.
  Thatched roof overhangs walls by 30+ cm to shed rain.
- **Capacity**: 2×3 m granary stores ~1-2 tonnes grain
  (1-2 years food for a family). Build multiple for larger communities.

**Beekeeping**: See [Animals → Beekeeping](../animals/beekeeping.md) for hive construction,
colony management, honey harvesting, and beeswax processing.
Bee pollination increases crop yields 20-30%.

**Dairy processing**:
- **Milk collection**: Clay or wooden vessels hold 2-5 liters per milking per cow.
  Strain fresh milk through woven cloth to remove hair and debris.
  Milk spoils within hours at ambient temperature, so begin processing immediately
  or cool in a stream or well.
- **Butter churning**: Fill a clay or wooden churn 1/3 to 1/2 with cream
  (skimmed from milk left standing 12-24 hours).
  Agitate with a wooden plunger for 20-40 minutes until butter granules form
  and separate from buttermilk.
  Yield: 3-5 kg butter per 100 liters milk.
  Wash butter in cold water to remove residual buttermilk (extends shelf life).
  Buttermilk byproduct is drunk fresh or used in baking. Salt butter at 1-3% for longer storage.
- **Cheese making**: Warm milk to 30-35°C.
  Add rennet (extracted from calf stomach lining, 1-2 ml per 10 liters milk)
  or plant rennet (fig sap, thistle flowers).
  Curd forms in 30-60 minutes as milk proteins coagulate.
  Cut curd into small pieces (1-2 cm) with a knife to release whey.
  Drain whey through cloth. Press curd in a wooden or clay mold for 6-24 hours.
  Salt the surface or mix salt into curd before pressing.
  Age 1-12 months in a cool (8-15°C), humid environment.
  Yield: 8-12 kg cheese per 100 liters milk.
  Hard cheeses keep for months to years; fresh cheeses consumed within days.
- **Yogurt**: Heat milk to 85°C for 30 minutes to pasteurize and denature proteins
  for a thicker texture. Cool to 42-45°C.
  Add starter culture (2-3 tablespoons from a previous batch).
  Incubate 4-8 hours at 40-45°C in a warm spot (near a hearth, in a hay-lined box).
  The milk thickens as lactic acid bacteria multiply and acidify it.
  Keeps 1-2 weeks if kept cool.
  If no starter exists, wild lactic bacteria from clean grasses or previous fermentation vessels
  can inoculate a batch, though results vary.
- **Ghee (clarified butter)**: Melt butter slowly at 100-120°C.
  Milk solids sink and brown; skim off any foam on the surface.
  Pour off the clear golden fat, leaving solids behind.
  Ghee keeps 6-12 months without refrigeration because the water and milk proteins
  that support spoilage are removed.
  Widely used in hot climates where butter spoils rapidly.

**Fishing techniques**:
- See [Animals → Aquaculture](../animals/aquaculture.md) for full detail on fish farming,
  pond construction, and managed stocking.
- **Hand lines**: Bone or iron hook (1-5 cm), linen or gut line,
  baited with insects, worms, or small fish.
  Fish from shore, boat, or weir pool. Simple, requires minimal gear.
  A single line catches 1-5 kg of fish per session in productive waters.
- **Woven basket traps**: Willow or reed woven into a cylindrical trap 40-60 cm long
  with a funnel entrance 5-10 cm wide.
  Bait with bread, meat scraps, or fermented grain.
  Fish swim in through the funnel but cannot find the exit.
  Check and empty daily. Effective in streams, ponds, and tidal areas.
  Can be set and left unattended while other work proceeds.
- **Weirs**: Stone or wooden fences built across streams to concentrate fish into pools
  or channels where they can be netted, speared, or trapped.
  Requires communal labor to build but provides reliable harvest.
  Check local regulations and fish migration patterns before constructing.

**Oil extraction**:
- **Seed pressing**: Fill a wooden screw press with oil seeds
  (flax/linseed, sesame, rapeseed, sunflower).
  Apply pressure via a screw mechanism that crushes the seeds against a solid base.
  Oil drains through gaps or a filter into a collection vessel.
  Yield: 25-35% oil by weight for most seeds.
  The remaining seed cake is high-protein animal feed.
  A screw press can be built with [Woodworking](../foundations/woodworking.md)
  and basic [Metallurgy](../metals/metallurgy.md) for the screw thread.
- **Flax/linseed oil**: 30-35% oil content.
  Dual purpose, serving as both food oil and industrial material.
  As a drying oil, it polymerizes on exposure to air, making it useful as a paint binder,
  wood finish, and waterproofing treatment for canvas and leather.
  Food-grade flax oil should be pressed fresh and used within weeks (it turns rancid).
  Industrial-grade oil can be stored longer if kept cool and dark.
- **Animal fat rendering**: See [Animals → Animal Materials](../animals/animal-materials.md)
  for tallow (beef/sheep fat) and lard (pig fat) extraction, clarification, and storage.
  Rendered fats are the primary cooking fat in many cultures and also serve as fuel for lamps,
  raw material for soap making, and leather dressing.

**Seasonal calendar**:
- **Spring**: Prepare soil with compost and ash amendments.
  Plow fields as ground thaws and dries.
  Sow wheat, barley, oats, peas, and beans as soon as soil can be worked.
  Prune fruit trees and grape vines before bud break.
  Lambing and calving season, requiring extra supervision and shelter.
  Repair fences and infrastructure damaged over winter.
  Start seeds in protected beds for transplanting after last frost.
- **Summer**: Weed fields regularly (weeds compete for nutrients, water, and light).
  Monitor for pest damage and apply companion planting or physical barriers as needed.
  Make hay: cut grass meadows with scythe, dry in windrows for 2-3 days,
  turn once, then stack in hayricks or barn.
  First harvests of early vegetables, soft fruit, and legumes.
  Thin root vegetable rows. Maintain irrigation channels during dry spells.
  Shear sheep for wool before hot weather sets in.
- **Autumn**: Main grain harvest.
  Cut grain with sickle, bind into sheaves, stook in fields to dry for 1-2 weeks,
  then thresh and winnow.
  Harvest root vegetables (potatoes, turnips, carrots, beets) before hard frost.
  Pick and store apples and other late fruit.
  Begin preservation: sun-dry fruit, salt meat, ferment vegetables, brew beer from excess grain.
  Press cider and apple juice.
  Sow winter cover crops on harvested fields. Plow under crop residues and green manure.
- **Winter**: Repair and maintain tools: sharpen blades, replace broken handles, mend fences.
  Select and set aside seed grain from the best harvest samples for spring planting.
  Plan next year's crop rotation: decide which fields get grain, legumes, or fallow.
  Maintain infrastructure: clear drainage ditches, repair roof thatch, service irrigation channels.
  Process stored food: mill grain into flour, render fat, make cheese from stored milk.
  Spin wool into yarn during indoor hours.

### Safety & Hazards

- **Grain dust explosions**: Suspended grain dust explodes at 50-100 g/m³.
  Ventilation, dust collection, no ignition sources, brass tools, ground equipment.
- **Machinery entanglement**: Threshers, mills — rotating shafts catch clothing.
  Guard moving parts. Tie back hair. No loose sleeves. Emergency stop within reach.
- **Animal handling**: Large animals can crush or trample.
  Use halters and restraints. Approach from side.
- **Silo hazards**: Crusted grain hides voids — walking on surface leads to engulfment
  and suffocation. Never enter alone.
  Phosphine fumigants are lethal — ventilate thoroughly before entry.
