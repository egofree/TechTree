# Performance

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: energy, transport

Insulation resistance of ~10-100 MΩ/km for new cable, sufficient for telegraph signals over thousands of kilometers using sensitive galvanometers (the mirror galvanometer could detect currents as small as 10⁻¹⁰ amperes). In energy storage, performance is quantified as specific energy (30-40 Wh/kg for flooded lead-acid), cycle life (200-1500 cycles depending on plate design), and self-discharge rate.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Performance specifications define whether a technology meets practical requirements. In [Energy Storage](../energy/storage.md), battery performance determines suitability for applications from automotive starting to off-grid solar. In [Telegraph Communication](../transport/telegraph.md), cable performance (insulation resistance, capacitance, signal attenuation) sets the maximum reliable transmission distance. In [Silicon Production](../silicon/mg-si-production.md), furnace performance metrics — throughput, energy consumption per tonne, and silicon grade — determine economic viability.

## Technical Details

Performance in the tech tree is always measured against specific use requirements, not abstract ideals. A lead-acid battery at 30-40 Wh/kg is wholly inadequate for an electric vehicle but perfectly suited for stationary storage or engine cranking. Submarine telegraph cable with 0.2-0.4 μF/km capacitance limits signaling speed to ~10-15 words per minute over transatlantic distances — acceptable for Morse code but useless for voice.

Key performance trade-offs recur across domains: specific energy vs. cycle life (thinner plates give higher energy but fewer cycles), signal bandwidth vs. distance (higher frequencies attenuate faster in cable), and throughput vs. quality (faster production tolerates wider specification ranges). Understanding these trade-offs is essential for appropriate technology selection during bootstrapping.
