# Vibration

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, health, photolithography

Internal vibrators (poker vibrators, 25-75 mm diameter, 10,000-18,000 vibrations/min) consolidate concrete by eliminating air voids. Insert vibrator vertically, withdraw slowly (25-50 mm/s), overlap radius of influence (100-300 mm). Over-vibration causes segregation; under-vibration leaves honeycombing.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Vibration — oscillatory mechanical motion — appears both as a useful tool and a hazardous exposure in industrial processes. In [Cement](../chemistry/cement.md) and concrete construction, vibration is deliberately applied to consolidate wet concrete, eliminating air voids that would weaken the cured structure. In [Occupational Health](../health/occupational-health.md), vibration from pneumatic tools and heavy machinery is a physical hazard causing hand-arm vibration syndrome (white finger) and whole-body vibration disorders. In [Cleanrooms](../photolithography/cleanrooms.md), vibration must be suppressed below 0.1 μm displacement for precision photolithography equipment.

## Technical Details

Concrete vibration demonstrates intentional use of vibration. Internal (poker) vibrators immerse directly into wet concrete, transmitting high-frequency oscillations (10,000-18,000 vibrations per minute) that fluidize the concrete mix, allowing trapped air to rise and escape. The radius of influence ranges from 100 mm for small vibrators to 300 mm for large units. Proper technique requires vertical insertion, slow withdrawal (25-50 mm/s) to allow concrete to fill the vibrator path, and overlapping insertion points to ensure complete consolidation.

Over-vibration is as harmful as under-vibration: prolonged vibration causes heavy aggregate to sink and cement paste to rise (segregation), creating weak zones. Under-vibration leaves honeycombing — interconnected voids that expose reinforcing steel to corrosion and reduce structural strength.

As a hazard, hand-arm vibration from pneumatic tools at 2.5-5.0 m/s² causes vibration white finger (Raynaud's phenomenon): numbness, blanching, and reduced grip strength. The ISO 5349 standard limits daily exposure, and engineering controls (vibration-damped tools, isolation mounts) reduce transmission.
