# Fluxes

Fluxes are chemical agents that clean metal surfaces by dissolving oxides, enabling metal-to-metal contact for welding, brazing, and soldering. Without flux, surface oxide layers prevent the formation of a sound metallurgical bond. In forge welding, silica sand (SiO₂) serves as the simplest flux—sprinkled on joint surfaces before heating, it melts at roughly 1710°C but dissolves iron oxide (wüstite, FeO) at forge welding temperatures, forming a fluid iron silicate slag that flows out of the joint under hammering.

Borax (sodium borate, Na₂B₄O₇) is more effective than sand. It melts at approximately 743°C and actively dissolves iron oxide. Borax can be applied as powder or as a paste mixed with water. The resulting glassy slag is easily displaced by hammer blows. For aluminum brazing, dedicated fluoride-based fluxes are required because aluminum oxide is exceptionally stable and resists dissolution by conventional fluxes.

In smelting and steelmaking, fluxes (limestone, dolomite, fluorspar) serve a different purpose: they combine with gangue minerals and impurities to form a fluid slag that separates from the molten metal. The slag composition is engineered to absorb phosphorus, sulfur, and silicon while maintaining the correct viscosity and density for clean separation. Flux selection and quantity directly affect metal quality, refractory life, and energy consumption in the furnace.
