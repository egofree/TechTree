# Stainless Steel

> **Type**: equipment | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: gas-handling, health, metals

Corrosion-resistant iron alloys containing minimum 10.5% chromium, which forms a self-healing chromium oxide (Cr₂O₃) passive film on the surface. The standard material for vacuum chambers, medical instruments, and corrosive-environment equipment.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Stainless steel appears in [Vacuum Systems](../gas-handling/vacuum.md) as the standard chamber material (grades 304L and 316L), in [Medical Instruments](../health/medical-instruments.md) for surgical tools and implants, and in [Non-Ferrous Metals](../metals/non-ferrous.md) as a specialty alloy. Its low outgassing rate and corrosion resistance make it indispensable for semiconductor and chemical process equipment.

## Technical Details

Key grades: **304/304L** (18% Cr, 8% Ni, austenitic, general purpose) is the workhorse stainless. **316/316L** adds 2-3% molybdenum for improved chloride pitting resistance — essential for marine and chemical applications. The "L" suffix indicates low carbon (<0.03%) to prevent sensitization (carbide precipitation at grain boundaries during welding, which causes intergranular corrosion).

Passivation removes free iron particles embedded from machining and grinding by immersion in nitric acid (20-50% at 25-60°C for 30 minutes) or citric acid (4-10% at 50-60°C for 30 minutes). This promotes formation of a continuous Cr₂O₃ passive film.

Electropolishing is the ultimate surface treatment: a concentrated sulfuric-phosphoric acid electrolyte dissolves a thin surface layer, enriching chromium at the surface while creating a micro-smooth finish. Outgassing rate: ~10⁻⁸ Torr·L/s·cm² after bake-out — a 10-100× improvement over mechanically polished surfaces.

For vacuum use, internal surfaces are electropolished, cleaned with acetone then isopropyl alcohol, dried with oil-free nitrogen, and baked at 150-400°C during initial pump-down to accelerate outgassing.
