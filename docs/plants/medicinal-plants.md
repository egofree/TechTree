### Overview

Identification, cultivation, preparation, and application of plant species with therapeutic properties for treating illness, injury, and disease. Plants synthesize hundreds of chemical compounds for defense against insects, fungi, and herbivores — many of these compounds have pharmacological effects on humans. Medicinal plants are the foundation of all pharmacology and the earliest healthcare technology available from Year 0.

Basic herbal remedies reduce mortality from infection, fever, pain, and digestive disorders. This directly improves labor productivity and population survival, creating the positive feedback loop that enables specialist labor to develop subsequent technologies. Of the 57 medicinal-use species cataloged in the botanical survey, five are prioritized in the tech tree as representative species covering the major therapeutic categories.

### Active Compound Extraction

Plants store active compounds in different tissues — leaves, stems, roots, bark, flowers, and seeds — each requiring appropriate extraction methods. The method chosen depends on the compound's solubility and heat sensitivity.

**Infusion (tea)**: Pour boiling water over dried or fresh plant material (typically leaves, flowers). Steep 10-15 minutes. Strain through cloth. Extracts water-soluble compounds: tannins, glycosides, some alkaloids. Use within 24 hours — water extracts spoil rapidly. Ratio: 1-2 g dried herb per 150 mL water.

**Decoction**: Boil coarse plant material (roots, bark, seeds, woody stems) in water for 15-30 minutes. Strain. Heat breaks down tough cell walls that infusion cannot penetrate. Suitable for Agrimonia pilosa roots, Artemisia bark preparations. Use within 24 hours or preserve with ethanol (≥15% by volume).

**Tincture (ethanol extraction)**: Macerate dried plant material in 40-70% ethanol for 14+ days, agitating daily. Strain and filter. Ethanol extracts alkaloids, flavonoids, resins, and essential oils that water cannot reach. Tinctures are shelf-stable for 2-5 years. Standard ratio: 1:5 (1 g dried herb per 5 mL solvent). Requires distillation capability for ethanol production.

**Steam distillation**: Pass steam through plant material. Volatile essential oils vaporize and condense with the steam. Separate the oil layer (lighter, floats). Used for aromatic compounds from Artemisia species. Yields typically 0.5-3% by weight. Requires distillation apparatus (glass or metal condenser).

### Preparation & Dosage

**Dosage principles**: Plant preparations vary in potency between harvests, growing conditions, and preparation methods. Start with the lowest dose that produces a therapeutic effect. Increase gradually. Weigh dried herbs on a balance (0.1 g minimum precision) for consistency.

**Harvest timing**: Active compound concentration varies with plant maturity and season. Flowering herbs (e.g., *Artemisia* species) generally peak in volatile oil content just before full bloom. Root crops (e.g., *Agrimonia pilosa*) concentrate medicinal compounds in autumn as above-ground growth dies back. Harvest at the wrong time and potency may be 2-5x lower than peak.

**Delivery methods**:
- **Oral**: Decoctions, infusions, and tinctures taken by mouth. Most common route. First-pass metabolism in the liver reduces bioavailability of some compounds.
- **Topical**: Crushed fresh leaves or poultices (mashed herb applied directly to skin) for wound care, burns, and skin infections. Aloe gel for burns. Arnica for bruising.
- **Inhalation**: Steamed herb vapors for respiratory complaints. Add dried herbs to boiling water, tent head with cloth, breathe vapors for 10-15 minutes.

**Storage**: Dry harvested plant material in a well-ventilated, shaded area (direct sunlight degrades active compounds). Store dried herbs in sealed containers, cool and dark. Label with species name, harvest date, and plant part. Dried herbs remain viable for 1-2 years; tinctures for 2-5 years. Water-based preparations spoil within 24 hours without preservative.

### Safety & Toxicity

**Proper identification is critical**: Many medicinal plants have toxic lookalikes. Positive identification requires verification of leaf shape, flower structure, stem characteristics, and smell. When in doubt, do not harvest. Train multiple people in identification to reduce error.

**Toxicity ranges**: Plant compounds do not have pharmaceutical-grade dose uniformity. The same species can vary 3-10x in potency depending on growing conditions, harvest time, and preparation. Species with narrow therapeutic windows (e.g., cardiac glycosides from Digitalis/foxglove — not in the tech tree but widely known) can be lethal at just 2-3x the therapeutic dose.

**Contraindications**: Pregnant women should avoid most medicinal plant preparations — many contain compounds that stimulate uterine contractions (emmenagogues) or cause developmental harm. Children require proportionally lower doses (weight-based). Individuals with liver or kidney disease metabolize and excrete compounds more slowly — reduce doses.

**Allergic reactions**: Any plant preparation can cause allergic reactions. Test topically on a small skin area and wait 30 minutes before widespread application. For oral preparations, start with half the standard dose on first use.

**Cumulative toxicity**: Some compounds accumulate in the body with repeated dosing. Do not exceed recommended treatment durations. Take breaks between courses (e.g., 2 weeks on, 1 week off) to allow elimination.

### Major Medicinal Plants (Reference Species)

Beyond the five tech-tree catalog species, the following plants are historically critical medicines that any civilization bootstrapping effort must prioritize. These species represent the most important pharmacological discoveries derived from plants:

#### Willow — *Salix alba*, *S. purpurea*, *S. fragilis* (Salicaceae)

**Active compound**: Salicin (a phenolic glycoside), converted in the body to salicylic acid. Willow bark contains 0.5-1.5% salicin by dry weight; *Salix purpurea* (purple osier) contains the highest concentrations (up to 2.3% in young bark).

**Therapeutic use**: Analgesic (pain relief), antipyretic (fever reduction), anti-inflammatory. The prototype for aspirin (acetylsalicylic acid, synthesized 1897 by Bayer from salicin structure).

**Harvesting**: Strip bark from 2-3 year old branches in spring when sap is rising (highest salicin concentration). Dry bark in shade. Young branch bark is richer in salicin than trunk bark. Do not strip bark in rings (girdles and kills the tree) — strip longitudinal strips from pruned branches.

**Preparation and dosage**:
- Decoction: 1-3 g dried bark per 250 mL water, boil 15 minutes. Drink 1 cup 3x daily for fever or pain
- Tincture (1:5 in 30% ethanol): 2-4 mL, 3x daily
- Peak effect: 1-2 hours after ingestion; duration 4-6 hours

**Toxicity**: Low. Gastric irritation at high doses (salicylic acid is harsh on stomach lining — the acetyl derivative aspirin was developed to reduce this). Do not give to children with viral fever (Reye's syndrome risk, same as aspirin). Avoid with bleeding disorders (inhibits platelet aggregation).

**Cultivation**: Fast-growing tree; prefers damp soils near waterways. Propagates easily from cuttings (30 cm hardwood cutting pushed into wet soil in autumn). Coppices vigorously — cut back every 3-5 years for sustained bark harvest. Hardy to -30°C.

#### Foxglove — *Digitalis purpurea* (Plantaginaceae)

**Active compounds**: Cardiac glycosides — digitoxin (0.2-0.4% in dried leaf), gitoxin, and digoxin. These compounds increase force of cardiac contraction while slowing heart rate.

**Therapeutic use**: Treatment of congestive heart failure and atrial fibrillation. One of the most important cardiac drugs in history — still used in modern medicine as digoxin.

**Narrow therapeutic window**: Therapeutic dose is 60-80% of the toxic dose. Signs of toxicity: nausea, vomiting, visual disturbances (yellow-green halos, xanthopsia), bradycardia (heart rate below 60 bpm). Lethal dose: ~5 mg digitoxin for an adult (approximately 2-3 g of dried leaf). **This plant demands precise dosing — train practitioners carefully before use.**

**Harvesting**: Collect leaves from first-year rosette (before flowering stem develops) in late summer. Dried leaf retains activity for 1 year if stored in airtight container away from light. Second-year flowering stem leaves contain lower, more variable glycoside levels.

**Preparation**: Infusion of 0.1-0.2 g dried leaf in 150 mL water (infuse 10 minutes). **Never exceed 0.2 g dried leaf per dose.** Tincture not recommended due to dosing difficulty with variable potency plant material.

**Cultivation**: Biennial; first year forms rosette, second year flowers and dies. Prefers acidic soil (pH 5.0-6.5), partial shade, well-drained conditions. Sow seeds on surface (light-germinated) in spring. Naturalizes readily in temperate climates.

#### Cinchona — *Cinchona officinalis*, *C. ledgeriana*, *C. succirubra* (Rubiaceae)

**Active compound**: Quinine and related quinoline alkaloids. Bark contains 0.5-8% total alkaloids depending on species and age; *C. ledgeriana* bark contains up to 8% quinine. Over 30 alkaloids identified; quinine is the most therapeutically important.

**Therapeutic use**: Antimalarial — kills *Plasmodium* parasites in the blood stage of infection. Also used for leg cramps and as a bitter tonic. Quinine was the sole effective antimalarial from the 17th century until synthetic chloroquine (1940s). Remains the treatment of choice for chloroquine-resistant malaria.

**Dosage**: 500-600 mg quinine sulfate every 8 hours for 7 days (malaria treatment). Therapeutic dose produces "cinchonism": tinnitus, headache, nausea — these reversible effects indicate adequate blood levels. Reduce dose if visual or auditory symptoms become severe.

**Harvesting**: Strip bark from trunk and major branches of trees 6-12 years old. Harvesting is destructive — the tree is felled or heavily pruned. Bark is dried in shade, ground to powder for extraction. Quinine concentration peaks in bark at 8-12 years of age. *C. succirubra* (red cinchona) is hardier but lower in quinine (1-3%); *C. ledgeriana* is highest but more delicate.

**Extraction**: Powder bark, extract with hot water acidulated with dilute sulfuric acid (if available) or tartaric acid from grape residues. Alkalize extract with lime (calcium hydroxide) to precipitate crude quinine. Recrystallize from ethanol for purification. Pure quinine forms white crystals.

**Cultivation**: Evergreen tree, 10-25 m tall. Native to Andean cloud forests at 1,500-3,000 m elevation. Requires cool (15-20°C), humid conditions with 1,500-2,500 mm rainfall. Propagated from seed or cuttings. Can be grown at lower elevations in the tropics but quinine content drops significantly below 1,000 m.

#### Opium Poppy — *Papaver somniferum* (Papaveraceae)

**Active compounds**: Over 40 alkaloids in the latex. Principal: morphine (9-14% of raw opium), codeine (1-3%), thebaine (1-3%), papaverine (0.5-1.5%), noscapine (4-8%). All concentrated in the milky latex exuded from scored seed pods.

**Therapeutic uses**:
- **Morphine**: Most potent natural analgesic. Indispensable for severe pain (surgery, trauma, terminal illness). Effective dose: 5-15 mg orally; duration 4-6 hours
- **Codeine**: Milder analgesic and antitussive (cough suppressant). 15-60 mg orally
- **Papaverine**: Smooth muscle relaxant (vascular spasm, GI cramping)
- **[Raw opium](../glossary/raw-opium.md)** (whole latex): Used as analgesic, antidiarrheal (paregoric)

**Harvesting (lancing)**: When petals fall, the green pod enlarges. Score the unripe pod surface with a multi-bladed lancing knife (3-4 shallow parallel cuts, 1-2 mm deep, not penetrating the pod wall). Latex exudes white, oxidizes to brown overnight. Collect the dried brown latex the following morning with a blunt scraper. Each pod yields 20-50 mg dried opium. A skilled worker processes ~5,000 pods/day. One hectare yields 8-15 kg raw opium.

**Toxicity and addiction**: Morphine causes respiratory depression at 2-3x therapeutic dose; lethal dose ~120-250 mg (naive adult). **Highly addictive** — regular use produces physical dependence within 2-3 weeks. Withdrawal symptoms: agitation, sweating, nausea, muscle pain, insomnia (peaks at 48-72 hours, resolves over 7-10 days). Strict dosage protocols and limited treatment courses are essential. Reserve for severe pain; do not use chronically except in terminal care.

**Cultivation**: Annual, 60-120 cm tall. Grows in temperate to subtropical climates. Sow seeds in early spring on finely prepared seedbed; do not transplant (taproot sensitive). Requires 400-600 mm water; well-drained calcareous soil (pH 6.5-7.5). 90-120 days from sowing to lancing. The poppy is also cultivated for edible seed (0% alkaloid content in seeds) and poppyseed oil (45-50% oil by weight from seeds).

**Storage**: Dried opium stores indefinitely in sealed containers away from light and moisture. Morphine sulfate powder is shelf-stable for years. Tincture of opium (laudanum: 10% opium in ethanol) remains pharmacologically active for decades.

### Plant Collection & Processing Detail

**Harvest timing for maximum potency**:
- **Leaves**: Harvest at peak flowering for maximum alkaloid and volatile oil content — the plant concentrates defense compounds in foliage during reproductive phase to protect flowers and seeds. For example, *Artemisia* species yield 2-3× more essential oil at early flowering than at vegetative stage. Harvest in the morning after dew has dried but before the heat of the day causes volatile oil evaporation.
- **Roots**: Dig in autumn after aerial parts die back, when the plant has translocated storage compounds (starches, alkaloids, glycosides) downward for winter reserves. Spring-harvested roots may contain only 30-50% of autumn peak potency. Wash roots immediately, remove fibrous side roots, and slice longitudinally for even drying.
- **Bark**: Strip in spring when sap rises — the cambium layer is active and bark separates easily from the wood. Spring-harvested willow bark contains 30-50% more salicin than bark stripped in autumn. Make longitudinal cuts (never girdle) and peel strips from pruned branches.
- **Flowers**: Collect just before full bloom when volatile oil content peaks but before pollination triggers metabolic changes that alter composition. Dry rapidly to preserve color and aroma.

**Drying practice**:
- Hang harvested material in loose bundles in a warm (30-40°C), well-ventilated area away from direct sunlight — UV radiation degrades many alkaloids and glycosides. Spread leaves and small stems on wire screens or slatted trays for even airflow around all surfaces. Turn daily. Drying time: 5-10 days depending on tissue thickness and humidity.
- **Endpoint**: Moisture content should reach 8-12% for stable storage. Bend test — stems should snap cleanly (not bend), leaves should crumble when crushed between fingers. Under-dried material (moisture >15%) develops mold in storage; over-dried material becomes brittle and loses volatile compounds.

### Specific Extraction Examples

**Willow bark salicin extraction**:
- Harvest bark from 2-3 year old branches in spring (highest salicin content, 1.0-1.5% by dry weight in *Salix purpurea*). Dry bark in thin strips in shade for 7-10 days. Grind dried bark to coarse powder (1-3 mm particles — fine powder degrades faster). Macerate at a 1:5 ratio (1 g bark to 5 mL solvent) in 25% ethanol for 5 days at room temperature, agitating twice daily. Filter through cloth, then through fine paper. Evaporate the filtrate at 40°C (water bath, not direct heat — salicin degrades above 60°C) to a soft extract consistency. The resulting soft extract contains concentrated salicin suitable for dosing by weight.
- **Dose**: 1-3 g dried bark equivalent per dose for headache or mild pain, providing approximately 60-120 mg salicin (the body converts salicin to salicylic acid, achieving plasma levels comparable to 300-600 mg aspirin). Repeat every 4-6 hours as needed.

**Peppermint oil distillation**:
- Harvest *Mentha × piperita* aerial parts at 10% flower opening (optimal menthol content in leaf oil glands). Steam distill the fresh or wilted herb for 1-2 hours — pass steam through the plant charge in a copper or stainless steel still, condense the vapor in a coiled tube cooled by water, collect the distillate. The essential oil separates from the water condensate by density (oil floats). Yield: 0.5-1.5% by weight of fresh herb.
- **Composition**: Menthol content 30-50% of the oil, with menthone (14-32%), menthyl acetate (2-8%), and 1,8-cineole (3-6%) as secondary constituents. The menthol provides the characteristic cooling sensation (activates TRPM8 cold receptors) and analgesic properties. Peppermint oil is used topically for headache (applied to temples), as a digestive antispasmodic (1-2 drops in warm water), and as an inhalant for sinus congestion.

**Quality control for herbal preparations**:
- Verify plant identity before processing using multiple characters (leaf shape, stem cross-section, flower structure, aroma). Misidentification is the leading cause of adverse reactions. Maintain a pressed herbarium voucher specimen for each batch — if a problem arises later, the voucher confirms the source material.
- Label every container with: botanical name (Latin binomial), plant part, harvest date and location, preparation method, batch number, recommended dose range, and expiration date. This labeling discipline enables traceability and dose consistency across batches — essential when potency varies with growing conditions and harvest timing.
