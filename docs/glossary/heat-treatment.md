# Heat Treatment

Heat treatment is the controlled heating and cooling of materials to alter their physical and mechanical properties without changing their shape. In stone tool making, some cherts improve flaking quality after slow heating to 250–350°C in sand or ash, held at temperature for several hours, then cooled slowly. This heat treatment makes the stone more glass-like and easier to pressure flake, but overheating causes crazing (surface cracks) that ruins the material.

In metallurgy, heat treatment is the primary method for controlling the hardness, strength, ductility, and toughness of metals. Steel heat treatment involves three main processes: hardening (heating above the critical temperature to form austenite, then quenching to form martensite), tempering (reheating quenched steel to reduce brittleness while retaining hardness), and annealing (heating and slow cooling to soften for machining). The specific temperatures and cooling rates depend on the carbon content and alloy composition.

Non-ferrous metals also benefit from heat treatment: aluminum alloys can be solution-treated and aged to increase strength, and copper alloys can be annealed to restore ductility after cold working. The key variables in any heat treatment are the target temperature, the hold time at temperature, the heating rate, and the cooling rate. Temperature uniformity throughout the workpiece is essential—uneven heating produces residual stresses, distortion, and inconsistent properties.
