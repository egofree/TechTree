# Temperature Zones

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, energy, polymers

Material traveling through a cement rotary kiln passes through distinct thermal zones: drying (100-200°C), preheating (200-800°C), calcination (800-1000°C where CaCO₃ decomposes), and the burning zone (1250-1450°C where clinker minerals form).

## Context in the Tech Tree

Temperature zones are the spatial regions within continuous thermal processing equipment, each serving a distinct function in the material transformation. In [Cement](../chemistry/cement.md) manufacture, the rotary kiln's temperature profile — from drying through calcination to clinker formation — determines cement quality and energy efficiency. In [Cooling](../energy/cooling.md) systems, temperature zones in heat exchangers define the thermal gradient that drives heat transfer. In [Thermoplastics](../polymers/thermoplastics.md) processing, barrel temperature zones in extruders control melting, mixing, and homogenization of the polymer melt.

## Technical Details

The cement kiln provides the clearest illustration of temperature zones. A 30-60 m rotary kiln inclined at 3-4° rotates at 1-3 RPM, moving material counter to hot gas flow. The calcination zone (800-1000°C) is the most energy-intensive step: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ requires ΔH = +178 kJ/mol. In modern precalciner kilns, 60-90% of calcination occurs in a separate vessel before material enters the kiln, dramatically reducing kiln length and fuel consumption.

In polymer extruders, three to six independently controlled barrel zones progressively increase temperature from the feed hopper to the die. The feed zone must be cool enough to prevent premature melting (which would cause bridging), while the metering zone near the die must be hot enough for proper melt flow.
