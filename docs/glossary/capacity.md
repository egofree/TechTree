# Capacity

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: animals, ceramics, computing, energy, foundations, knowledge, machine-tools, metals

One 30 m roll of 35 mm microfilm holds approximately 700-1000 pages. A library of 10,000 volumes averaging 200 pages each = 2 million pages = ~2500-3000 rolls of microfilm. Storage: a standard microfilm cabinet drawer holds 30-50 rolls — the entire 10,000-volume library fits in ~60-100 drawers occupying 2-3 m² of floor space.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Capacity measures the maximum output, storage, or throughput of a system. In [Libraries](../knowledge/libraries.md), it quantifies information storage density (microfilm vs. physical volumes). In [Energy Storage](../energy/storage.md), it measures battery ampere-hours and backup power duration. In [Blast Furnace](../metals/blast-furnace.md) production, it defines daily iron output. In [Casting](../machine-tools/casting.md), it limits pour size. Understanding capacity is essential for scaling systems to meet demand at every tech-tree stage.

## Technical Details

Capacity specifications vary by domain but share a common pattern: they define the useful output or storage limit under defined conditions. For lead-acid batteries, capacity is measured in ampere-hours (Ah) at a 20-hour discharge rate — a 100 Ah battery delivers 5A for 20 hours. Higher discharge rates reduce effective capacity (Peukert's law). Energy density of 25-35 Wh/kg sets the weight penalty for stored energy.

For information storage, the microfilm example demonstrates dramatic compression: a 10,000-volume library requiring hundreds of meters of shelving reduces to 2-3 m² of microfilm cabinet floor space. This matters for knowledge preservation in resource-constrained environments.

For metallurgical production, blast furnace capacity scales from 0.5-2 tonnes per day (small charcoal furnace) to 10-50+ tonnes per day (large coke furnace). Cupola furnaces for remelting iron range from 1-30 tonnes per hour. The capacity determines whether the output satisfies downstream demand for castings, structural shapes, and wire.

Capacity planning requires understanding both peak and sustained throughput, derating factors (temperature, duty cycle, feedstock quality), and the relationship between capacity and system scale — larger systems are more efficient per unit output but require proportionally larger capital investment.
