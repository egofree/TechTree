# Effects

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, energy, textiles

Tar saturates the fibers, making the rope water-repellent and rot-resistant. Significantly extends rope service life in marine environments (2-5× untreated rope life). Trade-offs: tarred rope is darker, stiffer, heavier, and rougher on hands. Tar makes rope harder to splice and knot (tar stiffens the strands).

## Context in the Tech Tree

Effects are the measurable consequences of a treatment, process condition, or design choice. Understanding effects — both beneficial and adverse — is essential for process optimization. In rope making, tarring produces water repellency and rot resistance at the cost of stiffness and handling difficulty. In ceramics firing, temperature and atmosphere effects determine the color, strength, and vitrification of the finished ware. In energy systems, the effects of temperature, pressure, and flow rate on efficiency and component life govern operating procedures.

## Technical Details

In rope making, the tarring effect is straightforward: pine tar or coal tar saturates the fiber structure, creating a hydrophobic barrier that prevents moisture absorption and microbial attack. In marine environments, this extends service life from months (untreated hemp) to years (tarred hemp). The trade-offs are equally direct: tar adds weight, reduces flexibility, makes splicing difficult (tar stiffens strands and prevents them from interlocking smoothly), and roughens the surface enough to cause hand injuries during hauling.

In ceramics kiln firing, the effects of atmosphere on the ware are pronounced. Reduction atmosphere (insufficient oxygen, producing CO) converts red iron oxide (Fe₂O₃) to black iron oxide (FeO), changing clay body color from red-brown to gray-black. Oxidation atmosphere maintains the red coloration. Temperature effects determine the degree of vitrification: under-fired ware remains porous and weak; over-fired ware deforms or melts.

In wind turbines, the effects of blade pitch angle, wind speed, and rotational speed on power output follow the relationship P = ½ρAv³Cp, where the power coefficient Cp varies with tip speed ratio. The cubic relationship between wind speed and power means that a 25% increase in wind speed doubles power output — a dramatic effect with direct implications for site selection.

In energy storage, the effects of temperature on battery performance are significant: lead-acid capacity decreases roughly 1% per °C below 25°C. Cold climates require insulated battery enclosures or derated capacity specifications.
