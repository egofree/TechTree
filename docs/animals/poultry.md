### Overview

Poultry farming is the managed raising of domesticated birds for meat, eggs, feathers, and other products. Poultry are the fastest-converting livestock — turning grain and forage into high-quality protein within weeks rather than months. They require less space, less feed, and less infrastructure than any mammalian livestock, making them the first animals a settlement should establish after securing grain production. Seven primary species span a wide range of sizes, temperaments, and specializations, from the universal chicken to the pest-hunting guinea fowl to the self-foraging pigeon.

### Species Profiles

**[Chickens](poultry-chickens.md)** (*Gallus gallus domesticus*):
The universal poultry species. Chickens are productive, adaptable, and easy to manage. Dual-purpose breeds provide both meat (1.5-4.5 kg at 12-16 weeks) and eggs (200-300 per year). They scratch and forage, controlling insects and weeds in orchards and gardens. Manure is nitrogen-rich (1.5% N). The foundation species for any poultry operation — start here.

**[Coturnix Quail](poultry-coturnix.md)** (*Coturnix coturnix japonica*):
The smallest poultry species but among the most prolific. Quail begin laying at 6-7 weeks, producing 250-300 tiny eggs (10-12 g each) per year. Extremely space-efficient — can be raised in stacked cages or small aviaries. Fast generation turnover (egg to egg in 8-10 weeks) enables rapid flock expansion. Best suited for egg production in space-constrained settlements.

**[Ducks](poultry-ducks.md)** (*Anas platyrhynchos domesticus*):
Hardy, disease-resistant, and excellent foragers in wet environments. Ducks produce rich eggs (70-80 g, higher fat content than chicken eggs) and premium meat. They thrive in marshy or riparian areas where chickens struggle. Excellent pest control in rice paddies and irrigated fields. Khaki Campbell and Pekin are the dominant egg and meat breeds respectively.

**[Geese](poultry-geese.md)** (*Anser anser domesticus*):
The largest poultry species, geese are primarily grazing birds that convert grass directly to meat and fat with minimal grain supplementation. Goose fat is a premium cooking fat. Geese are territorial and aggressive — effective as guard animals and weeders in specialty crops (cotton, strawberry, orchard). Seasonal layers (30-60 eggs/year) but long-lived (15-25 years).

**[Turkeys](poultry-turkeys.md)** (*Meleagris gallopavo*):
The largest poultry species raised primarily for meat. Heritage breeds (8-15 kg toms) grow slowly but breed naturally; commercial broad-breasted types reach 15-20 kg but require artificial insemination. Must be separated from chickens due to blackhead disease susceptibility. Require more space and different housing than chickens but yield a premium carcass with distinctive flavor.

**[Guinea Fowl](poultry-guinea-fowl.md)** (*Numida meleagris*):
Hardy African poultry specializing in pest control. A flock of 20-30 birds reduces tick populations by 70-90% on 1-2 hectares. Loud alarm calls serve as guardian warnings for all livestock. Extremely disease-resistant. Produce modest amounts of flavorful meat (1.2-1.6 kg) and small speckled eggs (35-40 g). Free-range specialists that forage 60-80% of their diet. Keets are fragile for the first 2 weeks.

**[Pigeons and Squab](poultry-pigeons.md)** (*Columba livia*):
Self-foraging birds that produce premium squab meat (350-500 g at 26-30 days) with minimal feed input. Pigeon guano is the richest common animal manure (4-5% N, 2-3% P, 1.5-2% K) — worth more as fertilizer than any other poultry product per kilogram. Both parents feed squab via crop milk, enabling rapid growth without external feed inputs. Homing ability enables messenger services over long distances. Minimal infrastructure — a simple loft suffices.

### Shared Principles

**Biosecurity**:
Disease prevention is the foundation of poultry health. Core practices apply across all species:
- Quarantine new birds for 30 days before introducing to existing flocks
- Disinfect housing between flocks — scrape clean, wash, and apply lime wash or lye solution to walls and floors
- Control human traffic — separate footwear and tools for each species area
- Prevent contact with wild birds (disease reservoir for avian influenza, Newcastle disease, and mycoplasma)
- Remove and compost dead birds promptly — cannibalism and scavenging spread disease
- Provide clean water daily — contaminated water is the primary disease vector in poultry operations

**Predator protection**:
All poultry are vulnerable to predation. Layered defense:
- **Housing**: Secure, enclosed structures with wire mesh (1-2 cm openings) on all vents and openings. Solid doors that close and latch at night.
- **Fencing**: Perimeter fencing (wire mesh or electric) around range areas. Electric fence at 10-15 cm and 30-40 cm above ground deters foxes, raccoons, and dogs.
- **Covered runs**: Hawks and owls are significant daytime and nighttime predators respectively. Covered runs or netting over range areas reduce aerial predation.
- **Lock-up at night**: Most ground predators hunt at night. Train birds to return to housing at dusk and close doors securely.
- **Guardian animals**: Guinea fowl alarm calls alert all poultry to danger. Geese are aggressive and territorial, deterring small predators. Livestock guardian dogs protect entire range areas.

**Feed formulation basics**:
Poultry diets center on energy (grains) and protein (legumes, oilseeds, animal protein):
- **Energy sources**: Corn, wheat, oats, barley, sorghum (milo). Corn is highest energy; oats and barley are lower energy but higher fiber.
- **Protein sources**: Soybean meal, peas, fish meal, meat scraps, insects. Growing birds need 20-30% protein; adult layers need 14-18%.
- **Calcium**: Oyster shell or limestone grit, free-choice for layers. Essential for eggshell formation.
- **Grit**: Small stones for gizzard grinding, especially for whole-grain diets.
- **Vitamins and minerals**: Greens (fresh forage), sprouted grains, or supplemental premix. Free-range birds obtain most vitamins from forage; confined birds need supplementation.

Basic starter ration: 50% cracked corn, 25% wheat, 20% soybean meal or peas, 5% supplement (oyster shell, grit, mineral mix). Adjust protein percentage for species and age.

**Incubation**:
Three options across all species:
- **Natural (broody hen)**: A broody hen (chicken, duck, goose) incubates eggs and raises young. Lowest cost, lowest labor, but limited to the hen's capacity (8-15 eggs depending on species). Not all breeds go broody reliably.
- **Artificial incubator**: Temperature 37.5°C, humidity 55-65%, automatic turning. Enables large-scale production independent of broody behavior. Requires reliable heat source and monitoring.
- **Surrogate mothers**: Chicken hens can incubate eggs of smaller species (quail, guinea fowl, some duck eggs). Useful when the target species has poor broody instinct.

**Brooding**:
All poultry chicks need supplemental heat for the first 3-6 weeks:
- Start at 35°C, reduce by 3°C per week until ambient temperature reached
- Draft-free enclosure with solid walls (brooder guard)
- Clean, dry bedding — paper towels for the smallest species (quail, keets) for the first few days
- Accessible feed and water at appropriate height for the species

### Manure Management

Poultry manure is a high-nitrogen soil amendment. Relative concentrations:

| Species | Nitrogen (%) | Phosphorus (%) | Potassium (%) |
|---------|-------------|----------------|---------------|
| Chickens | 1.5-2.0 | 1.0-1.5 | 0.5-0.8 |
| Ducks | 1.0-1.5 | 0.8-1.2 | 0.4-0.6 |
| Geese | 0.8-1.2 | 0.6-0.9 | 0.4-0.6 |
| Turkeys | 1.3-1.5 | 1.0-1.2 | 0.5-0.7 |
| Guinea Fowl | 1.5-1.8 | 1.0-1.3 | 0.5-0.7 |
| Pigeons | 4.0-5.0 | 2.0-3.0 | 1.5-2.0 |

All fresh poultry manure must be composted before garden application (3-6 months with carbon-rich bedding material). Direct application burns plant roots and may transmit pathogens. Pigeon guano is the exception in concentration — it is the most valuable fertilizer and should be collected and processed separately.

### Integration Strategies

**Multi-species range**:
Running multiple poultry species on shared range utilizes different ecological niches:
- Chickens scratch and eat insects, seeds, and vegetation at ground level
- Ducks forage in wet areas, eat slugs and snails that chickens avoid
- Guinea fowl hunt ticks and insects in tall grass and brush edges
- Geese graze grass, keeping pasture short for other species
- Turkeys forage in woodland edges for acorns, seeds, and insects

**Separation requirements**:
- Turkeys must be separated from chickens (blackhead disease)
- Waterfowl (ducks, geese) wet litter rapidly — house separately from land poultry
- Pigeons need dedicated lofts, not poultry housing
- Guinea fowl can share range with chickens but prefer separate roosting

**Sequential use of space**:
Run chickens on range first (scratch and fertilize), follow with crops. Use geese as weeders in established crop rows. Range turkeys on land not used by chickens. Rotate range areas annually to break parasite cycles.

### Safety

**Zoonotic diseases**: All poultry can carry *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter*. Wash hands after handling birds, eggs, or litter. Cook poultry meat and eggs thoroughly. Pregnant women should avoid cleaning poultry litter (listeria risk from contaminated dust).

**Respiratory hazards**: Poultry dust (feather dander, dried droppings, bedding particles) causes respiratory irritation and allergic reactions. Wear a mask when cleaning enclosed poultry housing. Ensure good ventilation in all housing.

**Avian influenza**: Wild waterfowl are natural reservoirs. Prevent contact between domestic poultry and wild birds. Report unusual mortality events immediately. High-pathogenicity strains are dangerous to both birds and humans.
