# Charcoal

> **Type**: equipment | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: energy, mining

→ copper/bronze smelting, iron smelting (1100-1300°C). Coal → steam boilers, coking feedstock (1200-1400°C). Coke → blast furnace iron, silicon reduction (1800-2100°C). Petroleum fractions → internal combustion engines. Synthetic gases → chemical feedstock, flexible fuel supply.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Charcoal is the first high-grade industrial fuel, produced by pyrolysis of hardwood in oxygen-limited conditions. It appears in [Fuel Production](../energy/fuels.md) as the essential bridge fuel that enables metallurgy, and in [Black Powder](../mining/black-powder.md) as a primary ingredient (15% by weight). Charcoal burns at 1000-1300°C with forced draft — hot enough for copper smelting, bronze casting, and bloomery iron production. It is the only solid fuel available before coal mining that can sustain the reducing atmosphere needed for metal extraction.

## Technical Details

Charcoal production involves heating wood to 400-600°C in the absence of oxygen, driving off volatile compounds (water, tar, methanol, acetic acid, combustible gases) and leaving nearly pure carbon. The yield is 20-40% by weight from hardwood — roughly half the wood's original energy content is lost as volatile gases during pyrolysis.

The simplest method is the earth-covered pit: stack hardwood billets, ignite, then cover with earth to restrict air. The fire smolders for 12-48 hours, carbonizing the wood. Batch retorts (iron or steel cylinders) offer better control and higher yield (25-35%): sealed retorts are heated externally, and the volatile gases are burned to sustain the process, making it largely self-heating after initial ignition.

Charcoal properties: nearly pure carbon, calorific value ~29 MJ/kg, burns at 1000-1300°C with forced draft. Porous structure provides excellent surface area for combustion and reduction reactions. Low sulfur and ash content make it suitable for metallurgy without contaminating the metal.

The bootstrap fuel sequence progresses through charcoal to coal, then coke, each offering higher temperatures or greater availability. Charcoal remains the fallback fuel wherever coal deposits are absent — the universal starting point for metallurgy that every civilization independently discovered.
