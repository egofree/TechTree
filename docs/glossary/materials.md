# Materials

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, computing, energy, foundations, gas-handling, health, knowledge, machine-tools, measurement, metals, optics, photolithography, plants

The physical substances from which all artifacts are constructed — metals, ceramics, polymers, glasses, natural fibers, stone, wood, and their composites. Materials are the most cross-cutting concept in the tech tree: every capability depends on specific materials, and the availability of those materials determines what can be built. A hardwood stick, cedar hearth board, sinew bowstring, and stone bearing block are the materials for friction-fire starting. Steel, copper wire, and rubber insulation are the materials for electrical generators.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Materials selection governs every design decision. In the foundations domain, stone, wood, clay, and plant fibers are the available materials. Each additional processing stage unlocks new material classes: ceramics (pottery, refractories, advanced ceramics), metals (copper, bronze, iron, steel, aluminum, tungsten), glass (soda-lime, borosilicate, fused silica), polymers (natural rubber, gutta-percha, thermoplastics, thermosets), and semiconductors (silicon, germanium, compound semiconductors).

The photolithography domain demands the most exacting material specifications: silicon wafers at 99.9999999% purity, photoresist polymers with precise dissolution characteristics, optical glass with homogeneity measured in parts per million, and cleanroom garments that shed no particles. Each of these materials requires its own upstream production chain.

## Technical Details

Material properties relevant to engineering design include mechanical (strength, hardness, ductility, toughness, fatigue resistance), thermal (melting point, thermal conductivity, thermal expansion), chemical (corrosion resistance, reactivity, stability), electrical (conductivity, dielectric strength, magnetic permeability), and optical (refractive index, transparency, absorption). Material selection matches these properties to the application requirements.

The tech tree's material progression follows increasing processing complexity: natural materials (wood, stone, fibers) require only physical shaping. Ceramics require thermal processing (firing). Metals require both thermal extraction (smelting) and mechanical working (forging, rolling, drawing). Polymers require chemical synthesis from petrochemical feedstocks. Semiconductors require ultra-pure materials and nanoscale processing. Each material class enables new capabilities while depending on the preceding classes for its production equipment.
