# Source

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, chemistry, energy, optics, polymers, silicon, textiles, vlsi-scaling

The origin or supply of raw materials, chemicals, and feedstocks used in industrial processes. Identifying reliable sources is the first step in any production chain.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Source identification spans the entire tech tree: [Kilns](../ceramics/kilns.md) need clay and fuel sources, [Lime](../ceramics/lime.md) needs limestone, [Alkalis](../chemistry/alkalis.md) need salt and limestone, [Solvents](../chemistry/solvents.md) need petroleum or coal tar, [Fuels](../energy/fuels.md) need coal or petroleum deposits, [Optical Coatings](../optics/optical-coatings.md) need specific metal compounds, [Natural Rubber](../polymers/natural.md) needs rubber trees, [Crystal Growth](../silicon/crystal-growth.md) needs high-purity polysilicon, [Mg-Si Production](../silicon/mg-si-production.md) needs quartz and carbon, [Fibers](../textiles/fibers.md) need plant or animal sources, and [Advanced Lithography](../vlsi-scaling/advanced-lithography.md) needs photoresist chemicals.

## Technical Details

BTX aromatics (benzene, toluene, xylene) come from coal tar distillation (4-8% BTX in coal tar) or petroleum catalytic reforming (platforming — Pt/Al₂O₃ catalyst at 500°C, 15-30 bar converts naphtha to aromatics + hydrogen). Coal tar was the original source before petroleum refining developed.

Silicon sources: quartz rock (>95% SiO₂) is reduced with carbon in an electric arc furnace at 1900°C to produce metallurgical-grade silicon (98-99% pure). Quartz sand is the most common and widely available source, found on every continent.

Fiber sources: plant fibers (flax, hemp, cotton, jute) from agricultural cultivation; animal fibers (wool, silk) from domesticated species. Each has specific growing conditions, harvesting cycles, and processing requirements.

Fuel sources progress from wood (universally available, low energy density) to charcoal (wood-derived, higher energy density) to coal (geological deposits, highest energy density among solid fuels) to petroleum and natural gas (liquid and gaseous, highest energy density).
