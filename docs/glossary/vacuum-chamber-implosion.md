# Vacuum Chamber Implosion

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: optics, silicon

Glass bell jars and vacuum chambers under vacuum can collapse violently if cracked or chipped. Wrap glass bell jars with tape or mesh for fragmentation retention. Inspect for chips, scratches, and star cracks before every use — never use damaged glassware under vacuum. Metal chambers are preferred for safety.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Vacuum chamber implosion is the catastrophic failure of a vessel under external atmospheric pressure when internal pressure is reduced below the vessel's structural capacity. In [Optical Inspection](../optics/inspection.md) and [Optical Coatings](../optics/optical-coatings.md), vacuum chambers are used for thin-film deposition and testing. In [Basic Devices](../silicon/basic-devices.md), vacuum environments are essential for semiconductor fabrication. The hazard is particularly acute with glass bell jars, which fail suddenly and produce high-velocity glass fragments.

## Technical Details

Atmospheric pressure (101,325 Pa, or ~14.7 psi) exerts enormous force on vacuum chamber walls. A 30 cm diameter glass bell jar at full vacuum sustains approximately 7,200 N (1,600 lbf) of external force distributed over its surface. Any defect — a chip, scratch, or star crack — concentrates stress and can trigger sudden catastrophic failure.

The implosion produces a shockwave as air rushes into the evacuated space, propelling glass fragments outward at high velocity. Fragment retention measures include: wrapping glass bell jars with tape (electrical or fiber-reinforced) or wire mesh to catch fragments; using polycarbonate safety shields around vacuum apparatus; and always wearing face protection during pump-down and venting operations.

Metal vacuum chambers (stainless steel, aluminum) are inherently safer because they yield plastically before failing — a dented chamber is a warning, not a catastrophe. For semiconductor and optical applications requiring glass viewports, laminated or tempered glass provides a measure of fragmentation retention.

Pre-use inspection protocol: examine all glass surfaces under bright light for chips, scratches, star cracks, and internal bubbles. Rotate the vessel to catch reflections that reveal surface defects. Reject any vessel with visible damage.
