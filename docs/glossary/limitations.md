# Limitations

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: animals, chemistry, machine-tools, metals

The practical constraints that bound what a given technology, material, or process can achieve. In the tech tree, limitations define the gap between a theoretically possible design and a buildable one. Wall thickness varies in deep-drawn parts (corners thin out). Draft animals provide only 0.5-1.5 HP sustained. Hand-filed parts cannot achieve better than ±0.1 mm tolerance. Recognizing and working within these limits — or systematically pushing them back — is central to bootstrapping.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Every domain encounters limitations that determine the pace of advancement. Early pottery is limited to shapes achievable by coiling and hand-forming; the potter's wheel removes that constraint but introduces the need for a bearing. Wire drawing is limited by die quality and lubrication; tungsten carbide dies extend wire gauge range. Blast furnace output is limited by air supply; mechanical blowers remove that bottleneck. Each limitation overcome unlocks new capabilities downstream.

In the animals domain, draft power limitations (breast strap harness: ~500 kg per horse; collar harness: ~1500-2000 kg) determine what tasks animals can perform versus where water wheels, windmills, or steam engines become necessary. In powder metallurgy, the inability to melt tungsten (mp 3422°C) in conventional furnaces limits processing to sintering approaches.

## Technical Details

Limitations in manufacturing processes often appear as geometric constraints. Cast parts have minimum wall thickness (3-5 mm for sand castings, 1-2 mm for investment castings) determined by metal fluidity and mold filling. Deep-drawn parts thin at corners proportional to the draw ratio. Forged parts have minimum fillet radii determined by die fill. Each process has a feasible envelope of shapes, sizes, and tolerances.

The bootstrap sequence is essentially a chain of limitations being overcome in order: fire → pottery → metals → machine tools → precision → chemistry → electricity → semiconductors. Each stage's limitations define what the next stage must solve, and the sequence cannot be reordered without specific prerequisites.
