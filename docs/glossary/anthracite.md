# Anthracite

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: energy, foundations

Black with a metallic luster, very hard (scratches glass). Conchoidal fracture (curved, shell-like breakage pattern). Energy density: 32-36 MJ/kg, the highest of any coal. Burns with a short blue flame, very little smoke. Difficult to ignite (requires a hot existing fire to start), but once lit it burns long and clean. Rare, found mainly in specific geological formations (notably Pennsylvania, South Wales, Vietnam).

## Context in the Tech Tree

Anthracite is the highest rank of coal and the hottest-burning solid fuel available before coke. It appears in [Fuel Production](../energy/fuels.md) as the pinnacle of the coal progression (lignite → bituminous → anthracite) and in [Fire-Making](../foundations/fire.md) as a high-temperature fuel for advanced hearths. Its rarity limits industrial use, but where deposits exist, anthracite provides clean, intense heat for metallurgy, steam generation, and lime burning without the smoke and volatiles of lower-rank coals.

## Technical Details

Anthracite is metamorphosed coal with carbon content above 86-98% and very low volatile matter (2-8%). Its energy density of 32-36 MJ/kg exceeds all other coals and approaches charcoal (~29 MJ/kg) in heating value, while being denser and more compact. The near-absence of volatiles means anthracite burns with a short, blue, nearly smokeless flame — ideal for applications where smoke contamination is unacceptable.

The difficulty of igniting anthracite is its primary drawback. It requires a hot existing fire (wood or kindling) to reach ignition temperature, and sustained draft to maintain combustion. Once established, however, anthracite fires burn steadily for many hours with minimal tending. This made it the preferred domestic heating fuel in coal-bearing regions during the 19th and early 20th centuries.

For bootstrapping industry, anthracite's value lies in its cleanliness and heat output. It can fire kilns and furnaces without contaminating products with tar or sulfur (sulfur content is typically <1%). However, its geological rarity — concentrated in a handful of formations worldwide — means the tech tree cannot depend on it. Where available, anthracite accelerates the transition from wood/charcoal to mineral fuels; where absent, bituminous coal and coke carry the same role.
