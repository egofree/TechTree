# Lining

> **Type**: material | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: energy, health, mining, metals

A protective or functional interior surface applied to vessels, pipes, shafts, and chambers to resist chemical attack, thermal degradation, or mechanical wear. In metallurgy, furnace linings of refractory brick (magnesite, dolomite, alumina) protect the steel shell from molten metal and slag at 1500-2000°C. In mining, shaft lining with timber, stone, or concrete prevents rock collapse. In chemical processing, lead or rubber linings protect vessels from acid corrosion.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Lining is fundamental to every high-temperature and corrosive process. The electric arc furnace uses basic refractory lining (magnesite brick or rammed dolomite, 300-500 mm thick) to contain molten steel. The blast furnace uses a graded lining — carbon or silicon carbide in the hearth, alumina or firebrick in the stack. Kilns for lime, cement, and ceramics use firebrick linings rated for their operating temperature. The submerged arc furnace for silicon production uses carbon or graphite refractory to withstand reducing conditions at 2000°C.

Shaft lining in mining follows the same structural principle: as the shaft deepens past weathered surface rock into solid ground, temporary timber lining gives way to permanent concrete or stone lining for long-term stability.

## Technical Details

Refractory lining selection matches the chemical environment. Basic linings (MgO, dolomite) resist basic slags in steelmaking. Acidic linings (SiO₂) suit glass melting. Neutral linings (Al₂O₃, chromite) work for specific applications. Carbon linings withstand reducing conditions where oxides would be consumed. Lining thickness ranges from 150 mm for small furnaces to 500+ mm for large steelmaking vessels.

Lining failure modes include thermal spalling (rapid temperature changes crack the refractory), chemical attack (slag components dissolve the brick), and mechanical erosion (molten metal flow wears the hot face). Preheating new linings slowly (50-100°C/hour) prevents thermal shock. Monitoring lining thickness with periodic inspections and measuring the external shell temperature identifies wear before breakthrough occurs.
