# Phase 1: Foundations — Surplus & Basics

**Timeline**: Years 0–10+
**Goal**: Food security, population/labor growth, and basic materials extraction/processing.
**Motto**: No surplus = no industry.
**Dependencies**: None — this is the starting point.

## Objectives

- Establish reliable food production to support specialist labor
- Produce charcoal for metallurgy and energy
- Develop basic tool-making from stone and wood
- Begin mining and prospecting for critical raw materials
- Create containers and early refractories from clay

## Key Technologies

### Fire-Making

**Friction fire (bow drill method)**:
- **Materials**: Straight hardwood stick (hearth board: cedar, cottonwood, or willow; drill: harder wood like oak or maple), bow (curved stick with cordage from animal sinew or plant fiber), bearing block (stone or hardwood with grease-filled depression)
- **Process**: Cut V-notch in hearth board, place tinder bundle (dry grass, bark shavings, bird nest material) underneath. Wrap bowstring once around drill. Press bearing block on top, bow back and forth. Ember forms in 30-90 seconds with practice. Transfer ember to tinder, blow into flame.
- **Critical detail**: Hearth board must be dry. Drill diameter ~1-2 cm. Bow length ~60-80 cm. Cordage tension must grip drill without stalling.

**Flint-and-steel (faster, requires preparation)**:
- **Materials**: High-carbon steel striker (wrought iron works poorly; needs ~0.5-1% carbon), flint or chert, char cloth (cotton linen heated in sealed tin until carbonized), tinder
- **Process**: Strike steel edge against flint at glancing angle. Sparks (~800°C) land on char cloth, which glows. Fold into tinder bundle and blow to flame.
- **Char cloth production**: Place 5×5 cm cotton squares in sealed tin with small vent hole. Heat tin in fire for 5-10 minutes until smoke stops. Cloth turns black but retains fiber structure. Store dry.

### Charcoal Production

Charcoal is the primary fuel for all metallurgy until coke arrives in Phase 4. Every kg of iron requires roughly 1-2 kg of charcoal.

**Earth-covered pit method (simplest)**:
- **Site selection**: Level ground, away from structures, downwind. Clay soil preferred (better seal).
- **Wood preparation**: Hardwood (oak, hickory, maple) gives denser, hotter charcoal. Cut to uniform lengths (~30-60 cm), split to ~5-10 cm diameter. Season 2-6 months (green wood produces more smoke, less charcoal). Stack ~50-200 kg per burn.
- **Pit construction**: Dig shallow pit 60-100 cm deep, 1-2 m diameter, sloping sides. Or use flat ground with earth mound.
- **Stacking**: Stand wood vertically in tight formation around central firing pole (5 cm diameter dry stick). Thickest pieces in center, thinner pieces outside. Leave air channels.
- **Firing**: Remove central pole, drop burning tinder into the hole. Wood ignites from center outward.
- **Controlled burn (CRITICAL)**: Cover partially with earth/leaves/sod once well lit. Leave small vent holes. The goal is *partial* combustion — enough heat to drive off water and volatiles (pyrolysis) but not so much that the wood burns to ash. Smoke transitions from white (steam) to yellow-brown (tar) to thin blue (nearly done).
- **Timing**: 12-48 hours depending on size. Small pits: ~12-24 hours. Large mound kilns: 24-48+ hours.
- **Cooling**: Seal all vents with earth. Let cool 24-48 hours. Opening too early causes reignition and loss.
- **Yield**: ~20-35% by weight (softwood less, hardwood more). ~100 kg wood → ~25 kg charcoal.
- **Quality indicators**: Charcoal should ring when struck, be lightweight, black throughout with retained wood grain structure. Brittle, easily crushed = underburned. White ash = overburned.

**Simple kiln method (higher yield, reusable)**:
- Build cylindrical stone or clay structure (~1 m tall, ~80 cm diameter) with grate at bottom and opening at top.
- Load wood from top, ignite from bottom through grate.
- Control air intake with adjustable door at base.
- Cover top with iron sheet or stone slab when smoking properly.
- Yield improves to ~30-40% by weight. Can be reused hundreds of times.

### Stone & Wood Tools

**Knapping (flint/obsidian/chert)**:
- **Hard hammer percussion**: Round cobble hammerstone (quartzite, granite, 200-500 g) struck against core to remove large flakes. Produces crude blanks.
- **Soft hammer percussion**: Antler billet or hardwood club for finer flake removal. Thinner, more controlled flakes.
- **Pressure flaking**: Antler tine or copper-tipped tool pressed against edge to remove tiny flakes for final shaping and sharpening. Produces edges sharper than surgical steel scalpels (obsidian ~3 nm edge width vs steel ~500 nm).
- **Tool types**: Hand axes (bifacial), scrapers (hide processing, wood shaping), projectile points (hunting), drill bits (rotating between hands or with bow drill attachment), burins (engraving, scoring).

**Ground stone tools**:
- **Pecking**: Repeatedly striking granite, basalt, or greenstone with hammerstone to rough out shape.
- **Grinding**: Rub against flat abrasive stone (sandstone) with water and sand slurry. Hours of grinding produce polished, sharp edges.
- **Tool types**: Axes (grooved for handle attachment), adzes (woodworking), mortars and pestles (grain processing), manos and metates (grain grinding).

**Wooden tools and implements**:
- **Hafting**: Attach stone heads to wooden handles using sinew, rawhide, or plant-fiber cordage, secured with pine pitch or birch tar adhesive.
- **Birch tar adhesive**: Heat birch bark in sealed container (similar to char cloth method) at 300-400°C. Black, sticky tar forms. Roll into balls. Soften with heat to apply. Strongest natural adhesive available.
- **Handles**: Split green wood, shape with stone tools, dry slowly to prevent checking.
- **Digging sticks**: Fire-harden tip of hardwood stick (heat until brown, not black) for soil penetration.

### Pottery & Kilns

Pottery provides containers for storage, transport, cooking, and — critically — refractory vessels for metallurgy and chemistry.

**Clay identification and processing**:
- **Source**: River banks, lake shores, floodplains. Clay = earth that becomes plastic and holds shape when wet, hardens permanently when fired.
- **Test**: Roll clay into pencil-thick coil, bend around finger. If it bends without cracking, it has adequate plasticity.
- **Processing steps**:
  1. **Dry and crush**: Break dried clay into powder. Remove stones, roots, organic matter.
  2. **Slake**: Soak in water for 24-48 hours until completely dissolved into slurry.
  3. **Screen**: Push through coarse screen (4-6 mesh) to remove remaining debris.
  4. **Settle and decant**: Let heavy particles settle, pour off fine clay suspension. Repeat for purity.
  5. **Dewater**: Pour onto absorbent surface (plaster slab, dry wood, canvas) until workable consistency.
  6. **Wedging**: Knead clay on flat surface to remove air bubbles and homogenize. 50-100 kneads. Air bubbles cause explosions during firing.

**Tempering (prevents cracking during drying and firing)**:
- **Grog**: Crushed, fired pottery (20-30% by volume). Best all-purpose temper.
- **Sand**: Clean, angular sand (15-25%). Easy to find, works well.
- **Crushed shell**: 10-20%. Calcine shells first (heat to 600°C, crush).
- **Organic**: Chaff, sawdust, shredded dung (burns out during firing, creates porous ware — good for water cooling, bad for holding liquids).

**Forming methods**:
- **Pinch pots**: Start with ball of clay, press thumb into center, pinch walls to even thickness (~6-8 mm). Good for small bowls, cups. Size limited to ~15 cm.
- **Coil building**: Roll clay into ropes (1-2 cm diameter). Build walls by stacking coils, blending each into the one below with smoothing tool. Allows large vessels (30+ cm). Most versatile early method.
- **Slab building**: Roll clay to uniform thickness (6-10 mm) with rolling pin. Cut and join slabs with slip (clay-water paste). Good for rectangular forms.
- **Mold pressing**: Press clay into/over existing form (basket, carved wood, previously fired pot). Ensures consistency.

**Drying**:
- Air dry slowly for 3-7 days (longer for thick walls). Cover loosely to prevent uneven drying. Rapid drying causes cracking. Bone-dry clay is lighter in color and cool to touch.

**Simple updraft kiln construction**:
- **Scove kiln (simplest firing structure)**: Stack dried pots upside-down in pyramid, surround with fuel (wood, dung), cover with sherds/broken pottery and earth. Light from bottom. Reaches ~600-800°C. Unreliable but requires zero construction.
- **Pit kiln**: Dig pit 30-60 cm deep. Line with stones. Load pots, cover with fuel and earth. More insulation = higher temperatures.
- **Simple updraft kiln (first purpose-built kiln)**:
  - **Construction**: Build box from mud bricks or packed earth, ~60 cm cube, with firebox opening at bottom and chimney opening at top. Place grate (clay bars or iron rods later) between firebox and ware chamber.
  - **Firing cycle**:
    1. **Water smoking (100-200°C)**: Gentle heat for 1-2 hours. Steam drives out. Too fast = explosion. Ware turns from dark to lighter.
    2. **Oxidation (350-600°C)**: Increase heat steadily. Organic matter burns out (smoke, smell). Clay minerals begin to transform.
    3. **Sintering (600-900°C)**: Maximum heat. Clay particles fuse (vitrification begins ~800°C for earthenware). Hold 30-60 minutes.
    4. **Cooling**: Seal kiln, cool slowly over 4-12 hours. Rapid cooling = cracking.
  - **Temperature indicators**: Smoke color, color of pots (red-orange glow visible through spy hole ≈ 800°C), color of chimney gases, Seger cones (pyramids of known melting clay placed inside — they slump at rated temperature).
  - **Fuel consumption**: ~2-4 kg wood per kg of pottery.

**Early refractory clay linings**:
- Fire clay (kaolin-rich, high alumina, low iron) withstands 1200-1400°C+ when fired. Found near coal measures, in weathered granite, or as residual clay deposits.
- For furnace linings: mix fire clay with 30-50% grog (crushed fired fire clay), form into shape, dry slowly, fire. Produces linings that survive multiple smelting cycles.

### Agriculture & Food

**Grain cultivation**:
- **Crops**: Wheat, barley, rye (temperate); rice, millet (subtropical); maize (Americas). Choose based on climate. Wheat and barley are most versatile for bread and brewing.
- **Land preparation**: Clear fields, break soil with digging sticks → hoes (stone blades on handles) → iron plows (Phase 2). One hectare feeds ~4-6 people per year with hand tools.
- **Planting**: Broadcast sowing (scatter seed by hand, rake in) or drill sowing (poke holes with dibber, drop seed). Seed rate: ~50-100 kg/hectare for wheat.
- **Harvesting**: Sickle (stone or iron blade, curved handle) cuts grain stalks. Threshing (beat with flail to separate grain from chaff). Winnowing (toss in wind — chaff blows away).
- **Yield targets**: 1:5 to 1:15 seed-to-harvest ratio (ancient averages). Modern with no inputs: ~1-2 tonnes/hectare.

**Animal domestication**:
- **Cattle**: Draft power (plowing, cart pulling), milk, meat, leather. Needs ~2 hectares pasture per animal.
- **Sheep/goats**: Milk, meat, wool (sheep), hides. Hardy, browse poor vegetation. ~0.5 hectare each.
- **Pigs**: Meat, fat (tallow for lubricants, soap). Eat waste, forage. Very efficient feed-to-meat ratio.
- **Poultry**: Eggs, meat, pest control. Minimal feed requirements.
- **Horses/oxen**: Primary motive power before steam. Oxen are stronger for plowing; horses are faster for transport. Both require ~5-10 kg forage daily.

**Food preservation**:
- **Drying**: Sun-dry fruit, meat (jerky), grain. Simplest method. Moisture below 15% prevents spoilage.
- **Smoking**: Meat/fish over wood fire at 50-80°C for hours to days. Combined with salting for long-term storage.
- **Salting**: Salt (from sea water evaporation or salt deposits) draws moisture from food. 10-20% salt concentration preserves meat for months.
- **Fermentation**: Vegetables (sauerkraut: 2-3% salt, anaerobic, 1-4 weeks), grain (beer: malted barley + water + yeast, 1-2 weeks), dairy (yogurt, cheese: lactic acid bacteria coagulate milk proteins).
- **Root cellaring**: Cool (3-10°C), humid (85-95% RH), dark underground storage for root vegetables, apples, cabbage. Maintained by ventilation control.

**Irrigation**:
- **Shaduf**: Counterweighted lever arm to lift water ~1-2 m. One operator lifts ~2,500 liters/hour.
- **Canal irrigation**: Divert river water through channels. Gravity-fed. Requires surveying (leveling) to maintain flow.
- **Bucket chain**: Row of people passing buckets. 5,000+ liters/hour with 10 people.

### Mining & Prospecting

**Surface prospecting**:
- **Visual indicators**: Malachite (green) and azurite (blue) stains on rock = copper ore. Red/rusty soil = iron ore. White/clear crystals = quartz. Yellow crystals = sulfur. Purple/green fluorite = fluorite. Black heavy sand = magnetite (iron).
- **Stream panning**: Pan stream gravels to concentrate heavy minerals. Gold (yellow, malleable), cassiterite (black, heavy = tin), magnetite (black, magnetic = iron), garnet (red = indicator of metamorphic deposits).
- **Streak test**: Rub mineral on unglazed porcelain. Hematite streaks red-brown (iron). Magnetite streaks black (iron). Pyrite streaks greenish-black (sulfur/iron).
- **Magnet test**: Magnetite (Fe₃O₄) is naturally magnetic. Attracted to lodestone or magnet. Iron indicator.

**Surface mining**:
- **Placer mining (stream deposits)**: Pan, sluice, or riffle-box for heavy minerals in stream gravels. No tools beyond shovel and pan.
- **Open-cut mining**: Strip vegetation and soil from exposed ore body. Break rock with fire-setting (heat rock face with fire, dash with water to crack) and stone/iron wedges.
- **Shallow pits**: Dig vertically 2-5 m into exposed ore. Timber shoring at 1-2 m intervals prevents collapse. Width: minimum 1.5 m for one worker.

**Ore identification reference**:
| Ore | Appearance | Source Of | Key Test |
|-----|-----------|----------|----------|
| Malachite | Bright green, banded | Copper | Dissolves in acid (green solution) |
| Azurite | Deep blue crystals | Copper | Same as malachite |
| Chalcopyrite | Brass-yellow, greenish tinge | Copper | Streak greenish-black |
| Hematite | Reddish, earthy or metallic | Iron | Red streak |
| Magnetite | Black, metallic, heavy | Iron | Magnetic |
| Cassiterite | Black/brown, very heavy | Tin | High specific gravity (7.0) |
| Fluorite | Purple/green/clear cubes | Fluorine | Fluoresces under UV, scratches calcite |
| Galena | Silver-gray, metallic cubes | Lead | Very heavy (SG 7.5), cubic cleavage |
| Pyrite | Brass-yellow ("fool's gold") | Sulfur/Iron | Streak greenish-black, brittle |

## Key Bootstrap Loop

```
Food surplus → specialist labor → better tools → more food → more specialists
Charcoal + clay → better kilns → hotter fires → better pottery → crucibles → Phase 2 metallurgy
```

## Practical Bottlenecks

- **Population growth**: Must be fast enough to create surplus labor for non-food-producing specialists. Target: 10-20% of population as specialists by Year 5.
- **Resource location**: Must find area with access to diverse minerals (quartz, iron, copper, coal, limestone, clay, sulfur). River valley with exposed geology is ideal.
- **Knowledge preservation**: Writing system needed early to pass techniques to next generation (see [Side Quest 1](../side-quests/sq-01-knowledge-preservation.md)).
- **Charcoal production rate**: Smelting 1 kg of iron requires 1-2 kg of charcoal. A single smithy might burn 5-20 kg of charcoal per day. Plan woodlots for sustainable supply (coppicing: cut trees at ground level, regrowth in 5-15 years).

## Safety Concerns

- **Mine collapses**: Shallow pits need timber shoring. Never mine alone. Test walls before undercutting.
- **Kiln/fire burns**: Use long tongs, leather aprons, gloves. Clear area of combustibles within 3 m.
- **Charcoal production CO**: Carbon monoxide is colorless, odorless, deadly. Never lean over smoking charcoal pit. Work upwind. Headache, dizziness, nausea = get to fresh air immediately.
- **Charcoal production fire risk**: Burning pits can spread to surrounding vegetation. Maintain firebreaks. Have water and dirt ready for suppression.
