# Briquetting

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, energy, metals

Loose biomass (sawdust, agricultural residues, wood chips) can be compacted into dense briquettes (40-80 mm diameter × 50-150 mm long, density 0.8-1.2 g/cm³) using a piston press or screw extruder at 100-200 MPa without binder (lignin softens at ~100°C under pressure, acting as a natural binder). Briquettes have uniform size, low moisture (<10%), and higher energy density than loose biomass. Suitable for gasifiers requiring consistent fuel dimensions.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Briquetting transforms loose, low-density biomass or coal fines into dense, uniform fuel blocks suitable for controlled combustion. In [Coal](../energy/fuels/coal.md), briquetting converts coal fines (waste from mining and handling) into usable fuel. ## Technical Details

Briquetting exploits lignin's thermoplastic behavior. At pressures of 100-200 MPa, the friction-generated heat (~100°C) softens lignin in biomass, which acts as a natural binder upon cooling. No external binder is needed for woody biomass. The resulting briquettes measure 40-80 mm in diameter by 50-150 mm long, with densities of 0.8-1.2 g/cm³ — roughly 4-6× the bulk density of loose sawdust.

The equipment is straightforward: a piston press or screw extruder forces biomass through a tapered die. Piston presses produce cylindrical briquettes at 0.5-2 tonnes/hour. Screw extruders produce continuous logs that are cut to length. Both require a hardened steel die and a robust frame to withstand the extreme pressures.

Briquettes solve a critical problem for gasifiers: fuel uniformity. A shaft gasifier requires consistent piece size (2-5 cm for small units) to maintain even gas flow through the fuel bed. Loose sawdust or irregular chips create channels and blockages that destabilize the gasification reaction. Uniform briquettes ensure predictable permeability, consistent gas quality, and reliable engine operation.
