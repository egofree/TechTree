### Overview

Poultry farming is the managed raising of domesticated birds for meat, eggs, feathers, and other products. Poultry are the fastest-converting livestock — turning grain and forage into high-quality protein within weeks rather than months. They require less space, less feed, and less infrastructure than any mammalian livestock, making them the first animals a settlement should establish after securing grain production. Seven primary species span a wide range of sizes, temperaments, and specializations, from the universal chicken to the pest-hunting guinea fowl to the self-foraging pigeon.

### Species Profiles

**[Chickens](poultry-chickens.md)** (*Gallus gallus domesticus*):
The universal poultry species. Chickens are productive, adaptable, and easy to manage. Dual-purpose breeds provide both meat (1.5-4.5 kg at 12-16 weeks) and eggs (200-300 per year). They scratch and forage, controlling insects and weeds in orchards and gardens. Manure is nitrogen-rich (1.5% N). The foundation species for any poultry operation — start here.

**[Coturnix Quail](poultry-coturnix.md)** (*Coturnix coturnix japonica*):
The smallest poultry species but among the most prolific. Quail begin laying at 6-7 weeks, producing 250-300 tiny eggs (10-12 g each) per year. Extremely space-efficient — can be raised in stacked cages or small aviaries. Fast generation turnover (egg to egg in 8-10 weeks) enables rapid flock expansion. Best suited for egg production in space-constrained settlements.

**[Ducks](poultry-ducks.md)** (*Anas platyrhynchos domesticus*):
Hardy, disease-resistant, and excellent foragers in wet environments. Ducks produce rich eggs (70-80 g, higher fat content than chicken eggs) and premium meat. They thrive in marshy or riparian areas where chickens struggle. Excellent pest control in rice paddies and irrigated fields. Khaki Campbell and Pekin are the dominant egg and meat breeds respectively.

**[Geese](poultry-geese.md)** (*Anser anser domesticus*):
The largest poultry species, geese are primarily grazing birds that convert grass directly to meat and fat with minimal grain supplementation. Goose fat is a premium cooking fat. Geese are territorial and aggressive — effective as guard animals and weeders in specialty crops (cotton, strawberry, orchard). Seasonal layers (30-60 eggs/year) but long-lived (15-25 years).

**[Turkeys](poultry-turkeys.md)** (*Meleagris gallopavo*):
The largest poultry species raised primarily for meat. Heritage breeds (8-15 kg toms) grow slowly but breed naturally; commercial broad-breasted types reach 15-20 kg but require artificial insemination. Must be separated from chickens due to blackhead disease susceptibility. Require more space and different housing than chickens but yield a premium carcass with distinctive flavor.

**[Guinea Fowl](poultry-guinea-fowl.md)** (*Numida meleagris*):
Hardy African poultry specializing in pest control. A flock of 20-30 birds reduces tick populations by 70-90% on 1-2 hectares. Loud alarm calls serve as guardian warnings for all livestock. Extremely disease-resistant. Produce modest amounts of flavorful meat (1.2-1.6 kg) and small speckled eggs (35-40 g). Free-range specialists that forage 60-80% of their diet. Keets are fragile for the first 2 weeks.

**[Pigeons and Squab](poultry-pigeons.md)** (*Columba livia*):
Self-foraging birds that produce premium squab meat (350-500 g at 26-30 days) with minimal feed input. Pigeon guano is the richest common animal manure (4-5% N, 2-3% P, 1.5-2% K) — worth more as fertilizer than any other poultry product per kilogram. Both parents feed squab via crop milk, enabling rapid growth without external feed inputs. Homing ability enables messenger services over long distances. Minimal infrastructure — a simple loft suffices.

### Shared Principles

**Biosecurity**:
Disease prevention is the foundation of poultry health. Core practices apply across all species:
- Quarantine new birds for 30 days before introducing to existing flocks
- Disinfect housing between flocks — scrape clean, wash, and apply lime wash or lye solution to walls and floors
- Control human traffic — separate footwear and tools for each species area
- Prevent contact with wild birds (disease reservoir for avian influenza, Newcastle disease, and mycoplasma)
- Remove and compost dead birds promptly — cannibalism and scavenging spread disease
- Provide clean water daily — contaminated water is the primary disease vector in poultry operations

**Predator protection**:
All poultry are vulnerable to predation. Layered defense:
- **Housing**: Secure, enclosed structures with wire mesh (1-2 cm openings) on all vents and openings. Solid doors that close and latch at night.
- **Fencing**: Perimeter fencing (wire mesh or electric) around range areas. Electric fence at 10-15 cm and 30-40 cm above ground deters foxes, raccoons, and dogs.
- **Covered runs**: Hawks and owls are significant daytime and nighttime predators respectively. Covered runs or netting over range areas reduce aerial predation.
- **Lock-up at night**: Most ground predators hunt at night. Train birds to return to housing at dusk and close doors securely.
- **Guardian animals**: Guinea fowl alarm calls alert all poultry to danger. Geese are aggressive and territorial, deterring small predators. Livestock guardian dogs protect entire range areas.

**Feed formulation basics**:
Poultry diets center on energy (grains) and protein (legumes, oilseeds, animal protein):
- **Energy sources**: Corn, wheat, oats, barley, sorghum (milo). Corn is highest energy; oats and barley are lower energy but higher fiber.
- **Protein sources**: Soybean meal, peas, fish meal, meat scraps, insects. Growing birds need 20-30% protein; adult layers need 14-18%.
- **Calcium**: Oyster shell or limestone grit, free-choice for layers. Essential for eggshell formation.
- **Grit**: Small stones for gizzard grinding, especially for whole-grain diets.
- **Vitamins and minerals**: Greens (fresh forage), sprouted grains, or supplemental premix. Free-range birds obtain most vitamins from forage; confined birds need supplementation.

Basic starter ration: 50% cracked corn, 25% wheat, 20% soybean meal or peas, 5% supplement (oyster shell, grit, mineral mix). Adjust protein percentage for species and age.

**Incubation**:
Three options across all species:
- **Natural (broody hen)**: A broody hen (chicken, duck, goose) incubates eggs and raises young. Lowest cost, lowest labor, but limited to the hen's capacity (8-15 eggs depending on species). Not all breeds go broody reliably.
- **Artificial incubator**: Temperature 37.5°C, humidity 55-65%, automatic turning. Enables large-scale production independent of broody behavior. Requires reliable heat source and monitoring.
- **Surrogate mothers**: Chicken hens can incubate eggs of smaller species (quail, guinea fowl, some duck eggs). Useful when the target species has poor broody instinct.

**Brooding**:
All poultry chicks need supplemental heat for the first 3-6 weeks:
- Start at 35°C, reduce by 3°C per week until ambient temperature reached
- Draft-free enclosure with solid walls (brooder guard)
- Clean, dry bedding — paper towels for the smallest species (quail, keets) for the first few days
- Accessible feed and water at appropriate height for the species

### Manure Management

Poultry manure is a high-nitrogen soil amendment. Relative concentrations:

| Species | Nitrogen (%) | Phosphorus (%) | Potassium (%) |
|---------|-------------|----------------|---------------|
| Chickens | 1.5-2.0 | 1.0-1.5 | 0.5-0.8 |
| Ducks | 1.0-1.5 | 0.8-1.2 | 0.4-0.6 |
| Geese | 0.8-1.2 | 0.6-0.9 | 0.4-0.6 |
| Turkeys | 1.3-1.5 | 1.0-1.2 | 0.5-0.7 |
| Guinea Fowl | 1.5-1.8 | 1.0-1.3 | 0.5-0.7 |
| Pigeons | 4.0-5.0 | 2.0-3.0 | 1.5-2.0 |

All fresh poultry manure must be composted before garden application (3-6 months with carbon-rich bedding material). Direct application burns plant roots and may transmit pathogens. Pigeon guano is the exception in concentration — it is the most valuable fertilizer and should be collected and processed separately.

### Integration Strategies

**Multi-species range**:
Running multiple poultry species on shared range utilizes different ecological niches:
- Chickens scratch and eat insects, seeds, and vegetation at ground level
- Ducks forage in wet areas, eat slugs and snails that chickens avoid
- Guinea fowl hunt ticks and insects in tall grass and brush edges
- Geese graze grass, keeping pasture short for other species
- Turkeys forage in woodland edges for acorns, seeds, and insects

**Separation requirements**:
- Turkeys must be separated from chickens (blackhead disease)
- Waterfowl (ducks, geese) wet litter rapidly — house separately from land poultry
- Pigeons need dedicated lofts, not poultry housing
- Guinea fowl can share range with chickens but prefer separate roosting

**Sequential use of space**:
Run chickens on range first (scratch and fertilize), follow with crops. Use geese as weeders in established crop rows. Range turkeys on land not used by chickens. Rotate range areas annually to break parasite cycles.

### Safety

**Zoonotic diseases**: All poultry can carry *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter*. Wash hands after handling birds, eggs, or litter. Cook poultry meat and eggs thoroughly. Pregnant women should avoid cleaning poultry litter (listeria risk from contaminated dust).

**Respiratory hazards**: Poultry dust (feather dander, dried droppings, bedding particles) causes respiratory irritation and allergic reactions. Wear a mask when cleaning enclosed poultry housing. Ensure good ventilation in all housing.

**Avian influenza**: Wild waterfowl are natural reservoirs. Prevent contact between domestic poultry and wild birds. Report unusual mortality events immediately. High-pathogenicity strains are dangerous to both birds and humans.

### Housing and Infrastructure

**Poultry house design**: A basic poultry house for 50 layers requires 5-10 m² of indoor floor space (0.1-0.2 m² per bird) plus 200-300 m² of outdoor run (4-6 m² per bird). Construction: timber frame (75 × 100 mm posts), solid walls (tongue-and-groove boards or plywood), corrugated metal or shingle roof with 20-30° pitch. Floor: concrete or compacted earth with 10-15 cm deep litter. Ventilation: 0.1 m² of opening per 10 birds at the roofline, covered with 2.5 cm hardware cloth. Door: solid wood, 0.8 m wide × 1.8 m high, self-closing latch. Pop hole (bird entrance): 30 × 30 cm, with sliding door operated by cord from outside. Total material cost: $200-500 using reclaimed lumber.

**Feed storage**: Poultry feed stored in rodent-proof metal bins (35-50 L capacity each) at below 15% moisture prevents mold and pest contamination. A flock of 50 layers consuming 5-7.5 kg of feed daily requires 150-225 kg of monthly storage. Three to four metal garbage cans with tight-fitting lids suffice. A single rat consumes 20-30 g of feed daily and contaminates 10× that amount — rodent control saves 5-10% of total feed costs annually.

**Water systems**: Nipple drinkers (6-8 birds per nipple) or bell drinkers (1 per 50 birds) provide clean water without spillage. A 50-bird flock requires 12-20 L of water daily (250-400 mL per bird), doubling in hot weather. Gravity-fed systems from an elevated reservoir (100-200 L tank on a 1.5-2.0 m platform) supply multiple drinker lines without pumps. In freezing climates, heated water bowls (150-250 watt) or twice-daily manual filling prevents ice formation. Water consumption drops of 10-20% reduce egg production by 15-30% within 48 hours.

### Production Metrics

**Egg production benchmarks**: A flock of 50 hybrid layers produces 40-45 eggs per day at peak (80-90% lay rate) from 22-72 weeks of age, declining 10-15% per year thereafter. Annual egg output per hen: 250-300 eggs (15-18 kg of egg mass). Feed conversion: 2.0-2.5 kg feed per kg of egg mass. Each egg contains approximately 6 g protein, 5 g fat, and 70 kcal. At a feed cost of $0.40-0.60 per kg, each egg costs $0.05-0.08 in feed to produce. Annual revenue from 50 layers: 12,500-15,000 eggs, retailing at $0.20-0.50 each ($2,500-7,500 total).

**Meat production benchmarks**: Broilers reach 2.0-2.5 kg at 6-8 weeks with a feed conversion ratio of 1.5-1.8 kg feed per kg of live weight — the most efficient of any terrestrial livestock. Dressed yield: 70-75% of live weight. A batch of 50 broilers consumes 150-225 kg of feed over 7 weeks at a feed cost of $60-135. Heritage breeds grow to 2.0-2.5 kg in 12-16 weeks on range with a feed conversion of 2.5-3.5 but require no medicated feed and reproduce naturally.

**Manure value**: A flock of 50 layers produces 5-7.5 kg of manure daily (approximately 1.8-2.7 tonnes per year). Composted poultry manure (1.5% N, 1.0% P, 0.5% K) applied at 2-5 kg per m² fertilizes 360-1,350 m² of garden beds annually — enough to sustain a substantial vegetable garden. Nitrogen value alone: 27-40 kg of N per year, equivalent to 130-190 kg of ammonium sulfate fertilizer ($40-60 value).

### Regional Adaptations

Poultry management adapts to climate through housing design and breed selection. In tropical regions (average 25-35°C), open-sided housing with wire mesh walls maximizes airflow; deep litter is replaced monthly to prevent ammonia buildup in high-humidity conditions. Chickens reduce feed intake by 10-20% above 30°C; providing electrolytes in water (1 g sodium chloride + 1 g potassium chloride per liter) maintains hydration and egg production. In arid regions, evaporative cooling (wet burlap curtains on the windward wall) reduces indoor temperatures by 5-8°C at the cost of 20-40 L of water per day. In cold climates (-20 to -30°C winters), insulated walls (R-10 to R-15), deep litter composting heat, and supplemental lighting (14-16 hours via 5-10 watt LED per 10 m²) maintain egg production through winter. Ducks excel in tropical and monsoon climates; geese thrive in cold temperate zones; guinea fowl are supreme in tick-endemic regions.

### Historical Development

Chickens were domesticated from red junglefowl in Southeast Asia approximately 8,000 years ago, with the earliest archaeological evidence from northern China (5400 BCE). Dispersal followed trade routes westward through India (2000 BCE), Mesopotamia (1500 BCE), Egypt (1400 BCE), and Europe (800 BCE). The Roman Empire industrialized poultry production — Columella's *De Re Rustica* (c. 50 CE) describes selective breeding, artificial incubation using warmed rooms or dung-heated ovens, and fattening operations. Modern poultry breeding began with the creation of hybrid layer and broiler lines in the 1940s-1950s, achieving a 300% increase in egg production and a 50% reduction in time to market weight within three decades. For bootstrapping, a founding flock of 10-20 chickens (2-3 hens per rooster for breeding, plus 10-15 additional hens for egg production) establishes a self-sustaining poultry operation producing 2,000-4,000 eggs per year within the first 6 months.

### Processing and Products

**Egg preservation methods**: Fresh eggs stored at 4°C maintain grade A quality for 4-6 weeks. Waterglass (sodium silicate solution, 1 part sodium silicate to 10 parts water) preserves eggs for 6-12 months at 10-15°C by sealing shell pores — submerge clean, unwashed eggs completely in the solution in a stone crock or food-grade plastic container. Mineral oil coating (dipping each egg in food-grade mineral oil, air-drying, storing at 10-15°C point-down) extends shelf life to 4-6 months. Salt curing (packing eggs in coarse salt for 30-40 days at room temperature) produces preserved eggs with concentrated, firm yolks — traditional in Chinese cuisine. Pickling (hard-boiled, peeled eggs in spiced vinegar brine with 5% acetic acid) preserves eggs for 3-6 months refrigerated. Each method preserves 90-95% of eggs without spoilage when hygiene standards are maintained.

**Meat preservation**: Poultry meat preserved by salting (dry salt rub of 3-4% body weight, refrigerated 5-7 days), smoking (hot smoke at 60-80°C for 2-4 hours or cold smoke at 20-30°C for 12-24 hours over hardwood), or drying (jerky: sliced breast meat salted at 2-3% and dried at 55-60°C for 6-12 hours to below 20% moisture) extends shelf life from days to months. Smoked poultry keeps 2-4 weeks at 10-15°C; poultry jerky keeps 3-6 months at room temperature in sealed containers. Rendered poultry fat (duck, goose) preserves for 6-12 months at room temperature or 2+ years refrigerated — ideal for confit preservation of additional meat.

**Feather utilization**: Chicken feathers (80-120 g per bird at processing) are 90% keratin protein by weight. Industrial feather meal (autoclaved at 140°C for 30-60 minutes, then ground) provides a slow-release nitrogen fertilizer (12-14% N) releasing over 4-6 months. Down and small feathers from ducks and geese fill pillows (500-800 g per pillow), quilts (800-1200 g per duvet), and insulated clothing. Primary flight feathers from geese (25-35 cm length) serve as quill pens — cut at a 45° angle and slit to create a functional writing nib lasting 3-5 pages before resharpening.

### Nutritional Requirements

**Protein requirements by species and age**: Chicks require 20-24% crude protein in starter feed (0-6 weeks), decreasing to 16-18% in grower (6-12 weeks) and 14-16% in layer ration. Ducklings need 18-22% starter protein; goslings 18-20%; turkey poults the highest at 28-30%. Quail chicks require the highest sustained protein at 24-28% for the first 3 weeks. Protein sources in order of biological value: fish meal (72-78% protein, excellent amino acid profile), soybean meal (44-48% protein, widely available), peas (22-26% protein), and insects (black soldier fly larvae, 40-45% protein on dry matter basis). Synthetic methionine supplementation (0.1-0.2% of diet) is often the limiting amino acid for poultry on all-plant diets.

**Calcium and phosphorus**: Laying hens require 3.5-4.0% calcium in their diet (compared to 0.9-1.0% for non-layers) — each egg shell contains approximately 2.0-2.5 g of calcium carbonate deposited over 18-20 hours during shell formation. Free-choice oyster shell or limestone grit (2-5 mm particle size) allows hens to self-regulate calcium intake. Phosphorus requirement: 0.3-0.4% available phosphorus in the diet, provided by grain (0.25-0.35% P) and supplemented by bone meal or dicalcium phosphate if needed. Calcium-to-phosphorus ratio should be maintained at 2:1 to 3:1 for layers.

**Energy requirements**: Metabolizable energy needs range from 11.5-13.5 MJ/kg of feed for most poultry species. Corn provides the highest energy density (13.5-14.0 MJ/kg), followed by wheat (12.5-13.5 MJ/kg), oats (10.5-11.5 MJ/kg), and barley (10.0-11.0 MJ/kg). Fats and oils (vegetable oil, animal tallow) added at 1-3% of diet increase energy density and improve feed palatability — each 1% added fat increases dietary energy by approximately 0.35 MJ/kg.

### Health Management Calendar

**Routine health schedule**: Weekly: observe flock for signs of illness (lethargy, ruffled feathers, abnormal droppings, reduced feed/water intake). Monthly: clean and disinfect waterers and feeders; check for external parasites (mite and lice examination at the vent area). Quarterly: complete coop cleanout, fresh bedding, and lime wash on walls. Annually: replace 20-25% of the laying flock with young pullets to maintain peak production. Vaccination schedule (where applicable): Marek's disease (day 1, subcutaneous), Newcastle/bronchitis (day 10-14, water or spray booster at 4-6 weeks), fowl pox (6-8 weeks, wing web stab). Deworming: treat with fenbendazole or ivermectin at the beginning and end of the grazing season (spring and autumn), observing a 7-14 day egg withdrawal period after treatment during which eggs should not be consumed by humans.

**Biosecurity protocols**: Footwear disinfection (walk-through boot bath with 2% sodium hydroxide or commercial disinfectant) at every poultry area entrance. Dedicated tools (rake, shovel, feed scoop) for each species area — never shared between turkey and chicken zones. Quarantine all new birds for 30 days in a separate building at least 15 m from the main flock. Rodent control: maintain bait stations or traps at the perimeter of all poultry housing; rodent populations above 5-10 per building significantly increase disease transmission risk. Wild bird exclusion: screen all openings with hardware cloth (under 2.5 cm mesh) and cover outdoor runs with netting to prevent contact with wild waterfowl (avian influenza reservoirs) and wild songbirds (Salmonella carriers).

**Egg quality assessment**: Candle eggs by holding each against a bright light source (LED flashlight, 100+ lumens) in a darkened room. Fresh eggs show a tight, centered yolk with minimal air cell (under 3 mm depth). Stale eggs have a large, mobile yolk and air cell above 5 mm. Blood spots (ruptured blood vessel during ovulation, visible as a red dot in the yolk) occur in 1-2% of eggs and are safe to eat — not a sign of fertilization. Meat spots (brown tissue fragments in the white) occur in less than 1% of eggs and are also harmless. Shell quality: measure shell thickness with a micrometer (target above 0.33 mm; below 0.28 mm indicates calcium deficiency). Shell-less or thin-shelled eggs occur in 1-3% of production and indicate nutritional deficiency, disease, or environmental stress requiring immediate investigation.

A well-managed mixed poultry operation of 30 chickens, 10 ducks, and 6 geese on 0.5-1.0 hectares produces 6,000-8,000 eggs, 80-120 kg of dressed meat, and 1.5-2.5 tonnes of high-nitrogen manure annually — a comprehensive contribution to settlement food security from a single integrated system. The key is matching species to their ecological niches: chickens for eggs and pest control, ducks for wet-area foraging and rich eggs, geese for pasture conversion and guardian duties.
Each species has distinct advantages: chickens are the universal starter, ducks excel in wet conditions, geese convert grass to meat, turkeys provide premium large carcasses, guinea fowl offer unparalleled pest control, pigeons produce self-foraging squab and premium guano, and quail deliver the fastest time-to-production of any poultry. A settlement establishing all seven species achieves resilience against species-specific diseases, climate variability, and feed supply fluctuations that single-species operations cannot match.
The poultry domain connects directly to every other domain in the tech tree: feed from agriculture, housing from construction, disease treatment from medicine, egg and meat preservation from food processing, feather insulation from textiles, and manure fertilizer from soil management. No other livestock category offers this breadth of integration with such low barriers to entry.
Seven species, seven niches, one integrated system: poultry farming is the gateway to settlement food security and the foundation upon which larger livestock operations can be built as resources and infrastructure develop.
A settlement that masters poultry farming gains a reliable, renewable source of high-quality protein that multiplies rapidly, requires modest infrastructure, and integrates with virtually every other domain of the knowledge base.
Starting with chickens and expanding to complementary species as infrastructure allows is the recommended development pathway for poultry in any bootstrapping scenario.
