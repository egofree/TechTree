# Selection Criteria

> **Type**: material | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: animals, chemistry, foundations, plants

The systematic process of choosing the best specimens for breeding, cultivation, or material use based on observable traits. Selection criteria drive domestication, seed saving, electrode materials, and raw material choice.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Selection criteria appear in [Food & Agriculture](../foundations/food-agriculture.md) for seed saving — save seed from the best plants, tallest stalks, largest grain heads, earliest maturing, and most disease-resistant individuals. In [Animal Domestication](../animals/domestication.md), breeding stock is selected for temperament, productivity, and hardiness. In [Electrolysis](../chemistry/electrolysis.md), electrode materials are selected based on overpotential, corrosion resistance, and conductivity. In [Structural Plants](../plants/structural-plants.md), species are selected for fiber strength, growth rate, and pest resistance.

## Technical Details

For seed saving, selected plants are marked before harvest with cordage ties. Threshed grain is dried to below 14% moisture for storage viability. Selection pressure is applied each generation: by saving only the best, desirable traits accumulate over seasons. Ancient grain yields improved from wild-type (~0.5 tonnes/hectare) to landrace varieties (~1-2 tonnes/hectare) through centuries of intentional selection.

For animal breeding, criteria include docility (easiest to handle), reproductive rate, feed conversion efficiency, and product quality (milk yield, fleece weight, egg production). Initial domestication focuses on the most tractable species — sheep, goats, and cattle before horses or poultry.

For electrode selection in electrolysis, the key criteria are: chemical stability in the electrolyte, electrical conductivity, overpotential for the desired reaction, and mechanical durability. Platinum is ideal but scarce; carbon electrodes are the bootstrap standard.
