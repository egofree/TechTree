# Degassing

> **Type**: equipment | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, machine-tools, metals

Dissolved hydrogen and nitrogen removal from molten steel using the RH (Ruhrstahl-Heraeus) degasser: a vacuum chamber with two snorkel tubes immersed in the ladle. Argon injected into one snorkel drives circulation (lift gas) — steel rises into the vacuum chamber, dissolved gases (H₂, N₂) flash out under vacuum (0.5-5 mbar). Steel cycles through the chamber 3-5 times over 15-25 min. Hydrogen reduced from 4-6 ppm to <2 ppm.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Degassing removes dissolved gases from molten metals that would otherwise cause porosity, cracking, or degraded mechanical properties in the finished product. In steelmaking, dissolved hydrogen causes cracking in heavy sections (rails, forgings, pressure vessels). In aluminum casting, dissolved hydrogen causes porosity. In lubricant manufacturing, degassing removes trapped air from formulated products. This is a secondary metallurgy step — it happens after primary melting and before casting.

## Technical Details

In steelmaking, the RH degasser is the most effective method: argon lift gas circulates steel through a vacuum chamber where dissolved gases flash out at pressures of 0.5-5 mbar. Hydrogen drops from 4-6 ppm to below 2 ppm, critical for heavy sections where hydrogen-induced cracking is a failure mode. The simpler VD (vacuum degassing) method places the entire ladle in a vacuum tank with argon stirring through a bottom porous plug — less efficient but simpler to operate.

In aluminum casting, dissolved hydrogen causes porosity visible as bright, rounded pores on a fracture surface. Degassing methods include bubbling dry nitrogen or chlorine gas through the melt (hydrogen diffuses into the bubbles), or simply letting the melt stand at temperature for 10-15 minutes (hydrogen diffuses out slowly). The simple fracture test — pour a small test bar and break it — reveals whether degassing is needed.

In vacuum system maintenance, degassing (bake-out) of chamber walls drives off adsorbed water and gases. Typical bake-out at 150-300°C for 24-48 hours under continuous pumping reduces outgassing rates by orders of magnitude, enabling lower ultimate pressures.
