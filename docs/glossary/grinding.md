# Grinding

Grinding is the mechanical comminution of solid materials into smaller particles using abrasive action. In mineral processing, ore is ground to 50–200 μm to liberate sulfide mineral grains from the surrounding gangue. Insufficient grinding leaves sulfide grains locked inside gangue particles, unavailable for flotation recovery. Over-grinding produces ultrafine particles (<10 μm) that float poorly and are difficult to handle in downstream processes.

The primary grinding tools are ball mills, rod mills, and hammer mills. A ball mill consists of a rotating cylindrical shell filled with grinding media (steel or ceramic balls) and the material to be ground. Tumbling action cascades the balls, crushing and abrading the charge. Rod mills use long steel rods that preferentially contact coarse particles, producing a more uniform product size. Hammer mills use rotating hammers to shatter brittle materials by impact.

In tool making, grinding with abrasive stones and wheels produces sharp edges and precise shapes on hardened materials that cannot be cut by conventional machining. Silicon carbide and aluminum oxide are the standard abrasive grains, with diamond and cubic boron nitride used for the hardest materials. Grinding is one of the earliest precision manufacturing techniques, dating back to stone tool production where abrasion shaped flint and obsidian into blades and points.
