# Animal-Derived Materials

> **Node ID**: animals.animal-materials
> **Domain**: Animals & Animal Husbandry

## Overview

Animals supply a suite of structural, binding, and chemical materials that no plant or mineral source can replicate. Leather, tallow, wool, horn, bone, sinew, and hide glue each fill niches where alternatives are either weaker, less durable, or nonexistent. These materials are harvestable from the first domesticated livestock without specialized infrastructure, and they scale from campside hand-processing to industrial production.

The chain runs from the carcass to finished material through a series of mechanical and chemical steps: fleshing and dehairing hides, rendering fat at controlled temperatures, scouring and carding wool, soaking and pressing horn, and boiling collagen into glue. Each process has specific temperature windows, reagent ratios, and time requirements that determine the quality of the output.

## Leather Tanning

Leather is animal hide preserved against rot by stabilizing its collagen fibers. Untreated hides putrefy within days. Tanning converts the raw protein matrix into a stable, flexible, durable material. Several methods exist, each producing leather with distinct properties.

> **For detailed leather production** — hide preparation (curing, soaking, fleshing, dehairing), chrome tanning, post-tanning operations, mechanical properties, industrial applications, quality grading, and testing standards — see [Leather Production](leather.md).

### Rawhide

The simplest preserved hide. No tanning agents involved, just mechanical preparation and drying.

**Process**: Flesh the fresh hide by scraping away fat and meat with a dull blade or flint scraper. Stretch the hide taut on a wooden frame using cord at 10-15 cm intervals. Dry in sun or air for 3-7 days depending on temperature and humidity. The dried hide is stiff, translucent, and extremely tough when wet.

**Applications**: Drum heads (stretches tight as it dries on the shell), shields (layered rawhide over wooden frame), lashings (cut into strips, soaked to soften, shrinks tight on drying), containers (folded and stitched with sinew), thongs for binding tool hafts.

### Brain Tanning

The oldest soft-tanning method, producing buckskin. Uses the animal's own brain tissue as the tanning agent. Brain matter contains lecithin and other emulsified oils that coat collagen fibers and prevent them sticking together as the hide dries.

**Process**: Dehair the hide by soaking in a lime solution (see [lime.md](../ceramics/lime.md)) or by wet-scraping with a blunt blade. Prepare the brain emulsion: 1-2 kg brain matter per hide, mashed and dissolved in warm water at 40-45°C. Work the emulsion into the dehaired hide by hand or with a blunt scraper, ensuring full penetration. Stretch and pull the hide repeatedly as it dries (3-6 hours of active working) to keep fibers separated. The hide transforms from stiff rawhide to soft, pliable buckskin.

**Smoke treatment**: Untreated brain-tanned leather re-stiffens when wet. Smoke fixes the softening. Smoke over a smoldering fire of willow or pine for 2-4 hours at 50-60°C. The aldehydes and phenolic compounds in wood smoke cross-link with the collagen, making the buckskin permanently soft and water-resistant.

### Vegetable Tanning

The dominant method for producing thick, durable leather suitable for soles, harnesses, belts, and heavy goods. Uses tannins, polyphenolic compounds found in tree bark, to bind collagen fibers.

**Tannin sources and concentrations**:
- Oak bark: 20-30% tannin by dry weight (highest common source in temperate zones)
- Hemlock bark: 10-15% tannin
- Chestnut wood: 6-12% tannin
- Mimosa (wattle) bark: 15-25% tannin
- Sumac leaves: 15-25% tannin

**Pit tanning**: The traditional large-scale method. Dig pits 1.5-2 m deep, 2-3 m square, lined with stone or timber. Layer hides with ground bark in each pit. Fill with water. Hides spend 6-12 months in the pit for sole leather (thick, hard), 2-4 months for harness leather (medium flexibility). Bark consumption runs 3-5 kg bark per kg of raw hide.

**Faster methods**: Rocker pits agitate the hides mechanically, reducing time by 30-50%. Drum tanning (rotating wooden drums with bark liquor) cuts the process to 2-4 weeks but requires more energy and equipment.

### Alum Tawing

Produces white, smooth leather used for gloves, bookbinding, and fine garments. Not true tanning (no permanent collagen cross-linking), but the treatment preserves the hide and makes it supple.

**Process**: Mix potassium aluminum sulfate (alum) at 200-300 g per kg hide with flour, egg yolk, and salt to form a paste. Work the paste into dehaired, delimed hides by hand. Stack hides and let stand for 2-4 weeks, working them periodically to ensure even penetration. The result is pale, soft leather with a smooth grain.

**Limitation**: Alum-tawed leather stiffens and degrades if soaked in water. Not suitable for outdoor or wet applications. The treatment can be partially reversed by prolonged water exposure.

### Oil Tanning

Produces chamois leather, the softest and most absorbent of all leathers.

**Process**: Work cod liver oil or neatsfoot oil (rendered from cattle feet) into a dehaired hide at 30-40°C. The oil oxidizes in contact with air and collagen, forming aldehydes that cross-link the fibers. Repeat the oiling and stretching cycle over 1-2 weeks until the hide is uniformly soft and flexible.

### Currying

Finishing steps applied after tanning to produce the final leather product.

**Shaving**: Reduce the leather to uniform thickness using a shaving knife or, later, a leather-splitting machine. Target thickness: 0.8-2.5 mm depending on application (0.8-1.2 mm for garment leather, 1.5-2.5 mm for harness and sole leather).

**Dyeing**: Iron salts (ferrous sulfate) produce black. Sumac or other tannin-rich materials produce brown. Walnut hulls produce dark brown to black. Alum with various mordants produces yellows and reds.

**Finishing**: Apply oils (neatsfoot, cod liver, tallow) and waxes (beeswax) to the grain surface. Burnish with a smooth stone or glass slicker to close the pores and produce a glossy finish.

**Yield**: One cattle hide produces 2-4 m² of finished leather. One sheep skin produces 0.3-0.6 m².

## Tallow and Lard Rendering

Animal fats yield two major products: tallow (from cattle and sheep) and lard (from pigs). Both are rendered by melting raw fat and straining out the connective tissue. The rendering temperature and fat source determine the properties of the finished product.

### Beef Tallow

Rendered from suet, the hard fat surrounding the kidneys and loins of cattle. Suet yields the highest quality tallow, with a melting point of 40-45°C and a dense, waxy texture.

**Process**: Mince suet into small pieces (1-2 cm). Heat in a large iron pot or cauldron at 80-100°C, stirring to prevent scorching. The fat liquefies and separates from the connective tissue (cracklings). Strain the liquid tallow through linen cloth to remove solids. Pour into molds and cool. Yield: 70-80% of raw fat weight.

### Pork Lard

Rendered from leaf fat (the sheet of fat surrounding the abdominal cavity of pigs). Leaf fat produces the whitest, mildest lard, best for food use.

**Process**: Mince leaf fat finely. Render at a lower temperature than tallow, 70-85°C, to preserve flavor and prevent browning. Strain through cloth. Cool in sealed containers. Yield: 75-85% of raw fat weight.

### Applications

| Use | Fat Type | Details |
|-----|----------|---------|
| Candles | Tallow | 40-60 g per candle, burns 2-4 hours, produces significant smoke and odor. Wick from twisted cotton or rush pith. Inferior to beeswax candles but cheaper and more available. |
| Lubrication | Tallow | Wheel hubs, machine slides, axle bearings. Apply liberally. Re-apply every 50-100 km of cart travel. Supplanted by petroleum lubricants once available. |
| Soap | Tallow | React with wood ash lye (potassium hydroxide) to produce soap. Ratio: ~1 kg tallow to 130-150 g KOH. See [lime.md](../ceramics/lime.md) for full soap-making process. |
| Waterproofing | Tallow | Work into leather boots, harness, and fabric. Reapply regularly. Also used to coat canvas and heavy cloth. |
| Food | Lard | Frying (smoke point ~190°C), pastry (produces flaky texture), preservation (sealing cooked meat in lard-filled crocks). |
| Ointment base | Tallow or lard | Mix with herbs and resins. Carries medicinal compounds into skin. |

## Wool Preparation

Wool is the most versatile animal fiber for textiles, combining warmth, elasticity, fire resistance, and the ability to absorb up to 30% of its weight in moisture without feeling wet. Preparing raw fleece for spinning requires four sequential steps: shearing, skirting, scouring, and carding or combing.

### Shearing

Clip the fleece from the sheep in one piece using hand shears or blade shears. Annual shearing in spring, before lambing and warm weather. Yield: 2-5 kg per sheep for common breeds; Merino sheep produce up to 8 kg of fine fleece. A skilled shearer processes 100-200 sheep per day with blade shears.

### Skirting

Remove the inferior wool from the edges of the spread fleece. Discard belly wool (short, dirty), tags (dung-encrusted locks from the hindquarters), and heavy vegetable matter. Retain 70-80% of the original fleece weight. Grade the remaining wool by fiber diameter and staple length.

### Scouring

Wash the skirted fleece to remove lanolin (10-25% of raw fleece weight), dirt, and suint (natural salts from sheep sweat).

**Process**: Fill tubs with hot water at 50-60°C. Add an alkali, either potash (potassium carbonate from wood ash, 1-2% solution) or soda ash (sodium carbonate). Immerse fleece, agitate gently by hand or with a wooden paddle. Remove fleece, rinse in 2-3 successive baths of clean warm water to remove all soap and suspended dirt. Dry on racks in a well-ventilated area. The spent wash water, rich in lanolin, can be cooled and the lanolin skimmed from the surface for use in waterproofing and lubricants.

### Carding vs. Combing

Two distinct fiber preparation methods that produce fundamentally different yarn types.

**Carding**: Use hand carders, flat wooden paddles (15 x 25 cm) covered with fine wire teeth. Place a handful of scoured wool on one carder, brush with the second carder in a rolling motion. This disentangles the fibers, removes remaining debris, and aligns them roughly. The carded fiber is rolled into a rolag, a cylindrical tuft. Carded fiber produces **woolen yarn**: fluffy, warm, with a fuzzy surface that traps air. Woolen yarn yields 200-800 m per kg.

**Combing**: Use metal wool combs with long, sharp teeth. Draw long-staple wool through the combs repeatedly. This aligns fibers strictly parallel and removes short fibers (noils). The combed fiber is called top. Combed fiber produces **worsted yarn**: smooth, dense, strong, with a lustrous surface. Worsteds are harder-wearing and take dye more evenly. Worsted yarn yields 400-1200 m per kg.

## Horn and Bone Working

Horn and bone provide the hardest and most workable biological materials available before metal tools become common. Both can be cut, carved, and shaped with simple tools, and horn becomes thermoplastic when heated.

### Horn

Horn is composed of keratin, the same protein in hair and nails. It is thermoplastic: softens at 100-150°C, becoming moldable like hard clay.

**Flattening horn sheets**: Soak horn in boiling water for 15-30 minutes until pliable. Remove, split along its natural seam with a knife. Press flat between two smooth boards with weights or a clamp. Let cool and dry under pressure. The resulting horn sheet is translucent, tough, and can be cut, drilled, carved, and polished.

**Products**: Combs (cut from flat sheets with a saw, teeth filed individually), buttons (punched from sheets or turned on a lathe), drinking vessels (molded from heated horn), lantern panes (thin horn sheets transmit a warm, diffuse light, used before glass became cheap), powder flasks, and musical instrument mouthpieces.

### Bone

Bone is harder and more brittle than horn. It does not become thermoplastic but can be worked with saws, files, knives, and abrasives.

**Preparation**: Boil fresh bones for 2-4 hours to remove grease and marrow. Dry thoroughly. Cut with a saw (coarse teeth for long bones, fine teeth for small work). Shape with knife, chisel, or file. Polish with progressively finer abrasives: sandstone, pumice, and finally burnishing with a smooth hard object.

**Products**: Knife handles (sawn to shape, pinned through tang), awls (sharpened bone splinters for piercing leather and wood), fish hooks (carved from ribs or splints), needles (shaped from flat bone splinters, eye drilled with a flint burin), buttons, dice, gaming pieces, and decorative inlays.

### Antler

Antler is denser and tougher than bone, with a solid outer sheath and spongy core. Worked the same way as bone but requires more force to cut. Preferred for high-wear tools: awls, pressure flakers for stone knapping (the compact outer layer is hard enough to press flakes from flint without crumbling), and comb teeth. Harvested from shed antlers (deer drop them annually) or from hunted animals.

## Sinew Processing

Sinew (tendon) is the connective tissue linking muscle to bone. It is one of the strongest natural fibers in tension, exceeding leather and most plant fibers. When dried, it shrinks and hardens, creating bindings that grip tighter than any knot.

**Harvesting**: Remove the long tendons from along the spine (backstrap sinew, longest and most useful) and from the lower legs (leg sinew, shorter but still strong) of deer, elk, and cattle. Peel away the surrounding connective tissue sheath while the tendon is still fresh and pliable.

**Drying and separating**: Air-dry the tendons for 1-2 weeks until completely stiff and brittle. Place on a flat anvil stone and pound with a smooth stone mallet. The dried tendon separates into individual white, translucent fibers ranging from fine thread to coarse string.

**Uses for sinew fiber**:
- **Sewing thread**: Dampen fine fibers, twist into thread, sew leather and fur. Sinew thread passes through its own needle hole more easily than plant fiber thread because it swells to fill the puncture when wet, then shrinks tight as it dries.
- **Bowstrings**: Bundle and twist multiple fibers into a cord. Leg sinew, though shorter, produces the strongest bowstrings.
- **Reinforcing bindings**: Wrap wet sinew around joints, arrow fletching, tool hafts, and bow limbs. As it dries, it shrinks up to 30% and hardens to a rigid, glassy finish.

### Sinew Glue

A secondary product from sinew. Soak pounded sinew fibers in water for 12-24 hours. Simmer at 70-80°C for 4-6 hours (do not boil vigorously, which breaks down the collagen chains). The collagen dissolves into a thick, sticky liquid. Strain through cloth. Cool to a firm gel. Dry into sheets for storage. Reconstitute by soaking in warm water. This glue is waterproof when dry and bonds wood, leather, and sinew with high strength.

## Hide Glue Production

Hide glue (also called animal glue or collagen glue) is the primary adhesive before synthetic resins. Made by hydrolyzing the collagen in hides, sinew, and bones into a soluble form that gels when cool and bonds when dried.

**Production**: Clean raw hides, sinew, or cracked bones to remove all fat and meat. Cut into small pieces. Soak in cold water for 12-24 hours to swell the collagen. Transfer to a glue boiler (copper or iron pot with a false bottom to prevent scorching). Simmer at 80-90°C for 6-12 hours. Do not exceed 90°C, as higher temperatures degrade the collagen chains and weaken the glue.

**Concentration and storage**: Strain the liquid through fine cloth to remove solids. Evaporate in shallow pans to 30-50% concentration. Cool to a firm gel. Slice into sheets or break into granules. Dry in a warm, ventilated area (not in direct sun, which can melt the gel). Store in a dry place. Properly dried hide glue keeps indefinitely.

**Reconstitution**: Soak glue granules or broken sheets in cold water for 2-4 hours to swell. Heat the swollen glue in a glue pot (double boiler) at 50-60°C until fully liquid. Never heat above 70°C. Working concentration: 15-25% glue by weight in water, adjusted based on the application.

**Mechanical properties**: When properly prepared and applied, hide glue achieves 10-15 MPa shear strength and 50-70 MPa tensile strength. These values rival early synthetic adhesives. The glue forms a bond by penetrating the pores of the substrate and mechanically interlocking as it dries and shrinks.

**Advantages**: Reversible with heat and moisture (useful for disassembly and repair). Creep-resistant (does not slowly deform under load, unlike many synthetic adhesives). Compatible with wood, leather, paper, and fabric. Takes stains and finishes well (invisible glue lines in woodworking).

**Limitations**: Not water-resistant. Prolonged moisture exposure softens and eventually dissolves the bond. Not suitable for outdoor or marine use without additional waterproofing.

## Blood and Byproducts

Animal blood, often wasted, has several industrial uses.

- **Blood meal**: Collect fresh blood, stir to prevent coagulation, boil to coagulate the proteins, press the solid mass, dry, and grind to powder. Blood meal is 10-12% nitrogen by weight, making it a potent fertilizer (applied at 100-200 kg/hectare). Also used as an adhesive additive in plywood and particle board (blood albumin adhesive).
- **Blood waterproofing for concrete**: Blood was historically used as an air-entraining and waterproofing admixture for mortar and concrete at 1-3% by weight of cement. The albumin creates microscopic air bubbles that improve workability and freeze-thaw resistance.
- **Blood-based tempera paint**: Mix blood with chalk or lime to produce a red-brown paint for marking livestock, painting buildings, and coating tools.

## Safety

- **Lime burns**: Quicklime and limewater are strongly alkaline (pH 12-13). Wear thick leather gloves when handling hides in lime solutions. Rinse skin immediately with clean water if contacted. Eye exposure requires immediate and prolonged flushing.
- **Tannin exposure**: Vegetable tanning liquors contain high concentrations of tannins and organic acids. Prolonged skin contact causes dermatitis. Wear gloves when handling hides in tanning pits.
- **Hot fat splatter**: Rendering tallow and lard at 80-100°C produces splattering when moisture in the fat vaporizes. Use a deep pot. Add fat gradually. Keep water away from hot fat. Long sleeves, leather apron, and eye protection.
- **Fleshing injuries**: The two-handed drawknife motion used for fleshing hides can slip. Cut away from the body. Keep blades sharp, as dull blades require more force and are more likely to slip.
- **Rendering fumes**: Heating fat produces acrolein and other irritating fumes. Render outdoors or in a space with good cross-ventilation. Prolonged exposure to rendering smoke causes respiratory irritation.
- **Hide glue temperature**: The glue pot operates at 50-60°C, hot enough to cause burns. Use tongs or a brush for application, not bare hands.
