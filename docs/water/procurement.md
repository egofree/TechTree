# Water Procurement

> **Node ID**: water.procurement
> **Domain**: [Water](./index.md)
> **Dependencies**: [`foundations`](../foundations/index.md)
> **Enables**: [`water.distribution`](distribution.md), [`water.basic-treatment`](basic-treatment.md), [`health.sanitation`](../health/sanitation.md)
> **Timeline**: Years 0-5
> **Outputs**: raw_water, groundwater, rainwater
> **Critical**: Yes — no water source means no settlement, no agriculture, no industry


Locating and extracting water from natural sources. Before any distribution network or treatment plant can function, a reliable source must be found, developed, and maintained. This capability covers the full range of procurement methods available from stone-age conditions through early industrial development.

A human requires 2-5 liters of drinking water per day for survival. At 50 liters per person per day for drinking, cooking, and basic hygiene, a settlement of 1,000 people needs 50 m³/day. Industrial processes (brewing, tanning, metalworking cooling) add 2-10× that volume. Water procurement is the first and most fundamental capability for any settlement — without it, nothing else proceeds.

Water sources fall into three categories: surface water (rivers, lakes, streams), groundwater (wells, springs, aquifers), and captured precipitation (rainwater, snowmelt). Each has different reliability, quality, and development effort.

## Process Description

## Water Source Identification

Several natural indicators reveal subsurface water:

- **Vegetation indicators**: Lush green patches in arid terrain, willow and cottonwood trees, reeds and rushes all signal shallow water tables. These species require roots within 1-3 m of the permanent water table.
- **Geological indicators**: Fault lines, valley floors, alluvial fans, and limestone karst regions often bear water. Dry riverbeds frequently conceal subsurface flow at 1-5 m depth.
- **Animal behavior**: Game trails converging on a point, bird flight patterns at dawn and dusk, and insect swarms near moist ground.
- **Topographic survey**: Contour mapping identifies natural catchments and potential reservoir sites.

**Strengths**:
- Zero technology required — observation-based
- Reliable across most climate zones
- Indigenous knowledge often encodes centuries of local water data

**Weaknesses**:
- Does not quantify yield or depth
- Seasonal indicators may be misleading during wet years
- Requires local knowledge or extended observation period

## Hand-Dug Wells

The most universal procurement method across civilizations.

**Prerequisites**: [Basic tools](../foundations/tools-basic.md), stone or timber for lining, rope and bucket.

**Materials**: Stone or brick for lining (5-20 m³), timber for headwall, rope (20-50 m of natural fiber), bucket.

**Procedure**:
1. Select site upslope of latrines, animal pens, and burial grounds (minimum 30 m separation).
2. Mark a circle 1.0-1.5 m in diameter. Begin excavation during dry season when water table is lowest.
3. Install temporary shoring (timber boards) every 1-2 m of depth to prevent wall collapse. Use a plumb line to maintain verticality.
4. Continue digging until water table is reached. The well should extend 1-3 m below the lowest seasonal water table to ensure year-round supply.
5. Line the well shaft with stone, brick, or timber from bottom to top. Leave openings (0.5-1 cm gaps) in the lower 2-3 m of lining to allow water inflow.
6. Backfill around the lining with gravel and sand to create a filter pack that prevents fine sediment from entering.
7. Construct a headwall 0.5-0.8 m above ground level to prevent surface runoff contamination. Install a drainage apron sloping away from the well (1 m radius minimum, concrete or compacted clay).
8. Install a wooden or metal cover to prevent debris and animals from entering. Attach a rope and bucket on a windlass (horizontal roller on a forked post).

**Calibration**: After completion, measure yield by bailing water at maximum rate for 1 hour. Record the volume recovered and the time for the water level to return to its static position. Typical hand-dug well yield: 500-5,000 L/day.

**Expected performance**: A properly constructed hand-dug well 10-20 m deep in an unconsolidated aquifer serves 50-200 people. Water temperature remains constant year-round (a sign of groundwater origin). Turbidity should be <5 NTU after initial development.

**Strengths**:
- Low technology — constructible with hand tools
- Large diameter allows periodic cleaning and deepening
- Long service life (50+ years with proper lining)
- Visual access for inspection and maintenance

**Weaknesses**:
- Slow to construct (2-8 weeks for 10-20 m depth with 3-5 workers)
- Limited to shallow aquifers (practical maximum ~30 m)
- Risk of collapse during construction without proper shoring
- Contamination risk if headwall and drainage apron are inadequate

## Driven Wells

A screened pipe driven into unconsolidated soil using a driving cap and hammer.

**Prerequisites**: [Iron or steel pipe](../metals/iron-steel.md) (3-8 cm diameter), driving cap, heavy hammer or drop weight.

**Materials**: Steel or iron pipe with screened (perforated) section at the bottom, driving cap, well seal (concrete or clay), hand pump.

**Procedure**:
1. Select site in sandy or gravelly soil (driven wells fail in clay, hardpan, or boulder-rich ground).
2. Attach the driving cap to the bottom of the pipe (protects threads and prevents soil from entering during driving).
3. Drive the pipe vertically using a post driver or heavy drop weight (10-30 kg). Maintain verticality with a spirit level.
4. After each 1 m of driving, test for water by pouring a small amount into the pipe and observing whether it drains (indicates the screen has reached permeable soil).
5. Continue driving until the screen section is 1-3 m below the water table.
6. Remove the driving cap. Install a well seal (concrete or bentonite clay) at the surface to prevent contamination.
7. Develop the well by surging: raise and lower a tight-fitting plunger in the pipe to dislodge fine sediment from the screen. Continue until water runs clear.
8. Install a hand pump or suction pump at the wellhead.

**Calibration**: Pump at maximum rate for 1 hour. Measure drawdown (water level drop from static) and recovery time. Yield: 200-2,000 L/day depending on aquifer permeability.

**Expected performance**: Suitable for sandy or gravelly soils at 5-15 m depth. Quick installation (1-4 hours). Limited to unconsolidated formations.

**Strengths**:
- Fast installation — hours, not weeks
- Small footprint
- Lower contamination risk than hand-dug wells (sealed system)
- Relatively inexpensive

**Weaknesses**:
- Only works in unconsolidated soils (sand, gravel)
- Small diameter prevents internal inspection or cleaning
- Screen can become clogged with fine sediment or biofilm
- Limited to shallow aquifers

## Spring Development

Springs are ideal water sources — naturally filtered groundwater emerging at the surface.

**Prerequisites**: [Stone or brick](../ceramics/index.md), [cement mortar](../chemistry/cement.md), [clay](../ceramics/index.md), pipe material (bamboo, clay, or metal).

**Materials**: Stone or concrete for collection basin (0.5-2 m³), clay or concrete seal, overflow pipe, delivery pipe, fencing material.

**Procedure**:
1. Excavate around the spring orifice to expose the point where water emerges from the ground. Dig 0.5-1 m below and around the orifice.
2. Construct a collection basin (spring box) from concrete, stone, or brick. Size: 0.5-2.0 m³ depending on flow rate. The basin should have an inlet at the spring orifice, an outlet at the top for overflow, and a cleanout drain at the bottom.
3. Seal around the spring orifice with clay or concrete to prevent surface water from entering the collection basin. Backfill with 20-30 cm of gravel around the basin, then cover with compacted clay.
4. Install an overflow pipe at the top of the basin to direct excess water to irrigation channels or drainage.
5. Connect a delivery pipe from the basin to a storage tank or distribution point. Use gravity flow — the basin must be higher than the point of use.
6. Fence the spring area to exclude livestock. Plant grass or vegetation uphill to reduce erosion and sediment runoff.

**Calibration**: Measure spring yield by timing how long it takes to fill a container of known volume. Record yield in L/second. Test water quality: turbidity <1 NTU, no fecal coliforms per 100 mL.

**Expected performance**: Typical spring yield: 0.1-10 L/s. A spring yielding 1 L/s produces 86,400 L/day — sufficient for roughly 1,700 people at 50 L/person/day. Spring water is typically of high quality (naturally filtered through soil and rock), requiring minimal treatment.

**Strengths**:
- No pumping required — gravity-fed
- High water quality (natural filtration)
- Continuous flow (no seasonal depletion if aquifer is stable)
- Some springs have operated for millennia

**Weaknesses**:
- Not available at all locations
- Yield varies seasonally (may dry up in drought)
- Vulnerable to contamination if collection basin is poorly sealed
- Freezing in cold climates can interrupt flow

## Rainwater Harvesting

Collecting precipitation from roofs or prepared surfaces.

**Prerequisites**: Roofing material (thatch, tile, or metal), gutter material, storage tank.

**Materials**: Roofing (existing or new), gutters and downspouts (timber, metal, or bamboo), first-flush diverter, storage cistern (ferrocement, stone, or clay).

**Procedure**:
1. Calculate roof catchment area and local annual rainfall to estimate collectable volume: Volume (L/year) = Roof area (m²) × Annual rainfall (mm) × Runoff coefficient (0.8 for metal, 0.6 for tile, 0.3 for thatch).
2. Install gutters along roof eaves with a minimum slope of 0.5% toward downspouts. Secure gutters every 1-1.5 m with brackets.
3. Install a first-flush diverter: a vertical standpipe that captures the first 1-2 mm of rainfall (washes dust, bird droppings, and debris off the roof). The standpipe fills first, then water flows to the storage tank. Empty the standpipe after each rain event.
4. Connect downspouts to the storage cistern. The cistern should be covered to prevent mosquito breeding and contamination.
5. Size the cistern to bridge dry periods. For household use: Monthly consumption = People × 50 L/day × 30 days. Cistern volume should cover the longest expected dry period (2-6 months depending on climate).

**Calibration**: After first installation, measure actual collection by reading cistern level before and after rain events. Compare to theoretical yield and adjust expectations.

**Expected performance**: A 50 m² metal roof in a region with 800 mm annual rainfall collects: 50 × 0.8 × 800 = 32,000 L/year = 88 L/day — sufficient for roughly 1.5 people at 50 L/person/day. Larger roofs or higher rainfall produce proportionally more.

**Strengths**:
- Very low technology — achievable from Year 0
- Rainwater is soft (low mineral content) and free of most pathogens
- Modular — expandable by adding catchment area or storage
- No pumping required if cistern is elevated

**Weaknesses**:
- Dependent on rainfall — unreliable in arid climates
- Large storage volumes needed to bridge dry seasons
- Thatch roofs yield lower quality water (dissolved organics)
- Freezing climates require frost-proof storage

## Surface Water Collection

Rivers, lakes, and streams provide the highest-volume sources but require more treatment than groundwater.

**Prerequisites**: Basic tools for intake construction, [pipes](../metals/iron-steel.md) or channels for conveyance.

**Materials**: Coarse screen (gravel basket or barred grille), settling basin materials (earth, stone, concrete), intake pipe or channel.

**Procedure**:
1. Locate intake upstream of settlement, sewage outfalls, and livestock crossing points (minimum 100 m upstream).
2. Construct a submerged intake: a gravel-filled basket or perforated pipe buried in the riverbank. Water flows through gravel into the pipe by natural hydraulic gradient. The gravel pre-filters sediment.
3. Connect intake to a settling basin (simple excavated pond) with 1-4 hour residence time. The basin allows suspended sediment to settle before water enters the distribution system.
4. For larger volumes, construct an infiltration gallery: horizontal porous pipes buried 2-5 m deep in riverbank gravels parallel to the river. Natural sand filtration yields cleaner water than direct abstraction.

**Calibration**: Measure intake flow rate and turbidity. Raw surface water typically has turbidity 10-1,000 NTU. After settling basin: target <10 NTU.

**Expected performance**: River intakes can supply 1,000-100,000+ m³/day depending on river flow. Infiltration galleries typically yield 10-50% of river flow per meter of gallery length.

**Strengths**:
- High volume capacity
- Often close to settlements (rivers were historical settlement sites)
- Continuous supply (most rivers flow year-round)

**Weaknesses**:
- Highest treatment requirement of all source types
- Vulnerable to upstream contamination
- Seasonal variation in quality (flood turbidity, drought concentration)
- Intake structures vulnerable to flood damage

## Scaling Notes

- **Household scale** (1-5 people): Rainwater harvesting from 20-50 m² roof with 2-5 m³ cistern. Driven well if soil permits. Cost: minimal.
- **Village scale** (50-500 people): Hand-dug wells (multiple), spring development, or small river intake with settling. Requires community labor organization.
- **Town scale** (5,000+ people): Multiple wells or large river intake with treatment. Requires [pumping capability](../energy/steam-power.md) and [distribution infrastructure](distribution.md).
- **City scale** (50,000+ people): River or lake intake with full treatment train. Aqueduct from distant mountain springs. Requires engineered infrastructure.

The jump from village to town scale is the critical threshold: it requires mechanized pumping, pipe networks, and treatment capability.

## Troubleshooting

| Problem | Probable Cause | Solution |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Well water turbid after rain | Surface runoff entering well through cracks in lining or inadequate headwall | Repair lining cracks with mortar. Raise headwall. Improve drainage apron. Add gravel filter pack around casing. |
| Well yield declining seasonally | Water table dropping during dry season; well not deep enough | Deepen well by 1-3 m below lowest recorded water table. Alternatively, reduce pumping rate to match sustainable yield. |
| Driven well screen clogged | Fine silt or iron bacteria plugging screen openings | Surge the well with a plunger. If ineffective, add dilute chlorine solution (100 mg/L) to dissolve biofilm. Worst case: pull pipe and replace screen section. |
| Spring flow stopped | Upstream aquifer depleted or diverted; collection basin clogged | Check for new construction or well drilling uphill that may have intercepted the aquifer. Clear collection basin of sediment. May be seasonal — monitor before investing in remediation. |
| Rainwater cistern empty at end of dry season | Cistern undersized for dry period duration | Add storage capacity. Reduce per-capita consumption. Supplement with well water during dry months. |
| River intake blocked | Flood debris, sediment, or ice blocking intake screen | Install a trash rack (coarse bar screen) upstream of intake. Clear debris regularly. In freezing climates, route intake below frost line. |
| Water tastes or smells of rotten eggs | Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) in groundwater — common in some aquifers | Aerate water by cascading from one tank to another (H₂S gas escapes). Alternatively, chlorinate to oxidize H₂S. Test for other contaminants. |

## Safety

- **Well construction collapses**: The leading cause of death during hand-dug well construction. Shore every meter of depth with timber boards before continuing. Never enter a well deeper than 1.5 m without shoring. Fatal collapses occur in sand, silt, and loose gravel — cohesive clay is safer but still requires shoring below 3 m.
- **Deep well gases**: Carbon dioxide (CO₂, heavier than air) and hydrogen sulfide (H₂S, toxic at 10 ppm) accumulate in deep wells. Always lower a lit candle before descending — if it extinguishes, do not enter. Ventilate by operating a fan or bellows at the surface for 15-30 minutes before entry. Use a safety harness with a rope tended by a second person at the surface.
- **Contaminated surface water**: Never drink untreated surface water. Waterborne pathogens — Vibrio cholerae (cholera, lethal if untreated), Salmonella typhi (typhoid, 10-20% mortality untreated), and Entamoeba histolytica (amoebic dysentery) — are major killers in all pre-industrial settings. All surface water must be [treated](basic-treatment.md) before consumption.
- **Well contamination**: The most common failure mode is surface runoff carrying fecal matter into the well. Prevent with a raised headwall (0.5 m+), drainage apron sloping away, tight-fitting cover, and locating wells at least 30 m uphill from latrines and animal pens.
- **Drowning**: Open wells and cisterns are drowning hazards, especially for children. Install tight-fitting covers and lockable lids on all water storage.

## Quality Control

- **Turbidity test**: Fill a clear glass container. If you can read text through 20 cm of water, turbidity is approximately <5 NTU (acceptable for most uses). If cloudy, settling or filtration is needed.
- **Sanitary survey**: Inspect the water source monthly. Check for: cracked well lining, standing water within 3 m of the wellhead, missing or broken cover, livestock access within 30 m, latrines uphill within 30 m.
- **Simple bacterial test**: Hydrogen sulfide (H₂S) presence/absence test — a strip or bottle that turns black if fecal contamination is present. Low cost, no laboratory required.
- **Yield measurement**: For wells, measure static water level and pumping level. The difference (drawdown) indicates aquifer response. Record seasonally to track long-term aquifer health.

## Variations and Alternatives

- **Qanats (infiltration galleries)**: Persian water tunnels that tap groundwater at the base of hills and deliver it to valleys via gently sloping underground tunnels. Gradient: 0.05-0.5%. No pumping required. Some have operated for 2,000+ years. Labor-intensive to construct but extremely long-lived.
- **Bored wells**: Auger-based drilling for deeper aquifers (15-50 m). Requires metal tools and mechanical advantage (windlass or capstan). Casing with clay or concrete to seal surface contamination.
- **Infiltration galleries**: Horizontal porous pipes buried in riverbank gravels. Natural sand filtration yields cleaner water than direct river abstraction. Suitable for community-scale supply.
- **Fog collection**: In coastal or mountainous areas with frequent fog, mesh collectors (polypropylene or metal screen) condense atmospheric moisture. Yield: 2-15 L/day per m² of mesh in suitable locations. Minimal infrastructure but highly location-dependent.
