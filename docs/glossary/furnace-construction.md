# Furnace Construction

Furnace construction encompasses the design and building of industrial heating vessels for smelting, heat treatment, calcination, and melting operations. A graphitization furnace is built as a long rectangular bed (15–25 m long, 3–5 m wide) of refractory brick. Electrodes are laid lengthwise in the bed, surrounded by a packing mixture of petroleum coke granules and sand that serves as both resistive heating element and thermal insulation. The assembly is covered with insulating material (coke breeze, sand, or slag wool).

Refractory selection is the primary design decision. Fireclay brick (Al₂O₃-SiO₂) withstands up to about 1300°C, silica brick up to 1650°C, and magnesite or dolomite brick for basic slag environments in steelmaking. Furnace geometry affects heat distribution: cylindrical furnaces provide uniform heating, rectangular furnaces suit batch processes with long charge lengths. Door and port design controls atmosphere and access.

In a bootstrap context, furnace construction typically progresses from simple pit kilns and crucible furnaces to shaft furnaces and reverberatory furnaces as refractory quality and blower capacity improve. The furnace lining lifetime depends on operating temperature, slag chemistry, and thermal cycling frequency. Proper construction with adequate expansion joints, supported arches, and appropriate refractory grades is essential—furnace failure during a melt is dangerous and destroys the charge.
