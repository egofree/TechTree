# Nylon

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, health, polymers

The first fully synthetic fiber, developed by DuPont in 1935. Nylon is a polyamide polymer produced from two petrochemical intermediates: adipic acid (from cyclohexane via benzene hydrogenation and HNO₃ oxidation) and hexamethylenediamine (from butadiene + HCN, or from acrylonitrile electrolytic dimerization). Both routes require significant petrochemical infrastructure, making nylon a late-stage polymer in the bootstrap sequence. Nylon 6,6 (the original) has a melting point of ~260°C and tensile strength of ~80 MPa.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Nylon marks the transition from natural and semi-synthetic materials to fully synthetic polymers. In the chemistry domain, nylon production demonstrates the capability for controlled polymerization of petrochemical monomers. In the health domain, nylon sutures and medical tubing replaced silk and catgut. In the polymers domain, nylon is a key engineering thermoplastic — used for gears, bearings, rope, fishing line, textile fibers, and mechanical components requiring toughness, wear resistance, and self-lubricating properties.

The synthetic rubber domain produces nylon intermediates alongside other petrochemical products. Nylon's development also created the model for systematic polymer engineering — the Carothers team at DuPont established the structure-property relationships that still guide polymer science.

## Technical Details

Nylon is produced by condensation polymerization: the amine end of hexamethylenediamine reacts with the acid end of adipic acid, releasing water and forming an amide bond. The reaction proceeds in a sealed vessel at ~280°C under nitrogen, building polymer chain length until the target molecular weight is achieved. The resulting nylon 6,6 salt is melt-spun into fibers through spinnerets, cold-drawn to 4-5× the original length to orient polymer chains and increase tensile strength.

Nylon's key properties are high toughness (notch-sensitive but resistant to repeated impact), good abrasion resistance (used for gear teeth and bearing surfaces), low friction coefficient (self-lubricating in many applications), and hygroscopic nature (absorbs moisture, which acts as a plasticizer — dimensional stability requires conditioning). Nylon degrades above ~180°C continuously, limiting high-temperature applications.
