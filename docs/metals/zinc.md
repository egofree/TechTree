## Problem

Zinc is the corrosion protection metal. Without galvanizing, steel structures rust and fail — a bare steel bridge in a marine atmosphere loses 50-100 μm/year of section thickness and becomes structurally unsafe within 10-20 years. Zinc sacrifices itself electrochemically to protect steel (electrode potential -0.76 V vs. SHE, compared to iron's -0.44 V). A galvanized coating of 85 μm provides 50-100+ years of service in rural atmospheres. No other common metal offers this combination of low cost, electrochemical protection, and ease of application.

Without zinc production, you cannot galvanize steel (bridges, buildings, vehicles, pipelines corrode rapidly), cannot make brass (the critical Cu-Zn alloy for valves, fittings, bearings, musical instruments), cannot produce zinc oxide (rubber vulcanization activator, sunscreen, pigments), cannot build zinc-air batteries (200-400 Wh/kg energy density for stationary storage), and cannot reliably die-cast complex parts (Zamak alloys melt at 381°C, enabling high-speed precision casting).

The bootstrap chain runs: zinc enables galvanized steel → long-life infrastructure → bridges, pipelines, marine structures that last 50-100+ years. Without it, every outdoor steel structure has a 5-15 year service life. Zinc's unique metallurgical challenge compounds this: it vaporizes before it melts under atmospheric pressure (bp 907°C, mp 420°C), making it one of the hardest metals to produce. Unlike copper or iron, which can be cast directly from a furnace, zinc requires distillation and condensation in an oxygen-free environment — the metal must be captured as a vapor and condensed without re-oxidation.

## Overview

Zinc (mp 420°C, bp 907°C) is produced by roasting sphalerite (ZnS) to ZnO, then reducing with carbon and distilling the zinc vapor. Its low boiling point makes it one of the harder metals to produce — it vaporizes before it melts under ordinary conditions. Zinc is essential for brass (Cu-Zn alloy), galvanizing steel, zinc oxide pigment, and die-casting alloys.

## Zinc Minerals and Ores

**Primary ore — sphalerite (ZnS)**: Zinc blende, the principal zinc mineral, occurs as brown, black, or yellow tetrahedral crystals (Mohs hardness 3.5-4, density 3.9-4.1 g/cm³). Typical zinc ore grades: 3-12% Zn. Sphalerite is almost always found with galena (PbS) — polymetallic ore bodies are the norm. Major deposits: Red Dog (Alaska, 17% Zn, 5% Pb), Mount Isa (Australia, 8% Zn, 6% Pb), Rampura Agucha (India, 13% Zn), Antamina (Peru, 8% Zn, 1% Cu). Global reserves: ~200 million tonnes of zinc metal.

**Secondary ore — smithsonite (ZnCO₃)**: Calamine, a secondary (supergene) zinc mineral formed by weathering of sphalerite. Occurs as botryoidal (grape-like) masses, white to blue-green, density 4.3-4.5 g/cm³. Historically important — the Romans produced brass by heating copper with smithsonite (cementation process) long before metallic zinc was isolated. Smithsonite ores are largely depleted; most modern zinc comes from sphalerite.

**Other zinc minerals**: Hemimorphite (Zn₄Si₂O₇(OH)₂·H₂O) — another supergene mineral, 54% Zn, found in oxidized zones of zinc deposits. Franklinite (ZnFe₂O₄) — a spinel-group mineral found in unique deposits at Franklin, New Jersey. Wurtzite (ZnS, hexagonal polymorph of sphalerite) — less common, similar composition.

**Zinc in industrial waste**: Galvanizer dross (6-15% Zn in Fe-Zn alloy, from hot-dip galvanizing baths) and brass foundry slag (5-20% Zn) are significant secondary zinc sources. Electric arc furnace (EAF) dust from steel recycling contains 15-30% Zn (zinc from galvanized scrap volatilizes during melting). Waelz kiln processing of EAF dust recovers 85-95% of the zinc as impure ZnO (50-60% Zn), which is sent to the primary zinc smelter. Secondary zinc provides 20-30% of global zinc supply.

## Ore Beneficiation

**Crushing and grinding**: Primary crusher (jaw or gyratory, reduces ore from 300-600 mm to 50-100 mm) → secondary crusher (cone, to 10-25 mm) → rod mill or ball mill (to 50-150 μm particle size, 80% passing). Energy consumption: 10-20 kWh per tonne of ore. Target grind size liberates sphalerite grains from gangue (silica, calcite, pyrite).

**Flotation**: Crushed ore is slurried in water (30-40% solids) with flotation reagents: collector (xanthate, 20-80 g/tonne — makes sphalerite surface hydrophobic), frother (MIBC methyl isobutyl carbinol, 10-30 g/tonne — stabilizes air bubbles), activator (CuSO₄, 100-400 g/tonne — copper ions activate sphalerite surface for xanthate adsorption), pH modifier (lime Ca(OH)₂ to pH 8-11 — depresses pyrite), and depressant (ZnSO₄ or NaCN for selective separation from galena — cyanide depresses sphalerite in the lead float, then CuSO₄ re-activates it for the zinc float).

**Flotation circuit**: Rougher float (3-5 min, pH 8-9) produces zinc concentrate (40-60% Zn, 1-5% Pb, 1-8% Fe, 25-33% S). Scavenger float recovers remaining zinc from tailings. Cleaner floats (2-3 stages) upgrade concentrate to 50-60% Zn. Tailings: 0.2-0.5% Zn (loss). Overall zinc recovery: 85-95%. Water consumption: 2-5 m³ per tonne of ore. Concentrate is thickened (settled to 60-70% solids), filtered (pressure filter to 8-10% moisture), and dried (rotary dryer, 100-150°C, to 6-8% moisture) for transport to the smelter.

## Roasting

**Purpose**: Convert ZnS to ZnO (sphalerite cannot be reduced directly by carbon — ZnS is thermodynamically more stable than ZnO). Sulfur is removed as SO₂ gas for sulfuric acid production. Reaction: 2ZnS + 3O₂ → 2ZnO + 2SO₂ (ΔH = -940 kJ/mol ZnS, strongly exothermic — the roaster is self-sustaining once ignited).

**Fluidized bed roaster** (modern standard): Feed dried zinc concentrate (50-55% Zn) into a cylindrical furnace (3-6 m diameter, 8-12 m tall) containing a fluidized bed of ZnO particles. Air blown upward through the bed at 0.3-0.8 m/s fluidizes the particles (bed expands 50-100%, appears to boil). Bed temperature: 900-1000°C (controlled by air rate and water injection). Residence time: 2-8 hours. SO₂ concentration in off-gas: 6-12% — suitable for sulfuric acid production (contact process). Roaster capacity: 100-800 tonnes of concentrate per day. ZnO calcine product: 55-62% Zn, 1-3% S (as sulfate), 5-10% Fe, 1-3% Pb.

**Multiple hearth roaster** (older design): 8-12 circular hearths stacked vertically, rabble arms (rotating steel arms with teeth) rake the concentrate across each hearth from outer edge to center (or center to edge alternately), dropping it to the hearth below. Temperature: 600-900°C, increasing downward. Residence time: 4-8 hours. Lower SO₂ concentration in off-gas (3-6%) than fluidized bed — less suitable for acid production. Largely replaced by fluidized bed roasters.

**Sintering** (for horizontal retorts): If the zinc calcine is too fine for the reduction furnace, it is sintered: mixed with 5-10% fine coke and fed onto a moving grate sinter machine (Dwight-Lloyd type, 1-4 m wide grate, 20-40 m long). Ignition hood lights the coke, and a suction fan draws air downward through the bed. Sintering temperature: 1100-1300°C. Product: porous, 10-50 mm lumps of ZnO suitable for charging into retorts. Also removes remaining sulfur (reduces S to <1%) and chloride (<0.1%). Sinter machine capacity: 500-2000 tonnes/day.

## Zinc Smelting — Pyrometallurgical (Distillation)

**Thermodynamics of zinc reduction**: The reduction ZnO + C → Zn + CO occurs at temperatures above ~950°C. At 1000°C, CO partial pressure exceeds atmospheric, driving the reaction. However, zinc vapor pressure at 907°C is 1 atm (boiling point) — the zinc product is a gas at reduction temperature. The challenge: condense zinc vapor without it re-oxidizing (zinc vapor + CO₂ → ZnO + CO, or zinc vapor + O₂ → ZnO). This is the fundamental metallurgical problem of zinc — the metal must be distilled off and condensed in an oxygen-free environment.

**Horizontal retort process** (historical, pre-1950): Charge sintered ZnO + coke (ratio 1:1 to 1:1.5 by weight) into fireclay retorts (cylindrical, 250-350 mm diameter, 1.5-2.0 m long, 25-35 mm wall thickness). Seal the open end with a fireclay condenser (500-700 mm long, slightly cooler than the retort) fitted with an extension tube. Heat in a furnace to 1100-1300°C using producer gas or natural gas. Zinc vapor distills from the retort and condenses in the condenser at 450-550°C (above zinc's melting point of 420°C, below its boiling point — liquid zinc collects and is periodically tapped). Each retort produces 20-40 kg of zinc per cycle (24 hours). A battery of 200-400 retorts yields 4-15 tonnes/day. Zinc purity: 98-99.5% (contaminated with Pb, Cd, Fe). Labor-intensive (charging and emptying retorts by hand). Largely obsolete.

**Imperial Smelting Furnace (ISF)** (blast furnace for zinc): Developed 1950s by Imperial Smelting Corporation, Avonmouth, UK. A blast furnace (5-8 m tall, 3-4 m hearth diameter) charges sintered ZnO + coke + preheated air (800-1000°C) at the tuyeres. Zinc vapor and CO gas rise through the furnace shaft. At the top, zinc vapor (concentration ~5-8%) is intercepted by a lead splash condenser: molten lead (450°C) is sprayed upward in a chamber, absorbing zinc vapor by rapid cooling below zinc's dew point. Lead absorbs zinc up to 2.5% by weight at 450°C. The lead-zinc alloy is cooled to 400°C, where zinc separates as a liquid (immiscible with lead above 2% Zn at this temperature) and is decanted.

**ISF performance**: Zinc output: 100-300 tonnes/day per furnace. Lead output: 30-100 tonnes/day (the furnace also reduces PbO from the lead-containing sinter). Coke consumption: 0.8-1.2 tonnes per tonne of zinc produced. Zinc purity: 98.5-99.5%. Advantage: processes mixed Pb-Zn concentrates without prior separation. Disadvantage: high coke consumption, complex condenser operation, lower purity than electrolytic zinc. ~10 ISF plants worldwide (mainly in China and India); electrolytic process dominates modern production.

**New Jersey Zinc vertical retort**: Large silicon carbide retort (200-300 mm square internal cross-section, 10-14 m tall) charged continuously from the top with ZnO-carbon briquettes (pressed from calcine + coal pitch binder, 75-100 mm diameter). Heated externally by producer gas to 1200-1300°C. Zinc vapor exits at the top and condenses in a refractory-lined condenser at 450-500°C. Continuous operation: 8-10 tonnes/day per retort. A battery of 8-12 retorts produces 60-100 tonnes/day. Purity: 99.0-99.5%. Disadvantage: silicon carbide retorts are expensive ($50,000-100,000 each), last 3-5 years before cracking. Obsolete since 1990s.

## Zinc Smelting — Hydrometallurgical (Electrolytic)

**Roast-leach-electrowin (RLE)**: The dominant zinc production process (80%+ of world output). Roast ZnS concentrate to ZnO (as described above). Leach the calcine in dilute sulfuric acid (150-200 g/L H₂SO₄) at 50-75°C for 1-4 hours: ZnO + H₂SO₄ → ZnSO₄ + H₂O. Solid-liquid separation: filter the leach slurry (30-40% solids) through a belt filter or pressure filter. Zinc extraction: 85-95% in the neutral leach stage. Residue (1-5% Zn, plus Pb, Ag) is re-leached under more aggressive conditions (hot acid leach: 150-200 g/L H₂SO₄, 85-95°C, 4-8 hours) to recover remaining zinc.

**Solution purification**: The zinc sulfate solution contains impurities that must be removed before electrowinning: (1) Iron precipitation: oxidize Fe²⁺ to Fe³⁺ with air or MnO₂, precipitate as jarosite (KFe₃(SO₄)₂(OH)₆) or goethite (FeOOH) at pH 3-4, 85-95°C. Removes 95-99% of iron. (2) Copper and cadmium cementation: add zinc dust (1-3 g/L) at 50-70°C — more electropositive metals (Cu, Cd, Ni, Co) plate out onto the zinc while zinc dissolves. Two-stage: first cementation at 70-75°C removes Cu and most Cd; second stage at 50-55°C with antimony trioxide (Sb₂O₃, 2-5 mg/L) as activator removes remaining Cd, Co, and Ni. Purified solution: 130-170 g/L Zn, <0.1 mg/L Cu, <0.1 mg/L Cd, <0.1 mg/L Co.

**Electrowinning**: Electrolyze the purified ZnSO₄ solution in aluminum cathodes and lead-silver alloy (0.5-1.0% Ag) anodes. Cell conditions: current density 400-600 A/m², cell voltage 3.0-3.5 V, temperature 35-40°C, electrolyte Zn²⁺ concentration 50-65 g/L (in cell), H₂SO₄ concentration 150-200 g/L (acid builds up as zinc deposits). Current efficiency: 85-92% (8-15% of current wasted on hydrogen evolution). Energy consumption: 3.0-3.5 kWh per kg of zinc deposited. Zinc deposits on aluminum cathodes as smooth, dense sheets (2-4 mm thick after 24-48 hours). Strip manually or by machine. Melt cathodes in an induction furnace at 450-500°C, cast into 20-25 kg ingots (jumbo ingots: 1000-2000 kg). Purity: 99.99% SHG (special high grade zinc, per ASTM B6: ≥99.99% Zn, ≤0.003% Pb, ≤0.003% Cd, ≤0.002% Fe).

**Plant scale**: A modern electrolytic zinc plant produces 100,000-300,000 tonnes/year of SHG zinc. Requires 100-150 MW electrical power (for electrowinning at 3.0-3.5 kWh/kg × 300,000 t/year = 900-1050 GWh/year ≈ 100-120 MW average). Sulfuric acid co-production: 1.5-2.0 tonnes H₂SO₄ per tonne of zinc (from roasting off-gas via contact process — see [Acids and Bases](../chemistry/acids-bases.md)). The acid is a valuable byproduct, sold to fertilizer plants and chemical industries.

## Troubleshooting — Electrolytic and Galvanizing Operations

| Problem | Probable Cause | Solution |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Low zinc extraction in leach (<85%) | Insufficient acid concentration or temperature | Increase H₂SO₄ to 150-200 g/L; raise temperature to 70-75°C; extend leach time to 3-4 hours; re-grind calcine to finer particle size |
| High iron in purified solution | Incomplete jarosite/goethite precipitation | Increase oxidation (add MnO₂ or air sparging); raise pH to 3.5-4.0; maintain 90-95°C; ensure adequate settling time |
| Cadmium breakthrough in purified solution | Insufficient zinc dust addition in cementation | Increase zinc dust to 2-4 g/L; add Sb₂O₃ activator (2-5 mg/L); raise first-stage temperature to 70-75°C |
| Low current efficiency in electrowinning (<80%) | Impurities (Co, Ni, Ge, Sb) in electrolyte; low temperature; high current density | Re-purify solution (additional cementation stage); maintain 35-40°C; reduce current density to 400-500 A/m² |
| Rough or burnt zinc deposit on cathodes | Excessive current density; organic contamination | Reduce current density; add licorice or glue (0.1-0.5 mg/L) as leveling agent; filter electrolyte through activated carbon |
| Zinc re-oxidizes in condenser (pyrometallurgical) | Air leak into condenser; CO₂ levels too high | Seal all condenser joints; maintain reducing atmosphere (CO-rich); control condenser temperature at 450-500°C (above zinc mp, below bp) |
| Blue-green fume from zinc bath (galvanizing) | Zinc overheating above 480°C; aluminum depletion | Reduce bath temperature to 450-460°C; add Al to 0.1-0.3% (suppresses Fe-Zn alloy growth) |
| Dross buildup in galvanizing bath (>5 cm) | Excessive iron dissolution from steel parts; bath too hot | Control immersion time (3-10 min); maintain 450-460°C; skim dross regularly; reduce flux contamination |
| Zinc coating too thin (<45 μm) | Withdrawal speed too fast; low bath temperature | Reduce withdrawal speed to 1-3 m/min; ensure bath at 450-460°C; increase immersion time for thicker sections |
| Zinc coating spalling or flaking | Excessive Fe-Zn alloy layer growth (brittle); steel surface contaminated | Reduce bath temperature and immersion time; ensure proper surface preparation (degrease → pickle → flux) |

## Zinc Alloys

**Brass (Cu-Zn)**: The most important zinc alloy. Brass produced since Roman times by cementation: heat copper metal with zinc oxide (smithsonite) and charcoal in a covered crucible at 1000-1200°C. Zinc vapor reduces and alloys with copper directly. Modern method: melt copper (mp 1085°C), add zinc (mp 420°C — add late to minimize volatilization losses of 2-5%). Common alloys: cartridge brass (70% Cu, 30% Zn) — excellent cold working, tensile strength 300-900 MPa (depending on cold work), used for ammunition cases, hardware. Muntz metal (60% Cu, 40% Zn) — harder, stronger, used for structural bolts, condenser tubes. Admiralty brass (71% Cu, 28% Zn, 1% Sn) — tin inhibits dezincification in seawater. Free-cutting brass (62% Cu, 35% Zn, 3% Pb) — lead particles act as chip breakers for machining at 100-300 SFM. See [Copper & Bronze](copper-bronze.md).

**Zamak die-casting alloys**: Zamak 3 (96% Zn, 4% Al, 0.03% Mg, 0.002% Cu) — the most common zinc die-casting alloy. Melting point: 381°C (low — energy-efficient casting). Tensile strength: 280 MPa (as-cast), 35% elongation. Impact strength: 58 J. Density: 6.6 g/cm³. Cast at 400-425°C into steel dies at 13-27 MPa injection pressure. Dimensional accuracy: ±0.05 mm for 25 mm dimensions. Used for automotive components, hardware, toys, electrical fittings. Casting cycle: 5-15 seconds (high productivity — 200-500 shots/hour per machine). Die life: 100,000-1,000,000 shots (steel dies). Zamak 5 (adds 1% Cu for higher strength: 330 MPa tensile, 35 J impact).

**Galvanizing alloys**: Pure zinc (99.99%) or zinc-1% aluminum (Galfan) or zinc-55% aluminum-1.6% silicon (Galvalume/Zincalume) for hot-dip coating of steel. Galvalume coating: 20-40 μm thick, corrosion resistance 2-6× that of pure zinc in atmospheric exposure (salt spray test: 500-1500 hours to red rust for Galvalume vs. 150-500 hours for pure zinc at 20 μm thickness). See [Galvanizing](iron-steel.md).

## Galvanizing

**Hot-dip galvanizing**: Clean steel (degrease in hot alkali, 60-80°C, 5-10 min → acid pickling in 10-15% HCl at 20-35°C for 5-30 min to remove mill scale → flux in ZnCl₂/NH₄Cl solution at 50-70°C → dry). Immerse in molten zinc bath (450-460°C, 99.99% Zn ± 0.1-0.3% Al to suppress Fe-Zn alloy growth). Immersion time: 3-10 minutes depending on section thickness. Withdraw at 1-3 m/min (speed controls coating thickness). Coating: outer pure zinc layer (eta phase) + inner Fe-Zn alloy layers (zeta: FeZn₁₃, delta: FeZn₇, gamma: Fe₃Zn₁₀). Total coating thickness: 45-200 μm (depending on steel thickness and immersion time). Coating weight: 300-1200 g/m². Service life in rural atmosphere: 50-100+ years to first maintenance at 85 μm coating thickness. In industrial/marine atmosphere: 15-50 years. Zinc consumption: 30-50 kg per tonne of galvanized steel.

**Electrogalvanizing**: Electroplate zinc from ZnSO₄ or ZnCl₂ electrolyte onto steel strip at 20-50 A/dm². Coating thickness: 2.5-20 μm (thinner than hot-dip). Advantages: precise thickness control, no Fe-Zn alloy layer (better formability — coating does not crack during stamping), one-side coating possible. Used for automotive body panels. Disadvantage: no Fe-Zn alloy layer means lower abrasion resistance than hot-dip.

## Zinc Oxide Production

**Direct process (American process)**: Reduce zinc metal (or zinc-containing scrap) and oxidize the zinc vapor in air. Charge metallic zinc (or zinc dross, 85-95% Zn) into a refractory furnace. Heat to 1000-1200°C — zinc vaporizes (bp 907°C). Zinc vapor contacts air and oxidizes: 2Zn + O₂ → 2ZnO. ZnO particles form as white smoke (fume), collected in bag filters (fabric filter bags at 150-250°C, PTFE or fiberglass media, 99.9% collection efficiency for submicron particles). Particle size: 0.1-1.0 μm (controlled by vapor concentration, temperature, and cooling rate). Production rate: 10-50 kg ZnO per hour per furnace. Purity: 99.0-99.7% ZnO. Impurities: PbO (0.01-0.5%), CdO (0.001-0.05%), Fe₂O₃ (0.001-0.01%).

**Indirect process (French process)**: Melt zinc ingots (99.99% purity) in a crucible at 500-600°C. Vaporize the zinc at 900-1000°C. Channel the zinc vapor into a combustion chamber where it burns in air with a bright blue-white flame (the same reaction that produces the blue flame when burning zinc in air). ZnO fume is collected in bag filters. Higher purity than direct process: 99.5-99.9% ZnO (uses pure zinc feedstock). Particle size: 0.05-0.5 μm (finer, more reactive). Used for rubber vulcanization (activates organic accelerators — 3-8 phr ZnO in rubber compounds), ceramics (glaze opacifier and flux at 800-1200°C), pharmaceuticals (zinc oxide ointment, 10-40% ZnO in petrolatum base for diaper rash and sun protection — ZnO blocks UV-A and UV-B), and pigments (Chinese white, zinc white — less opaque than TiO₂ but lower cost).

**Wet process**: Precipitate ZnO from zinc sulfate solution by reaction with sodium carbonate: ZnSO₄ + Na₂CO₃ → ZnCO₃↓ + Na₂SO₄. Filter and wash the zinc carbonate precipitate. Calcine at 300-500°C: ZnCO₃ → ZnO + CO₂. Product: very fine ZnO (0.01-0.1 μm), high surface area (10-50 m²/g), used for specialized applications (sunscreen — nano ZnO is transparent on skin, catalyst). Production cost: 2-3× higher than thermal processes.

## Properties and Applications

**Physical properties of zinc**: Atomic number 30, atomic weight 65.38. Density: 7.13 g/cm³ (lighter than copper 8.96, heavier than aluminum 2.70). Melting point: 419.5°C. Boiling point: 907°C. Thermal conductivity: 116 W/(m·K) at 25°C (good — 30% of copper). Electrical conductivity: 16.6 × 10⁶ S/m (27% IACS — moderate conductor). Coefficient of thermal expansion: 30.2 × 10⁻⁶/°C (high — similar to lead, much higher than steel at 12 × 10⁻⁶/°C). Crystal structure: hexagonal close-packed (HCP) — limited slip systems make zinc brittle at room temperature; it becomes ductile above 100°C.

**Mechanical properties of pure zinc**: Tensile strength: 110-150 MPa (as-cast). Elongation: 2-10% at room temperature (brittle), 40-60% at 100-150°C (ductile). Hardness: 40-50 HB (soft — comparable to aluminum). Creep: zinc creeps significantly at room temperature under sustained load (unlike steel and aluminum) — zinc roofing sheets sag over time if not adequately supported (maximum span: 600-900 mm between supports for 0.6-0.8 mm thick sheet). Zinc is NOT suitable for structural applications — its role is corrosion protection, alloying, and die casting.

**Corrosion protection mechanism**: Zinc protects steel by two mechanisms: (1) Barrier — the zinc coating physically isolates the steel from the environment. (2) Cathodic (sacrificial) protection — zinc is anodic to steel (electrode potential Zn²⁺/Zn = -0.76 V vs. SHE; Fe²⁺/Fe = -0.44 V). When the coating is scratched and steel is exposed, zinc corrodes preferentially (sacrificially) and protects the underlying steel from rusting. Even a bare steel area of 5-10 mm diameter will not rust as long as zinc surrounds it within ~5 mm — the zinc provides electrical protection through the electrolyte film on the surface. This is why galvanized steel resists corrosion even when scratched, dented, or cut.

## Cadmium Recovery

**Cadmium co-production**: Cadmium occurs with zinc in sphalerite at 0.1-0.5% Cd. During zinc roasting, Cd reports to the calcine as CdO. In electrolytic zinc plants, cadmium is leached along with zinc (CdO + H₂SO₄ → CdSO₄ + H₂O). During solution purification, cadmium is cemented out with zinc dust as a Cd-Cu sponge (80-90% Cd). This sponge is re-dissolved in dilute H₂SO₄, purified, and electrowon in a separate circuit: Cd²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cd at cadmium cathodes, current density 40-80 A/m², cell voltage 2.5-2.8 V. Cadmium melting point: 321°C. Cast into sticks or balls. Global cadmium production: 20,000-25,000 tonnes/year (byproduct of zinc). Uses: Ni-Cd batteries (Cd anode, NiOOH cathode, 1.2V/cell, 40-60 Wh/kg, 500-2000 cycles), pigments (cadmium yellow CdS, cadmium red CdSe — brilliant, light-fast colors), and electroplating (cadmium plating on steel fasteners for aerospace — excellent corrosion resistance in marine environments, 5-25 μm coating thickness). Cadmium is toxic (kidney damage, Itai-itai disease from Cd-contaminated rice in Japan, 1950s-60s) — occupational exposure limits: 0.002 mg/m³ (OSHA).

## Environmental and Safety

**Zinc toxicity**: Zinc is relatively low-toxicity to humans (essential trace element, RDA 8-11 mg/day). Zinc fume fever: inhalation of zinc oxide fume (particle size <1 μm, generated by welding or cutting galvanized steel) causes flu-like symptoms 4-8 hours after exposure — fever, chills, muscle aches, headache. Resolves in 24-48 hours without treatment. Not cumulative. Prevent by adequate ventilation and respiratory protection when welding galvanized steel. Zinc phosphide (Zn₃P₂, used as rodenticide) is highly toxic — releases phosphine gas (PH₃) in the stomach.

**Zinc in the environment**: Zinc is the 24th most abundant element in Earth's crust (70 ppm). Essential micronutrient for plants (deficiency causes stunting, chlorosis at <15-20 mg Zn/kg soil) and animals (zinc fingers in DNA-binding proteins, >300 zinc-dependent enzymes). Toxic to aquatic life at low concentrations: LC₅₀ for Daphnia magna: 70-680 μg/L (48-hour); for rainbow trout: 170-680 μg/L (96-hour). Effluent limit: 0.5-1.0 mg/L total zinc. Zinc-galvanized structures in soil: zinc slowly corrodes (1-5 μm/year in neutral pH soil) — minimal environmental impact. Acidic soils (pH <5.5) accelerate zinc dissolution.

**Recycling**: Zinc is 100% recyclable without loss of properties. Global zinc recycling rate: ~30% (lower than lead at 95% or aluminum at 70% because much zinc is used as coating (galvanizing) which dissipates into the environment over decades). Recycled zinc sources: brass scrap (sorted by color and composition), zinc die-cast scrap (sorted by alloy), galvanized steel scrap (zinc recovered as ZnO in EAF dust via Waelz kiln — rotary kiln at 1100-1200°C, carbon reduces ZnO to Zn vapor, re-oxidized and captured as crude ZnO powder, 50-65% Zn). Energy savings from recycling vs. primary production: ~70% (electrowinning is energy-intensive at 3.0-3.5 kWh/kg; remelting requires only ~0.5 kWh/kg).

## Historical Production

**Ancient brass (pre-metallic zinc)**: The Romans produced brass by the cementation process long before metallic zinc was isolated. Heat copper metal with zinc carbonate (calamine/smithsonite) and charcoal in a sealed crucible at 1000-1100°C for 4-8 hours. Zinc oxide is reduced to zinc vapor, which immediately alloys with copper: Cu + Zn(vapor) → Cu-Zn alloy. Maximum zinc content achievable: ~28% (limited by zinc vapor pressure in the crucible). Roman brass coins (aes aurichalcum, "golden copper") contained 15-28% Zn. The cementation process remained the only way to make brass until metallic zinc was produced in India/China circa 1200-1400 CE and in Europe circa 1740 (William Champion, Bristol, UK — vertical retort process).

**Indian zinc production**: The Zawar mines in Rajasthan, India, produced metallic zinc by distillation from the 12th-14th centuries — the earliest known industrial zinc production. Retorts (earthenware, 150-200 mm diameter, 300-400 mm long) charged with ZnS concentrate + organic matter (rice husks, cow dung). Heated in inverted position in a furnace — zinc vapor condensed in a cooler collection vessel below. Estimated production: 100-500 tonnes of zinc per year over 200+ years. Archaeological evidence: >200,000 retort fragments found at the site.

**18th-19th century European zinc**: William Champion patented the vertical retort process in 1738 (UK). Johann Ruberg built the first horizontal retort zinc smelter at Lieja (Silesia, now Poland) in 1798. By 1850, Silesian zinc smelters produced 20,000-30,000 tonnes/year using 5,000-10,000 horizontal retorts. Galvanizing invented by Stanislas Sorel in 1837 (France) — patent for coating iron with zinc by dipping in molten zinc after acid pickling. The demand for galvanized steel (corrugated roofing, water tanks, telegraph wire) drove zinc production from 50,000 tonnes in 1850 to 500,000 tonnes by 1900. Electrolytic zinc process developed by Anaconda Copper Company (Montana, USA) in 1916 — now dominates (>80% of world production).

## Sheet and Strip Production

**Rolling**: Cast zinc slabs (200-400 mm thick) are hot-rolled at 150-250°C (above zinc's recrystallization temperature of ~100°C but well below melting point) to 2-10 mm strip. Roll force: 200-500 tonnes. Speed: 50-200 m/min. Cold-roll to final gauge (0.2-2.0 mm) at room temperature — zinc work-hardens rapidly and must be annealed between passes at 100-150°C for 1-4 hours. Zinc strip for battery cans: 0.25-0.50 mm, tensile strength 150-200 MPa, elongation 20-40%. Zinc strip for building applications (flashings, gutters): 0.6-0.8 mm, must meet ASTM B69 (minimum tensile 110 MPa, minimum elongation 20%).

**Zinc sheet in construction**: Flat zinc sheet used for roofing, flashing, and rainwater goods (gutters, downspouts) in Europe since the 19th century. Zinc roofing sheet: 0.7-1.0 mm thick, rolled in long coils (500-1000 mm wide, 20-50 m long). Installed with standing seams (20-30 mm high folds at joints, no solder or sealant required — thermal expansion absorbed by the fold). Service life: 80-120 years in urban atmospheres (zinc forms a protective patina of ZnCO₃·Zn(OH)₂ — basic zinc carbonate, a dense, adherent gray layer that slows further corrosion). Patina formation takes 6-24 months depending on atmospheric SO₂ and CO₂ content. Corrosion rate after patina formation: 1-3 μm/year in rural/urban atmospheres, 3-8 μm/year in marine/industrial atmospheres.

## Zinc in Batteries

**Zinc-carbon (Leclanché) cell**: The cheapest primary (non-rechargeable) battery. Zinc can (anode, 0.25-0.50 mm thick, simultaneously serves as container and negative electrode) filled with electrolyte paste (NH₄Cl 15-25%, ZnCl₂ 5-10%, water, starch or flour thickener). Central carbon rod (cathode current collector) surrounded by MnO₂ depolarizer (electrolytic manganese dioxide, 85-95% MnO₂, mixed with carbon black 5-10% for conductivity). Cell voltage: 1.5V open circuit. Energy density: 60-100 Wh/kg. Discharge: Zn + 2MnO₂ + 2NH₄Cl → ZnCl₂ + 2MnOOH + 2NH₃. Shelf life: 2-4 years at 20°C. Zinc corrosion (self-discharge) limits shelf life — amalgamation with Hg (0.1-1.0% Hg) was historically used to suppress corrosion, but mercury is now banned in most countries. Modern zinc-carbon cells use cleaner zinc alloys (Zn + 0.01-0.05% In, Ga, or Bi) to reduce gassing.

**Alkaline zinc-MnO₂ cell**: Zinc powder anode (high surface area, 50-200 m²/g, amalgamated or alloyed) in KOH electrolyte (30-40% KOH, gelled with carboxymethyl cellulose). MnO₂ cathode (electrolytic grade). Cell voltage: 1.5V. Energy density: 100-150 Wh/kg (50-80% higher than zinc-carbon). Advantages: higher capacity, better high-rate performance, lower internal resistance (10-50 mΩ vs. 100-500 mΩ for zinc-carbon). Discharge: Zn + 2MnO₂ → ZnO + Mn₂O₃. Zinc powder production: atomize molten zinc (450-500°C) with high-pressure air or inert gas through a nozzle — the spray solidifies into irregular powder particles (50-300 μm diameter). See [Energy Storage](../energy/storage.md).

**Zinc-air battery**: Atmospheric oxygen serves as cathode reactant — no need to carry oxidizer internally. Zinc anode (cast or pressed zinc plate or zinc powder in KOH gel) + air cathode (porous carbon with MnO₂ catalyst, PTFE binder, 0.2-0.5 mm thick, allows O₂ diffusion from air). Cell voltage: 1.65V open circuit. Energy density: 200-400 Wh/kg (very high — limited only by zinc, not by stored oxidizer). Used for hearing aid batteries (button cell, 5-15 mAh, 1-4 weeks of use) and stationary energy storage (100 kWh - 10 MWh units, electrically rechargeable or mechanically rechargeable by replacing zinc electrodes). Discharge: 2Zn + O₂ → 2ZnO. Recharge (electrical): reverse reaction, but zinc redeposition is non-uniform (dendrite formation shorts the cell) — limited to ~100-500 cycles with current technology.

## Zinc Dust and Powder

**Production**: Atomize molten zinc with high-pressure air (0.3-1.0 MPa) — produces spherical particles 5-150 μm diameter. Distillation: evaporate zinc at 900-1000°C and condense in a cold chamber with inert atmosphere — produces extremely fine powder (1-10 μm, high surface area). Ball milling: grind zinc flakes or granules in a ball mill (steel balls, 50-200 rpm, 4-24 hours) — produces flake-shaped particles (5-50 μm diameter, 0.5-2.0 μm thick). Zinc dust: <45 μm particle size (passes 325 mesh sieve). Zinc powder: 45-150 μm.

**Applications of zinc dust**: (1) Zinc-rich paints: 85-95% zinc dust in an organic (epoxy) or inorganic (ethyl silicate) binder. Applied at 50-150 μm dry film thickness, providing cathodic protection to steel substrate — the zinc particles are in electrical contact with each other and the steel, forming a galvanic cell. Zinc loading must exceed the critical pigment volume concentration (CPVC, typically 60-65% by volume) to ensure particle-to-particle contact. Service life: 15-30 years to first maintenance in marine atmosphere at 75 μm dry film thickness. (2) Sherardizing: tumble steel parts with zinc dust at 350-450°C for 2-12 hours in a rotating drum — zinc diffuses into the steel surface forming a Zn-Fe alloy layer (10-75 μm thick). Produces a uniform, matte gray coating on complex shapes (nuts, bolts, springs) that cannot be hot-dip galvanized. Coating composition: gamma phase (Fe₃Zn₁₀) nearest steel, delta phase (FeZn₇) in the middle, zeta phase (FeZn₁₃) at the surface. (3) Metallurgical reducing agent: zinc dust used to precipitate gold from cyanide solution (Merrill-Crowe process: 2Au(CN)₂⁻ + Zn → 2Au + Zn(CN)₄²⁻) and to cement cadmium and copper from zinc sulfate solution in electrolytic zinc plants.

## Zinc in Medicine and Biology

**Zinc as essential nutrient**: Zinc is the second most abundant trace element in the human body (after iron) — 2-4 g total body zinc. Over 300 enzymes require zinc as a cofactor (carbonic anhydrase, alcohol dehydrogenase, DNA/RNA polymerases, superoxide dismutase). Daily requirement: 8 mg (adult women) to 11 mg (adult men). Zinc deficiency symptoms: growth retardation (children gain 0.5-1.0 cm/year less height), impaired immune function (40% increased risk of pneumonia in zinc-deficient children), delayed wound healing, hair loss, impaired taste/smell (hypogeusia). Zinc supplementation (10-20 mg/day as zinc sulfate or zinc gluconate) reduces duration of common cold by 1-2 days when started within 24 hours of symptom onset (meta-analysis of 13 RCTs). Zinc oxide in calamine lotion (ZnO + Fe₂O₃ in a water-glycerin base) relieves itching from insect bites, poison ivy, and sunburn by forming a protective drying barrier on the skin.

**Zinc phosphide rodenticide**: Zn₃P₂ (density 4.6 g/cm³, dark gray, mp 420°C). When ingested by rodents, stomach acid releases phosphine gas (PH₃): Zn₃P₂ + 6HCl → 3ZnCl₂ + 2PH₃↑. PH₃ is a potent metabolic poison — disrupts mitochondrial electron transport. Lethal dose (LD₅₀) in rats: 12-40 mg/kg. Bait formulation: 1-2% Zn₃P₂ in grain (wheat or rice) — lethal to a 250 g rat in 1-3 hours after ingesting 1-2 g of bait. No secondary poisoning risk (PH₃ dissipates — predators eating poisoned rodents are not affected, unlike anticoagulant rodenticides which accumulate). Handle with extreme care — toxic to humans at the same dose (occupational exposure limit: 0.5 mg/m³ for Zn₃P₂, 0.3 ppm for PH₃).

## Zinc-Based Solar Cells

**CZTS (Cu₂ZnSnS₄)**: A quaternary semiconductor for thin-film photovoltaic cells, composed of earth-abundant, non-toxic elements (unlike CdTe or CIGS which use scarce/expensive Cd, Te, In, Ga). Band gap: 1.4-1.5 eV (near-ideal for single-junction solar cells per the Shockley-Queisser limit). Crystal structure: kesterite (tetragonal). Fabrication: sputter or solution-deposit Cu, Zn, Sn, and S precursors onto molybdenum-coated glass substrate, then anneal at 500-600°C in sulfur vapor. Efficiency record: 12.6% (2018, IBM) — still well below the theoretical maximum of ~32% and below competing technologies (CIGS at 23.4%, CdTe at 22.1%). Main challenges: difficult to control Cu/Zn disorder in the crystal lattice, secondary phases (ZnS, Cu₂SnS₃) form easily and degrade performance, and VOC deficit (open-circuit voltage is 500-700 mV below the band gap potential — indicates high recombination). Research continues due to the compelling advantage of using only abundant, inexpensive elements.

**ZnO transparent conductor**: Zinc oxide doped with aluminum (AZO, Al-doped ZnO, typically 2% Al₂O₃ in ZnO) or gallium (GZO) is a transparent conductive oxide (TCO) — simultaneously transparent to visible light (>80% transmission at 400-700 nm) and electrically conductive (resistivity 2-8 × 10⁻⁴ Ω·cm). Used as a lower-cost alternative to indium tin oxide (ITO, In₂O₃:Sn) for transparent electrodes in solar cells, flat-panel displays, and touch screens. Sputter deposit AZO films (200-500 nm thick) from a ZnO:Al₂O₃ ceramic target at room temperature in Ar/O₂ atmosphere. Sheet resistance: 5-20 Ω/sq. Indium is scarce and expensive ($300-800/kg) — zinc is abundant and cheap ($2-3/kg), making AZO attractive for large-area applications despite slightly higher resistivity than ITO.

## Zinc in Agriculture

**Zinc fertilizer**: Zinc sulfate monohydrate (ZnSO₄·H₂O, 36% Zn) is the most common zinc fertilizer, applied at 5-25 kg/ha to correct zinc-deficient soils. Zinc deficiency is the most widespread micronutrient deficiency in world agriculture — affects 50% of cereal-growing soils globally (particularly calcareous, high-pH soils where Zn is immobilized as ZnCO₃ and Zn(OH)₂). Chelated zinc (Zn-EDTA, 14% Zn) is more effective on high-pH soils (EDTA keeps Zn soluble at pH >7) but costs 5-10× more than zinc sulfate. Foliar spray: 0.5-1.0% ZnSO₄ solution at 200-400 L/ha, applied 2-3 times during the growing season. Zinc increases wheat yield by 10-25% on deficient soils. Rice: zinc deficiency causes "khaira disease" (brown spots on leaves, stunted growth) — controlled by dipping seedling roots in 2% ZnO suspension before transplanting.

**Zinc in animal nutrition**: Zinc oxide (ZnO) added to livestock feed at 100-3000 mg/kg (pharmacological dose for post-weaning diarrhea control in piglets — ZnO at 2000-3000 mg/kg reduces diarrhea incidence by 30-50% and improves growth rate by 10-20%). Standard dietary requirement: 40-100 mg Zn/kg feed for cattle, swine, and poultry. Zinc proteinate (chelated Zn with amino acids) has higher bioavailability than ZnO (120-150% relative bioavailability vs. 100% for ZnSO₄). Environmental concern: high-dose ZnO in pig feed leads to Zn accumulation in manure (500-2000 mg Zn/kg dry manure vs. 100-300 mg/kg without supplementation) — repeated application of Zn-rich manure to farmland elevates soil Zn to potentially phytotoxic levels (>300 mg/kg).

## Zinc-Anode Coatings for Steel in Concrete

**Galvanized rebar**: Hot-dip galvanized steel reinforcing bar (rebar) for concrete structures exposed to chlorides (marine environments, bridge decks, parking structures). Coating thickness: 85-200 μm (heavier than structural galvanizing — concrete is highly alkaline, pH 12.5-13.5, and zinc corrodes slowly in alkaline solution). In concrete, zinc initially reacts with Ca(OH)₂ to form calcium hydrozincate (Ca[Zn(OH)₃]₂·2H₂O), which passivates the surface. Zinc corrodes at 0.5-2.0 μm/year in sound concrete (no chloride penetration) — a 100 μm coating provides 50-200 years of service life before the steel is exposed. When chlorides reach the rebar level, zinc provides additional decades of cathodic protection before the coating is consumed. Service life of galvanized rebar in marine concrete: 75-150 years (vs. 25-50 years for uncoated black rebar, 50-100 years for epoxy-coated rebar). Cost premium: 20-35% over black rebar.

## Recycling and Sustainability

**Zinc recycling economics**: Zinc is recovered from three main scrap streams: (1) Brass scrap: sorted by color and spark-tested for composition, remelted in induction furnaces at 1000-1100°C with flux cover (borax + silica) to prevent zinc volatilization. Zinc recovery: >98%. (2) Galvanized steel scrap: fed to electric arc furnaces (EAF) for steel recycling. Zinc boils off (bp 907°C, well below steel melting point of 1538°C) and is collected as EAF dust (15-30% Zn). The dust is processed in a Waelz kiln (rotary kiln, 50 m long × 3.5 m diameter, 1100-1200°C, coke breeze reductant) — zinc is reduced, vaporized, re-oxidized, and collected in a bag filter as crude ZnO (50-65% Zn). This crude ZnO is fed to primary zinc smelters. Waelz kiln recovery: 85-95% of zinc in EAF dust. (3) Die-cast scrap: Zamak parts sorted by alloy grade, remelted at 400-450°C. Closed-loop recycling in die-casting foundries — scrap runners and flash are remelted with minimal treatment. Overall zinc recycling rate: 30-40% globally (limited by dissipation of zinc from galvanized structures into the environment over decades of service).

## Architectural Zinc

**Zinc roofing and cladding**: Rolled zinc sheet (0.7-1.0 mm, Ti-Cu alloyed zinc — Zn + 0.06-0.20% Ti + 0.08-0.15% Cu for improved creep resistance and tensile strength) used for roofing, wall cladding, and rainwater goods. Installed with standing seams or batten seams (folded joints, no solder). Titanium content refines the grain structure, preventing the intergranular creep that causes pure zinc sheets to sag between supports. Sheet width: 500-1000 mm, coil length: 10-50 m. Typical roof panel: 0.8 mm thick, 530 mm wide, installed on 220 mm centers with 25 mm standing seams.

**Patina development**: New zinc has a bright silver metallic appearance. Over 6-24 months of atmospheric exposure, it develops a natural patina of zinc carbonate hydroxide (Zn₄CO₃(OH)₆·H₂O — hydrozincite) that gives the surface its characteristic blue-gray matte finish. The patina is self-healing — scratches that expose fresh zinc rapidly form new patina. Pre-weathered zinc (artificially patinated by acid/phosphate treatment at the factory) is available for architects who want the aged appearance immediately. Color stability: the blue-gray patina is permanent and does not continue to darken — zinc roofs from the 19th century in Paris and Brussels look the same as 10-year-old roofs.

**Zinc wall cladding**: Flat lock panels (300-600 mm × 600-1200 mm, interlocking folded edges) or rhomboid tiles (diagonal flat-lock panels) create a smooth, monolithic building skin. Joint width: 20-25 mm (visible seam). Wind load resistance: 1.5-3.0 kPa (suction) depending on panel size and fastener spacing. Impact resistance: hail resistance up to 25 mm diameter without denting (0.8 mm zinc at 45° slope). Fire resistance: non-combustible (zinc melts at 420°C but does not ignite — classified A1 per EN 13501-1). Acoustic performance: zinc panels on a 100 mm mineral wool insulation substrate achieve Rw 45-55 dB sound reduction. Thermal movement: zinc expands 22 mm per 10 m per 100°C temperature change — joints must accommodate this (standing seams and sliding clips allow thermal movement without buckling). Installed cost: $40-80/m² for zinc cladding (vs. $25-50/m² for painted steel, $60-120/m² for copper, $80-150/m² for stainless steel).

## Zinc Anodes for Cathodic Protection

**Sacrificial anodes**: Zinc anodes protect buried steel pipelines, ship hulls, offshore platforms, and underground storage tanks from corrosion. The zinc anode is electrically connected to the steel structure and buried in the same electrolyte (soil or seawater). Because zinc is more electrochemically active than steel (Zn²⁺/Zn = -0.76 V vs. Fe²⁺/Fe = -0.44 V SHE), the zinc corrodes preferentially, supplying protective current to the steel surface. In seawater: zinc anode output current ~12 mA/m² of exposed steel surface at a driving potential of ~0.25V. Anode consumption rate: 10-12 kg/A-year (Faraday's law: 1 kg of zinc supplies ~820 Ah of protective current). For a 50 m buried steel pipeline requiring 50 mA of protection: annual zinc consumption = 50 mA × 10 kg/A-year × 1 year = 0.5 kg/year. A 15 kg zinc anode provides 30 years of protection.

**Anode composition**: MIL-SPEC zinc anodes (MIL-A-18001): Zn + 0.1-0.5% Al + 0.005-0.05% Cd + 0.001-0.005% Ti. Aluminum prevents passivation (pure zinc develops an insulating ZnO/Zn(OH)₂ film in some soils, reducing current output). Cadmium (now being phased out for environmental reasons) improves uniform corrosion (prevents intergranular attack). Zinc anode casting: pour molten zinc (450-480°C) into steel or cast iron molds, insert a steel core rod (for structural attachment) during casting. Anode sizes: 10-100 kg for pipeline anodes, 5-50 kg for hull anodes, 0.5-5 kg for tank anodes. Backfill: anodes buried in soil are packed in a chemical backfill (75% gypsum CaSO₄·2H₂O + 20% bentonite clay + 5% Na₂SO₄) to maintain low resistivity contact with the surrounding soil (<5 Ω·m).

## Zinc in Coinage

**Zinc in coins**: Many modern coins use zinc as a core material (cheaper than copper or nickel). US cent (penny) since 1982: zinc core (97.5% Zn, 2.5% Cu electroplated shell) — weight 2.5 g, diameter 19.05 mm. Pre-1982 cents were 95% Cu, 5% Zn (weight 3.11 g). The switch saved ~$30 million/year in copper costs at 1982 metal prices. Euro 1 and 2 coins: "Nordic Gold" alloy (Cu 89%, Al 5%, Zn 5%, Sn 1%) — the zinc improves golden color and tarnish resistance. Canadian 5-cent (nickel): pure nickel plating over steel core (1982-1999); since 2000, cupro-nickel plating over steel, with some issues using zinc in the alloy. Zinc coins are resistant to corrosion in normal handling (perspiration pH 4-7) but dissolve in strong acids or alkalis. Vending machine compatibility: zinc coins have the correct electrical conductivity (27% IACS for pure Zn) and magnetic signature (non-magnetic) to pass coin validators.

## Global Zinc Supply Chain

**Mine production**: Global zinc mine production: ~12-13 million tonnes Zn content per year (2023). Top producing countries: China (35%), Peru (12%), Australia (10%), India (7%), USA (5%), Mexico (5%). Major mines: Red Dog (Alaska, USA, 500,000 t/year Zn), Rampura Agucha (India, 600,000 t/year), Antamina (Peru, 350,000 t/year), McArthur River (Australia, 280,000 t/year). Mine life: 10-30 years for major deposits. Depletion rate: ~5% of reserves consumed per year — sufficient known reserves for ~40-50 years at current production rates. Exploration: zinc deposits are typically found alongside lead and silver (polymetallic VHMS or SEDEX deposits) — exploration for zinc also discovers lead, silver, copper, and gold.

**Smelter production**: RLE (roast-leach-electrowin) plants dominate modern zinc production. Largest plants: Korea Zinc (Onsan, South Korea, 600,000 t/year), Nyrstar (Budel, Netherlands, 280,000 t/year), Hindustan Zinc (India, 800,000 t/year total across multiple sites), Glencore (multiple sites globally, 1.1 million t/year total). Energy intensity: 3.0-3.5 kWh/kg Zn for electrowinning + 1.0-2.0 kWh/kg for roasting and auxiliary processes = 4.0-5.5 kWh/kg total. At $0.05-0.10/kWh electricity cost, energy represents 15-30% of total production cost. Total production cost: $1,800-2,500/t Zn (varies with electricity price, ore grade, and byproduct credits for sulfuric acid and cadmium).

## Zinc in Rubber Vulcanization

**ZnO as vulcanization activator**: Zinc oxide is used at 3-8 phr (parts per hundred rubber) in virtually all sulfur-vulcanized rubber compounds (tires, hoses, belts, seals, shoe soles). ZnO reacts with stearic acid (1-3 phr) to form zinc stearate in situ, which solubilizes the sulfur-based accelerator (e.g., MBTS, TMTD, CBS) and dramatically increases the vulcanization rate. Without ZnO, sulfur vulcanization of natural rubber at 150°C takes 60-120 minutes to reach 90% cure; with 5 phr ZnO + 2 phr stearic acid + 1 phr accelerator, cure time drops to 5-15 minutes. This is economically essential — a tire press cycle of 10-20 minutes per tire depends on rapid vulcanization. Alternative activators (MgO, CaO) are 3-10× slower. Global ZnO consumption for rubber: ~500,000 tonnes/year (30-35% of total ZnO production). Environmental concern: ZnO is toxic to aquatic organisms at low concentrations (EC₅₀ for Daphnia magna: 60-160 μg/L). Tire wear particles containing ZnO wash into waterways — estimated 10,000-40,000 tonnes of Zn released annually from tire wear globally. Research into reduced-ZnO and ZnO-free rubber compounds is ongoing (MgO-based alternatives show promise but reduce cure rate by 30-50%).

## Zinc Dust in Corrosion-Resistant Primers

**Zinc-rich primers** (expanded detail): Two-component epoxy zinc-rich primer consists of zinc dust (85-93% by weight in the dry film) dispersed in an epoxy resin binder (Epon 828 or equivalent bisphenol-A epoxy, 5-10% by weight) cured with a polyamide or amine hardener. The key requirement: zinc particle loading must exceed the critical pigment volume concentration (CPVC, typically 62-67% by volume) so that zinc particles are in direct electrical contact with each other and with the steel substrate. Below CPVC, binder fills the gaps between particles and electrical contact is broken — the cathodic protection mechanism fails and the coating provides only barrier protection (inferior). Film thickness: 50-100 μm dry. Applied by airless spray (1500-2500 psi, 0.015-0.021" tip) in a single coat. Overcoat with an epoxy intermediate coat (100-200 μm) and polyurethane topcoat (50-75 μm) for appearance and UV resistance. Total system dry film thickness: 200-375 μm. Service life in C5-M marine environment (ISO 12944): 15-25 years to first maintenance. Zinc consumption rate: ~5-15 μm/year of the zinc-rich layer (the zinc slowly corrodes to protect the steel — the primer is a consumable coating).
