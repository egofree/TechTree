# Shell

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: energy, metals, silicon

The cylindrical outer structure of a blast furnace, boiler, or pressure vessel. The shell contains the reaction zone and must withstand high temperatures, thermal cycling, and internal pressure.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Shell design appears in the [Blast Furnace](../metals/iron-steel/blast-furnace.md) for iron smelting, [Steam Power](../energy/steam-power.md) boilers, [Electric Furnaces](../energy/electric-furnaces.md), [Aluminum](../metals/aluminum.md) reduction cells, and [Mg-Si Production](../silicon/mg-si-production.md). In each case, the shell must contain the process while allowing material flow and heat management.

## Technical Details

Blast furnace shell: cylindrical shaft of stone or brick, typically 6-10 m tall, widening from throat (top, ~2 m diameter) to bosh (widest part, ~4 m diameter), then narrowing to the hearth (bottom, ~1.5 m diameter). The profile creates a column of burden that moves downward under gravity while hot gases rise through it.

Boiler shells are riveted or welded steel cylinders designed to withstand steam pressure. Early boilers used riveted construction; modern practice uses welded seams. Shell thickness is calculated from the operating pressure, diameter, and material strength using the thin-wall pressure vessel formula: t = P·D/(2·S), where P is pressure, D is diameter, and S is allowable stress.

Refractory lining inside the shell protects the steel from direct contact with molten metal or slag. Water cooling of the shell (via external sprays or internal cooling plates) extends refractory life and prevents shell failure from overheating.
