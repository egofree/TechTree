# Loading

> **Type**: equipment | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, energy, machine-tools, metals

The process of placing raw materials, charge, or workpieces into processing equipment — kilns, furnaces, presses, and reactors. Loading procedures directly affect product quality, process efficiency, and equipment safety. In kiln firing, loading density determines airflow and temperature uniformity. In powder metallurgy, encapsulating parts in sealed containers before hot isostatic pressing prevents gas from entering surface-connected pores.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Loading appears throughout the ceramics, metals, and energy domains. Lime kilns are loaded with 10-20 cm limestone pieces and fuel (coke or charcoal) in alternating layers through the top charge hole. Pottery kilns require careful placement of ware to allow hot gas circulation — overloaded kilns fire unevenly. Electric arc furnaces charge scrap steel via basket or conveyor, with charge weight of 1-100 tonnes. Blast furnaces charge alternating layers of iron ore, coke, and limestone at the top, with the burden descending by gravity as material is consumed at the bottom.

The loading pattern determines process uniformity. In lime kilns, stone size grading prevents channeling (where gas flows preferentially through gaps between large pieces, leaving small pieces under-fired). In blast furnaces, burden distribution controls gas flow and reduction efficiency across the furnace cross-section.

## Technical Details

For batch processes (kilns, pot furnaces), loading follows a pattern optimized for the specific process. Pottery kilns stack ware on shelves with 5-10 cm clearance between pieces. Lime kilns alternate fuel and stone layers with careful size grading. For continuous processes (blast furnaces, rotary kilns), charging is ongoing — skip hoists or conveyor belts deliver charge material to the top while product is tapped from the bottom.

In powder metallurgy, encapsulation loading involves sealing parts in a glass or metal container under vacuum before hot isostatic pressing. Surface-connected porosity must be encapsulated to prevent pressurizing gas from equalizing pressure inside pores, which would prevent collapse during the HIP cycle.
