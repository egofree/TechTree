# Technique

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: glass, health, machine-tools, plants, polymers

After cleaning and shaping the root canal, gutta-percha points are coated with sealer (zinc oxide-eugenol or epoxy-based cement) and packed into the canal. Heat-softened gutta-percha flows to fill irregularities. Lateral condensation (multiple points packed side by side with finger spreaders) or warm vertical compaction (System B Heat Source at 200°C softens gutta-percha for down-packing) are common techniques.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Technique refers to the specific methods, procedures, and skilled operations used to transform materials into useful products. Across the bootstrapping sequence, the right technique is often as important as the right material — a skilled glassblower can produce functional apparatus with simple tools, while a novice may waste expensive raw materials. In [Advanced Glassblowing](../glass/advanced-glassblowing.md), techniques such as lampworking, fusing, and annealing determine whether glass apparatus survives thermal stress. In [Machining](../machine-tools/machining.md), cutting speed, feed rate, and tool geometry constitute the technique that produces precision parts. In [Structural Plants](../plants/structural-plants.md), specific processing techniques unlock structural properties from natural fibers.

## Technical Details

Techniques accumulate through iterative refinement. Early practitioners develop empirical methods that are later systematized and optimized. For example, gutta-percha dental techniques evolved from simple cold-packed points to sophisticated warm vertical compaction that achieves three-dimensional canal seal. In polymer processing, the technique of heating gutta-percha to 80-100°C (above its glass transition of ~70°C) makes it pliable without decomposition, a narrow window that requires controlled application.

Glassworking techniques illustrate the progression: basic blowing and shaping at the furnace, progressing to precision lampworking with gas-oxygen torches for borosilicate, then to hydrogen-oxygen torch work for fused silica at 1800-2100°C. Each material demands its own technique, adapted to its thermal and mechanical properties.
