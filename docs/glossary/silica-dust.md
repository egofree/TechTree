# Silica Dust

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, mining

The primary health hazard in mining and mineral processing. Respirable crystalline silica particles (<10 µm) cause silicosis — an irreversible and fatal lung disease. Prevention through dust suppression is mandatory.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Silica dust hazards appear in [Cement Production](../chemistry/cement.md), [Drilling](../mining/drilling.md), and [Mining Processing](../mining/processing.md). Any process that generates fine silica particles — drilling, crushing, grinding, or sieving quartz-bearing rock — creates this hazard. Wet methods suppress 90-95% of dust generation.

## Technical Details

Rock drilling generates respirable crystalline silica particles smaller than 10 µm that penetrate deep into the lungs. The body's immune response creates fibrotic scar tissue that progressively reduces lung capacity. Silicosis has no cure — prevention is the only option.

Wet drilling: water is fed through the drill steel to the bit face, suppressing dust at the source. This reduces airborne dust by 90-95%. Where wet drilling is impractical, respiratory protection (P100 filters, minimum) is required. Ventilation systems in enclosed spaces must maintain air velocity above 0.5 m/s to prevent dust accumulation.

Additional hazards: crystalline silica is also a carcinogen (IARC Group 1). Chronic exposure increases risk of tuberculosis, lung cancer, and kidney disease. The permissible exposure limit is 50 µg/m³ (OSHA), but many operations exceed this without proper controls.
