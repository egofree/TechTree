# Cotton

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: plants, textiles

Requires warm climate, 150+ frost-free days, moderate but reliable water. Very labor-intensive to harvest by hand — each boll must be picked individually. Cottonseed can be pressed for oil (food/lamp fuel), making it a dual-purpose crop.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Cotton is the primary seed fiber in the tech tree and the only widely cultivated plant fiber that does not require retting or decortication. After ginning separates lint from seed, cotton fibers are carded, spun into yarn, and woven into cloth — the backbone textile material for clothing, medical bandaging, and industrial uses. The cottonseed byproduct yields oil for cooking or lamp fuel, and protein-rich meal for animal feed after gossypol removal.

## Technical Details

Cotton cultivation demands 150-180 frost-free days and consistent warmth (25-35°C optimal). Three cultivated species cover different climate zones: Upland cotton (*G. hirsutum*) for temperate and subtropical regions (90% of world production), Pima/Egyptian cotton (*G. barbadense*) for hot arid climates with extra-long staple (33-38 mm), and Tree cotton (*G. arboreum*) for tropical drought-prone areas.

Harvesting is labor-intensive: hand-picking yields 20-50 kg seed cotton per person per day, with multiple passes through the field as bolls open progressively over 4-6 weeks. Gin turnout is 30-42% lint by weight. Under hand-cultivation, yields range from 500 kg/ha (dryland) to 1,000-1,500 kg/ha (irrigated with improved varieties).

Ginning separates lint from seed. The roller gin (Churka gin) processes 2-5 kg clean lint per hour, preserving long-staple quality. The Whitney saw gin processes 50-200 kg lint per hour but damages long fibers, making it suitable only for short-staple upland cotton. Cotton is unique among natural fibers in becoming weaker when wet, a property that limits its use in marine applications.
