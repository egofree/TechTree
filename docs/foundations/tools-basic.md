### Stone & Wood Tools

**Knapping (flint/obsidian/chert)**:
- **Hard hammer percussion**: Round cobble hammerstone (quartzite, granite, 200-500 g) struck against core to remove large flakes. Produces crude blanks. Strike at the edge of the core at roughly 60 degrees, following the natural fracture plane of the stone. One solid blow removes more material than several tentative taps.
- **Soft hammer percussion**: Antler billet or hardwood club for finer flake removal. Thinner, more controlled flakes. The softer hammer absorbs some shock, reducing the chance of shattering the core. This stage shapes the blank into a rough preform.
- **Pressure flaking**: Antler tine or copper-tipped tool pressed against edge to remove tiny flakes for final shaping and sharpening. Produces edges sharper than surgical steel scalpels (obsidian ~3 nm edge width vs steel ~500 nm). Work slowly along the edge, removing flakes 2-5 mm wide. Pressure flaking is how you get the final cutting edge and symmetrical form.
- **Heat treatment**: Some cherts improve flaking quality after slow heating to 250-350°C in sand or ash, held at temperature for several hours, then cooled slowly. Heat treatment makes the stone more glass-like and easier to pressure flake. Test on a small sample before committing good material. Overheating causes crazing (surface cracks) and ruins the stone.
- **Tool types**: Hand axes (bifacial), scrapers (hide processing, wood shaping), projectile points (hunting), drill bits (rotating between hands or with bow drill attachment), burins (engraving, scoring), knives (cutting, butchering).

**Strengths**:
- Obsidian pressure-flaked edges are ~3 nm wide — sharper than surgical steel scalpels (~500 nm), producing cleaner cuts with less tissue damage
- Flint and chert are widely available in sedimentary geology — raw material is free and abundant in most regions
- Heat treatment at 250-350°C improves flaking quality of marginal cherts, expanding the range of usable stone

**Weaknesses**:
- Stone edges are brittle — obsidian fractures on contact with bone or hard materials, requiring frequent re-sharpening
- Heat treatment is risky: overheating causes crazing (surface cracks) that ruins the stone permanently
- Pressure flaking produces sharp edges but cannot create complex shapes like curved blades or hollow-ground tools

**Ground stone tools**:
- **Pecking**: Repeatedly striking granite, basalt, or greenstone with hammerstone to rough out shape. Pecking leaves a rough but controlled surface. The process is slow but produces tools far tougher than flaked stone, capable of withstanding heavy impact.
- **Grinding**: Rub against flat abrasive stone (sandstone) with water and sand slurry. Hours of grinding produce polished, sharp edges. A full-sized axe head takes 6-12 hours to grind to completion, depending on the hardness of the stone.
- **Tool types**: Axes (grooved for handle attachment), adzes (woodworking, blade set perpendicular to handle for hollowing), mortars and pestles (grain processing, 15-30 cm bowl depth), manos and metates (grain grinding, flat stone with hand-held grinder), grooved mauls (heavy splitting tools, 2-5 kg).
- **Hafting ground stone axes**: Cut a tapered slot in a hardwood handle, fit the grooved stone head into the slot, and bind tightly with rawhide or wet sinew. As the binding dries, it shrinks and locks the head in place. Add pine pitch or birch tar for extra security. A well-hafted ground stone axe can fell a 30 cm tree in 20-40 minutes.

**Strengths**:
- Ground stone axes withstand heavy impact that would shatter flaked stone — capable of felling 30 cm trees in 20-40 minutes
- Grinding produces polished surfaces that reduce friction and resist wear — ground axe heads last 10-50× longer than flaked equivalents
- Pecking and grinding techniques work with tough stone (granite, basalt, greenstone) that is too hard for knapping

**Weaknesses**:
- Grinding a full axe head takes 6-12 hours of continuous abrasion against sandstone with water slurry — extremely labor-intensive
- Hafting failure (binding loosens, adhesive cracks) sends the stone head flying — a significant safety hazard during tree felling
- Grooved axe heads require a specific stone shape (oval or rectangular with a circumferential groove) that not all stone sources provide

**Wooden tools and implements**:
- **Hafting**: Attach stone heads to wooden handles using sinew, rawhide, or plant-fiber cordage, secured with pine pitch or birch tar adhesive. The binding wraps around the stone head and handle in a figure-eight pattern. Wet rawhide shrinks as it dries, creating a tight, self-tightening grip. A properly hafted axe survives hundreds of strikes before re-binding.
- **Birch tar adhesive**: Heat birch bark in sealed container (similar to char cloth method) at 300-400°C. Black, sticky tar forms. Roll into balls. Soften with heat to apply. Strongest natural adhesive available. Birch tar is waterproof and remains slightly flexible, so it absorbs shock without cracking. Store tar balls wrapped in leaves in a cool, dry place. They keep indefinitely.
- **Handles**: Split green wood, shape with stone tools, dry slowly to prevent checking. Ash and hickory make the best tool handles because they absorb shock without splitting. A handle for a felling axe is 70-90 cm long, 3-4 cm thick at the grip, and flared at the end to prevent the hand from sliding off. A shorter handle (35-50 cm) suits a hatchet or adze.
- **Digging sticks**: Fire-harden tip of hardwood stick (heat until brown, not black) for soil penetration. A fire-hardened digging stick penetrates packed soil that would blunt a raw wood tip. Length 1-1.5 m, diameter 2-3 cm. Attach a foot-step (crosswise peg or stone weight lashed near the tip) to push with the foot for deeper digging.
- **Wooden bowls and spoons**: Carve from a greenwood block using a curved knife (crook knife) or burning and scraping. Burn the hollow with embers, scrape the char with a stone or shell, repeat until the bowl reaches the desired depth. Seal with oil or fat to reduce water absorption.

**Strengths**:
- Birch tar adhesive is the strongest natural adhesive available — waterproof, slightly flexible, and absorbs shock without cracking
- Fire-hardened digging sticks penetrate packed soil that would blunt raw wood tips — durable enough for repeated use
- Rawhide hafting shrinks as it dries, creating a self-tightening grip that holds stone heads securely through hundreds of strikes

**Weaknesses**:
- Birch tar requires heating birch bark to 300-400°C in a sealed container — hot tar causes severe burns and sticks to skin
- Wooden tool handles split if dried too fast (checking) — ash and hickory must be dried slowly over weeks
- Pine pitch adhesive softens above 80°C, limiting use near fires and forges

**Cordage and rope making**:
- **Fiber extraction**: Harvest plant stems (hemp, flax, jute, nettle, dogbane). Ret in water for 1-2 weeks to decompose pectin binding fibers. Beat stems against stone or wood to separate bast fibers from woody core (shives). Comb through coarse teeth to separate and align. The resulting long, aligned fibers are called tow or sliver.
- **Reverse-wrap technique (2-strand)**: Hold two fiber strands side by side. Twist both clockwise until they kink, then fold in half, and they naturally wrap counterclockwise around each other. Continue adding fibers by splicing in new strands before reaching the ends. Produces uniform cordage. This is the default method for making thin cordage (1-4 mm) used in binding, sewing, net-making, and fishing line.
- **3-strand rope**: Lay three completed 2-strand cords side by side and twist together. Stronger and more flexible than 2-strand. Rope diameter determined by starting strand thickness. Three-strand rope is the standard for loads, hauling, and construction.
- **Strength by diameter**: 1 cm hemp rope holds ~100 kg; 2 cm holds ~400 kg. Wet natural fiber rope is stronger than dry (fibers swell and grip tighter). These are working loads; breaking strength is 5-8 times higher, but never approach breaking strength in practice.
- **Splicing**: To join two ropes or repair breaks, unlay strands 10-15 cm, interleave with matching strands from the other end, and wrap tightly with thin cordage. A good splice retains ~80% of rope strength. Practice splicing on scrap rope until the tapered ends blend smoothly; a lumpy splice snags on pulleys and edges.
- **Rope maintenance**: Keep natural fiber rope dry when not in use. Rot and mildew weaken rope rapidly in damp storage. Coil properly (following the natural lay of the rope) to prevent kinks. Inspect ropes before critical use: look for chafed spots, broken strands, or discoloration indicating rot. Retire suspect rope to non-critical tasks.

**Strengths**:
- Hemp rope at 1 cm diameter holds ~100 kg working load; at 2 cm holds ~400 kg — sufficient for construction, hauling, and lifting
- Wet natural fiber rope is stronger than dry (fibers swell and grip tighter) — performs well in maritime and wet conditions
- Plant fiber cordage requires only retting (1-2 weeks in water) and hand-twisting — achievable from Year 0

**Weaknesses**:
- Retting takes 1-2 weeks and produces foul-smelling water from decomposing pectin — must be done away from drinking water sources
- Natural fiber rope degrades in damp storage: rot and mildew reduce breaking strength by 30-50% within months
- Splicing retains only ~80% of rope strength vs. a continuous rope — every joint is a weak point

**Antler and bone tools**:
- **Pressure flaker**: Antler tine (deer, elk) for pressure flaking flint. Harder than wood, softer than stone, making it ideal for controlled flake removal. An antler tine lasts for many hours of flaking before it rounds over. Sharpen on a coarse stone to restore the tip.
- **Awls**: Sharpened bone splinters (deer rib, bird bone) for piercing leather, basketry weaving, and drilling holes in wood. Awls don't remove material like a drill; they push fibers apart. Thread a cord through the hole and pull to enlarge it.
- **Fish hooks**: Carved from bone or antler with stone flakes. Barbed designs carved with a burin. Gorge hooks (straight double-pointed pieces lashed at midpoint) are the simplest form. Bait the gorge, swallow it lengthwise, and it turns sideways in the fish's throat. Composite hooks, with a bone point lashed to a wooden shank, allow larger hooks than a single bone piece permits.
- **Needles with eyes**: Split bone or antler, grind slender with abrasive stone, drill eye with stone drill bit or punch through with an awl. Essential for tailored clothing. Bone needles are surprisingly strong for their size; a well-made needle sews through heavy leather without breaking. Keep needles in a dry bone or bark tube to protect the fine tip.

**Woodworking joints (stone/bronze tools)**:
- **Mortise-and-tenon**: Cut rectangular hole (mortise) in one piece with chisel and auger; shape protruding tenon on mating piece. Peg through both for permanent joint. Foundation of timber framing. A tight fit should require light mallet taps to seat the tenon. Tenon width is roughly one-third of the timber thickness.
- **Dovetail**: Splay-cut interlocking pins and tails. Resists pull-out without fasteners. Used for chests, boxes, drawer joints. The angle of the splay is typically 1:6 to 1:8 along the thickness. Too steep and the joint weakens the pins; too shallow and it doesn't lock.
- **Lap joint (half-lap)**: Remove half the thickness from each piece where they overlap. Simple with stone adze. Used for framing, cross-bracing. A variant, the cross-lap, joins two pieces at any angle by notching each to half depth.
- **Pegged joints**: Drill holes through overlapping members, drive hardwood pegs (oak, ash). Pegs swell with moisture and lock tight. Replaces nails in timber construction. Offset the peg hole slightly (1-2 mm) toward the joint line to draw the pieces together as the peg is driven, creating a clamped effect called drawboring.
- **Edge jointing**: Join boards edge-to-edge for wide panels. Match the edges flat and true with a plane or sharp adze. Apply glue (hide glue, fish glue, or casein) and clamp with bar clamps or wedged frames. Peg through for reinforcement if the joint carries load.

**Shelter construction**:
- **Wattle-and-daub**: Weave flexible saplings or split wood between vertical posts (wattle). Pack both sides with clay-soil-straw mixture (daub). Let dry 1-2 weeks. Produces solid, insulated walls. ~10 cm wall thickness. A 3 m by 4 m wattle-and-daub wall panel takes 2-3 days to build, including drying time. The clay-soil mix should be roughly 70% soil, 20% clay, 10% chopped straw by volume. The straw acts as reinforcement, reducing cracking as the wall dries.
- **Post-and-beam**: Vertical posts (15-20 cm diameter) set in ground holes 60-90 cm deep. Horizontal beams notched into post tops. Stone or bronze tools sufficient for all joinery. Spacing 1.5-2.5 m between posts. Pack stones around the base of each post in the hole for drainage, then backfill with tamped earth. Treat buried post ends with char (light charring over fire) to slow rot. A properly built post-and-beam frame lasts 20-40 years before post bases need replacement.
- **Floor construction**: For a raised floor, lay horizontal beams between the lowest wall plates, then lay split logs or planks across the beams. For an earthen floor, pack the ground level with tamped clay-soil mix 5-10 cm thick, then seal with a thin layer of diluted animal blood or linseed oil. An earthen floor is warm, cheap, and surprisingly durable if kept dry.
- **Thatched roof**: Bundle reed, straw, or long grass (~60 cm bundles). Tie to roof poles with cordage, overlapping from eave to ridge like shingles. 30-40 cm thatch depth sheds water for 20-30 years. Roof pitch minimum 45° for water run-off. Steeper pitch (50-60°) lasts longer because water clears faster. A 5 m by 8 m roof needs 300-500 bundles and 2-3 workers for a week. Harvest thatching reed in winter when dry and stiff.
- **Simple door and shutters**: Split planks with a wedge and mallet, smooth with an adze. Peg planks to two horizontal battens. Hang on wooden pivot hinges (wooden pins set into holes in the lintel and sill). A pivot hinge is simpler than strap hinges and requires no ironwork.

**Strengths**:
- Wattle-and-daub walls provide insulation (~10 cm thick) from readily available materials (saplings, clay, straw) — no timber framing needed
- Post-and-beam construction lasts 20-40 years when post ends are charred to slow rot — permanent shelter from basic materials
- Thatched roof at 45-60° pitch sheds water for 20-30 years — 300-500 bundles cover a 5×8 m roof

**Weaknesses**:
- Wattle-and-daub walls are not load-bearing beyond a single story — limited to low structures
- Thatched roof is flammable: a single spark from a chimney or hearth can ignite dry thatch, destroying the entire structure
- Post-and-beam posts rot at ground level within 20-40 years even when charred — replacement requires jacking up the structure

**Advanced woodworking**:
- **Steam bending**: Place green wood strip (1-3 cm thick) inside a sealed wooden steam box with a steam inlet from a boiling water pot. Steam for 1-2 hours per cm of thickness. Pull the softened strip out, bend it around a wooden form, clamp it in place, and let it dry for 24-48 hours. The lignin in the wood resets around the new shape. Used for boat ribs, chair backs, barrel staves, and any curved structural member. Over-steaming makes the wood brittle; under-steaming causes springback. Oak, ash, and elm bend well. Pine and spruce tend to split. Always bend with the grain, not across it.
- **Wooden wheel construction (solid disc)**: Cut 3-4 planks to form a disc 60-100 cm in diameter and 4-6 cm thick. Join planks with wooden dowels or strap the rim with a heated iron band that shrinks tight as it cools. Bore a central hole for the axle, 5-8 cm diameter. Solid disc wheels handle heavy loads at low speed on rough terrain. Spoked wheels require a lathe-turned hub and steam-bent felloes, which are an advanced step. Fit the iron tire band while hot; as it cools, it shrinks and compresses the wheel tight. A loose tire is dangerous and will throw the wheel apart under load.
- **Wooden gears**: Lantern gears use two parallel wooden discs connected by cylindrical wooden pegs (2-3 cm diameter) running between them. The pegs serve as gear teeth. Crown gears are a single disc with pegs protruding from one face. Both types appear in watermills and windmills for transferring rotary motion. Hardwood pegs (oak, ash) wear but are easy to replace. Gear ratios are set by the number of pegs: a 12-peg driver turning a 36-peg driven gear gives 3:1 reduction. Wooden gears run quietly and tolerate misalignment better than metal gears, but they have low load capacity and wear faster in dusty conditions. Oil or tallow lubrication extends peg life.
- **Wood finishing**: Scrape surfaces smooth with a steel or stone scraper held at 80 degrees to the wood. Sand with progressively finer abrasives: coarse sandstone, then fine sandstone, then dried horsetail stems (natural emery). Rub with linseed oil or beeswax to seal the surface and slow moisture absorption. A well-finished wood surface resists rot and checking.

### Basket Weaving

**Materials preparation**:
- **Willow**: Harvest one-year-old shoots (long, straight, flexible) in winter when sap is low. Soak in water for 3-5 days to restore flexibility before weaving. Split thicker rods lengthwise with a cleave for finer weft material.
- **Reed**: Cut common reed (Phragmites) from wetlands in late summer. Dry thoroughly, then soak before use. Reed is stiffer than willow, better for structural ribs and warp stakes.
- **Split oak and ash**: Pound green oak or ash logs with a mallet to split along the grain into splints. Shave splints to uniform width (5-10 mm) with a knife. Boil or steam to make flexible. These heavier splints produce rugged work baskets.
- **Rush and cattail**: Harvest leaf blades in summer, dry flat, then soak before weaving. Soft and easy to work, but less durable than willow or oak. Best for mats and hats.

**Coil basketry**:
- Bundle long grass or sweetgrass into a coil 2-3 cm in diameter. Wrap the coil continuously with a thinner fiber (raffia, split cane, plant roots) and stitch each new wrap to the previous row. The coil spirals outward from the center, growing the basket floor then the walls. A 20-30 cm diameter basket takes 4-8 hours of steady work. Coil baskets are naturally tight-woven and can hold fine grain without lining. The stitching fiber also creates decorative patterns when dyed with plant-based colors (walnut hulls for brown, berries for red, indigo for blue).

**Twill weaving**:
- Drive warp stakes into the ground in parallel rows. Weave weft material over and under the stakes in repeating patterns (over-two-under-two, over-three-under-one). Willow, reed, or split oak splints 5-10 mm wide work well. This method suits rectangular burden baskets and large storage containers. The rigid warp stakes keep consistent spacing and make it easy to produce flat-bottomed shapes. Varying the over-under pattern creates diagonal or diamond designs in the basket wall. Twill weave is stronger than plain over-under-one weave because each weft passes over more warps, creating a tighter interlock.

**Plaiting**:
- Interweave flat strips (3-8 mm wide) of rush, palm leaf, or bark in 3-strand or 4-strand patterns. No frame or stakes required; the weaver holds the working material directly. Plaiting produces mats, hats, and simple containers. It's the fastest basket technique and the easiest to learn, though the resulting containers are less rigid than coil or twill work. A plaited mat 1 m by 2 m takes 2-4 hours with prepared materials. Plaited hats provide sun protection during field work, and a wide-brimmed hat from rush strips takes 1-2 hours.

**Twining**:
- A technique related to plaiting where two weft strands twist around each other between each warp element. The twist locks the weft in place and produces a tighter, more flexible fabric than simple plaiting. Used for soft bags, flexible mats, and clothing accessories. Twined bags made from dogbane or nettle fiber are lightweight, strong, and can carry heavy loads without stretching.

**Basket uses**:
- **Storage**: grain, dried food, seeds, small tools. Coil baskets with tight stitching keep out insects.
- **Fish traps**: funnel-shaped woven traps with an inverted cone entrance. Fish swim in but can't find the exit. Anchor with stones in flowing water. A trap 60-80 cm long, 20-30 cm in diameter at the wide end, takes 3-5 hours to weave. Bait with scraps or grain. Check traps daily. Fish traps are one of the highest-return tools for labor invested: a single well-placed trap in a stream can catch several fish per day with no ongoing effort beyond checking and rebaiting.
- **Sieves**: woven with deliberate 2-5 mm gaps between weft strands for sifting flour, separating chaff from grain, or washing gravel to find small particles. The gap size is controlled by the spacing of the warp stakes during weaving. Multiple sieves with different gap sizes allow graded separation: coarse (5 mm) for removing chaff, medium (3 mm) for sifting bran from flour, fine (1-2 mm) for producing smooth flour.
- **Burden baskets**: rectangular or conical back-carrying baskets with cordage shoulder straps. Carry 15-25 kg of firewood, harvested crops, or building materials. Wattle-weave construction holds up to repeated heavy loads.
- **Water-tight containers**: pitch-sealed baskets. Melt pine pitch or birch tar, brush it over the interior surface of a tightly woven basket, and let it cool. The pitch fills gaps and makes the container hold water. Useful for storage and short-distance water transport.
- **Cradle boards**: woven back-carriers for infants. Flexible willow or reed frame with a woven back, padded with soft plant fiber or animal hide. Keeps the baby secure and close to the caregiver's body during daily work.

### Basic Pottery (Hand-Built)

**Clay sourcing**:
- Dig clay from riverbanks, lake shores, or exposed clay pits. Good clay is smooth, plastic, and free of organic debris. Test quality by rolling a coil about 5 cm long and 1 cm thick, then bending it around a finger. Good clay bends 90 degrees without cracking. If it cracks, it needs more aging or different processing. Remove rocks, roots, and debris by hand. Soak the clay in water for 24 hours, then knead it on a flat surface to remove air bubbles and achieve uniform consistency.
- **Clay processing**: For finer work, slake the clay (dissolve it completely in water to form a thin slip), then pour it through a coarse screen to remove pebbles and organic matter. Let the screened slip settle for several hours, then pour off the clear water from the top. The remaining sludge is clean, refined clay. Dry it on a plaster bat or absorbent stone until it reaches working consistency. This extra processing step removes impurities that cause weak spots in the fired pot.
- **Wild clay identification**: Clay deposits often appear as sticky, blue-gray or red-orange layers in eroded banks. Rub a small amount between your fingers; clay feels smooth and soapy, not gritty. If it feels gritty, it has too much silt or sand (which can work as a natural temper, but the clay body may be weak). Test-fire a small ball of the clay: if it holds its shape and rings when tapped, you have usable clay.

**Tempering**:
- Mix prepared clay with sand, grog (crushed fired pottery), or crushed shell at 20-30% by volume. Tempering reduces shrinkage during drying and prevents thermal shock cracks during firing. Without temper, thick-walled vessels almost always crack. Grog is the most reliable temper; sand is the easiest to source. Crush shell to 1-3 mm fragments. Mix temper into clay thoroughly by kneading for 10-15 minutes.
- **Testing temper ratio**: Make three small test tiles (flat discs 5 cm across, 1 cm thick) with different temper amounts: 20%, 30%, and 40%. Dry and fire them alongside your regular pots. The tile that survives firing without cracking and rings when tapped gives you the right temper ratio for that clay source. Different clay beds need different temper levels, so test whenever you switch to a new clay source.

**Pinch pot**:
- Form a ball of clay 8-12 cm in diameter. Press the thumb into the center and pinch the walls outward between thumb and fingers, rotating the ball as you work. Aim for even wall thickness of 5-8 mm. Pinch pots produce simple round bowls with 500 mL to 2 L capacity. The most basic pottery form, achievable on the first attempt with decent clay. Rotate the pot continuously as you pinch; working one spot too long thins the wall and creates a weak point. Smooth the rim by running a wet finger around it.

**Coil building**:
- Roll clay into coils 1-2 cm in diameter and 30-50 cm long. Score the rim of a pinch-pot base with a sharp stick, apply a thin layer of slip (clay mixed with water to a paste), and lay the first coil on top. Blend the coil into the base wall with fingers or a smooth stone. Stack and blend successive coils to build vessel walls upward. Coil building allows vessels 10-30 cm tall with 2-10 L capacity. Keep the walls even and support the interior with one hand while blending the outside.
- **Shaping the vessel**: As the walls grow, shape the profile by pressing outward for a belly or inward for a neck. Use a wooden paddle on the outside and a stone anvil held inside to thin and shape the walls. This paddle-and-anvil technique can produce thin, even walls on large vessels. Work on one section at a time, letting lower coils stiffen slightly before adding weight above. If the walls start to sag, stop and let the clay firm up before continuing.

**Slab construction**:
- Roll clay to 8-12 mm thickness on a flat surface using a wooden rolling pin or smooth stone. Cut flat panels with a knife or taut cord. Join edges by scoring and applying slip, then pressing together firmly. Slab building suits rectangular containers, flat-bottomed trays, and box shapes that coils can't easily produce. A simple slab-built box with four walls and a floor is a good second project after pinch pots. Reinforce interior corners with thin clay coils pressed into the seam and blended smooth.

**Drying**:
- Air-dry formed pots slowly for 1-2 weeks before firing. Cover loosely with cloth or grass to slow moisture loss. Rapid drying causes warping and cracking, especially on thick-walled pieces. Pots are ready to fire when they feel dry to the touch and no longer feel cool against the cheek (residual moisture makes clay feel cool). Bone-dry clay is fragile; handle carefully when loading into the pit.

**Surface decoration**:
- **Incising**: Carve lines or patterns into leather-hard clay (partially dried, no longer sticky) with a sharp stick or bone tool. Grooves show clearly after firing. Simple geometric patterns (chevrons, crosshatch, zigzag) are easy to execute and look effective.
- **Impressing**: Press cord-wrapped sticks, shells, or carved wooden stamps into the soft clay surface. Creates repeating patterns quickly. A carved wooden stamp lets you produce identical decorative bands on multiple pots, which is useful if making a set of storage jars.
- **Burnishing**: Rub the surface of a leather-hard pot with a smooth stone (river pebble, polished bone) in firm, overlapping strokes. This compresses the clay surface and produces a glossy sheen after firing. Burnished pots are less porous and easier to clean. Burnish after the pot has dried to leather-hard but before it reaches bone-dry; the clay must still have enough moisture to compress under the stone.
- **Slip decoration**: Apply a thin layer of differently colored liquid clay (slip) to the pot surface before drying. Red or white slip on a darker body clay produces contrast. Apply with fingers, a rag, or by dipping. Slip can also be trailed on in lines using a spout made from a hollow reed or bone tube, creating freehand painted designs.
- **Combing**: Drag a multi-toothed tool (carved wood, notched shell, or bone comb) through the leather-hard surface. Produces parallel grooves in patterns. Fast way to add visual texture to a large pot.

**Common failures**:
- **Cracking during drying**: caused by uneven wall thickness, insufficient tempering, or drying too fast. Remedy: maintain consistent wall thickness, temper at 20-30%, dry slowly under cover.
- **Exploding during firing**: moisture trapped in the clay wall turns to steam and blows the pot apart. The loud, unmistakable solution is to dry pots thoroughly before firing. Pre-warming pots near the fire for an hour before pit firing helps drive off the last moisture.
- **Thermal shock cracking**: pots that cool too fast after firing, or that are heated unevenly during use. Tempering with grog is the best preventive measure.
- **Weak joints**: coil or slab joints that separate during drying or firing. Caused by insufficient scoring, inadequate slip, or joining leather-hard clay to wet clay. Both surfaces must be at the same moisture level when joined, and both must be scored and slipped.

**Pit firing**:
- Dig a pit 30-50 cm deep and wide enough to hold all the dry pots with 5-10 cm clearance on each side. Place the thoroughly dried pots (air-dried for at least 1-2 weeks) in the pit. Surround and cover with dry grass, then hardwood on top. Cover the fuel with a layer of soil, leaving small vents for air. Ignite through the vents. Burn for 2-4 hours. Peak temperature reaches 600-900 degrees Celsius, enough to vitrify the clay body. Let cool slowly (overnight) to avoid thermal shock. Expected yield: 60-80% of pots survive without cracks. Fired pottery is waterproof, heat-resistant, and stores food safely.
- **Firing colors**: Pit-fired pots take on varied color from the combustion environment. Areas exposed to air turn orange or red (oxidized iron in the clay). Areas buried in ash or smothered by fuel turn gray or black (reduced iron). Polished or burnished surfaces develop a deeper sheen. These color variations are a natural feature of pit firing, not a defect.
- **Improving yield**: Nest small pots inside larger ones to protect them. Place pots on a bed of dry grass so they don't touch cold soil directly. Build up heat slowly by starting with small flames and adding fuel gradually. Rushing the fire is the main cause of pot loss.

### Simple Machines

**Lever**:
- A rigid beam resting on a fulcrum. Mechanical advantage equals the effort arm length divided by the load arm length. A crowbar 1.5 m long with the fulcrum placed 10 cm from the load gives a mechanical advantage of 14:1, meaning 7 kg of effort moves a 100 kg load. A hand spike, a simple 2 m pole slipped under a stone with a rock as fulcrum, lets one person lift a 500 kg stone with roughly 35 kg of downward effort. Levers are the foundation of most heavy construction in pre-industrial settings.
- **Class 1 (fulcrum in the middle)**: seesaws, crowbars, scissors, pliers. The fulcrum sits between the effort and the load. Can multiply force or distance depending on arm lengths.
- **Class 2 (load in the middle)**: wheelbarrows, nutcrackers, bottle openers. The load sits between the fulcrum and the effort. Always multiplies force. A wheelbarrow with the load 30 cm from the wheel and the handles 120 cm from the wheel gives MA = 4:1.
- **Class 3 (effort in the middle)**: tweezers, fishing rods, human forearm. The effort is applied between the fulcrum and the load. Sacrifices force for range of motion and speed. Not used for heavy lifting, but essential for tools requiring precision and reach.

**Inclined plane**:
- A ramp. Mechanical advantage equals the ramp length divided by the rise height. A 5 m ramp rising 1 m gives MA = 5:1. A 500 kg stone block that takes 500 kg of force to lift vertically needs only 100 kg of force to push up the ramp. Friction adds some resistance; rollers or a wetted surface reduce it. Inclined planes are how most large stones were moved throughout history.
- **Construction ramps**: For building a wall or raised structure, build a compacted earth ramp on one side. Lengthen the ramp as the wall grows taller to keep the slope manageable. A 10 m long ramp rising 2 m (MA = 5:1) lets two people roll a 200 kg block into position. Dismantle the ramp when construction finishes. Temples, pyramids, and castle walls were all built with this method.

**Wedge**:
- Two inclined planes joined back to back. An axe head with a 30-degree edge angle gives roughly MA = 2:1 for splitting wood. The wedge converts downward striking force into lateral spreading force that separates the wood fibers. Wedges also appear in chisels, knives, and plow blades. A wooden wedge driven into a split log with a mallet is the standard way to split firewood. For splitting large logs, drive a steel wedge into the end grain with a sledge, then drive a second wedge behind it to widen the split. Wooden wedges, soaked in water after driving, expand and hold splitting force overnight in stubborn logs.

**Screw**:
- An inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder. A wooden screw press for oil or paper pulp, with a 10 cm diameter screw and 2 cm thread pitch, gives MA of roughly 15:1. Cutting internal threads in wood requires a tap, and external threads require a die, both of which can be made from hardened steel. Wooden screws are also used in presses for pressing grapes, cheese, and extracted plant oils. Archimedes' screw, a helical blade inside a tilted cylinder, lifts water 1-3 m when turned by hand or animal power. Each rotation lifts a pocket of water up one thread pitch. Used for irrigation and mine drainage since antiquity.

**Wheel and axle**:
- Mechanical advantage equals wheel diameter divided by axle diameter. A windlass with a 50 cm crank handle turning a 5 cm drum gives MA = 10:1. That means 5 kg of effort on the crank lifts a 50 kg water bucket. The windlass is the standard well-head mechanism. Cart wheels are another application: the wheel diameter is much larger than the axle, so the cart rolls over obstacles that would stop a sled.
- **Cart construction**: A simple two-wheel cart uses solid disc wheels (see wooden wheel construction above) mounted on a fixed wooden axle. The bed is a platform of planks 1.5-2 m long and 0.8-1.2 m wide, lashed or pegged to cross-beams that sit on the axle. Load capacity with 80 cm diameter wheels on a flat road: 200-400 kg. Animals pull better than people; a single ox can pull a loaded cart all day at walking pace. Lubricate the axle with tallow or oil to reduce friction and wear.
- **Potter's wheel (treadle)**: A heavy flywheel (30-50 cm diameter stone or wooden disc) connected by a spindle to a small throwing head at tabletop height. The potter kicks the flywheel with a foot treadle to maintain rotation while both hands shape the clay. This is the bridge between hand-built pottery and fully mechanized wheel-thrown production. See [Ceramics → Pottery](../ceramics/pottery.md) for the full treatment.

**Pulley**:
- A single fixed pulley changes the direction of force but adds no mechanical advantage (MA = 1:1). You pull down instead of lifting up, which is easier on the body. A single movable pulley, where one end of the rope is fixed and the pulley moves with the load, gives MA = 2:1. A block and tackle system with 4 pulleys (two fixed, two movable) gives MA = 4:1, letting a single person lift 200 kg with 50 kg of pull. Pulleys are essential for construction hoisting, rigging, and well systems.
- **Pulley construction**: A wooden block with a groove around the circumference, rotating on a wooden or metal axle pin. The rope rides in the groove. A well-made wooden pulley with a smooth, polished groove lasts for years under moderate loads. For heavy loads, bind the pulley block with iron bands to prevent splitting. Multiple sheaves (pulleys) can share a single axle block, giving the block and tackle its name.

**Compound machines in practice**:
- **Well windlass**: combines wheel-and-axle (crank handle turning a drum) with a rope and bucket. A 60 cm crank on a 6 cm drum gives MA = 10:1. One person raises 40 kg of water with modest effort.
- **Treadwheel crane**: a large wooden wheel (2-3 m diameter) with a person walking inside it, turning an axle that winds a rope. The large wheel-to-axle ratio (MA up to 30:1) allows lifting multi-ton stone blocks. Used extensively in medieval cathedral construction.
- **Screw press with lever**: a screw press driven by a long lever arm instead of a direct handle. The lever feeds force into the screw, and the screw feeds force into the pressing plate. Combined MA can exceed 100:1, enough to extract oil from seeds or press paper pulp.
- **Block and tackle on a tripod**: three poles lashed together at the top, forming a freestanding gantry. Suspend a 4-sheave block and tackle from the apex. Used for lifting heavy stones onto walls, positioning timber beams, or hauling equipment. The tripod is self-supporting and doesn't require attachment to a building frame.
- **Well sweep**: a Class 1 lever with the fulcrum (a forked post) near one end. A long pole (3-5 m) pivots on the fork, with a bucket hanging from the short end and a counterweight (stone or heavy log) on the long end. Pull the long end down to lower the bucket into the well, and the counterweight helps raise the full bucket. Simple, reliable, and needs no rope or pulley. Used for shallow wells (5-10 m depth).

**Strengths**:
- Block and tackle with 4 pulleys gives MA = 4:1, letting a single person lift 200 kg with 50 kg of pull — sufficient for construction hoisting
- Screw press with lever arm achieves MA exceeding 100:1 — enough force to extract oil from seeds or press paper pulp
- Well windlass (50 cm crank on 6 cm drum, MA = 10:1) enables one person to raise 40 kg of water with modest effort

**Weaknesses**:
- Wooden pulleys wear at the axle pin and can split under heavy loads — require regular inspection and replacement
- Rope under tension in block-and-tackle systems stores enormous energy — a rope failure releases that energy suddenly, creating a hazard
- Wooden screws are difficult to cut accurately without metal taps and dies — thread quality varies, producing uneven pressure in screw presses

### Safety & Hazards

- **Sharp tool injuries**: Stone and metal edges cause lacerations. Always cut away from body. Keep edges sharp (dull tools require more force and slip more often). Leather hand protection for chopping/splitting.
- **Birch tar burns**: Birch tar production involves heating bark to 300-400°C. Hot tar causes severe burns because it sticks to skin and continues burning. Handle with long tools. Cool burns with water.
- **Eye protection**: Stone knapping produces sharp flakes that fly in unpredictable directions. Eye protection essential during flintknapping. Work in clear area.
- **Fire hazards**: Birch tar is flammable. Hafting involves heating pitch near open flame. Keep water or sand nearby for fire suppression.
- **Pit firing safety**: Pit fires reach 600-900°C. Don't reach into a burning pit. Let the fire burn out and cool overnight before digging out pots. The soil cap holds heat long after flames die. Mark the pit location so nobody steps on it.
- **Steam bending burns**: Steam escaping from a steam box can scald skin. Use gloves when handling steamed wood. Open the box away from your face. The wood itself holds heat for several minutes after removal.
- **Heavy lifting with simple machines**: Block and tackle systems under heavy load store enormous energy in the rope. A rope failure or knot slip releases that energy suddenly. Never stand under a suspended load. Inspect ropes for wear before each lift. Use splices rather than knots for critical loads (splices retain 80% strength vs 50-60% for most knots).
- **Knapping cuts**: Obsidian and flint flakes are surgically sharp and easy to overlook in grass or dirt. Sweep the knapping area clean before and after work. Pick up all flakes and debris. A flake embedded in a bare foot can cause deep, slow-healing cuts. Wear foot protection if knapping on the ground.
