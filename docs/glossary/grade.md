# Grade

Grade is a classification system that ranks materials by quality, purity, or mechanical properties. In cotton grading, classification considers color (white to yellowish), leaf content (trash), and preparation quality (ginned condition). USDA grades range from Strict Low Middling (lowest usable) through Middling, Strict Middling, Good Middling, to Strict Good Middling at the top. Higher grade means less trash and brighter color, determined by visual comparison to official standards.

In metals, grade designates the specific composition and properties of an alloy. Steel grades are defined by carbon content, alloy additions, and mechanical properties (yield strength, tensile strength, elongation). AISI/SAE designations (e.g., 1018, 4140, 304) encode composition. Higher-grade steels contain tighter compositional ranges and fewer impurities, enabling more predictable performance but requiring more controlled steelmaking processes.

In abrasives, grade refers to grit size—the particle diameter of the abrasive grain. Coarse grades (40–80 grit) remove material quickly but leave a rough surface. Medium grades (100–220) provide intermediate stock removal. Fine grades (320–600) produce smooth finishes. The grade must be selected to match the task: too coarse damages the workpiece, too fine wastes time. Across all materials, understanding grade systems is essential for selecting the right input for each process step.
