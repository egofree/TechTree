# Carbon Monoxide

> **Type**: material | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, chemistry, energy, machine-tools, metals

Producer gas is ~25% CO — odorless, colorless, lethal. CO binds to hemoglobin 200× more strongly than oxygen. Symptoms: headache, dizziness, nausea → unconsciousness → death. Concentrations >1000 ppm are immediately dangerous. NEVER operate gasifier indoors or in enclosed spaces. CO detector mandatory. Vent all exhaust to atmosphere.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Carbon monoxide (CO) plays a dual role: it is an essential reducing agent in metallurgy and a deadly poison to human life. In [Iron & Steel](../metals/iron-steel.md) and [Blast Furnace](../metals/iron-steel/blast-furnace.md) production, CO reduces iron oxide to metallic iron. In [Wood Gasification](../chemistry/wood-gasification.md), CO is a primary combustible component of producer gas. In [Charcoal Production](../energy/fuels/charcoal.md), [Forming](../machine-tools/forming.md), and [Pottery](../ceramics/pottery.md), CO is an unavoidable byproduct of combustion in enclosed spaces. Managing CO exposure is a life-safety requirement at every stage where fuel is burned.

## Technical Details

CO is produced by incomplete combustion of carbon-containing fuels. In a wood gasifier, producer gas contains ~25% CO alongside hydrogen, nitrogen, and CO₂. In a blast furnace, CO is the primary reducing agent that strips oxygen from iron ore (Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂). In any enclosed combustion — kiln, furnace, forge, or hearth — CO accumulates if ventilation is inadequate.

The hazard is extreme because CO is odorless, colorless, and produces no irritating effects to warn of exposure. It binds to hemoglobin with ~200× the affinity of oxygen, forming carboxyhemoglobin that blocks oxygen transport. Symptoms progress from headache and dizziness (100-200 ppm) through nausea and confusion (200-400 ppm) to unconsciousness and death (>1000 ppm). Chronic low-level exposure causes persistent fatigue and cognitive impairment.

Prevention requires active ventilation (at minimum a high vent and a low vent for airflow in any enclosed space with combustion), CO detectors in all workspaces where fuel is burned, and strict prohibitions against operating gasifiers, charcoal kilns, or combustion equipment indoors. All exhaust must be vented directly to atmosphere. When working in enclosed furnace areas, portable CO monitors with audible alarms at 35 ppm (OSHA PEL) provide essential protection.
