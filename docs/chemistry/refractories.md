# Refractory Materials

**Node ID**: `chemistry.refractories`
**Parent**: [Chemistry](index.md)
**Era**: Early Industrial
**Tags**: `material`, `capability`, `critical`

## Overview

Refractories are heat-resistant ceramic materials that line furnaces, kilns, incinerators, and reactors — maintaining structural integrity at temperatures above 1500°C while resisting chemical attack by molten metals, slags, and hot gases. Without refractories, no metallurgy, glass melting, cement production, or high-temperature chemical processing is possible. They are consumable materials — furnace linings wear and must be periodically replaced. A typical steelmaking shop consumes 6-15 kg of refractories per tonne of steel produced.

## Raw Materials

**Alumina-silicate system** (fireclay and high-alumina):
- **Fireclay** (kaolinite, Al₂O₃·2SiO₂·2H₂O): Mined from clay deposits. When calcined at 1200-1400°C, forms mullite (3Al₂O₃·2SiO₂) + silica glass. Al₂O₃ content 25-45%. Service temperature: 1200-1500°C. The most widely used refractory by volume — inexpensive, workable, adequate for many applications.
- **Bauxite** (gibbsite, Al(OH)₃): Source of high-alumina refractories (Al₂O₃ 60-90%). Calcined at 1500-1600°C to produce dense, stable corundum (α-Al₂O₃). Service temperature: 1600-1800°C. Used in steelmaking furnace roofs and sidewalls.
- **Sillimanite, andalusite, kyanite**: Natural aluminosilicate minerals (Al₂SiO₅ polymorphs) that convert to mullite + silica at 1300-1550°C. Used for high-quality, low-shrinkage refractories.

**Basic refractories** (magnesia and dolomite):
- **Magnesite** (MgCO₃): Calcined to periclase (MgO) at 1700-2000°C. Dead-burned magnesite (dense, low-porosity) is the backbone of basic oxygen furnace (BOF) linings. MgO melting point: 2852°C — the highest of common refractory oxides. Also resistant to basic slags (CaO-based steelmaking slags).
- **Dolomite** (CaCO₃·MgCO₃): Calcined to CaO·MgO at 1600-1800°C. Less expensive than magnesite but hydrates in moist air (CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ — expansion destroys the brick). Used in AOD converters and cement kiln burning zones. Tar-bonded dolomite (impregnated with coal tar pitch) resists hydration — the tar carbon fills pores and forms a protective carbon barrier.
- **Chrome-magnesite**: Blend of chromite (FeCr₂O₄) and magnesite — combines slag resistance (chromite) with hot strength (MgO). Used in non-ferrous metallurgy (copper, lead smelting).

**Silica refractories** (SiO₂ >93%):
- Made from quartzite rock (SiO₂ >97%), converted to cristobalite and tridymite at high temperature. Unique property: very low thermal expansion above 600°C (actually slight contraction above 1000°C). Used almost exclusively for coke oven walls and glass tank furnace crowns (hot-face temperature 1550-1650°C). Not suitable below 600°C — large volume change at 573°C (α→β quartz transition) causes spalling.

**Carbon refractories**:
- Carbon (graphite) blocks: hot-pressed petroleum coke + coal tar pitch binder, fired to 1000-1400°C in reducing atmosphere. Exceptional thermal shock resistance, high thermal conductivity, unreactive with many metals. Used for blast furnace hearth and bosh (withstands molten iron and slag at 1500°C). Also used in aluminum electrolysis cells (carbon cathode blocks). Limitation: oxidizes in air above 400°C (requires reducing atmosphere or protective coating).

**Zirconia and zircon**:
- **Zirconia (ZrO₂)**: Melting point 2715°C. Stabilized with CaO, MgO, or Y₂O₃ to prevent destructive phase transformation (monoclinic → tetragonal → cubic) during heating. Used for continuous casting nozzles and tundish metering orifices in steelmaking — resists erosion by molten steel.
- **Zircon (ZrSiO₄)**: Used in glass contact refractories (resists dissolution by soda-lime glass melt). Also in foundry ladle nozzles.

## Manufacturing Process

**Shaped refractories (bricks and shapes)**:
1. **Raw material preparation**: Crush and grind to target particle size distribution. Grading: coarse (3-6 mm) for skeleton, medium (1-3 mm) for packing, fine (<0.1 mm) for bonding and sintering. The particle size distribution is critical — optimized for maximum packing density (minimum porosity → maximum strength and slag resistance).
2. **Mixing**: Dry blending of graded aggregates, followed by addition of binder (water + clay for fireclay bricks; sulfite lye or phosphoric acid for basic bricks; coal tar pitch for carbon-bonded bricks). Mix in pug mill or high-intensity mixer.
3. **Forming**: Pressing under 50-200 MPa in hydraulic or friction screw presses. Large shapes may be air-rammed or vibration-cast. Pressing density directly determines fired density and porosity.
4. **Drying**: Remove moisture at 80-110°C (24-48 hours). Prevents steam spalling during firing.
5. **Firing (sintering)**: Tunnel kiln at 1300-1800°C (temperature depends on composition). 12-48 hours. Sintering bonds particles by partial melting (liquid-phase sintering) or solid-state diffusion. Fireclay: 1300-1400°C. High-alumina: 1500-1600°C. Magnesite: 1700-1800°C. Shrinkage: 2-8% (controlled by raw material selection and pressing density).

**Unshaped refractories (monolithics)**:
- **Castables**: Refractory aggregate + calcium aluminate cement (CAC) binder. Mixed with water on-site, poured or pumped into forms, vibrated to consolidate. CAC hydrates and sets like Portland cement but withstands 1500-1700°C after firing. Low-cement castables (LCC, 3-8% CaO) use ultra-fine alumina powder (fume) to improve particle packing and reduce cement content — higher density, better hot strength, superior slag resistance. Ultra-low-cement castables (ULCC, <3% CaO) for the most demanding applications.
- **Gunning mixes**: Dry or pre-mixed refractory material pneumatically sprayed onto hot furnace surfaces to repair worn areas without cooling the furnace. Applied at 20-40 m/s impact velocity. Adhesion by impact bonding. Used for in-situ repair of BOF linings, cement kilns, and incinerators — extends campaign life without costly shutdowns.
- **Ramming mixes**: Semi-dry material tamped into place with pneumatic rammers. Used for furnace hearths, ladle bottoms, and around tap holes. Typically magnesite or alumina-based with resin or tar binder.
- **Plastic refractories**: Clay-bonded, putty-like consistency — pressed into place by hand or pneumatic ram. Used for patching and complex shapes where forms are impractical.

## Properties and Selection Criteria

| Max service temp (°C) | 1400 | 1700 | 1800+ | 1650 | 2000+ (reducing) |
| Cold crush strength (MPa) | 25-60 | 50-100 | 40-80 | 30-60 | 30-50 |
| Thermal conductivity (W/m·K at 1000°C) | 1.2-1.5 | 1.5-2.0 | 3.0-5.0 | 1.5-2.0 | 15-40 |
| Thermal shock resistance | Good | Good | Poor | Moderate | Excellent |
| Slag resistance (basic) | Poor | Moderate | Excellent | Poor | Good |
| Slag resistance (acidic) | Good | Good | Poor | Excellent | Good |

**Thermal shock resistance** is critical — refractory linings undergo temperature cycling (heating and cooling during furnace operation). Spalling (fracture and flaking) occurs when thermal stress from temperature gradients exceeds the material's tensile strength. Materials with low thermal expansion and high thermal conductivity resist thermal shock. Carbon is excellent (high conductivity, moderate expansion). Magnesite is poor (high expansion, lower conductivity) — requires careful heating/cooling schedules.

## Applications by Industry

**Steelmaking**: BOF converters use MgO-C bricks (magnesite-carbon — carbon improves thermal shock resistance and reduces slag wetting). Campaign life: 10,000-25,000 heats (line is periodically patched with gunning mix between relining). Electric arc furnaces: MgO-C sidewalls, water-cooled roof panels. Ladles: alumina-spinel or MgO-C working lining. Continuous casting tundish: alumina or zirconia metering nozzles. Blast furnace: carbon hearth, SiC or alumina bosh, fireclay stack.

**Glass manufacturing**: Silica crown (hot-face 1600°C), alumina-zirconia-silica (AZS) fused-cast blocks in glass contact areas — produced by melting raw materials in an electric arc furnace and casting into molds. AZS contains 30-40% ZrO₂ in an alumina-silica glass matrix — zirconia crystals resist dissolution by glass melt. Glass contact blocks slowly dissolve into the glass — refractory corrosion limits furnace campaign to 8-15 years.

**Cement production**: Rotary kiln burning zone: magnesite-spinel or dolomite bricks (resist CaO-SiO₂-Al₂O₃ clinker melt attack). Transition zones: high-alumina or fireclay. Preheater tower: alkali-resistant castables (alkali vapor condensation attacks alumina-silicate refractories — causes hot-phase formation and spalling).

**Non-ferrous metallurgy**: Copper reverberatory furnace: magnesite-chrome. Aluminum melting furnace: high-alumina (resistant to Al₂O₃ formation at metal-refractory interface — molten aluminum reduces SiO₂ from fireclay). Zinc distillation: silicon carbide (withstands zinc vapor at 900-950°C — SiC resists zinc attack better than alumina-silicates).

**Petrochemical**: FCC unit: high-alumina castable with steel fiber reinforcement (withstands erosion by catalyst particles at 700°C). Hydrogen reformer: high-alumina castable (resistant to hydrogen attack at 900°C — hydrogen reduces SiO₂ from fireclay, causing volume changes). Ethylene cracking furnace: high-alumina castable (1200°C, exposure to carburizing atmosphere).

## Safety & Hazards

- **Crystalline silica dust**: Exposure during refractory installation, demolition, and cutting causes silicosis. Use wet cutting methods, respiratory protection (P100 masks), local exhaust ventilation. OSHA PEL: 0.05 mg/m³ (respirable fraction).
- **Refractory ceramic fiber (RCF)**: Synthetic vitreous fiber used as high-temperature insulation — possible carcinogen (IARC Group 2B). Handle with gloves and respirator. Replace with biosoluble fiber alternatives where possible.
- **Thermal burns**: Newly installed refractory linings retain heat for hours after furnace shutdown. Temperature verification with contact pyrometer before entry. Lock-out/tag-out procedures mandatory.
- **Carbon monoxide**: Carbon-containing refractories (MgO-C, carbon blocks) can generate CO during heating as carbon reacts with residual oxygen. Ventilate furnaces during heat-up before personnel entry.
- **Heavy metals**: Chrome-bearing refractories (chrome-magnesite) contain Cr⁶⁺ (hexavalent chromium, carcinogen) after service in oxidizing conditions. Disposal of spent chrome-bearing refractories regulated as hazardous waste in many jurisdictions.
 - **Manual handling**: Refractory bricks are dense and heavy (2.5-3.5 kg/brick for standard 230×114×64 mm straight). A typical relining job requires placing 5,000-50,000 bricks — ergonomic injury risk. Use mechanical lifting aids where possible.

## Fireclay Brick Specifications

Fireclay bricks are the workhorse of the refractory world. Their properties depend directly on alumina content, which determines the proportion of mullite (3Al₂O₃·2SiO₂, melting point 1850°C) to glassy silica phase.

**Classification by alumina content**: Low-duty (25-30% Al₂O₃, pyrometric cone equivalent PCE 15-20, service to 1200-1300°C) for boiler linings and moderate-temperature furnaces. Medium-duty (30-35% Al₂O₃, PCE 20-27, service to 1350-1450°C) for steel reheating furnaces and kiln linings. High-duty (35-45% Al₂O₃, PCE 27-33, service to 1450-1500°C) for high-temperature zones in kilns and furnaces. Super-duty (>45% Al₂O₃) begins to overlap with high-alumina brick classification.

**Physical properties**: Apparent porosity 15-25% (higher porosity means lower thermal conductivity but also lower strength and slag resistance). Cold crushing strength 15-50 MPa. Bulk density 2.0-2.4 g/cm³. Thermal conductivity 0.8-1.5 W/m·K at 1000°C. Modulus of rupture 5-15 MPa at room temperature. Linear thermal expansion 0.5-0.7% from room temperature to 1400°C.

**Dimensional tolerances**: Standard brick (230×114×64 mm) is manufactured to ±1-2 mm tolerance. Larger shapes (arch bricks, wedges, keys) require tighter tolerance for proper fitting without excessive mortar joints. Refractory mortar joints are weak points in furnace linings — thin joints (<2 mm) are preferred and are achieved by grinding brick contact surfaces.

## Silica Brick Properties

Silica bricks are specialized refractories for applications requiring structural stability above 600°C. Their unusual volume stability at high temperature comes from the conversion of quartz to cristobalite and tridymite during firing.

**Composition and mineralogy**: 93-97% SiO₂, with 1-3% CaO (added as lime bond during manufacturing), and minor Fe₂O₃ and Al₂O₃. The brick must be fired sufficiently to convert >80% of the quartz to the high-temperature cristobalite and tridymite forms. Residual quartz causes permanent expansion during service as it slowly converts, potentially disrupting furnace linings.

**Critical temperature behavior**: Below 600°C, silica bricks are vulnerable to large volume changes from the α→β phase transitions of cristobalite (around 270°C) and tridymite (around 160°C). These inversions involve 2-5% volume change and can crack the brick if heating or cooling is too rapid through these temperature ranges. Once above 600°C, silica bricks become remarkably stable, with near-zero thermal expansion. This makes them ideal for coke oven walls and glass furnace crowns that operate continuously above 1000°C.

**Service performance**: PCE 31-33. Maximum service temperature 1650-1700°C. Load-bearing capacity at 1600°C is exceptional — the brick softens very little even within 50°C of its failure point. Bulk density 1.75-1.85 g/cm³ (lighter than fireclay). Thermal conductivity 1.5-2.0 W/m·K at 1000°C.

## Magnesite and Basic Refractories

Magnesite bricks (MgO >85%) are the foundation of basic steelmaking refractories. Their resistance to basic slags (CaO-rich) makes them irreplaceable in basic oxygen furnaces and steel ladles.

**Dead-burned magnesite**: Raw magnesite (MgCO₃) is calcined at 1700-2000°C to produce dense, inert periclase (MgO) clinker. This "dead burning" causes grain growth and densification, reducing reactivity with moisture (MgO is mildly hygroscopic at lower calcination temperatures). The resulting grains are hard, dense (3.4-3.6 g/cm³), and chemically stable. Periclase melting point: 2852°C, the highest among common refractory oxides.

**MgO-C bricks**: Modern steelmaking uses magnesite-carbon composite bricks (MgO-C). Graphite (8-20% by weight) is added to the magnesite mix. Carbon provides three benefits: (1) non-wetting by slag, reducing chemical corrosion; (2) high thermal conductivity, improving thermal shock resistance; (3) reducing environment at the hot face that protects MgO from slag attack. Bonded with resin or pitch, fired to develop carbon bond. PCE 38-40, service temperature 1700-1800°C.

**Chrome-magnesite**: A blend of chromite (FeCr₂O₄, 10-30%) and magnesite (40-70% MgO). Used in copper, lead, and nickel smelting furnaces where resistance to both acidic and basic slags is needed. The chromite spinel phase provides structural integrity while MgO provides basic slag resistance. Phosphoric acid or chrome ore bond. Caution: Cr⁶⁺ formation in service creates disposal hazards.

## Insulating Firebrick and Castables

**Insulating firebrick (IFB)**: Porosity is deliberately maximized (60-80%) to reduce thermal conductivity. Manufactured by mixing fireclay with a combustible filler (sawdust, polystyrene beads, or foaming agents) that burns out during firing, leaving a network of pores. Bulk density 0.5-1.0 g/cm³ (vs 2.0-2.4 for dense fireclay). Thermal conductivity 0.15-0.5 W/m·K at 500°C, roughly one-third to one-tenth of dense brick. Cold crushing strength only 2-8 MPa (fragile, cannot bear structural loads). Used as backup insulation behind dense working linings. Temperature grades: 1100°C, 1260°C, 1400°C, 1500°C.

**Castable refractories**: Calcium aluminate cement (CAC, 15-25% of mix) bonds refractory aggregate (tabular alumina, bauxite, or magnesite) when mixed with water on site. Installation by pouring into forms (casting), pumping (shotcreting), or pneumatic spraying (gunning). Cures at room temperature like Portland cement (24-hour initial set), then must be dry-fired according to a controlled schedule (ramp 50°C/hour to 150°C hold, then 100°C/hour to 600°C hold, then free heating to service temperature) to drive off free and chemically bound water without steam spalling. Service temperature 1500-1700°C for alumina-based castables. Low-cement castables (LCC, 3-8% CaO) and ultra-low-cement castables (ULCC, <3% CaO) use silica fume and reactive alumina to improve flow, density, and hot strength.

## Refractory Brick Installation

**Mortar joints**: Refractory mortar (fireclay-based or chemically bonded) joins bricks. Joint thickness should be minimized (1-2 mm maximum) because mortar is weaker than the brick and is preferentially attacked by slag. Heat-setting mortars (fireclay + plasticizer) develop ceramic bond at service temperature. Air-setting mortars (with sodium silicate or phosphoric acid binder) develop strength at room temperature but may shrink at high temperature.

**Expansion joints**: Refractory linings expand when heated. Fireclay bricks expand 0.5-0.7% (5-7 mm per meter of lining length). Without expansion joints, the lining buckles and collapses. Expansion joints (5-10 mm wide, filled with ceramic fiber blanket or cardboard that burns out) placed every 1-2 meters of linear length. The exact spacing depends on the brick type, service temperature, and furnace geometry.

**Drying and heat-up schedule**: New refractory linings contain construction moisture and chemically bound water that must be driven off slowly. A typical heat-up schedule: 50°C/hour to 150°C, hold 12-24 hours (remove free water), 50°C/hour to 350°C, hold 6-12 hours, 75°C/hour to 600°C, hold 4-8 hours (remove chemically bound water from castables), then free heating to operating temperature. Too-rapid heat-up causes steam pressure to build within the refractory, resulting in spalling (explosive shedding of the hot face).

## Refractory Selection by Process Temperature

**Below 1000°C**: Fireclay brick or insulating firebrick. Applications: boiler linings, incinerators, forge furnaces, flue ducts. The most forgiving temperature range — thermal shock is moderate, chemical attack is limited, and inexpensive fireclay bricks perform adequately.

**1000-1400°C**: Medium to high-duty fireclay, high-alumina (60-70% Al₂O₃) bricks. Applications: steel reheating furnaces (soaking zone 1250-1300°C), ceramic kilns, non-ferrous melting furnaces (aluminum at 700-800°C, copper at 1100-1200°C). Slag resistance becomes important. For aluminum furnaces, high-alumina or non-wetting castables (containing BaSO₄ additive) resist aluminum penetration.

**1400-1700°C**: High-alumina (80-90% Al₂O₃), magnesite, or silica bricks. Applications: steelmaking furnaces (BOF, EAF at 1600-1700°C), glass tank furnaces (1550-1600°C), cement kiln burning zone (1450°C). Chemical attack from slag, glass melt, or cement clinker is severe. Careful matching of refractory chemistry to the process environment is essential.

**Above 1700°C**: Magnesite, alumina, zirconia, or carbon refractories. Applications: steel ladle slag lines, non-ferrous smelting furnaces, incineration of hazardous waste, and specialized high-temperature processes. At these temperatures, few materials survive, and refractory consumption rates are high.

## Refractory Wear Mechanisms

**Chemical corrosion**: Slag attack is the dominant wear mechanism in steelmaking and non-ferrous metallurgy. Molten slag (CaO-SiO₂-FeO-MnO-Al₂O₃ system for steelmaking) dissolves refractory oxides by forming low-melting phases at the slag-refractory interface. Basic slags (high CaO) dissolve acidic refractories (silica, fireclay). Acidic slags (high SiO₂) dissolve basic refractories (magnesite). The chemical mismatch between slag and refractory is the primary selection criterion. Corrosion rate: 0.1-5 mm per heat in BOF steelmaking, reduced to <0.5 mm/hearth with MgO-C bricks that develop a dense, protective slag layer (in situ refractory).

**Thermal spalling**: Repeated heating and cooling cycles create thermal stress (σ = E × α × ΔT, where E = elastic modulus, α = thermal expansion coefficient, ΔT = temperature gradient). When stress exceeds the refractory's tensile strength (typically 5-15 MPa), cracks initiate and propagate, causing pieces to spall off the hot face. Materials with high thermal expansion (magnesite: 13-14 × 10⁻⁶/°C) and low thermal conductivity are most susceptible. Carbon refractories (high conductivity, moderate expansion) have excellent spalling resistance. Controlled heat-up and cool-down schedules minimize thermal shock.

**Abrasion and erosion**: Moving molten metal, slag, and process gases physically erode refractory surfaces. Areas of high velocity (tapholes, pour pads, burner ports, slag line) wear faster. Hot strength (modulus of rupture at 1300-1500°C) is the best predictor of erosion resistance. Direct-bonded bricks (sintered at high temperature to form direct ceramic-ceramic bonds between grains, without glassy intergranular phase) have superior hot strength and erosion resistance.

**Structural spalling**: Penetration of slag or vapor into the refractory creates a chemically altered zone (the "reaction zone") with different thermal expansion and density from the original brick. During temperature cycling, the boundary between altered and unaltered zones becomes a stress concentration point, causing the altered layer to detach. Common in cement kilns (alkali salt penetration) and glass furnaces (batch dust infiltration). Mitigation: low-porosity bricks, anti-penetration coatings, or periodic "sealing" heats that glaze the hot face.

## Refractory Testing Standards

**Cold crushing strength (CCS)**: Load a 50 mm cube or cylinder of refractory material in compression until failure (ASTM C133). Record maximum load / cross-sectional area. CCS correlates with abrasion resistance and structural load-bearing capacity. Typical values: fireclay 15-50 MPa, high-alumina 50-100 MPa, magnesite 40-80 MPa.

**Pyrometric cone equivalent (PCE)**: A standard pyrometric cone (a triangular pyramid of defined refractory composition) is heated alongside the test sample. The PCE is the cone number that softens and bends at the same temperature as the sample. PCE 15 ≈ 1430°C, PCE 20 ≈ 1520°C, PCE 26 ≈ 1600°C, PCE 31 ≈ 1680°C, PCE 38 ≈ 1790°C. PCE gives the maximum service temperature under no-load conditions (actual service temperature with mechanical load is lower).

**Thermal conductivity**: Measured by hot wire method (ASTM C1113) or guarded hot plate. Critical for estimating heat loss through furnace linings and calculating the required insulation thickness. A typical furnace lining: 230 mm working brick + 115 mm insulating brick + steel shell. Heat loss: 1-5 kW/m² depending on temperature and lining design. Adding 50 mm of ceramic fiber blanket reduces heat loss by 30-50%.

## Refractory Maintenance and Repair

**Gunning repair**: Pneumatically applied repair material extends furnace campaign life without full relining. Dry gunning: pre-damped refractory mix is fed through a gunning machine, conveyed by compressed air through a hose (25-40 mm ID, 10-30 m length), and projected onto the hot furnace surface at 20-40 m/s impact velocity. Water added at the nozzle (8-12% of dry mix weight) activates the hydraulic bond. Application thickness: 50-200 mm per pass. Bond strength depends on impact velocity and surface preparation (remove loose material, install anchors if needed). Gunning can repair 5-20 m²/hour. Shotcreting (wet gunning): pre-mixed wet refractory pumped and sprayed, gives better density and less rebound (waste material that bounces off the surface).

**Hot repair techniques**: Some furnace repairs can be performed without cooling the furnace below operating temperature. Ceramic welding: a stream of refractory powder (silica or alumina) is projected into the damaged area while simultaneously injecting fuel gas (propane or natural gas) and oxygen. The combustion heat locally melts the refractory powder, fusing it to the hot surface. Used for coke oven wall repair at 1000-1200°C without shutting down the battery. Injectables: low-viscosity refractory castable pumped into voids and gaps behind the lining through injection ports drilled through the steel shell. Fills cavities caused by lining erosion without dismantling the furnace.

**Campaign life tracking**: Furnace lining wear is monitored by: (1) infrared thermography of the external steel shell (hot spots indicate lining thinning or failure), (2) laser profilometry of the hot face during shutdown inspections (maps remaining lining thickness), (3) historical tracking of refractory consumption per tonne of product (typical steelmaking: 4-8 kg refractory per tonne of steel for BOF linings, 2-4 kg/t for ladles). BOF lining campaigns: 10,000-25,000 heats before full relining, with intermediate gunning repairs every 500-2000 heats. Cement kiln: 6-18 months per lining campaign for the burning zone. Glass furnace: 8-15 years for the full campaign, limited by AZS block dissolution into the glass melt.

## Refractory Raw Material Processing

**Magnesite processing**: Raw magnesite (MgCO₃) is mined from deposits in China, Russia, North Korea, Turkey, and Brazil. Beneficiation: crush, grind, and remove silica and iron impurities by flotation or magnetic separation. Calcination stages: (1) light-burned at 900-1100°C (reactive, used for Mg(OH)₂ production and refractory castables), (2) dead-burned at 1700-2000°C in shaft or rotary kilns (dense, inert periclase grains for brickmaking). Dead-burned magnesite density: 3.3-3.5 g/cm³. Fuel: heavy fuel oil, natural gas, or petroleum coke. Kiln energy consumption: 8-12 GJ per tonne of dead-burned magnesite.

**Bauxite processing for high-alumina refractories**: Bauxite (Al₂O₃ 55-70%, remainder SiO₂, Fe₂O₃, TiO₂) is calcined at 1500-1600°C to produce dense, stable corundum (α-Al₂O₃) grains. The calcination drives off chemically bound water and converts the aluminum hydroxide minerals (gibbsite, boehmite) to corundum. The resulting "calcined bauxite" has bulk density 3.0-3.3 g/cm³ and is graded by Al₂O₃ content: 70%, 80%, 85%, 90% grades. Higher alumina content commands higher prices and provides higher refractoriness. Guyana, China, and Brazil are major bauxite sources for refractories.

**Silica brick raw materials**: Quartzite rock (SiO₂ >97%) is the primary raw material. The quartzite is crushed to 0-5 mm particle size, mixed with 2-3% milk of lime (Ca(OH)₂ as bond) and 0.5-1.0% iron oxide (mineralizer), pressed into bricks at 50-100 MPa, and fired at 1450-1500°C for 48-96 hours. The long firing time is necessary to convert quartz to cristobalite and tridymite (>80% conversion required for dimensional stability in service). During firing, the bricks expand 10-15% (quartz-to-cristobalite volume increase), requiring special kiln setting to accommodate growth. The conversion is monitored by X-ray diffraction of production samples.

## Refractory Fiber Insulation

**Ceramic fiber blanket**: Alumina-silica fiber (45-60% Al₂O₃, balance SiO₂) produced by melting raw materials at 2000°C and attenuating the melt into fibers (diameter 2-4 μm, length 50-200 mm) by blowing or spinning. Formed into blankets (density 64-160 kg/m³, thickness 13-50 mm) needled for dimensional stability. Classification temperature: 1260°C (standard), 1400°C (high-alumina), 1600°C (zirconia-modified). Thermal conductivity: 0.08-0.25 W/m·K at 500°C (far lower than insulating firebrick at 0.15-0.5 W/m·K). Applications: furnace backup insulation, expansion joint filler, gasketing, and high-temperature sealing.

**Refractory ceramic fiber (RCF) safety**: RCF fibers are respirable (diameter 2-4 μm, length >10 μm) and classified as IARC Group 2B (possibly carcinogenic). During installation and removal, airborne fiber concentrations can exceed 1 fiber/cm³. Control measures: wet methods (spray binder to suppress dust), respiratory protection (P100 respirator minimum), protective clothing (disposable coveralls), and enclosed handling systems. European regulations (REACH) require substitution with biosoluble fiber alternatives (calcium-magnesium-silicate compositions that dissolve in lung fluid with half-life <30 days) where technically feasible. Biosoluble fibers have slightly lower maximum service temperature (1200-1260°C) but eliminate the long-term health risk.

**Calcium silicate board**: Hydrated calcium silicate (CaSiO₃·H₂O) boards, density 200-1000 kg/m³, provide rigid insulation for temperatures up to 1000-1100°C. Thermal conductivity 0.06-0.15 W/m·K at 200°C. Used for furnace backup insulation, refractory backup behind brick linings, and as permanent formwork for castable refractories. Non-fibrous (crystalline structure), avoiding the respiratory hazards of fibrous insulation. Can be machined to precise shapes with woodworking tools. Water resistant but not waterproof.

## Refractory Recycling and Sustainability

**Spent refractory management**: When furnace linings are replaced, the spent refractory material (typically 60-90% unchanged, only the hot-face 10-40% being degraded) can be partially recycled. Chrome-bearing refractories (containing Cr⁶⁺ after service) require specialized disposal as hazardous waste in many jurisdictions. MgO-C bricks can be reclaimed: the carbon is burned off, the MgO grains are reprocessed into new refractory aggregates. Alumina-silicate spent bricks are crushed and used as aggregate for low-duty castables or as construction fill. Recycling rate: 15-30% of total refractory consumption in developed countries.

**Energy and carbon footprint**: Refractory manufacturing is energy-intensive. Fireclay bricks: 2-4 GJ/tonne. High-alumina: 4-8 GJ/tonne. Magnesite (dead-burned): 8-15 GJ/tonne (the highest, due to the extreme calcination temperature). Firing is the dominant energy consumer (natural gas or heavy fuel oil in tunnel kilns). Carbon dioxide emissions: 0.2-0.8 tonnes CO₂ per tonne of refractory product, plus process emissions from calcination of carbonates (MgCO₃ → MgO + CO₂, CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂). The refractory industry is investigating electric firing and hydrogen kilns to decarbonize, but the very high temperatures required (1700-2000°C for magnesite) make this technically challenging.

## Zirconia and Zircon Refractories

**Zirconia properties**: ZrO₂ has a melting point of 2715°C, making it one of the most refractory oxides. However, pure zirconia undergoes a destructive phase transformation from tetragonal to monoclinic (accompanied by 3-5% volume change) at ~1170°C on cooling. This transformation causes cracking and disintegration. Stabilization: adding 6-12 mol% CaO, MgO, or Y₂O₃ forms a solid solution (cubic fluorite structure) that is stable through all temperature ranges. Partially stabilized zirconia (PSZ) retains some transformable tetragonal phase that provides transformation toughening (the volume change absorbs crack energy, increasing fracture toughness from 2-3 to 8-12 MPa·m¹/²).

**Zircon (ZrSiO₄)**: A naturally occurring mineral (zircon sand) with melting point ~2200°C (decomposes to ZrO₂ + SiO₂ above 1670°C). Used in glass contact refractories (glass tank furnace sidewalls and throats), foundry ladle nozzles, and as a foundry sand for investment casting. Zircon resists wetting by molten glass and has low solubility in soda-lime glass melt (dissolution rate ~0.1 mm/day at 1500°C). Fused zirconia-alumina (AZS, Al₂O₃-ZrO₂-SiO₂) blocks, produced by electric arc fusion and casting, contain 30-40% ZrO₂ in an alumina-silica glass matrix and are the standard glass contact refractory for soda-lime glass furnaces (8-15 year campaign life).

## Refractory Standards and Specifications

**ASTM and ISO standards**: Key test methods include ASTM C133 (cold crushing strength), ASTM C20 (apparent porosity, water absorption, and bulk density by boiling water method), ASTM C1113 (thermal conductivity by hot wire), ASTM C583 (modulus of rupture at elevated temperature), ASTM C832 (thermal shock resistance by ribbon brick test), and ISO 10081 (classification of dense shaped refractory products by alumina and silica content). These standards ensure consistent quality specification and comparison between manufacturers. Each brick shipped from a refractory plant is accompanied by a certified test report with physical properties measured on production samples from the same lot.

## Refractory Logistics and Supply Chain

**Delivery and storage**: Refractory bricks are shipped on pallets (500-1000 kg each) wrapped in plastic film for moisture protection. Storage life: unlimited for fired bricks if kept dry. Castable and gunning mixes (containing calcium aluminate cement) have a shelf life of 6-12 months when stored in original packaging under cover (cement hydrates absorb moisture from the air, pre-hydrating and losing strength). Storage temperature: 5-35°C, protected from rain and direct sunlight. A typical relining project for a steelmaking ladle requires 20-50 tonnes of refractory bricks and 5-15 tonnes of castable, delivered on 2-5 truckloads with 1-2 weeks lead time from the refractory manufacturer.

**Refractory selection decision tree**: Start with process temperature (determines refractoriness requirement), then consider the chemical environment (basic vs. acidic slag, reducing vs. oxidizing atmosphere), then mechanical loading (abrasion, impact, structural load), then thermal cycling frequency (determines thermal shock requirement), and finally cost and availability. For most applications, fireclay bricks are the first choice and are upgraded only when they fail prematurely.
