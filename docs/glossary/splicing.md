# Splicing

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, foundations, textiles

The technique of interweaving rope strands to create permanent joins, loops, or sealed ends without knots. Splicing retains significantly more rope strength than knotting and is preferred for all critical load-bearing connections.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Splicing appears in [Rope Making](../textiles/rope-making.md) as the primary joining method, in [Basic Tools](../foundations/tools-basic.md) for cordage construction, and in [Cement](../chemistry/cement.md) for bag closure and material handling. Rope is fundamental to construction, shipping, mining, and agriculture, making splicing an essential skill.

## Technical Details

To splice two ropes or repair breaks, unlay strands 10-15 cm, interleave with matching strands from the other end, and tuck each strand over and under the rope body in sequence. A good splice retains ~80-95% of rope strength compared to only 45-70% for knots, because the splice distributes load across multiple tucks rather than concentrating stress at a single bend.

Three primary splice types: the **eye splice** creates a permanent loop with 3-4 tucks per strand through the rope body — used for attaching ropes to posts, rings, or hooks. The **back splice** seals a rope end against unraveling using a crown knot followed by tucked strands. The **short splice** joins two rope ends permanently by interleaving all six strands (3+3), though the doubled diameter blocks passage through pulleys.

Practice on cheap cordage before attempting critical splices. Taper the final tucks (reduce strand thickness) for a smoother profile. Whip or tape the rope end before unlaying to control the strands during work.
