# Powder Preparation

> **Type**: process | **Tier**: foundational | **Domains**: health, ceramics, metals

Dry plant material or purified drug at 40-60°C until constant weight. Grind in mortar with pestle, then through a series of sieves (coarse to fine). Target particle size <150 μm (passes through a 100-mesh sieve). Finer particles dissolve faster and absorb more consistently. For potent drugs, geometric dilution ensures uniform distribution of the active ingredient.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Powder preparation is a foundational material processing step that appears across multiple domains. In [Pharmacology](../health/pharmacology.md), drug powders must be uniformly sized for consistent dosing — particle size directly affects dissolution rate and bioavailability. In [Advanced Ceramics](../ceramics/advanced-ceramics.md), ceramic powders must be fine and uniform to achieve dense, strong sintered products. In [Powder Metallurgy](../metals/powder-metallurgy.md), metal powders with controlled particle size distribution are pressed and sintered into complex shapes that would be difficult or impossible to cast or machine.

## Technical Details

The core principle is that smaller, more uniform particles behave more predictably in subsequent processing. Particle size controls dissolution rate (pharmaceuticals), sintering temperature and final density (ceramics), and packing efficiency and green strength (powder metallurgy).

Geometric dilution is the standard technique for distributing potent active ingredients: mix the active with an equal weight of inert diluent (lactose, starch), then double the mixture with more diluent, repeating until the active is uniformly distributed at the target concentration. Without this step, a small quantity of potent drug mixed into a large volume of excipient forms pockets of dangerously high and low concentration.

Sieving is the simplest size classification method — a stack of sieves with decreasing mesh sizes (coarse on top, fine on bottom) is shaken mechanically or by hand. For sub-sieve particles (<45 μm), sedimentation in liquid or air classification is used. Ball milling and jet milling achieve the finest powders, with jet milling capable of producing particles below 5 μm using high-velocity gas streams to cause inter-particle collisions.
