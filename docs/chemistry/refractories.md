# Refractory Materials

## Overview

Refractories are heat-resistant ceramic materials that line furnaces, kilns, incinerators, and reactors — maintaining structural integrity at temperatures above 1500°C while resisting chemical attack by molten metals, slags, and hot gases. Without refractories, no metallurgy, glass melting, cement production, or high-temperature chemical processing is possible. They are consumable materials — furnace linings wear and must be periodically replaced. A typical steelmaking shop consumes 6-15 kg of refractories per tonne of steel produced.

## Raw Materials

**Alumina-silicate system** (fireclay and high-alumina):
- **Fireclay** (kaolinite, Al₂O₃·2SiO₂·2H₂O): Mined from clay deposits. When calcined at 1200-1400°C, forms mullite (3Al₂O₃·2SiO₂) + silica glass. Al₂O₃ content 25-45%. Service temperature: 1200-1500°C. The most widely used refractory by volume — inexpensive, workable, adequate for many applications.
- **Bauxite** (gibbsite, Al(OH)₃): Source of high-alumina refractories (Al₂O₃ 60-90%). Calcined at 1500-1600°C to produce dense, stable corundum (α-Al₂O₃). Service temperature: 1600-1800°C. Used in steelmaking furnace roofs and sidewalls.
- **Sillimanite, andalusite, kyanite**: Natural aluminosilicate minerals (Al₂SiO₅ polymorphs) that convert to mullite + silica at 1300-1550°C. Used for high-quality, low-shrinkage refractories.

**Basic refractories** (magnesia and dolomite):
- **Magnesite** (MgCO₃): Calcined to periclase (MgO) at 1700-2000°C. Dead-burned magnesite (dense, low-porosity) is the backbone of basic oxygen furnace (BOF) linings. MgO melting point: 2852°C — the highest of common refractory oxides. Also resistant to basic slags (CaO-based steelmaking slags).
- **Dolomite** (CaCO₃·MgCO₃): Calcined to CaO·MgO at 1600-1800°C. Less expensive than magnesite but hydrates in moist air (CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ — expansion destroys the brick). Used in AOD converters and cement kiln burning zones. Tar-bonded dolomite (impregnated with coal tar pitch) resists hydration — the tar carbon fills pores and forms a protective carbon barrier.
- **Chrome-magnesite**: Blend of chromite (FeCr₂O₄) and magnesite — combines slag resistance (chromite) with hot strength (MgO). Used in non-ferrous metallurgy (copper, lead smelting).

**Silica refractories** (SiO₂ >93%):
- Made from quartzite rock (SiO₂ >97%), converted to cristobalite and tridymite at high temperature. Unique property: very low thermal expansion above 600°C (actually slight contraction above 1000°C). Used almost exclusively for coke oven walls and glass tank furnace crowns (hot-face temperature 1550-1650°C). Not suitable below 600°C — large volume change at 573°C (α→β quartz transition) causes spalling.

**Carbon refractories**:
- Carbon (graphite) blocks: hot-pressed petroleum coke + coal tar pitch binder, fired to 1000-1400°C in reducing atmosphere. Exceptional thermal shock resistance, high thermal conductivity, unreactive with many metals. Used for blast furnace hearth and bosh (withstands molten iron and slag at 1500°C). Also used in aluminum electrolysis cells (carbon cathode blocks). Limitation: oxidizes in air above 400°C (requires reducing atmosphere or protective coating).

**Zirconia and zircon**:
- **Zirconia (ZrO₂)**: Melting point 2715°C. Stabilized with CaO, MgO, or Y₂O₃ to prevent destructive phase transformation (monoclinic → tetragonal → cubic) during heating. Used for continuous casting nozzles and tundish metering orifices in steelmaking — resists erosion by molten steel.
- **Zircon (ZrSiO₄)**: Used in glass contact refractories (resists dissolution by soda-lime glass melt). Also in foundry ladle nozzles.

## Manufacturing Process

**Shaped refractories (bricks and shapes)**:
1. **Raw material preparation**: Crush and grind to target particle size distribution. Grading: coarse (3-6 mm) for skeleton, medium (1-3 mm) for packing, fine (<0.1 mm) for bonding and sintering. The particle size distribution is critical — optimized for maximum packing density (minimum porosity → maximum strength and slag resistance).
2. **Mixing**: Dry blending of graded aggregates, followed by addition of binder (water + clay for fireclay bricks; sulfite lye or phosphoric acid for basic bricks; coal tar pitch for carbon-bonded bricks). Mix in pug mill or high-intensity mixer.
3. **Forming**: Pressing under 50-200 MPa in hydraulic or friction screw presses. Large shapes may be air-rammed or vibration-cast. Pressing density directly determines fired density and porosity.
4. **Drying**: Remove moisture at 80-110°C (24-48 hours). Prevents steam spalling during firing.
5. **Firing (sintering)**: Tunnel kiln at 1300-1800°C (temperature depends on composition). 12-48 hours. Sintering bonds particles by partial melting (liquid-phase sintering) or solid-state diffusion. Fireclay: 1300-1400°C. High-alumina: 1500-1600°C. Magnesite: 1700-1800°C. Shrinkage: 2-8% (controlled by raw material selection and pressing density).

**Unshaped refractories (monolithics)**:
- **Castables**: Refractory aggregate + calcium aluminate cement (CAC) binder. Mixed with water on-site, poured or pumped into forms, vibrated to consolidate. CAC hydrates and sets like Portland cement but withstands 1500-1700°C after firing. Low-cement castables (LCC, 3-8% CaO) use ultra-fine alumina powder (fume) to improve particle packing and reduce cement content — higher density, better hot strength, superior slag resistance. Ultra-low-cement castables (ULCC, <3% CaO) for the most demanding applications.
- **Gunning mixes**: Dry or pre-mixed refractory material pneumatically sprayed onto hot furnace surfaces to repair worn areas without cooling the furnace. Applied at 20-40 m/s impact velocity. Adhesion by impact bonding. Used for in-situ repair of BOF linings, cement kilns, and incinerators — extends campaign life without costly shutdowns.
- **Ramming mixes**: Semi-dry material tamped into place with pneumatic rammers. Used for furnace hearths, ladle bottoms, and around tap holes. Typically magnesite or alumina-based with resin or tar binder.
- **Plastic refractories**: Clay-bonded, putty-like consistency — pressed into place by hand or pneumatic ram. Used for patching and complex shapes where forms are impractical.

## Properties and Selection Criteria

| Property | Fireclay | High-Alumina (70%) | Magnesite | Silica | Carbon |
|----------|----------|---------------------|-----------|--------|--------|
| Max service temp (°C) | 1400 | 1700 | 1800+ | 1650 | 2000+ (reducing) |
| Cold crush strength (MPa) | 25-60 | 50-100 | 40-80 | 30-60 | 30-50 |
| Thermal conductivity (W/m·K at 1000°C) | 1.2-1.5 | 1.5-2.0 | 3.0-5.0 | 1.5-2.0 | 15-40 |
| Thermal shock resistance | Good | Good | Poor | Moderate | Excellent |
| Slag resistance (basic) | Poor | Moderate | Excellent | Poor | Good |
| Slag resistance (acidic) | Good | Good | Poor | Excellent | Good |
| Cost (relative) | 1.0× | 2-4× | 3-6× | 2-3× | 3-5× |

**Thermal shock resistance** is critical — refractory linings undergo temperature cycling (heating and cooling during furnace operation). Spalling (fracture and flaking) occurs when thermal stress from temperature gradients exceeds the material's tensile strength. Materials with low thermal expansion and high thermal conductivity resist thermal shock. Carbon is excellent (high conductivity, moderate expansion). Magnesite is poor (high expansion, lower conductivity) — requires careful heating/cooling schedules.

## Applications by Industry

**Steelmaking**: BOF converters use MgO-C bricks (magnesite-carbon — carbon improves thermal shock resistance and reduces slag wetting). Campaign life: 10,000-25,000 heats (line is periodically patched with gunning mix between relining). Electric arc furnaces: MgO-C sidewalls, water-cooled roof panels. Ladles: alumina-spinel or MgO-C working lining. Continuous casting tundish: alumina or zirconia metering nozzles. Blast furnace: carbon hearth, SiC or alumina bosh, fireclay stack.

**Glass manufacturing**: Silica crown (hot-face 1600°C), alumina-zirconia-silica (AZS) fused-cast blocks in glass contact areas — produced by melting raw materials in an electric arc furnace and casting into molds. AZS contains 30-40% ZrO₂ in an alumina-silica glass matrix — zirconia crystals resist dissolution by glass melt. Glass contact blocks slowly dissolve into the glass — refractory corrosion limits furnace campaign to 8-15 years.

**Cement production**: Rotary kiln burning zone: magnesite-spinel or dolomite bricks (resist CaO-SiO₂-Al₂O₃ clinker melt attack). Transition zones: high-alumina or fireclay. Preheater tower: alkali-resistant castables (alkali vapor condensation attacks alumina-silicate refractories — causes hot-phase formation and spalling).

**Non-ferrous metallurgy**: Copper reverberatory furnace: magnesite-chrome. Aluminum melting furnace: high-alumina (resistant to Al₂O₃ formation at metal-refractory interface — molten aluminum reduces SiO₂ from fireclay). Zinc distillation: silicon carbide (withstands zinc vapor at 900-950°C — SiC resists zinc attack better than alumina-silicates).

**Petrochemical**: FCC unit: high-alumina castable with steel fiber reinforcement (withstands erosion by catalyst particles at 700°C). Hydrogen reformer: high-alumina castable (resistant to hydrogen attack at 900°C — hydrogen reduces SiO₂ from fireclay, causing volume changes). Ethylene cracking furnace: high-alumina castable (1200°C, exposure to carburizing atmosphere).

## Safety & Hazards

- **Crystalline silica dust**: Exposure during refractory installation, demolition, and cutting causes silicosis. Use wet cutting methods, respiratory protection (P100 masks), local exhaust ventilation. OSHA PEL: 0.05 mg/m³ (respirable fraction).
- **Refractory ceramic fiber (RCF)**: Synthetic vitreous fiber used as high-temperature insulation — possible carcinogen (IARC Group 2B). Handle with gloves and respirator. Replace with biosoluble fiber alternatives where possible.
- **Thermal burns**: Newly installed refractory linings retain heat for hours after furnace shutdown. Temperature verification with contact pyrometer before entry. Lock-out/tag-out procedures mandatory.
- **Carbon monoxide**: Carbon-containing refractories (MgO-C, carbon blocks) can generate CO during heating as carbon reacts with residual oxygen. Ventilate furnaces during heat-up before personnel entry.
- **Heavy metals**: Chrome-bearing refractories (chrome-magnesite) contain Cr⁶⁺ (hexavalent chromium, carcinogen) after service in oxidizing conditions. Disposal of spent chrome-bearing refractories regulated as hazardous waste in many jurisdictions.
- **Manual handling**: Refractory bricks are dense and heavy (2.5-3.5 kg/brick for standard 230×114×64 mm straight). A typical relining job requires placing 5,000-50,000 bricks — ergonomic injury risk. Use mechanical lifting aids where possible.
