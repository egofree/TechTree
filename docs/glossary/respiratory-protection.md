# Respiratory Protection

> **Type**: safety | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: health, mining, ceramics, silicon

Disposable dust mask rated P1 (80% filtration at 0.3 μm), P2 (94%), P3/P100 (99.97%). For silica dust, only P100 class provides adequate protection. Half-face respirator with P100 filter cartridges requires tight seal and mandatory fit testing. For toxic gases (CO, H₂S, PH₃), supplied-air or SCBA is required — cartridge respirators do not protect against these hazards.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Respiratory protection is the last line of defense when engineering controls cannot eliminate airborne hazards. In [Mining](../mining/drilling.md) and [Mineral Processing](../mining/processing.md), silica dust from rock drilling causes silicosis — an irreversible, progressive lung disease. In [Ceramics](../ceramics/kilns.md), kiln firing produces CO and sulfur gases. In [Silicon Production](../silicon/mg-si-production.md), furnace operations generate SiO₂ fume and CO. In [Black Powder](../mining/black-powder.md) manufacturing, dust explosions are a constant risk. Respiratory protection is documented within health and safety practices across all dusty or gas-producing processes.

## Technical Details

The hierarchy of respiratory protection, from least to most protective: disposable dust masks (P1/P2/P100 ratings), half-face elastomeric respirators with replaceable cartridges, full-face respirators (also protects eyes), powered air-purifying respirators (PAPR — battery-powered blower pushes filtered air into a loose-fitting hood), supplied-air respirators (airline from clean air source), and self-contained breathing apparatus (SCBA — firefighter-type tank).

For silica dust, the critical hazard in mining and ceramics, only P100 class (99.97% filtration at the most penetrating particle size of 0.3 μm) provides adequate protection. Silica particles are typically 0.5-5 μm — right in the respirable range that penetrates deep into the lungs. The mechanism of harm is that silica crystals cause macrophages (lung immune cells) to release inflammatory cytokines, leading to fibrosis (scarring) that progressively reduces lung function. There is no cure; prevention is the only option.

Fit testing is mandatory for tight-sealing respirators: quantitative fit testing measures particle concentration inside vs. outside the mask while the wearer performs exercises (breathing normally, turning head, talking, bending over). A fit factor ≥100 (99% protection) is the minimum passing standard. Beards, sideburns, and even stubble break the face seal — workers who cannot achieve a seal must use PAPR or supplied-air systems.

For toxic gases like carbon monoxide (CO, from incomplete combustion), hydrogen sulfide (H₂S, from sulfur-containing minerals), and phosphine (PH₃, from semiconductor processing), cartridge respirators are inadequate — these gases have poor warning properties (CO is odorless, H₂S paralyzes the olfactory nerve at dangerous concentrations) and can be immediately dangerous to life at concentrations above the cartridge's breakthrough time.
