# Finishing

Finishing is the set of secondary processes applied after primary forming or machining to achieve the final dimensions, surface quality, and appearance of a component. After hobbing gear teeth, for example, all tooth edges must be deburred with a fine file or abrasive wheel. Mating gear pairs are then lapped together with fine abrasive compound to seat the tooth surfaces. The engagement cycle is tested by rotating through full mesh—any tight spots indicate a high tooth requiring further lapping.

In metal casting, finishing includes removing gates, risers, and flash; grinding parting lines smooth; and applying surface treatments such as plating, anodizing, or painting. In printing, finishing encompasses trimming, binding, and folding operations that convert printed sheets into final products. In textile production, finishing covers bleaching, dyeing, and calendering that give fabric its final hand and appearance.

Finishing operations often determine the perceived quality of a product even though they add cost without changing the core functionality. In bootstrap manufacturing, the level of finishing applied should be matched to the application: a machine frame needs dimensional accuracy but not a polished surface, while a bearing journal needs both dimensional precision and smooth surface finish to function correctly.
