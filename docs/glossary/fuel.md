# Fuel

Fuel is any material that stores energy in chemical form and releases it through combustion or electrochemical reaction. In bootstrap metallurgy, charcoal with forced air (electric blower or hand-cranked fan) provides the primary heat source. Aluminum melts at 660°C, requiring roughly 750°C for adequate fluidity—any charcoal-burning furnace that reaches dull red heat can melt aluminum. For iron and steel production, temperatures of 1200–1600°C are needed, demanding forced-draft furnaces with refractory linings.

Liquid fuels derived from petroleum (gasoline, diesel, kerosene) offer higher energy density and easier handling than solid fuels. Gasoline provides approximately 44 MJ/kg, diesel 45 MJ/kg, and charcoal roughly 30 MJ/kg. Gaseous fuels (natural gas, producer gas, biogas) offer the cleanest combustion and easiest flow control but require pressure-tight distribution systems.

Fuel selection for each process depends on required temperature, heating rate, atmosphere control (reducing vs. oxidizing), cost, and availability. In a bootstrap context, charcoal from managed woodland is often the first fuel available, supplemented by coke as coal processing develops. Energy density, flame temperature, and ash content are the key parameters that determine suitability for each application.
