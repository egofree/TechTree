Beekeeping (apiculture) is the managed cultivation of honey bees (*Apis mellifera*) for honey, beeswax, propolis, and pollination services. Honey bees are the only domesticated insect — they require no feeding (they forage), produce multiple high-value materials, and provide pollination that increases crop yields 20-30% on average. A single well-managed hive yields 10-25 kg honey and 0.5-2 kg beeswax per year while supporting agriculture within a 1-2 km foraging radius.

The species *Apis mellifera* (Western honey bee) is the primary managed bee worldwide. Native to Europe, Africa, and western Asia, it has been introduced to every continent except Antarctica. Colonies are perennial superorganisms — a single queen, 10,000-80,000 workers, and 0-2,000 drones functioning as a reproductive unit. Workers maintain the hive at 34-36°C brood temperature and 50-60% relative humidity regardless of external conditions, using collective thermoregulation (wing-muscle vibration for heating, evaporative cooling via water spreading for cooling).

## Log Hive Beekeeping

**Principle**: A hollow tree trunk section provides a natural cavity that mimics the wild bee nest environment. Bees build comb freely inside. This is the oldest form of managed beekeeping — humans have harvested honey from log hives for at least 10,000 years (evidence from cave paintings in Valencia, Spain, dated ~8000 BCE).

**Prerequisites**: [`foundations.tools-basic`](../foundations/tools-basic.md) (axe, auger, knife), [`foundations.fire`](../foundations/fire.md) (optional, for charring interior).

**Materials**: One hollow log section 60-100 cm long, 30-50 cm diameter. Two wooden discs for end closures. Wedges or clay for sealing. Small amount of beeswax for baiting.

**Procedure**:
1. Select a straight hardwood log (oak, chestnut, beech) 60-100 cm long, 30-50 cm diameter. Hollow it by burning and scraping, or find a naturally hollow section.
2. Char the interior lightly over fire to sterilize and smooth surfaces.
3. Close both ends with fitted wooden discs secured with wedges or clay. Ensure a snug fit — bees will propolize any gaps.
4. Bore a single entrance hole 2-3 cm diameter near one end, facing southeast if possible.
5. Rub interior walls with beeswax and crushed lemon balm to attract swarms.
6. Position the log hive 1-3 m above ground in a tree or on a stand. Secure against wind.
7. Wait for a swarm to occupy (late spring to early summer). Check weekly. A baited hive in a good location has a 30-50% chance of attracting a swarm per season.
8. Harvest in late summer by removing one end disc and cutting out comb. Separate honey comb (upper) from brood comb (contains larvae).

**Expected yield**: 5-10 kg honey per hive per year. 0.2-0.5 kg beeswax. Colony must rebuild all comb after destructive harvest — consumes 6-8 kg honey per kg of wax produced.

**Strengths**:
- No specialized tools required beyond axe and auger
- Mimics natural nest cavity — high swarm acceptance rate
- Good winter insulation from thick wood walls
- Can be made from readily available forest materials

**Weaknesses**:
- Destructive harvest damages or destroys the colony
- Cannot inspect brood or monitor queen health
- Heavy and difficult to move once occupied
- Comb is attached to walls — cannot remove individual combs

## Skep Beekeeping

**Principle**: An inverted woven basket provides a lightweight, portable hive enclosure. Bees attach comb directly to the interior walls. Skeps were the dominant hive type in northern Europe from the early Middle Ages through the 1800s. Still used for swarm collection.

**Prerequisites**: [`plants.fiber-plants`](../plants/fiber-plants.md) (basket weaving skill), [`foundations.tools-basic`](../foundations/tools-basic.md) (knife, cutting tools).

**Materials**: Straw (wheat, rye) or willow withies, 2-3 kg. Hazel sticks for rigidity. Cow dung mixed with clay for weatherproofing (2-3 kg). Beeswax for baiting (50-100 g).

**Procedure**:
1. Weave an inverted basket 30-40 cm diameter at base, 25-35 cm height. Use coiled straw or willow withies reinforced with hazel sticks.
2. Coat exterior with a mixture of cow dung and clay — dries to a hard, water-resistant shell.
3. Rub interior with beeswax and crushed lemon balm.
4. Place skep on a flat stone or wooden stand 30-50 cm above ground. Entrance faces south or southeast.
5. Bait with swarm attractant during swarming season (late spring to early summer).
6. Once occupied, monitor entrance activity. Strong colonies show heavy traffic at the entrance.
7. Harvest in late summer by tipping the skep and cutting out comb with a sharp knife.

**Expected yield**: 5-10 kg honey per hive per year. 0.2-0.5 kg beeswax. A skilled basket weaver produces one skep in 2-3 hours.

**Strengths**:
- Lightweight and portable — easy to reposition
- Cheap and fast to make from agricultural by-products
- Excellent for swarm catching and temporary housing
- No woodworking tools required

**Weaknesses**:
- Destructive harvest kills or severely weakens the colony
- Poor winter survival in cold climates (thin walls, no insulation)
- No internal structure — cannot inspect individual combs
- Illegal in some jurisdictions (cannot inspect for disease)

## Top-Bar Hive Beekeeping

**Principle**: A horizontal trough with removable wooden bars across the top allows bees to build comb downward from each bar. Individual bars can be lifted for non-destructive inspection and harvest. This is the recommended intermediate technology for bootstrap beekeeping — it provides modern management capability without precision woodworking.

**Prerequisites**: [`foundations.tools-basic`](../foundations/tools-basic.md) (saw, hammer, measuring tools), sawn lumber (planks).

**Materials**: Sawn lumber — 5-10 boards, 80-120 cm long. Wooden bars 3-3.5 cm wide (one per comb). Legs or stand material. Roof panel (separate, tilted for rain runoff). Beeswax for starter strips (thin line melted onto bar center to guide comb).

**Procedure**:
1. Construct a horizontal trough 80-120 cm long, 30-40 cm wide, 25-30 cm deep from sawn planks.
2. Cut wooden top bars 3-3.5 cm wide to span the trough opening. The 3-3.5 cm width matches natural bee comb spacing (bee space).
3. Attach legs or build an elevated stand 50-80 cm above ground for predator protection and working comfort.
4. Make a separate removable roof panel, tilted 5-10° for rain runoff.
5. Melt a thin line of beeswax along the center of each bar as a starter strip — guides bees to build straight comb centered on each bar.
6. Bait with beeswax and lemon balm as with other hives.
7. Transfer a swarm or split colony into the hive. Close the roof.
8. Inspect every 7-10 days during active season by lifting individual bars.
9. Harvest honey by removing bars with capped honey comb. Cut comb from bar, leaving 1 cm stub for re-attachment. Return bar to hive.

**Expected yield**: 10-25 kg honey per hive per year. 0.5-1.5 kg beeswax. Colony survives harvest because individual combs can be removed without destroying the brood nest.

**Strengths**:
- Non-destructive inspection and harvest preserves colony
- No precision woodworking required — saw and hammer sufficient
- Natural comb size allows bees to build cells appropriate to their needs
- Easy to manage at waist height (elevated stand)

**Weaknesses**:
- Comb is fragile without wire support — cannot use centrifugal extractor
- Limited to horizontal expansion (no stacking supers)
- Requires sawn lumber (more processed material than logs or baskets)
- Comb must be cut from bars during harvest (crush-and-strain method)

## Frame Hive Beekeeping (Langstroth)

**Principle**: Stackable rectangular boxes (supers) with precisely spaced removable frames. The critical innovation is "bee space" (7-9 mm) — bees respect this gap and do not glue surfaces together with propolis or comb. Frames hold foundation sheets embossed with hexagonal cell pattern (5.2-5.4 mm cell diameter) that guides comb construction. This enables non-destructive extraction via centrifugal force, comb reuse, and easy colony inspection.

**Prerequisites**: [`foundations.tools-basic`](../foundations/tools-basic.md) (saw, hammer, square), [`metals.copper-bronze`](../metals/copper-bronze.md) (wire for frame reinforcement, nails), precision woodworking capability (consistent dimensions ±1 mm).

**Materials**: Sawn lumber for boxes and frames. Beeswax foundation sheets or plastic foundation. Thin wire (copper or steel) for frame wiring. Nails or screws. Metal clips or staples. Paint or preservative for exterior.

**Procedure**:
1. Construct rectangular boxes (supers) from planed lumber. Standard dimensions: 50.5 cm × 41.0 cm × 24.5 cm (deep), 17.0 cm (medium), or 14.5 cm (shallow) height. Maintain bee space (7-9 mm) between frame surfaces and box walls.
2. Construct wooden frames: deep (24 × 43 cm), medium (17 × 43 cm), shallow (14 × 43 cm). Wire each frame horizontally with 2-4 strands of thin wire.
3. Embed beeswax foundation sheets into frames by melting wax along wire contacts with a heated spur embedder.
4. Stack boxes: bottom board → brood chamber (1-2 deep supers with frames) → queen excluder (optional metal grid, 4.0 mm spacing) → honey supers (medium or shallow) → inner cover → telescoping outer cover.
5. Introduce bees: transfer frames of brood and bees from an established colony, or install a package of bees (1-1.5 kg with caged queen).
6. Inspect every 7-10 days. Add supers when bees occupy 70-80% of existing space.
7. Harvest by removing capped honey supers. Uncap cells with a heated knife. Extract honey by centrifugal force in a radial or tangential extractor (2-4 frames, 5-10 minutes per batch).
8. Return empty combs to hive for reuse — bees refill them, saving the 6-8 kg honey per kg wax that comb construction costs.

**Expected yield**: 15-25 kg honey per hive per year (experienced beekeepers report 30-40 kg in strong nectar regions). 0.5-2 kg beeswax (cappings yield highest grade). Comb is reusable for 5-7 years.

**Strengths**:
- Maximum honey yield through comb reuse and super stacking
- Non-destructive centrifugal extraction preserves comb and colony
- Easy inspection enables disease monitoring and swarm prevention
- Standardized dimensions allow interchangeability between hives

**Weaknesses**:
- Requires precision woodworking (±1 mm tolerance on bee space)
- Higher material cost (wire, foundation, multiple boxes, metal fittings)
- Heavier to lift when full (a deep super with honey weighs 30-35 kg)
- Cell size on foundation may not match natural bee preferences (potential impact on varroa resistance)

## Colony Management

**Principle**: Honey bee colonies are superorganisms that self-regulate temperature, humidity, and brood production. Management consists of providing space, preventing swarming, monitoring health, and ensuring adequate winter stores. The beekeeper works with — not against — colony instincts.

**Prerequisites**: [`foundations.fire`](../foundations/fire.md) (smoker), protective equipment (veil, gloves). Hive tool (flat steel bar 20-25 cm).

**Procedure — Swarm prevention and colony splitting**:
1. Inspect colonies every 7-10 days during spring buildup. Look for queen cups (swarm cells) on comb bottom edges.
2. If colony has 8+ frames of brood and is building queen cells, split the colony. Move 3-4 frames of brood, honey, and bees (with the old queen) to a new hive at least 3 m away.
3. The original hive will raise a new queen from a young larva — workers build an emergency queen cell and feed larva exclusively royal jelly (queen development: 16 days vs 21 for workers).
4. Alternatively, introduce a mated queen to the queenless half for faster buildup.
5. Add space (supers or empty bars) before colonies become congested.

**Procedure — Queen identification**:
The queen is 20-25 mm long (longer than workers at 12-15 mm), with a tapered abdomen extending past her wings. She is the only reproductive female. An unmated virgin queen takes a nuptial flight 5-10 days after emergence, mating with 10-20 drones in flight, storing sperm in her spermatheca for her entire reproductive life (2-5 years). She determines colony temperament — replacing an aggressive queen with a gentle one transforms colony behavior within 4-6 weeks.

**Procedure — Disease monitoring**:
- **Varroa destructor** mite: External parasite feeding on bee fat body tissue. Monitor with sugar roll or alcohol wash (sample 300 bees, count mites). Treatment threshold: 2-3 mites per 100 bees. Treatments: [oxalic acid drip](../glossary/varroa-destructor.md) (2-3% solution, broodless period), formic acid pad, thymol crystals. Without treatment, most colonies collapse within 2-3 years.
- **American foulbrood** ([AFB](../glossary/american-foulbrood.md)): Bacterial disease (*Paenibacillus larvae*) killing larvae. Sunken, perforated cappings; ropiness test (infected larvae string out 2+ cm). Spores persist 50+ years. Treatment: burn infected hive and equipment. No cure once established.
- **Nosema**: Fungal gut parasite (*Nosema apis*, *N. ceranae*). Dysentery (brown spotting on hive front), reduced lifespan. Treat with fumagillin or re-queen with resistant stock.

**Expected yield**: Well-managed colonies maintain 50,000-80,000 bees at summer peak, collecting 0.5-1.5 kg nectar per day during peak flow. A colony consumes 15-30 kg pollen per year for brood production.

**Strengths**:
- Non-destructive management preserves colony productivity
- Regular inspection catches disease early before colony loss
- Queen replacement can change colony temperament in one generation
- Splitting doubles hive count and produces surplus colonies for sale

**Weaknesses**:
- Requires weekly time commitment during active season (7-10 day inspection cycle)
- Disease pressure (varroa, AFB) demands ongoing vigilance and treatment
- Swarm prevention requires timely intervention — missing by even a few days can result in swarm loss
- Colony splitting reduces honey yield in the split year (both halves rebuild)

## Honey Harvesting and Extraction

**Principle**: Honey is ripe when bees cap cells with wax at moisture content below 20%. Harvesting removes capped honey while returning empty comb (in frame hives) or cutting comb (in top-bar/log/skep hives). Smoke masks alarm pheromone and triggers feeding response, making bees docile.

**Prerequisites**: [`foundations.fire`](../foundations/fire.md) (smoker), [`foundations.tools-basic`](../foundations/tools-basic.md) (knife, hive tool), [`textiles.weaving`](../textiles/weaving.md) (cloth for straining), [`ceramics.pottery`](../ceramics/pottery.md) (storage containers).

**Materials**: Smoker (metal can with bellows). Sharp knife or heated uncapping knife. Muslin or canvas bags for crush-and-strain. Cloth (linen or cotton) for filtering. Containers for collection. Centrifugal extractor (optional, for frame hives only).

**Procedure — Smoking**:
1. Burn dried wood chips, burlap, or dry leaves in the smoker. Puff gently to establish cool white smoke.
2. Direct 2-3 puffs of cool smoke across hive entrance. Wait 30 seconds.
3. Remove cover; direct smoke under the cover across the top bars. Wait 30 seconds.
4. Smoke masks alarm pheromone (isopentyl acetate) and triggers feeding response — bees gorge on honey, making them docile.
5. Re-apply smoke every 5-10 minutes during inspection. Do not over-smoke — excessive smoke drives bees out of the hive.

**Procedure — Extraction (skep and log hives)**:
1. Cut comb sections from hive with a sharp knife.
2. Separate honey comb (upper portion) from brood comb (contains larvae — return to hive if possible).
3. Crush honey comb in a muslin or canvas bag.
4. Strain through coarse cloth (linen or cotton) into a container.
5. Let gravity drain for 12-24 hours.
6. Destructive method — colony must rebuild comb.

**Procedure — Extraction (top-bar and frame hives)**:
1. For top bars: cut comb from bars with a knife.
2. For frame hives with wired foundation: uncap cells by slicing off wax cappings with a heated knife.
3. Extract honey by centrifugal force in a radial or tangential extractor (rotating basket that flings honey out of cells without destroying comb). A hand-cranked tangential extractor holds 2-4 frames, processes in 5-10 minutes.
4. Return empty combs to hive — bees refill them.

**Honey moisture and ripeness**:
- Properly ripened honey: 17-20% moisture content. Bees ripen nectar (initially 70-80% water) by fanning and evaporation, capping cells when moisture drops below 20%.
- Above 20% moisture: fermentation risk (osmophilic yeasts multiply). Test by hydrometer or refractometer.
- If moisture is high, heat gently to 40-45°C to evaporate excess (do not exceed 45°C — destroys enzymes and flavor).

**Expected yield**: See table below for yield by hive type. Honey stores indefinitely at proper moisture (<18%) due to high osmotic pressure, low pH (3.2-4.5), and hydrogen peroxide production by glucose oxidase.

| Hive Type | Annual Honey Yield | Annual Beeswax Yield | Harvest Method | Colony Survival |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|----------------|-----------------|
| Log hive | 5-10 kg | 0.2-0.5 kg | Destructive | Poor to fair |
| Skep | 5-10 kg | 0.2-0.5 kg | Destructive | Poor |
| Top-bar | 10-25 kg | 0.5-1.5 kg | Non-destructive | Good |
| Frame hive | 15-25 kg (up to 40 kg) | 0.5-2.0 kg | Non-destructive | Good |

**Strengths**:
- Non-destructive methods (top-bar, frame) preserve colony for repeated harvests
- Centrifugal extraction in frame hives allows comb reuse, saving 6-8 kg honey per kg wax
- Honey is a shelf-stable food requiring no processing beyond straining
- Multiple harvests per season possible in strong nectar flows

**Weaknesses**:
- Destructive methods (log, skep) kill or severely weaken colonies
- Extraction equipment (centrifugal extractor) requires materials and construction
- Honey moisture must be monitored — fermented honey is unsuitable for storage
- Processing leaves residual wax and propolis that require cleanup

## Beeswax Processing

**Principle**: Beeswax (melting point 62-64°C, density 0.95 g/cm³) is less dense than water and floats when melted. Rendering separates wax from comb debris by melting in water and skimming the solidified wax cake. Multiple melting and filtering cycles increase purity.

**Prerequisites**: [`foundations.fire`](../foundations/fire.md) (heat source), [`ceramics.pottery`](../ceramics/pottery.md) (heat-resistant container), [`textiles.weaving`](../textiles/weaving.md) (filter cloth).

**Materials**: Comb fragments and cappings (from honey extraction). Water. Heat-resistant pot (stainless steel or enamel — wax reacts with iron). Muslin or cheesecloth for straining. Tall container for cooling.

**Procedure**:
1. Combine comb and cappings with water in a heat-resistant pot.
2. Heat to 62-65°C (beeswax melts at 62-64°C). Maintain temperature — do not boil.
3. Stir to release wax from comb structure. Old brood comb (dark, contains cocoon fibers and propolis) yields less clean wax than light cappings.
4. Strain the melted mixture through fine cloth (muslin or cheesecloth) into a tall container. Debris (old cocoons, propolis, pollen) is caught in the cloth.
5. Beeswax floats (SG 0.95) and solidifies as a cake on the surface as the water cools.
6. Remove the solidified wax cake. Scrape debris from the bottom surface.
7. Re-melt and filter through activated charcoal or fine filter paper for white wax (removes residual pigments). Repeated melting and filtering increases purity.

**Expected yield**: 0.5-2 kg beeswax per hive per year. Cappings wax (from frame hives) is the highest grade — light yellow, clean. Brood comb wax is dark brown with impurities — requires more purification. Color indicates purity and age: light yellow (fresh cappings) → dark brown (old brood comb). Stable indefinitely if kept cool and away from direct sunlight.

**Strengths**:
- Beeswax is a high-value co-product with many industrial applications
- Rendering is a simple water-based process requiring no chemicals
- Multiple grades obtainable by filtering — highest grade (white wax) commands premium
- Indefinite shelf life when stored properly

**Weaknesses**:
- Low yield relative to honey (wax:honey ratio approximately 1:10-15 by weight)
- Bees consume 6-8 kg honey to produce 1 kg wax — excessive wax harvesting weakens colonies
- Brood comb wax contains impurities requiring extra purification steps
- Wax is flammable (flash point ~210°C) — requires temperature control during rendering

## Spring (Colony Buildup)

First inspection when temperature exceeds 15°C. Check for queen presence (eggs and young larvae present). Assess food stores — feed sugar syrup (1:1 sugar:water by weight) if stores are low before nectar flow begins. Add supers or bars as population expands. Monitor for swarm cells (queen cups on comb bottom edges) and split colonies or add space to prevent swarming. Replace old or damaged comb. Entrance reducer open to medium setting as temperatures rise. Pollen availability drives brood production — protein from pollen is essential for larval development. A colony consumes 15-30 kg pollen per year.

**Timing**: Weeks 10-22 (March-May, Northern Hemisphere). Nectar flow begins with early spring bloom (willow, dandelion, fruit trees).

## Summer (Honey Flow)

Peak nectar collection period. Add supers ahead of colony needs (bees need space to store incoming nectar). Inspect every 7-10 days for swarm signs, queen health, and disease. Harvest honey when frames are 80%+ capped. Maintain water source. In hot climates, provide shade or ventilation to prevent bees from expending energy on hive cooling rather than foraging. Monitor for robbing behavior (bees from other colonies stealing honey) — reduce entrance if robbing is observed. Queen may slow laying during extreme heat.

**Timing**: Weeks 22-35 (June-August). Main nectar flow from clover, basswood/linden, wildflowers. Second harvest possible in late summer.

## Autumn (Preparation)

Final honey harvest — leave 15-20 kg honey on the hive for winter food. Assess colony strength; combine weak colonies. Treat for [varroa mites](../glossary/varroa-destructor.md) if present (primary bee parasite — monitoring and treatment essential). Reduce entrance size to prevent robbing by other bees and wasp intrusion. Install mouse guard at hive entrance. Ensure hive is weather-tight. Tilt hive slightly forward (2-3°) so rainwater drains out rather than pooling on the floor board. Remove queen excluders if used — the cluster must be able to move upward through the hive during winter.

**Timing**: Weeks 35-44 (September-October). Nectar flow ends. Goldenrod and aster may provide late flow.

## Winter (Dormancy)

Minimal intervention. Bees form a tight cluster around the queen, maintaining 20-35°C at cluster center by vibrating flight muscles. The cluster slowly moves upward through the honey stores over winter. Do not open hive below 10°C — breaking the cluster exposes brood to cold and wastes energy. Heft hive periodically to estimate remaining honey stores by weight (a full deep frame weighs ~3.5 kg, empty ~0.5 kg — experienced beekeepers can judge stores by tilting the hive from the back). If dangerously light, feed fondant or candy board on top bars (not liquid syrup — bees cannot evaporate moisture in cold weather). Clear dead bees from entrance to maintain ventilation. Check for animal damage (woodpeckers, mice). Ensure hive is not exposed to persistent cold wind.

**Timing**: Weeks 44-10 (November-February). Colony consumes 15-20 kg honey for heating energy. Winter mortality rate: 10-30% in managed colonies (varroa-related losses account for most).


## Honey

- **Food**: 3,030 kcal/kg energy density. Primarily fructose (38%) and glucose (31%), with water (17-20%), maltose (7%), and trace minerals/vitamins. Requires no preparation — eat directly.
- **Wound treatment**: Osmotic effect draws moisture from bacteria (dehydrating them). Glucose oxidase enzyme produces low levels of hydrogen peroxide (antiseptic). Low pH (3.2-4.5) inhibits microbial growth. Apply medical-grade honey directly to wounds — Manuka-type honey (high methylglyoxal content) is particularly effective. See [`health.medicine`](../health/medicine.md).
- **Mead fermentation**: 1 kg honey + 4 L water + yeast (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae*) → ~5 L mead at 8-12% ABV. Fermentation time: 2-6 weeks at 18-22°C. Honey's antimicrobial properties require adequate yeast pitching and nutrient supplements (fermentation nutrients, or add fruit juice for tannins and acids). Mead is the oldest fermented beverage — evidence dates to 7000 BCE in China.
- **Preservative**: High osmotic pressure and low pH preserve fruits, herbs, and meats when immersed in honey.

## Beeswax

(melting point 62-64°C, density 0.95 g/cm³)

- **Investment casting (lost-wax method)**: Critical application for metallurgy — see [`metals.copper-bronze`](../metals/copper-bronze.md). Wax pattern → ceramic shell mold → melt out wax → pour molten metal. Enables complex shapes impossible with sand casting.
- **Writing tablets**: Wooden frame filled with beeswax, inscribed with stylus, smoothed by gentle heating. Reusable writing surface — standard in antiquity before cheap paper. See [`knowledge.writing`](../knowledge/writing.md).
- **Candles**: Beeswax burns cleaner than tallow (no smoke, no rancid odor, brighter flame). Burn rate: 8-10 hours per 100 g. Dip wick (cotton or linen) repeatedly in melted wax to build up diameter.
- **Waterproofing**: Rub beeswax into leather, canvas, or paper. Heat gently to penetrate fibers. Water-repellent finish for boots, bags, tents.
- **Belt dressing**: Apply to leather belts in machinery to maintain grip and flexibility — see [`chemistry.lubricants`](../chemistry/lubricants.md).
- **Wood polish**: Beeswax dissolved in turpentine (1:3 ratio by weight) produces furniture polish. Penetrates wood grain, fills pores, provides protective coating.
- **Dental temporary fillings**: Wax plug seals cavities temporarily — softens at body temperature, molds to cavity shape.
- **Sealant**: Wax seals on jars, letters, containers. Takes impressions well when warm.

## Propolis

Resin mixture collected by bees from tree buds (especially poplar and birch), mixed with beeswax and enzymes. Bees use it to seal gaps and sterilize the hive interior. Antimicrobial (flavonoids, caffeic acid phenethyl ester). Human applications: wound treatment (antibacterial/antifungal), wood varnish (hard, water-resistant finish), tincture (propolis dissolved in alcohol for medicinal use — see [`health.pharmacology`](../health/pharmacology.md)). Yield: 50-100 g per hive per year.

## Royal Jelly (Advanced)

Secreted by nurse bees, fed exclusively to queen larvae. Nutritional supplement — contains proteins, fatty acids, B vitamins. Harvest requires specialized queen-rearing techniques. Limited yield and high labor.

## Venom (Advanced)

Bee venom (apitoxin) contains melittin (50% of dry weight), phospholipase A₂, and hyaluronidase. Used in immunotherapy for bee sting allergy (desensitization protocols). Collection requires electric stimulus frames. Yield: ~0.1 μg venom per bee per stimulation.

## Pollination Services

A single honey bee colony forages within a 1-2 km radius from the hive (up to 5 km in scarce conditions). Each forager makes 10-15 trips per day, visiting 50-100 flowers per trip. A strong colony deploys 15,000-30,000 foragers per day during peak season. Pollination increases yields in [`crop rotation`](../agriculture/crop-rotation.md) systems and is essential for [`seed saving`](../agriculture/seed-saving.md) in many species.

**Crops benefitting from bee pollination**:
- **Fruit trees**: Apple, pear, cherry, plum — 80-90% yield increase with adequate pollination. Many varieties are self-incompatible and require cross-pollination between cultivars.
- **Berries**: Strawberry, raspberry, blueberry, blackberry — improved fruit size and symmetry.
- **Cucurbits**: Squash, melon, cucumber, pumpkin — dependent on bee pollination (many have separate male and female flowers).
- **Legumes**: Clover, alfalfa — seed production for livestock forage depends on bee pollination.
- **Oilseed crops**: Canola/rapeseed, sunflower — yield increase 20-40%.

**Hive placement for pollination**:
Position hives 50-100 m from crop boundary. Shelter from prevailing wind. Morning sun exposure warms the colony early, initiating foraging sooner. Provide a clean water source within 50 m (bees need water for hive temperature regulation and brood food dilution). Standard pollination contract: 2-4 hives per hectare of orchard; 1-2 per hectare for field crops.

**Scale estimate**: At 2-4 hives per hectare of orchard, pollination services increase fruit yield by 80-90%. Revenue from pollination contracts often exceeds honey revenue for commercial beekeepers (typical pollination fee: $50-150 USD per hive per season for almond pollination in California).

## Bootstrap Sequence

1. **Log hive / tree colony** (Year 0-2): Harvest honey from wild colonies in hollow trees. Observe bee behavior. Learn to use smoke. Minimal investment — follow bees to their nests, cut out comb. Establish first managed log hives near settlements. Risk: destructive harvest weakens or kills colonies.
2. **Skep beekeeping** (Year 2-5): Construct woven skeps. Bait and capture swarms. Learn seasonal management by observation. Harvest by cutting comb — accept colony losses. Begin rendering beeswax. Multiply colonies by collecting multiple swarms each spring.
3. **Top-bar hive** (Year 5-8): Construct top-bar hives from sawn lumber. Transfer colonies from skeps. Non-destructive inspection enables disease monitoring and swarm prevention. Honey yields double. Begin systematic pollination of adjacent crops. Select calm colonies for breeding.
4. **Frame hive with extraction** (Year 8-10+): Precision woodworking enables Langstroth-style frame hives. Centrifugal extractor preserves comb. Multiple supers per colony. Maximum yield. Queen rearing and colony multiplication under control. Pollination contracts for agriculture. Full product range (honey, wax, propolis) at commercial scale.


## Bee Stings

Each sting delivers 50-140 μg venom (apitoxin). Pain response is immediate (melittin damages cell membranes). Remove stinger promptly by scraping sideways with a fingernail or hive tool (pinching injects more venom from the attached venom sac, which continues pulsing for 2-3 minutes after detachment). Lethal dose: approximately 10 stings per kg body weight (500-700 stings for a 70 kg adult). Most healthy adults tolerate 10-20 stings without systemic effects beyond local swelling lasting 24-48 hours. Beekeepers develop tolerance over repeated exposure — higher baseline IgG blocks allergic response.

## Anaphylaxis

1-3% of the population is hypersensitive to bee venom (IgE-mediated). Symptoms: generalized hives, throat swelling, difficulty breathing, rapid pulse, drop in blood pressure. Onset within 5-30 minutes. Fatal if untreated (airway obstruction or cardiovascular collapse). Treatment: epinephrine auto-injector (0.3 mg IM for adults, 0.15 mg for children) injected into outer thigh. Second dose after 5-15 minutes if symptoms persist. Emergency medical attention required even after epinephrine — biphasic reactions recur 4-12 hours later in 20% of cases. Anyone keeping bees should identify hypersensitive individuals in the household and keep an emergency plan.

## Smoke Inhalation

Smoker fuel produces carbon monoxide and particulate matter. Use in well-ventilated outdoor areas only. Avoid inhaling smoke directly — position yourself upwind. Never use synthetic materials as smoker fuel (plastics, rubber) — toxic fumes harm bees and beekeeper. Symptoms of CO exposure: headache, dizziness, nausea at 100-200 ppm; loss of consciousness at 600-700 ppm.

## Falls from Height

Log hives positioned 1-3 m above ground in trees require ladder access. Inspect ladder condition before each use. Maintain three points of contact when climbing. Work with a partner when accessing elevated hives. A fall from 2 m onto hard ground causes fractures in 10-15% of cases.

## Protective Equipment

- **Veil**: Mesh hood preventing stings to face and neck — the most critical protection. Bees target the face and breath (CO₂ attracts them). Round veil (Elizabethan style) or folding veil attached to a hat.
- **Gloves**: Leather or canvas, reaching to elbow. Cuff should overlap with sleeve. Experienced beekeepers often work without gloves for dexterity, accepting occasional stings.
- **Smoker**: Essential tool. Maintained lit throughout hive work. Reduces stings by 80-90%. Fuel: dried wood chips, burlap, pine needles, dried herb stalks. Never use synthetic materials (toxic fumes harm bees).
- **Hive tool**: Flat steel bar (20-25 cm) for prying apart frames glued together with propolis. Never force frames — pry gently at multiple points. Also used for scraping propolis and wax buildup.
- **Suit or coveralls**: Light-colored (bees are less defensive around light colors — dark colors resemble predators like bears), smooth fabric (bees snag on wool and become agitated). Tuck pants into boots or secure with straps to prevent bees crawling up legs.

## Apiary Safety Practices

- Work hives during midday (10:00-15:00) when most foragers are away from the hive.
- Move slowly and deliberately — rapid movements trigger defensive response.
- Never block the flight path (stand to the side or behind the hive, not in front of the entrance).
- Avoid strong scents (perfume, sweat, bananas — isoamyl acetate in bananas mimics alarm pheromone).
- Wash bee suit after each use — residual alarm pheromone on fabric triggers defensive behavior on next visit.
- Keep hives at least 3 m apart to prevent drift (bees returning to wrong hive, spreading disease).
- Inspect hives every 7-10 days during active season for signs of disease (foulbrood, nosema, chalkbrood) or queen failure.
- Maintain apiary hygiene: sterilize tools between hives using lye solution or flame, replace old comb every 3-5 years to reduce pathogen buildup.
