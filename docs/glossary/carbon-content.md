# Carbon Content

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: metals, silicon

| | Wrought Iron | Steel | Cast Iron |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Carbon** | 0.02-0.08% | 0.05-1.0% | 2.5-4.0% |
| **Carbon form** | Slag inclusions (fibrous) | Dissolved in ferrite + cementite (Fe₃C) | Graphite flakes in iron matrix |

## Context in the Tech Tree

Carbon content is the single most important variable in ferrous metallurgy, determining whether iron is wrought (malleable, forgeable), steel (strong, heat-treatable), or cast iron (brittle, castable). In [Iron & Steel](../metals/iron-steel.md), carbon content controls the properties of every ferrous product. In [Crystal Growth](../silicon/crystal-growth.md), carbon contamination in silicon must be minimized. Understanding carbon content is essential for controlling the bloomery-to-blast-furnace-to-steelmaking progression.

## Technical Details

The iron-carbon system defines three fundamentally different materials separated by carbon content alone. Wrought iron (0.02-0.08% C) is nearly pure iron with slag inclusions — soft, ductile, excellent for forging and forge welding, but unable to hold an edge or be hardened. Steel (0.05-1.0% C) combines strength and ductility, responding to heat treatment (quenching hardens, tempering adjusts the hardness-toughness balance). Cast iron (2.5-4.0% C) has carbon present as graphite flakes or cementite — extremely hard and brittle, but low melting point (1150-1250°C) makes it easy to cast into complex shapes.

The practical consequences are dramatic. Wrought iron melts at ~1538°C — essentially unachievable in early furnaces. Cast iron melts at ~1150-1250°C, within reach of a well-designed blast furnace with forced draft. This is why blast furnaces naturally produce cast iron: the high carbon content lowers the melting point enough for the metal to become fully liquid.

Controlling carbon content during steelmaking is the core technical challenge. The Bessemer converter and basic oxygen furnace reduce carbon by blowing oxygen through molten iron (C + O → CO gas). The open-hearth furnace uses slower, more controllable oxidation. Precise carbon control — hitting a target of ±0.01% — determines whether the final steel meets specification for its intended application.
