# Steam Distillation

> **Type**: process | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, health, plants

A distillation technique where live steam is passed through plant material or a mixture, causing volatile compounds to co-distill with water at a temperature below their normal boiling points. Essential for extracting heat-sensitive aromatic compounds.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Steam distillation appears in [Distillation](../chemistry/distillation.md) as a specialized technique, in [Pharmacology](../health/pharmacology.md) for extracting medicinal compounds, and in [Medicinal Plants](../plants/medicinal-plants.md) for producing essential oils. It is the primary method for obtaining aromatic compounds from plant material without thermal degradation.

## Technical Details

Live steam is injected into the still pot containing plant material. Water and the organic compound form a heterogeneous azeotrope, allowing co-distillation at a temperature below either pure component's boiling point. Essential oils (boiling points 200-300°C) distill at 95-100°C with steam, preserving thermally fragile fragrance and medicinal compounds.

Equipment: steam boiler, still pot (with a grate or basket to hold plant material above the water line), condenser, and Florentine flask separator. Steam consumption: 2-5 kg steam per kg of plant material. The condensate separates into two layers in the Florentine flask — essential oil (lighter, floats) and hydrosol (aqueous layer). Multiple distillations of the hydrosol increase yield.

Yields are typically low: 0.5-3% by weight for most herbs. Lavender yields ~1% essential oil, peppermint ~1.5%, clove buds ~15%. The process works because steam carries volatile molecules that would decompose if heated to their pure boiling points under atmospheric pressure.

For medicinal extraction, steam distillation produces concentrated essential oils used topically, in inhalation preparations, or as starting materials for further chemical processing.
