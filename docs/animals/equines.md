### Overview

Equines — horses (*Equus caballus*) and donkeys (*Equus africanus asinus*) — are the primary large draft and transport animals for a bootstrapping civilization. Horses provide the greatest pulling power per animal, while donkeys offer superior endurance, disease resistance, and ability to thrive on poor forage. Together they cover the full range of traction, packing, and riding needs from farm work to long-distance transport. Both species were domesticated by 3500-4000 BCE and have been central to every major agricultural and transport system since.

### Breeds by Use

#### Draft Horses

Heavy breeds bred for pulling power. Mature weight 600-900 kg. Calm temperament suited to repetitive field work.

- **Percheron**: 700-900 kg. Originated in France. Grey or black coat. Exceptional pulling power combined with enough speed for road transport. Single Percheron pulls 800-1200 kg on level ground. Adaptable to varying climates. Most versatile heavy draft breed.
- **Clydesdale**: 700-900 kg. Originated in Scotland. Bay coat with white markings on face and legs. Feathered lower legs require regular cleaning to prevent skin infections ("scratches" or pastern dermatitis). Slightly less pulling power than Percheron but more showy and tractable. Excellent for logging and heavy hauling.
- **Shire**: 800-1100 kg. Largest draft breed. Black, bay, or grey. Maximum pulling power — a single Shire can move 2000+ kg on wheels. Requires more feed than lighter breeds. Best suited to heavy plowing and freight hauling on prepared roads.
- **Suffolk Punch**: 700-900 kg. Chestnut coat (always). Compact, deep-bodied. Excellent for plowing — bred specifically for farm work. Less common but highly efficient on the small farm because it requires less feed per unit of work than larger breeds.

#### Riding and Light Draft Horses

- **Arabian**: 380-450 kg. Refined build, distinctive dished face profile. Exceptional endurance — can sustain 8-12 km/h trot for 50+ km. Intelligent and responsive. Foundation breed for many modern light horse types. Hardiness from desert origin: tolerates heat and sparse forage. Useful as a light riding horse and for herding other livestock.
- **Quarter Horse type** (general-purpose light horse): 450-550 kg. Compact, muscular. Short-distance speed and agility. Good cattle-working horse. Versatile for riding, light draft, and farm utility work.

#### Pack Donkeys

- **Standard donkey**: 180-250 kg. Grey-brown coat with characteristic dorsal stripe and shoulder cross ("cross of Jerusalem"). Carries 50-80 kg packs. Sure-footed on rough terrain. Essential for mountain transport where wheeled vehicles cannot go. Feeds on scrub and poor-quality forage that would not sustain horses.
- **Mammoth Jackstock**: 400-550 kg. Largest donkey breed, developed for producing draft mules when crossed with horse mares. Also used directly as a draft and pack animal — carries 100-150 kg. Rare but extremely valuable. A single Mammoth Jackstock jack (male) can service 10-15 mare breedings per year for mule production.

#### Mules (Horse × Donkey Hybrid)

- Produced by breeding a male donkey (jack) to a female horse (mare). 350-550 kg depending on parent breeds. Sterile — cannot reproduce. Combine horse size and strength with donkey intelligence, hardiness, and disease resistance. Preferred by experienced teamsters for difficult terrain and heavy work. More sure-footed than horses, less prone to panic. Working lifespan 20-30 years. A mule is worth more than either parent in working value.

### Harness Fitting

Proper harness fitting prevents injury, maximizes pulling efficiency, and extends working life.

**Collar types**:
- **Full (neck) collar**: Horse-shoe shaped padded collar that fits around the neck, resting on the shoulders. Distributes draft force across the broadest muscle mass. Used for heavy pulling (plowing, logging, freight). Must be fitted to individual horse — a poorly fitted collar causes shoulder sores that can become infected and sideline the animal for weeks. Measure neck at widest point and match to collar size. Collar should allow two fingers' width between collar and throat.
- **Breast collar (breastplate)**: Strap across the chest, connecting to traces on either side. Simpler and cheaper than full collar. Distributes force across the breastbone rather than shoulders. Adequate for light work and vehicles (carts, wagons on level ground). Not suitable for heavy pulling — concentrates force on a smaller area, causing fatigue and potential injury with sustained heavy loads.

**Key harness components**:
- **Hames**: Curved metal or wooden arms that fit inside the collar, providing attachment points for traces and other harness parts.
- **Traces**: Leather or rope straps connecting hames to the load (via a singletree or whippletree). Must be equal length to distribute load evenly.
- **Hip strap (breeching)**: Strap around the hindquarters. Provides braking — when the animal slows or stops, the breeching bears against the rump, allowing the animal to hold back a loaded wagon on downhill slopes. Essential for any wheeled vehicle work. Without breeching, the vehicle runs into the animal on downhills.
- **Belly band (girth)**: Holds harness in position on the body. Must be snug but not restrictive — test by sliding a flat hand underneath.
- **Lines (reins)**: Long reins running from the bit to the driver. For draft work, lines run along the animal's sides, not over the back.

**Fitting checklist**:
1. Collar sits flat on shoulders, no gaps at top or bottom
2. Traces are level and equal length
3. Breeching sits 10-15 cm below the tail head
4. Belly band allows a flat hand underneath
5. No rubbing or pinching at any point after 15 minutes of movement
6. Check for chafing after every work session — treat any redness immediately with clean water and rest

### Shoeing Basics

Equine hooves grow continuously (6-9 mm/month) and wear down with use on hard or rocky surfaces. Domestic working equines on roads, rocky ground, or in wet conditions need horseshoes to prevent excessive wear, cracking, and lameness.

**Horseshoe nails**:
- Made from mild steel or iron. Head is rectangular, shaft tapers to a point. Driven through the shoe and out through the hoof wall (the insensitive outer portion of the hoof), then clenched (bent over) on the outside to lock the shoe in place. Proper nail placement avoids the sensitive inner hoof structures (quick) — hitting the quick causes bleeding and pain.
- 6-8 nails per shoe (3-4 per side). Nail holes are punched in the shoe during forging.

**Hot vs cold shoeing**:
- **Hot shoeing**: Heat the shoe in a forge until glowing, hold it briefly against the trimmed hoof. The hot shoe burns a precise mark showing exactly where the shoe contacts the hoof — any gaps or high spots are immediately visible. Also sears the hoof horn, sealing it and reducing moisture loss. The burned mark smells distinctively of keratin (burnt hair). Requires a small forge and fuel. Preferred method for working animals.
- **Cold shoeing**: Shape the shoe cold on an anvil, fit to the hoof by eye and test-fitting. Faster and requires less equipment. Adequate for donkeys and light-use animals. Less precise fit — may require more frequent adjustments.

**Reset schedule**:
- Shoes must be reset (removed, hooves trimmed, shoes refitted or replaced) every 6-8 weeks. Hoof growth between resets changes the fit. Leaving shoes too long causes pinching, improper angles, and lameness.
- Each reset: remove old nails, trim hoof with nippers and rasp, check shoe for wear (replace if thinned more than 50%), refit and nail. A reset takes 30-45 minutes per animal with practice.
- Donkeys on soft ground may not need shoes — their hooves are harder and more wear-resistant than horses'. Assess each animal individually.

**Tools for shoeing**: hoof nippers, rasp, hoof knife, driving hammer, clincher (bends nail ends), pincers (pulls old nails), shoe pullers, apron. Forge for hot shoeing. Stock of pre-forged shoes in several sizes, or bar stock iron/steel to forge custom.

### Training Progression

Equines must be trained gradually. Rushing training causes injuries, behavioral problems, and dangerous animals.

**0-6 months (foal)**:
- Imprint handling at birth (if possible): touch all body parts, handle feet, introduce halter. Builds trust and reduces fear responses permanently.
- Halter breaking at 3-6 months. Teach leading, tying, standing quietly for handling. Use positive reinforcement (feed rewards) and pressure-release. Never drag — apply gentle pressure, release instantly when the foal yields.

**6-18 months (weanling/yearling)**:
- Continue handling: pick up all four feet daily, brush, lead over varied terrain. Desensitize to unfamiliar objects (tarps, plastic bags, loud noises). Introduce to harness components (let them sniff and wear a surcingle without load).

**18-24 months (longeing)**:
- Teach longeing: working in circles on a long line (10-12 m) at walk, trot, and canter. Builds fitness, teaches voice commands ("walk", "trot", "whoa"), and establishes respect for the handler's space. 15-20 minutes per session. Introduce a light saddle or harness (without load).

**24-30 months (saddle/bitting)**:
- Introduce bit and bridle. Start with a mild snaffle bit (jointed mouthpiece). Allow the animal to mouth and accept the bit before attaching lines.
- Under saddle (riding horses): introduce rider weight gradually. First mounting in a confined area (round pen or small paddock). Short sessions (10-15 minutes) at walk.
- In harness (draft): introduce pulling — start with a light drag (tire or log, 10-20 kg). Progress to empty cart. Keep initial sessions short and positive.

**30-36 months (light work)**:
- Begin actual work at light loads. Draft horses: pull 100-200 kg for 1-2 hours. Riding horses: carry rider for 30-60 minutes at walk and trot. Increase load and duration gradually over 6 months.

**4-5 years (full work capacity)**:
- Skeletal maturity is complete. Full work capacity reached. Draft horses: 500-800 kg pulls for 4-6 hours. Riding horses: sustained work at all gaits.

**Donkey training notes**: Donkeys are more cautious than horses — their freeze-and-assess response ("stubbornness") is a survival trait. Force-based training backfires. Use patience, clear cues, and food rewards. Donkeys trained with respect are exceptionally reliable workers.

### Feed Requirements by Workload

**Horses (500 kg example)**:
| Work Level | Dry Matter/day | Hay/Pasture | Grain Supplement | Notes |
|------------|---------------|-------------|-----------------|-------|
| Maintenance (idle) | 8-10 kg | 8-10 kg grass hay | None | 2% body weight |
| Light work (1-2 hr/day) | 10-12 kg | 8-10 kg | 1-2 kg oats | 2-2.5% body weight |
| Moderate work (2-4 hr/day) | 12-14 kg | 8-10 kg | 2-4 kg oats or barley | 2.5% body weight |
| Heavy work (4-6 hr/day) | 14-16 kg | 8-10 kg | 4-5 kg oats or barley | 2.5-3% body weight |

- Always provide hay or pasture as the base — grain is a supplement, not a replacement. Equine digestive systems are designed for continuous forage consumption.
- Working horses need grain supplement of 2-5 kg/day depending on intensity. Oats are the traditional grain (lowest starch, safest). Barley is higher energy but must be rolled or crushed. Corn (maize) is highest energy but riskiest — too much causes laminitis and colic.
- Water: 30-50 L/day at rest, up to 70 L with heavy work in heat. Must be clean and constantly available. Equines will reduce water intake if water is dirty or unfamiliar, leading to impaction colic.
- Salt: provide free-choice salt (loose or block). Working horses in heat may need 50-100 g salt/day supplemented in feed.
- Pasture requirement: 1.0-1.5 hectares per horse on productive grassland.

**Donkeys (200 kg example)**:
| Work Level | Dry Matter/day | Forage | Grain Supplement |
|------------|---------------|--------|-----------------|
| Maintenance | 4-5 kg | 4-5 kg poor-quality hay | None |
| Light work | 5-6 kg | 5-6 kg | 0-0.5 kg |
| Heavy work | 6-8 kg | 5-6 kg | 1-2 kg |

- Donkeys are highly efficient digestors of poor-quality forage. They thrive on scrub, thistles, and dry grasses that would not sustain horses or cattle. Grain supplement rarely needed except under heavy work.
- Water: 15-25 L/day. Donkeys tolerate dehydration better than horses and can go 24-48 hours without water in moderate conditions. In desert conditions, donkeys can lose up to 30% body weight from dehydration and recover when water becomes available.

### Dental Care

Equine teeth erupt continuously throughout life to compensate for grinding wear. Uneven wear creates sharp points and hooks that cause pain and reduce feed efficiency.

**Floating (rasping)**:
- File down sharp enamel points on the outer edges of upper cheek teeth and inner edges of lower cheek teeth. These points develop because the upper jaw is wider than the lower jaw, so the teeth do not wear evenly.
- Frequency: every 6-12 months for domestic equines. Working horses on grain may need more frequent care.
- Tools: equine dental float (long-handled rasp with carbide blade or file surface). A speculum (gag) holds the mouth open for access.
- Signs of dental problems: dropping feed (quidding), undigested grain in manure, head-tossing while eating, weight loss despite adequate feed, foul breath.

**Wolf teeth removal**:
- Wolf teeth are small, vestigial premolars that appear in front of the upper cheek teeth in 50-70% of horses (rare in donkeys). They serve no purpose and interfere with the bit, causing pain when reins apply pressure.
- Remove wolf teeth before bitting (around 18-24 months). Extraction with wolf tooth elevators and forceps — a brief procedure but requires restraint and skill. If left in place, they cause head-tossing and resistance to the bit.

### Colic Prevention

Colic (abdominal pain, usually from digestive disruption) is the leading cause of death in domestic horses. Prevention is far more effective than treatment.

**Risk factors and prevention**:
- **Feed changes**: Never change feed abruptly. Transition over 7-10 days by gradually increasing the new feed while decreasing the old. Sudden changes disrupt gut microflora, causing gas, impaction, or fermentation colic.
- **Water access**: Constant clean water. Dehydration causes impaction colic (dry feed mass blocks the intestine). In cold weather, offer warm water (10-15°C) — horses drink 40% more warm water than ice-cold water in winter.
- **Regular exercise**: Horses evolved to graze while walking 15-30 km/day. Stall confinement reduces gut motility. Provide daily turnout or exercise. Even 30 minutes of walking helps.
- **Sand ingestion**: Feed on mats or in buckets, not directly on sandy ground. Sand accumulates in the gut and causes irritation and impaction.
- **Deworming**: Internal parasites (strongyles, ascarids) damage intestinal blood supply and cause colic. Regular deworming per veterinary schedule (every 8-12 weeks in high-exposure environments).
- **Dental maintenance**: Poorly chewed feed passes undigested, increasing impaction risk.

**Signs of colic**: pawing, looking at flanks, rolling, sweating, elevated heart rate (>50 bpm), reduced or absent gut sounds. Immediate veterinary attention required. Walking the horse gently may help in mild cases — never let a colicking horse roll violently (can twist intestine, which is fatal without surgery).

### Breeding Management

**Stallion behavior management**:
- Stallions (intact males) are aggressive and territorial, especially during breeding season (spring). House separately from mares. Double-fencing between stallion paddock and mare paddock (1.5 m gap between fences).
- Handling: experienced handlers only. Use a chain over the nose or through the bit for additional control during breeding. Never turn a stallion loose with mares unattended unless in a controlled breeding pasture.
- For most operations, keep one stallion per 15-20 mares. Hand-breeding (leading the stallion to a haltered mare under controlled conditions) is safer and more predictable than pasture breeding.
- Gelding (castration) eliminates stallion behavior. Performed at 6-18 months if the animal will not be used for breeding. Geldings are safer, easier to manage, and can be pastured with mares without risk of unplanned breeding.

**Foaling**:
- Gestation: 335 days average (range 320-350 for horses, 340-390 for donkeys). Single foal per pregnancy; twins are rare and typically only one survives.
- Foaling at 11-12 months gestation (340-365 days from conception). Signs of approaching foaling: udder enlargement (2-4 weeks prior), waxing of teats (1-4 days prior), relaxed tail head and vulva (days prior), restlessness.
- Foaling usually occurs at night. Normal delivery takes 20-30 minutes from water breaking to foal delivery. If no progress after 30 minutes of active labor — call for veterinary assistance (dystocia is a life-threatening emergency).
- Newborn foal should stand within 1-2 hours and nurse within 2-3 hours. Colostrum (first milk) provides essential antibodies — foals are born without immune protection. If the foal does not nurse within 4 hours, milk the mare and bottle-feed colostrum to the foal.
- Wean at 4-6 months. Gradual weaning (reduced contact over 1-2 weeks) is less stressful than abrupt separation.

**Donkey-specific breeding notes**:
- Donkey gestation is longer than horses (365 days average). Foals are smaller and hardier.
- Jenny (female donkey) fertility is slightly lower than mare fertility. Foaling complications are rare — donkeys are notably easy birthers.
- Cross-breeding to produce mules: jack donkey × mare horse. Mule foals are larger than donkey foals and require the same care as horse foals.

### Housing and Fencing

**Stabling**:
- 15-25 m² per horse in stalls (3 × 4 m minimum). Ceiling height 3 m minimum. Adequate ventilation is critical — ammonia from urine damages equine lungs. Provide drainage in stall floor.
- Donkeys: 10-15 m² per adult. Hardier than horses, tolerate cold well with adequate forage, but need shelter from prolonged rain and wind.

**Fencing**:
- Post-and-rail at 1.2-1.5 m height for horses and donkeys. Wooden or metal posts at 2-3 m intervals, with 3-4 horizontal rails or boards. Wire fencing (woven wire, not barbed wire) is acceptable but must be visible to prevent running through.
- Never use barbed wire with equines — legs catch in barbed wire, causing severe lacerations that are difficult to heal.

**Daily turnout**:
- Horses require daily exercise; prolonged stall confinement causes behavioral problems (cribbing, weaving, stall-walking) and hoof deterioration. Minimum 2-4 hours turnout per day in a paddock or pasture.

### Products and Applications

- **Draft power**: Single horse pulls 500-800 kg on level ground at 4-5 km/h for 4-6 hours. Two-horse team doubles capacity. See [draft-power](./draft-power.md) for full hitch configurations and implements.
- **Transportation**: Riding speed 8-12 km/h trot, 30-40 km/h canter (short distances). Donkey pack transport: 50-100 kg (standard), 100-150 kg (Mammoth Jackstock) over rough terrain.
- **Manure**: 20-30 kg/day per horse, 10-20 kg/day per donkey. Excellent fertilizer — composted equine manure provides nitrogen, phosphorus, and potassium to crops. Hot-composting (55-65°C for 3+ days) kills parasite eggs and weed seeds.
- **Mare milk**: 1.5-2.5 L/day. Used in Central Asian steppe traditions for fermented drinks (kumis). Lower fat than cow milk.
- **Meat and hides**: Secondary products in most cultures, but equine leather is extremely durable (used for sole leather, belts, harnesses).

### Manure Management

Equine manure is a valuable resource. Each working horse produces 20-30 kg/day of manure (7-11 tonnes/year), containing approximately 0.5% nitrogen, 0.3% phosphorus, and 0.6% potassium by fresh weight.

**Composting**: Mix manure with bedding (straw, wood shavings) at a ratio of roughly 2:1 manure to bedding by volume. Turn pile every 3-5 days to maintain aerobic conditions. Internal temperature should reach 55-65°C to kill parasite eggs and weed seeds. Finished compost in 6-8 weeks in warm weather, 3-4 months in cold. Apply 5-10 tonnes per hectare to cropland.

### Safety

**Handling safety**:
- Always alert an equine to your presence before approaching — speak calmly, approach from the side (not directly from behind). Equines have a blind spot directly behind them; startling them triggers a kick reflex.
- Never stand directly behind any equine. When passing behind, either keep a hand on the animal's hindquarters (so it knows where you are) or walk well out of kicking range (3+ meters).
- Tying: always tie to a solid post at wither height with a quick-release knot. Never tie to a fence rail the animal could pull loose (panicked animals injure themselves pulling against broken fencing).
- Leading: lead from the left side (near side), walk beside the animal's head or shoulder, never ahead of it.

**Kick zones**:
- Direct rear kick: can reach 2-3 m behind the animal. Force sufficient to shatter bones or kill. Mules kick with exceptional accuracy.
- Cow kick (forward/sideways from front legs): less powerful but can catch handlers working near the front legs.
- Kick force: 1000+ kg impact force from a 500 kg horse — lethal to humans.

**Falls and riding injuries**:
- Always wear a helmet when riding. Concussions from falls are the most common serious riding injury.
- Donkeys are less likely to bolt or panic than horses. Mules are intermediate — less likely to panic than horses but more deliberate about what they will and will not do.
