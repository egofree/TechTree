Lime Production

Node ID: ceramics.lime Domain: Ceramics & Refractories Timeline: Years 5-15 Outputs: lime, quicklime, mortar

Lime production:

  • Process: Heat limestone (CaCO₃) in kiln to 900-1000°C for 4-8 hours. CaCO₃ → CaO (quicklime) + CO₂. Kiln construction similar to pottery kiln but larger (1-2 m tall).
  • Slaking: Add water to quicklime — vigorous exothermic reaction (CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂, temperature reaches ~150°C). Produces slaked lime (hydrated lime), a soft white powder. Safety: slaking generates intense heat and steam — add quicklime to water slowly, never the reverse. Wear eye protection and leather gloves. Perform outdoors or in well-ventilated area.
  • Uses: Mortar (lime + sand + water, 1:3 ratio), whitewash (lime + water), plaster, soil amendment (reduces acidity), hide processing (dehairing), sewage treatment, sugar refining, paper bleaching.
  • Storage: Keep quicklime in dry, airtight containers. Quicklime absorbs moisture and CO₂ from air, gradually reverting to calcium carbonate (air-slaking). Once slaked, store lime putty (slaked lime + excess water) in covered pit — improves with age (months to years) as particle size decreases → more plastic and workable mortar.

Potash/Lye from wood ashes:

  • See glass production potash section. Concentrated lye (KOH) produced by running water through hardwood ash bed, then boiling to desired concentration. Test strength: float an egg — if it bobs with dime-sized area exposed, concentration is ~10% (correct for soap making).

Lime kiln design (shaft kiln):

  • Construction: Vertical shaft 3-6 m tall, 1-2 m diameter at the firing zone. Built from firebrick or stone. Alternate layers of crushed limestone (10-20 cm pieces) and fuel (wood or charcoal). Load from top, extract lime from bottom. Continuous operation — add new layers at top while drawing finished lime below.
  • Efficiency: 2-3 tonnes fuel per tonne of quicklime produced. Draw lime every 6-8 hours from the discharge arch at base. Test lime quality: quicklime should slake vigorously within 5 minutes. Underburned (contains unconverted CaCO₃ — slakes slowly, leaves gritty residue). Overburned (sintered, hard — slakes very slowly, produces coarse particles).
  • Lime cycle: CaCO₃ (limestone) → [heat 900°C] → CaO (quicklime) + CO₂ → [add water] → Ca(OH)₂ (slaked lime) → [exposure to air, CO₂] → CaCO₃ (re-hardened limestone). This cycle is the basis of all lime-based construction materials.

Hydraulic lime:

  • Limestone containing 5-20% clay (aluminosilicate) impurities produces hydraulic lime when fired at 1000-1100°C. The clay reacts with lime to form calcium silicates and aluminates, which set by hydraulic reaction (with water) rather than by CO₂ absorption. Sets underwater. Essential for hydraulic structures (canals, harbor walls, foundations in wet ground). Feebly hydraulic (5-10% clay) sets in 2-3 weeks; eminently hydraulic (20-25% clay) sets in 2-4 hours.
  • Natural cement: Overfired hydraulic lime (1100-1200°C) produces a quick-setting cement. Grind the resulting clinker to fine powder. Mix with water — sets in 15-30 minutes. Stronger than hydraulic lime. Roman cement (natural cement from septaria nodules) was the standard water-resistant binder before Portland cement.

Lime mortar and plaster:

  • Mortar mixing: Slaked lime putty + sand + water. Ratio 1:2 to 1:3 (lime:sand by volume). Mix on board or in mortar tub. Beat vigorously with hoe — "fat" mortar should hold shape when cut with trowel. Let stand 30 minutes before use (water fully penetrates lime particles). Initial set: carbonation begins at surface (Ca(OH)₂ + CO₂ → CaCO₃ + H₂O). Full hardening: months to years as CO₂ slowly penetrates to core. Keep mortar damp for 7+ days after application — prevents cracking from too-rapid drying.
  • Limewash (whitewash): Dilute slaked lime with water to paint consistency. Apply in 3-5 thin coats, brushing each into the previous while damp. Disinfecting (alkaline pH kills bacteria and mold). Bright white, reflective. Used on interior walls, tree trunks (insect barrier), and exterior masonry. Add pigment (iron oxide for red, charcoal for gray) for colored wash.

Early soap making:

  • Cold process: Heat animal fat (tallow or lard) to 40-50°C. Slowly add lye solution (KOH or NaOH) with stirring. Ratio: ~1 kg fat to 130-150 g KOH (or 100-120 g NaOH). Stir to trace (mixture thickens, drips leave visible marks). Pour into mold, cure 4-6 weeks. Result: soft soap (KOH) or hard soap (NaOH).
  • Hot process and graining out: Heat fat to 38°C (saponification temperature). Add lye gradually with constant stirring. Continue heating for 2-3 hours until mixture thickens. Add salt (NaCl) to "grain out" — salt causes soap to separate and float to top as curds (graining). Remove soap curds, press to remove excess liquid, mold and dry. Glycerol remains in the lye solution (recoverable by distillation). Salt addition: ~100 g per kg of fat. Produces harder, longer-lasting soap than cold process.
  • Lye safety: NaOH and concentrated KOH cause severe chemical burns. Wear eye protection and gloves when handling. If skin contact occurs, flush with copious water for 15+ minutes. Never add water to solid lye (vigorous spattering) — add lye to water slowly.
