# Coke Production

> **Node ID**: energy.fuels.coke
> **Domain**: Energy
> **Timeline**: Years 15-25
> **Outputs**: coke, coal_gas, coal_tar, ammonia_liquor, benzol

## Overview

Coke is to coal what charcoal is to wood — the refined, high-energy, low-impurity fuel that enables high-temperature industrial processes. Raw coal contains 10-30% volatile matter (tars, gases, moisture) that interferes with metallurgical processes: tars contaminate metal, volatile gases cause uneven combustion, and sulfur transfers from fuel to iron, degrading its quality. Coke — nearly pure carbon, hard, porous, and consistent — eliminates these problems.

Coke is the only solid fuel capable of sustaining the 1800-2100°C temperatures required for blast furnace ironmaking and silicon reduction. Without coke, iron production remains limited to small-scale bloomery operations. With coke, blast furnaces produce iron by the tonne, enabling the full industrial bootstrap.

## Coal Selection for Coking

Not all coal makes good coke. Only certain bituminous coals ("coking coals" or "metallurgical coals") have the right properties:

- **Caking property**: The coal must soften, swell, and re-solidify into a coherent porous mass when heated. Coals that do not cake (anthracite, some sub-bituminous coals) produce powder, not coke. Free swelling index (FSI) of 4-9 is typical for coking coals.
- **Volatile matter**: 20-30% is ideal. Too low (<15%) — the coal doesn't soften enough to fuse. Too high (>35%) — excessive shrinkage and weak coke.
- **Ash content**: <10% preferred. Ash reduces coke quality and introduces impurities into blast furnace iron. Low-ash coals are more valuable.
- **Sulfur content**: <1% preferred. Sulfur in coke transfers to iron as FeS, causing hot shortness (brittle when hot). Desulfurization of iron is possible but adds cost and complexity.
- **Phosphorus**: <0.05%. Phosphorus cannot be removed from steel (it causes cold shortness — brittle when cold). Low-phosphorus coking coal is a strategic resource.
- **Test method**: Heat a sample of crushed coal in a covered crucible at 900°C for 10 minutes. The resulting button should be swollen, coherent, and hard when cool — not powdery or crumbly.

## Beehive Oven Method

The simplest and earliest coke production method. Used from the 1700s through the early 1900s.

- **Construction**: Dome-shaped brick or stone chamber, ~3-4 m diameter, 2 m tall at the crown. Opening at top (trunnel head) for charging coal and ignition, side opening (door) for air control and coke removal. Floor slopes slightly toward the door for drainage.
- **Process**:
  1. **Charge**: 3-6 tonnes of crushed coal (5-15 cm pieces) through top opening. Level the charge.
  2. **Ignite**: Light coal at top surface using kindling. Or place hot coke from a previous charge on top of the new coal.
  3. **Carbonization**: Fire burns downward through the coal bed. Volatile gases (coal tar, ammonia, coal gas) burn at the top, providing heat for carbonization below. The burning volatiles are the primary heat source — very little of the fixed carbon is consumed.
  4. **Air control**: Cover top opening with loose bricks or an iron lid — restrict air to maintain carbonization temperature (~900-1100°C) without full combustion. Adjust air through the side door.
  5. **Duration**: 48-72 hours for full carbonization. The charge shrinks ~15-20% as volatiles are driven off.
  6. **Quench**: Once carbonization is complete (no more visible flame from volatiles, red glow throughout), spray with water carefully — massive steam eruption. Or seal oven completely and let cool inertly over 24-48 hours (dry cooling produces better coke).
  7. **Remove**: Rake coke out through the side door with iron rakes. Load into barrows or carts.
- **Yield**: ~60-70% by weight from good coking coal.
- **Limitation**: Volatile byproducts are burned rather than captured — wasteful of valuable chemical feedstocks but extremely simple. A battery of 50-100 beehive ovens operating in sequence provides continuous coke supply.

## By-Product Recovery Oven (Advanced)

The modern method — captures all volatile byproducts while producing higher-quality coke.

- **Design**: Horizontal silica brick chamber, 12-15 m long, 0.4-0.5 m wide, 4-6 m tall. Heated externally by burning collected coal gas in flues built into the chamber walls. Coal charge ~15-20 tonnes per oven. Carbonization time ~16-20 hours.
- **Operation**: Battery of 20-100 ovens. Coal is charged from the top through charging holes. As one oven completes carbonization, the coke is pushed out by a mechanical ram into a quenching car. The next charge is loaded immediately — near-continuous operation.
- **Byproduct recovery**: All volatile gases are drawn off through an offtake pipe to a chemical recovery plant. The gas is cooled, scrubbed, and fractionated to recover coal tar, benzol, ammonia, and sulfur. Cleaned gas (coke oven gas) is burned to heat the ovens — the process is self-sustaining.
- **Coke quality**: More uniform and consistent than beehive oven coke. Lower moisture, higher fixed carbon, better CSR (Coke Strength after Reaction).

## Coke Quality Specifications

| Property | Blast Furnace Grade | Foundry Grade | Test Method |
|----------|--------------------|---------------|-------------|
| CSR (Strength after reaction) | >60 | >55 | React with CO₂ at 1100°C, tumble test |
| CRI (Reactivity index) | <25 | <30 | Weight loss in CO₂ at 1100°C |
| Ash content | <10% | <12% | Burn sample, weigh residue |
| Sulfur | <0.8% | <1.0% | Furnace — sulfur transfers to iron |
| Moisture | <5% | <5% | Weigh before/after drying at 105°C |
| Fixed carbon | >85% | >80% | By difference from other measures |
| Size | 25-80 mm | 25-100 mm | Screen analysis |
| Porosity | 40-50% | 45-55% | Apparent density test |

CSR and CRI are the critical metrics for blast furnace coke — high CSR means the coke maintains structural strength even while being consumed by the CO₂ reaction, supporting the heavy burden of iron ore and limestone above. Low CRI means the coke doesn't react prematurely.

### Field Quality Tests

- **Drop test**: Drop a piece of coke from 2 m onto a steel plate. Good coke fractures into a few large pieces. Poor coke shatters into many small fragments.
- **Appearance**: Silver-gray, metallic luster, cellular (visible pores), lightweight for its size. Black, dense, non-porous = undercoked. Powdery, light, white-edged = overcoked.
- **Sound**: Good coke rings when struck with a hammer. Dull thud = weak, poorly coked.

## Quenching Methods & Safety

### Wet Quenching (Standard)

- Spray water onto incandescent coke in the quenching car. Produces massive steam plume (5-10 tonnes of steam per tonne of coke). Quenching time: 3-5 minutes.
- **Risk**: Water trapped in porous coke can cause steam explosion if coke is handled too soon. Always allow 30+ minutes cooling after quenching before handling.
- **[Never quench in confined spaces](../glossary/never-quench-in-confined-spaces.html)** — steam displaces oxygen. Workers have asphyxiated in quenching stations.
- **Water pollution**: Quench water picks up phenols, cyanides, and ammonia from the coke. Treat before discharge.

### Dry Quenching (Advanced)

- Pass inert gas (nitrogen) over hot coke to cool it in a sealed chamber. Recovers sensible heat (~1.5 MJ/kg) for steam generation via waste heat boiler. No water contact, no steam cloud.
- **Advantages**: No water pollution. Recovered heat generates electricity or process steam. Drier coke (no absorbed moisture). Better coke quality (no thermal shock from water).
- **Disadvantage**: Requires gas-tight cooling chamber, circulation fans, and waste heat boiler — more complex and expensive than wet quenching.

## Coke Oven Gas Utilization

By-product ovens yield ~300-350 m³ of gas per tonne of coal carbonized. Composition (typical):

| Component | Volume % | Note |
|-----------|----------|------|
| Hydrogen (H₂) | 50-60% | Main energy carrier, also valuable for ammonia synthesis |
| Methane (CH₄) | 20-30% | High heating value |
| Carbon monoxide (CO) | 5-8% | Toxic, combustible |
| Nitrogen (N₂) | 3-5% | Diluent |
| Carbon dioxide (CO₂) | 2-3% | Diluent |
| Hydrocarbons (C₂H₄, etc.) | 2-3% | Condensible — recover as benzol |

- **Heating value**: ~16-18 MJ/m³ (richer than producer gas at 5-6 MJ/m³, comparable to natural gas at 35-40 MJ/m³). Sufficient to heat the coke ovens themselves (self-sustaining process) and supply surplus for steelworks heating, boiler fuel, and town gas.
- **Uses**: Oven heating (primary), steel reheating furnaces, boiler fuel, town gas supply (lighting and heating). Must be cooled and scrubbed of tar, naphthalene, and H₂S before use in burners.
- **Hydrogen recovery**: The 50-60% H₂ content makes coke oven gas a potential hydrogen source for ammonia synthesis (Haber-Bosch process) — critical for fertilizer production.

## Coke Screening and Sizing

Coke from the ovens is a mixture of sizes that must be screened to meet the specifications of different consumers:

| Size Grade | Size Range | Primary Consumer | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|-------|
| Blast furnace coke (large) | 40-80 mm | Blast furnaces | Optimal: 50-75 mm. Below 25 mm causes gas flow problems. Above 80 mm causes uneven burden distribution. |
| Blast furnace coke (nut) | 20-40 mm | Blast furnace (supplement) | Blended with larger coke. Excessive fines (<15 mm) reduce furnace permeability. |
| Foundry coke | 50-100 mm | Cupola furnaces (iron casting) | Larger, denser, lower reactivity than blast furnace coke. Must maintain structure longer in the cupola. |
| Coke breeze | <15 mm | Sintering plants, boiler fuel | Too small for furnaces — used as fuel for iron ore sintering or steam generation. ~5-15% of total coke production. |

- **Screening process**: Roller screens (rotating parallel shafts with discs that pass undersize) or vibrating screens with multi-deck mesh. Screening throughput: 100-500 tonnes/hour per screen unit. Fines generation during handling: each transfer point (conveyor-to-conveyor, conveyor-to-bin) generates ~1-3% additional fines from breakage.
- **Coke strength after handling**: Coke degrades during transport from oven to furnace. Typical degradation: 5-15% size reduction from oven discharge to blast furnace charging. The tumble test (ASTM D2930: 10 kg coke tumbled in a drum at 24 RPM for 1400 revolutions) simulates this degradation — stability factor >55 and hardness factor >60 are required for blast furnace use.

## Byproduct Recovery in Detail

Coal tar (~3-5% of coal weight) is one of the most valuable chemical feedstocks in the industrial chain:

- **Distillation fractions**:
  - Light oil (<180°C) → benzene, toluene, xylene (solvents, chemical synthesis)
  - Middle oil (180-240°C) → naphthalene (mothballs, phthalic anhydride for dyes), phenol (resins, disinfectant), cresols (disinfectants, wire enamel)
  - Heavy oil (240-300°C) → creosote (wood preservative for railway sleepers, telephone poles, fence posts)
  - Anthracene oil (300-400°C) → anthracene (dye precursor)
  - Pitch residue (>400°C) → road tar, electrode binder for aluminum smelting, roofing waterproofing
- **Ammonia**: Recovered from gas stream as ammonium sulfate fertilizer (~1% of coal weight). Scrub gas with sulfuric acid → (NH₄)₂SO₄ crystallizes out.
- **Sulfur**: Recovered from gas (as H₂S) as elemental sulfur (Claus process) or sulfuric acid feedstock. Environmental necessity — H₂S is toxic and corrosive.
- **Benzol (crude benzene)**: Recovered from coke oven gas by absorption in wash oil. Contains benzene, toluene, xylene. Refined by distillation. Benzene is carcinogenic — handle with full respiratory protection.
- **Naphthalene recovery**: Crystallizes from cooled coal tar at ambient temperature. Recovered by centrifuging or pressing the tar. Purity >95% after washing. Used in phthalic anhydride production (precursor for dyes and plasticizers), mothballs, and concrete plasticizers. Yield: ~3-5 kg per tonne of coal carbonized.
- **Ammonium sulfate production**: Coke oven gas is scrubbed with dilute sulfuric acid (5-10% H₂SO₄) in a saturator vessel at 50-60°C. Ammonia reacts to form (NH₄)₂SO₄ crystals, which are centrifuged, dried, and sold as fertilizer (21% N content). Typical yield: 8-12 kg (NH₄)₂SO₄ per tonne of coal. This was historically the primary source of nitrogen fertilizer before the Haber-Bosch process.
- **Coke oven gas as fuel gas**: After tar, naphthalene, benzol, ammonia, and H₂S removal, the cleaned gas (primarily H₂ + CH₄) has a calorific value of ~16-18 MJ/m³. It fuels the coke oven heating flues (consuming ~40-45% of the gas produced), with the surplus available for steelworks reheating furnaces, boiler fuel, or town gas distribution. A typical by-product battery produces 300-350 m³ gas per tonne of coal — roughly self-sustaining for oven heating with 55-60% surplus.

## Byproduct Recovery Economics

The byproduct recovery oven transforms coking from a purely fuel-producing operation into a chemical feedstock factory. Revenue distribution for a typical by-product coke oven battery:

- **[Coke](../glossary/coke.html)** (primary product): ~70-75% of revenue. 680-750 kg coke per tonne of coal charged.
- **Coal tar**: ~5-8% of revenue. 30-50 kg per tonne of coal. Distilled into 6+ marketable fractions.
- **Benzol**: ~3-5% of revenue. 8-12 kg per tonne of coal. Source of benzene (chemical synthesis) and toluene (solvent, TNT explosive production).
- **Ammonium sulfate**: ~2-3% of revenue. 8-12 kg per tonne of coal. Nitrogen fertilizer.
- **Coke oven gas (surplus)**: ~5-10% of revenue. Surplus gas sold as fuel after oven heating needs are met.
- **Sulfur**: ~1% of revenue. Recovered as elemental sulfur or sulfuric acid.

Total byproduct credit can offset 25-35% of the operating cost of coke production. Beehive ovens (no byproduct recovery) waste this entire value stream — burning the volatile gases for heat rather than recovering them as chemicals.

## Coke in the Blast Furnace

Coke serves four critical functions in the blast furnace:

1. **Fuel**: Provides heat for the endothermic reduction reactions. Burns at the tuyere level to generate 1800-2100°C hot blast temperature.
2. **Reducing agent**: Carbon (as CO) reduces iron oxide: Fe₂O₃ + 3CO → 2Fe + 3CO₂.
3. **Structural support**: Coke pieces are the only solid in the blast furnace that maintains structural integrity at high temperature. They support the weight of the burden (iron ore + limestone) above, maintaining permeability for gas flow. If coke strength is inadequate (low CSR), it crumbles and the furnace chokes.
4. **Carburizer**: Carbon from coke dissolves into molten iron, lowering its melting point and producing pig iron (3-4% C). Without carburization, iron would remain solid at furnace temperature (~1200°C at the hearth) and could not be tapped as a liquid.

## Coke vs. Charcoal

| Property | Coke | Charcoal |
|----------|------|----------|
| Source | Coal (mined) | Wood (renewable) |
| Fixed carbon | 85-90% | 80-90% |
| Energy density | 28-31 MJ/kg | ~29 MJ/kg |
| Strength (CSR) | 60-70 | 30-50 (weaker) |
| Max flame temp | 1800-2100°C | 1100-1300°C |
| Sulfur content | 0.5-1.0% | <0.05% |
| Phosphorus | Variable | Very low |
| Scale potential | Unlimited (mined) | Limited (forest) |
| Byproducts | Coal tar, gas, ammonia, benzol | Wood tar, vinegar, gas |

**Key difference**: Coke is stronger (essential for large blast furnaces), burns hotter (essential for silicon reduction), and is available in unlimited quantities from mining. Charcoal is cleaner (no sulfur/phosphorus) but weaker and limited by forest resources. The transition from charcoal to coke was the pivotal moment that enabled large-scale iron and steel production.

## By-Product Yields and Economic Value

Coal tar distillation produces specific yields per tonne of coal carbonized. Light oil (<180°C fraction) yields 0.8-1.2 kg benzene, 0.3-0.5 kg toluene, and 0.1-0.3 kg xylene per tonne of coal. Benzene is the feedstock for cyclohexane (nylon precursor) and phenol (resins). Toluene feeds TNT explosive manufacture and polyurethane production. Naphthalene from the middle oil fraction (180-240°C) crystallizes at 3-5 kg per tonne of coal, serving as the precursor for phthalic anhydride, which in turn produces dyes, plasticizers, and concrete admixtures.

Ammonium sulfate recovery provides 8-12 kg of (NH₄)₂SO₄ per tonne of coal, containing 19-21% nitrogen by weight. Before the Haber-Bosch process, coke oven ammonia supplied most of the world's nitrogen fertilizer. Even after synthetic ammonia, ammonium sulfate remains valuable as a fertilizer that also delivers sulfur, an essential plant nutrient. The saturator method (scrubbing coke oven gas with 5-10% H₂SO₄ at 50-60°C) is straightforward: crystals form continuously, are centrifuged, washed, and dried at 100-120°C.

Coke oven gas, after tar and ammonia removal, has a net calorific value of 16-18 MJ/m³. A battery processing 1000 tonnes of coal per day produces 300,000-350,000 m³ of gas. Roughly 40-45% fuels the oven heating flues. The remaining 55-60% (surplus) supplies reheating furnaces in the rolling mill, boiler fuel for steam generation, or can be fed to a town gas distribution network. The hydrogen fraction (50-60%) can also be separated by pressure swing adsorption or cryogenic means as a feedstock for ammonia synthesis.

## Domestic and Specialty Coke Grades

Coke screening produces several marketable size fractions beyond blast furnace and foundry grades:

| Size Grade | Size Range | Application | Notes |
|-----------|-----------|-------------|-------|
| Blast furnace | 25-75 mm | Iron blast furnace | Optimal size for permeability and gas flow |
| Foundry coke | 50-100 mm | Cupola furnaces | Dense, low-reactivity, large lumps |
| Domestic coke | 15-40 mm | Space heating, cooking | Smokeless solid fuel for residential use |
| Coke breeze | <15 mm | Sintering, boiler fuel | 5-15% of total production |

Domestic coke (15-40 mm) is a smokeless fuel valued for urban heating before natural gas distribution. It burns cleanly with low particulate emissions, making it acceptable for residential grates and stoves. Phurnacite-type smokeless briquettes, made from coke breeze bound with pitch and re-baked, provide a uniform domestic fuel product at 15-25 mm ovoid shape, 28-30 MJ/kg calorific value, and 2-4 hour burn time per charge.
