# Carding

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: animals, textiles

(Aligning fibers for spinning): Hand cards: Pair of rectangular paddles (10 × 20 cm) covered in card cloth (stiff wire teeth, 1-2 cm long, set in leather or rubber backing). Charge one card with teased fiber. Brush cards together — teeth pull fibers into parallel alignment. Transfer rolled rolag (cylindrical roll of carded fiber, 10-15 cm long, 3-5 cm diameter) from card. Each rolag feeds one spindle-full of yarn. Rate: 0.5-1 kg of carded fiber per hour.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Carding is the fiber preparation step that disentangles, cleans, and roughly aligns raw fibers for spinning. In [Fiber Preparation](../textiles/fibers.md), carding transforms scoured wool from a matted mass into organized rolags. In [Spinning](../textiles/spinning.md), carded rolags feed the spinning wheel or drop spindle. In [Animal Materials](../animals/animal-materials.md), carding prepares wool and hair fibers. Carding produces woolen yarn — fluffy, warm, with a fuzzy surface — distinct from the smooth, dense worsted yarn produced by [combing](./combing.md).

## Technical Details

Hand carding uses a pair of rectangular paddles (10 × 20 cm) covered in card cloth — stiff wire teeth 1-2 cm long set in a leather or rubber backing. The process: charge one card with a small amount of teased (loosened) fiber, then brush the second card against it in a rhythmic rolling motion. The wire teeth catch and disentangle fibers, removing remaining debris and aligning them roughly parallel. The carded fiber is transferred off the cards as a rolag — a cylindrical roll 10-15 cm long and 3-5 cm in diameter.

Each rolag feeds one spindle-full of yarn. Production rate with hand cards: 0.5-1 kg of carded fiber per hour, making carding the throughput bottleneck in hand-spinning workflows.

A drum carder (Machine Tools stage) dramatically increases throughput. A rotating drum (20-40 cm diameter) covered with card cloth feeds against a smaller infeed roller. Continuous output as a carded batt (flat sheet of aligned fiber) at 5-10 kg/hour — an order of magnitude faster than hand carding.

Carded fibers produce woolen yarn, which traps more air than worsted yarn, making it warmer but weaker and less uniform. The choice between carding and combing depends on fiber length, desired yarn properties, and the end product.
