### Overview

The American bison (*Bison bison*) is the largest terrestrial animal in North America, with mature bulls weighing 700-900 kg and cows 400-550 kg. Bison are naturally hardy — they evolved on the Great Plains and require no shelter in most climates, no grain finishing, and have resistance to many cattle diseases. Bison meat is significantly leaner and higher in protein than beef, commanding premium prices. However, their wild temperament demands specialized handling facilities and experienced management. Bison production is a long-term investment: 18-24 months to slaughter on grass, but with far lower input costs than cattle once the infrastructure is established.

### Physical Characteristics

**Size and conformation**:
- Massive forward-leaning build: the hump over the shoulders is composed of muscle (not fat), supporting the massive head used for snow-plowing in winter and fighting during rut. Head is disproportionately large relative to body — a mature bull skull weighs 15-20 kg with horns.
- Horns: curved upward and inward, 30-60 cm length (measured along the outer curve). Horn core is bone, sheath is keratin. Both sexes have horns; bull horns are thicker, cow horns are more curved.
- Coat: thick, woolly undercoat with long guard hairs. The coat provides insulation to -40°C. Bison shed their winter coat in large ragged patches each spring — the shaggy appearance is normal.

**Athletic capability**:
- Jump height: 1.8 m vertical (necessitates tall fencing). Can clear standard cattle fencing from a standing start.
- Speed: 55-65 km/h at full gallop — faster than a horse over short distances. Unpredictable directional changes.
- Swimming: capable swimmers, crossing rivers willingly.
- Aggression: mature bulls are dangerous, especially during rut (July-September). Bisons' fight-or-flight response is strongly biased toward "fight." A charging bison is faster than a human can run and delivers lethal force.

### Handling Facilities

Bison cannot be handled like cattle. Their wild temperament, speed, and strength require purpose-built, reinforced facilities.

**Fencing**:
- Exterior perimeter: 1.8 m minimum height (2.0 m preferred). Heavy-duty woven wire (hinged joint or fixed knot) with steel posts at 3-5 m intervals. Top wire must be high-tensile. Many producers add a single strand of barbed wire at the top as a visual and psychological deterrent (bison test fences with their heads — barbed wire discourages testing).
- Interior fencing: 1.5-1.8 m. Same construction. Bison respect solid fencing they cannot see through — solid wood or steel panel fencing (1.8 m) is more effective than wire for handling areas.
- Gates: heavy steel frame, 1.8 m height, self-closing latches. Bison learn to open simple latches — use latch designs that require manual dexterity. Gate posts set in concrete.
- Never use electric fencing as primary containment — bison charge through it. Electric fencing may work as a psychological deterrent for interior paddock division after bison are trained to it, but not for perimeter.

**Corrals and handling systems**:
- Steel pipe construction throughout — wood is destroyed by bison impact. Minimum 2-inch (5 cm) schedule 40 steel pipe for panels. Posts set in concrete.
- Hydraulic squeeze chute: essential for safe veterinary work, tagging, and loading. A manual cattle chute will be destroyed — bison exert 3-4× the force of cattle against restraint. Hydraulic systems provide controlled, consistent pressure.
- Curved sweep tub and solid-sided alley: bison move better when they cannot see handlers and follow the animal ahead of them. Solid sides prevent bison from seeing escape routes. Curved design uses their natural circling behavior.
- Working radius: maintain 5+ meter safety zone between handlers on foot and bison in open pens. Handlers should always have an escape route (climb-over gates, designated safe areas). Never enter a confined space with bison without a solid barrier between you and the animal.

**Handling principles**:
- Work bison in groups — solitary bison become aggressive. Minimum group size for calm handling: 5-6 animals.
- Move slowly and quietly. Yelling and rapid movement trigger flight responses that lead to fence-breaking and injuries.
- Use flag sticks (PVC pole with a flag or plastic bag) to direct movement — never use electric prods (causes panic and charging).
- Plan handling for cool weather (below 20°C). Heat stress during handling causes collapse and death. Early morning is best.
- Limit time in confined handling to 30-60 minutes per session. Extended confinement causes extreme stress.

### Reproduction and Calving

**Breeding**:
- Seasonal breeders: rut occurs July-September. Bulls join the cow herd during rut and are removed afterward to prevent fighting outside the breeding season.
- Bull-to-cow ratio: 1 bull per 10-15 cows. Multiple bulls in the same herd during rut will fight — dominance hierarchies are established through head-butting charges that can be fatal.
- Gestation: 270-285 days (average 283). Single calf per pregnancy; twins are rare.

**Calving**:
- Calves born in spring (April-June) with minimal assistance needed. Calving difficulty (dystocia) is extremely rare in bison — they have been selected by nature for easy calving on open range. Over 95% of births are unassisted.
- Calf birth weight: 15-25 kg. Up and nursing within 30 minutes. Calves are red-brown at birth (called "red dogs") — coat darkens to brown by 3-4 months.
- Calves nurse for 6-8 months but begin grazing at 2-3 weeks. Wean naturally or separate at 6-7 months.
- Calf mortality: 5-10% in well-managed herds (compared to 3-8% for beef cattle). Main causes: predation, exposure (severe storms), and abandonment.

**Cow productivity**:
- Bison cows remain productive for 20-25 years (vs 10-15 for cattle). Annual calving rate: 85-95% in good conditions.
- First calving at 3 years (later than cattle at 2 years). The longer productive lifespan compensates for later maturity.

### Grass-Finished Production

Bison are ideally suited to grass-finishing — no grain is needed or desired for quality meat.

**Pasture management**:
- Stocking rate: 2-4 hectares per cow-calf pair on native grassland (varies widely by region and rainfall). Bison range further than cattle and utilize rough terrain more effectively.
- Bison graze a wider variety of plants than cattle, including tough native grasses (switchgrass, big bluestem, little bluestem, Indiangrass) that cattle avoid. They also readily eat sedges, forbs, and brush.
- Rotational grazing works well: divide pasture into paddocks, rotate bison every 3-7 days. Rest periods of 30-60 days allow grass recovery. Bison actually improve native grassland health — their wallowing (rolling in dirt) creates shallow depressions that collect water and support diverse plant communities.
- Winter feeding: in northern climates, bison can be maintained on standing winter pasture (stockpiled grass) with supplemental hay only during ice crusting or deep snow. They use their massive heads to sweep snow aside and access forage. This dramatically reduces winter feed costs compared to cattle.

**Finishing for slaughter**:
- 18-24 months on grass to reach slaughter weight (350-450 kg live weight for a 20-month grass-finished bison).
- No grain feeding needed. Grain-finished bison meat loses the leanness advantage that the market values. Grass-finished is the standard and premium product.
- Slaughter at 18-20 months for optimal tenderness. Older animals (24+ months) produce tougher meat.

**Meat yield**:
- Dressing percentage: 56-60% of live weight (comparable to beef cattle).
- A 400 kg live animal yields approximately 225-240 kg dressed carcass, producing 160-190 kg of retail cuts.
- Premium cuts: tenderloin, ribeye, strip loin, T-bone. Ground bison is the most common retail product (40-50% of total meat by weight).

## 6. Scaling Notes

A 20-cow bison herd on 40-80 hectares of native grassland produces 17-19 calves per year (85-95% calving rate). At 18-20 months, each calf dresses 225-240 kg — total annual meat production of 3,800-4,560 kg dressed carcass.

**Minimum viable scale**: 5-6 animals is the minimum herd size for psychological well-being (solitary bison become stressed and aggressive).

**Fencing investment**: $8-15 per linear meter for 1.8 m steel post and woven wire construction. A 100-hectare perimeter (approximately 4 km) requires $32,000-60,000 amortized over 20-30 years.

**Revenue**: Approximately $800-1,200 per cow per year from calf sales at 18-20 months, with minimal input costs (no grain, no shelter, veterinary costs under $50 per animal per year).

| Problem | Probable Cause | Solution |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Bison testing/breaking fence | Inadequate height or construction | Upgrade to 2.0 m; steel pipe panels in high-traffic areas; barbed wire strand at top as deterrent |
| Aggressive behavior during handling | Heat stress, solitary confinement | Work bison in groups (5+ minimum); work in cool weather (below 20°C); limit confined time to 30-60 minutes |
| Weight loss in winter | Ice crusting preventing grazing | Provide supplemental hay; bison normally maintain on standing pasture |
| Low calving rate | Nutritional deficiency, old cows | Assess body condition; cull cows over 20 years; ensure adequate summer pasture |
| Parasite load | Overstocking, wet conditions | Fecal egg counts; deworm annually if needed; rotate pastures to break parasite cycle |

## 8. Safety

**Working with bison is inherently dangerous.** They are not domesticated animals — they are managed wildlife. Even "tame" bison can react unpredictably.

- **Never** enter a pen with mature bison on foot without a solid escape route. Bison kill more people in North America than any other large animal (including bears).
- **Bull behavior during rut**: testosterone-driven bulls are extremely dangerous July-September. They charge without warning, attack vehicles, and can overturn small tractors. Do not enter bull pastures during rut on foot.
- **Cow protection of calves**: bison cows defend calves aggressively. Maintain maximum distance during calving season.
- **Vehicle safety**: use a truck or tractor when checking bison in open pasture. A bison can outrun and overturn an ATV. Stay in a vehicle if bison show agitation signs (head-lowering, pawing, snorting, hunching the back).
- **Escape planning**: every handling area must have a designated safe zone (climb-over gate, elevated platform, or vehicle access). Practice emergency procedures before working bison.
- **Response to charge**: do not run (bison reach 55-65 km/h). Move laterally to get behind a solid barrier. If no barrier, lie flat — bison may lose interest or step over rather than trample. Last resort only.

## 9. Quality Control

**Meat quality**: Cook bison to medium-rare (57°C internal) for steaks and roasts — the low fat content (2-3%) makes it cook faster than beef and overcooking produces dry, tough meat. Ground bison to 71°C. Carcass aging at 0-2°C for 10-14 days improves tenderness (shorter than beef's 21-28 days due to lower fat content).

**Body condition scoring**: Assess ribs, spine, and hip bones. Working bison should carry moderate flesh — ribs felt but not visible. Winter weight loss of 10-15% is normal and expected on standing pasture.

**Handling stress**: Bison meat quality degrades with pre-slaughter stress (dark-cutting condition). Minimize handling time, work in cool weather, and process promptly after handling. Stress cortisol levels in bison rise 3-5× during extended confinement.

## 10. Variations and Alternatives

**Cattle alternative**: Domestic cattle ([`cattle`](cattle.md)) are more manageable, reach slaughter weight faster with grain, and have established handling infrastructure. Bison trade ease of handling for lower input costs and superior grassland adaptation.

**Regional adaptations**: In northern Great Plains, bison withstand -40°C and use heads as snowplows to access forage under 30-60 cm of snow. In southern climates (Oklahoma, Texas), bison adjust to heat but benefit from shade above 32°C. On semi-arid shortgrass prairie (30-40 cm annual precipitation), bison outperform cattle by 20-30% in calf weaning weights per hectare.

**Predator management**: Bison defend themselves and calves against predators more effectively than cattle. Defensive circling (calves in center, adults facing outward) deters most predators. Predation losses typically 2-5% of calves vs 5-15% for cattle under similar conditions.
