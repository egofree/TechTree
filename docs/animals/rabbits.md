### Overview

Rabbits (*Oryctolagus cuniculus*) are the highest-efficiency terrestrial livestock for converting plant material to meat. A single doe (female rabbit) can produce 180-300 kg of dressed meat per year through her offspring — more protein output per unit of feed than any other mammal. Rabbits require minimal space (stackable cages), reproduce rapidly (28-31 day gestation, 6-12 kits per litter, 4-6 litters per year), and reach slaughter weight in 10-12 weeks. Additionally, Angora rabbits produce one of the finest natural fibers in the world (12-16 microns), and rabbit manure is uniquely "cold" — it can be applied directly to growing crops without composting. For a bootstrapping civilization, rabbits are the fastest path to reliable animal protein production with minimal infrastructure investment.

### Breeds

#### Meat Breeds

- **New Zealand White**: 4-5.5 kg mature weight. White coat (albino). The industry standard meat breed. Rapid growth: kits reach 2 kg live weight at 8 weeks. Calm temperament. Excellent mothers (good nest-building, high milk production, rarely cannibalize kits). Dressing percentage: 55-60%. Most widely available breed.
- **Californian**: 3.5-5 kg. White body with dark points (nose, ears, feet, tail). Second most popular meat breed. Slightly smaller than New Zealand White but similar growth rate. Often crossed with New Zealand White for hybrid vigor (crossbred kits grow 10-15% faster than purebreds).
- **Rex**: 3.5-4.5 kg. Short, plush, velvety fur (guard hairs same length as undercoat). Dual-purpose: meat and pelt. Rex pelts are the most commercially valuable rabbit pelts — the dense, uniform fur is used for garment trim, hats, and toys. Growth rate slightly slower than New Zealand White.

#### Fiber Breeds (Angora)

- **English Angora**: 2.5-3.5 kg. Entire body covered in long, silky fiber including face and ears. Finest Angora fiber (12-14 microns). Requires daily grooming to prevent matting. Yield: 400-500 g/year per rabbit. Most labor-intensive Angora breed.
- **French Angora**: 3.5-4.5 kg. Long fiber on body, clean face and ears. Coarser than English (14-16 microns) but easier to maintain. Yield: 500-800 g/year. Guard hairs provide structure to the yarn. Most popular Angora breed for hand-spinners.
- **Giant Angora**: 4.5-6+ kg. Largest Angora breed. High yield: 800-1200 g/year. Fiber diameter 14-16 microns. Requires less frequent grooming (fiber is less prone to matting). The workhorse of Angora production.

#### Dual-Purpose Breeds

- **Flemish Giant**: 5-7 kg (up to 10 kg). Largest rabbit breed. Slow growth rate — not economical for meat alone. Useful as a terminal sire (breed Flemish Giant buck to New Zealand White does for larger fryers). Docile temperament.
- **Silver Fox**: 4-5 kg. Black or blue coat with silver-tipped guard hairs. Dual-purpose meat and pelt. Hardy and good mothers. Rare breed.

### Housing and Cage Systems

**Cage construction**:
- Wire cages are standard: welded wire mesh (2.5 × 5 cm or 2 × 2.5 cm for floors, 5 × 5 cm or 5 × 10 cm for walls and top). Wire floors allow droppings to fall through, keeping the cage clean and reducing disease. 14-gauge (1.6 mm) wire for durability.
- Floor space: 0.15-0.25 m² per kg of body weight. Minimum cage dimensions:
  - Fryers (growing kits): 0.1-0.15 m² per rabbit (group housing to 8 weeks)
  - Breeding doe with litter: 0.5-0.8 m² (nest box + exercise area)
  - Adult buck: 0.25-0.4 m²
  - Angora rabbit: 0.3-0.5 m² (extra space reduces fleece matting)
- Height: 35-45 cm (enough for the rabbit to sit upright on hind legs).

**Resting board**:
- Wire floors can cause sore hocks (ulcerative pododermatitis) in heavy breeds. Provide a solid resting board (wood, plastic, or rubber mat) covering 25-30% of the cage floor. Allows rabbits to rest off the wire. Replace or sanitize when soiled.

**Cage arrangement**:
- Stackable cages (2-3 tiers) maximize vertical space. Install metal or plastic drop pans between tiers to catch droppings and urine. All-wire cages can be suspended from ceiling joists with "J" clips or cage hangers.
- Position in a well-ventilated area protected from rain, direct sun, and wind. Rabbits tolerate cold well (down to -10°C with adequate bedding) but are extremely susceptible to heat — temperatures above 30°C cause heat stress, and sustained temperatures above 35°C can be fatal. In hot climates, provide shade, ventilation, and cool water bottles (frozen water bottles placed in the cage for rabbits to lie against).

**Nest box**:
- Essential for kindling (birthing). A wooden or metal box, 30 × 25 × 20 cm (width × depth × height), with an open front. Place in the doe's cage 3-5 days before her due date. Line with straw or pine shavings — the doe will pull her own fur to line the nest, creating a warm, insulated environment for kits.
- Without a nest box, does kindle on the wire floor and kits die of exposure or fall through the mesh.

### Feeding

**Digestive system**:
- Rabbits are hindgut fermenters with a unique digestive strategy: they produce two types of feces. Hard, round pellets (normal feces, 60% of output) and cecotropes (soft, nutrient-rich pellets produced in the cecum). Rabbits eat cecotropes directly from the anus (coprophagy) — this second pass through the digestive system extracts B vitamins, amino acids, and microbial protein. Coprophagy is normal and essential; preventing it causes malnutrition.
- This dual-digestion system makes rabbits exceptionally efficient at extracting protein from poor-quality forage.

**Diet components**:
- **Grass hay** (unlimited): Timothy, orchard grass, brome, or mixed grass hay. The foundation of the diet — provides fiber essential for gut health and dental wear. Feed free-choice (always available). Avoid alfalfa hay for adult rabbits (too high in calcium and protein — causes urinary stones and obesity).
- **Pellets** (measured): Commercial rabbit pellets are 16-18% protein, 2-4% fat, 14-20% fiber. Feed 100-150 g/day per adult rabbit (adjust for size and production status). Pregnant and nursing does receive unlimited pellets. Growing fryers receive unlimited pellets until 12 weeks, then restrict to prevent obesity.
- **Fresh greens** (supplement): Grass, clover, dandelion leaves, plantain, vegetable trimmings. Introduce gradually to prevent digestive upset. Never feed iceberg lettuce (too much water, causes diarrhea). Avoid potatoes, rhubarb, and chocolate (toxic).
- **Water**: 50-100 mL per kg body weight per day (100-300 mL for a typical adult). Nursing does drink 2-4× normal amounts. Use nipple waterers or crocks. Must be clean and available at all times.

**Feed requirements by production stage**:

| Stage | Pellets/day | Hay | Notes |
|-------|------------|-----|-------|
| Adult maintenance (buck, dry doe) | 100-130 g | Unlimited | Maintain body condition |
| Pregnant doe | 150-250 g (increase gradually) | Unlimited | Increase in last 2 weeks |
| Nursing doe (with litter) | Unlimited (300-500 g+) | Unlimited | Peak demand at 3 weeks post-kindling |
| Growing fryer (4-12 weeks) | Unlimited | Unlimited | Restrict after 12 weeks |
| Angora (fiber production) | 130-180 g | Unlimited | Higher protein for fiber growth |

### Reproduction

**Breeding management**:
- Does reach sexual maturity at 4-5 months (small breeds) to 6-7 months (large breeds). Bucks mature at 5-6 months (small) to 7-8 months (large). Do not breed before maturity.
- Does are induced ovulators (like camelids) — no estrus cycle. Ovulation occurs 10-13 hours after mating. This means breeding can be scheduled at any time.
- Always take the doe to the buck's cage (never the reverse — does are territorial and may attack an intruding buck). If the doe is receptive, mating occurs within 30-60 seconds. Observe the buck fall off to the side after a successful mating (characteristic behavior). Return the doe to her cage after 1-2 matings.

**Gestation and kindling**:
- Gestation: 28-31 days (average 30-31). Shortest gestation of any domestic mammal.
- Palpate the doe's abdomen at 10-14 days post-breeding to confirm pregnancy (grape-sized embryos can be felt in the lower abdomen — requires practice to distinguish from intestines).
- Place the nest box in the doe's cage 3-5 days before her due date (day 25-26). Provide straw for nesting material. The doe will pull fur from her dewlap and belly to line the nest.
- Kindling usually occurs at night, taking 10-30 minutes. Litter size: 6-12 kits (average 8). First-time does may have smaller litters.
- Kits are born altricial: naked, blind, deaf. They depend entirely on the nest for warmth and the doe for milk. Eyes open at 10-12 days. Fur begins growing at 5-7 days.
- **Check the nest** 12-24 hours after kindling: remove any dead kits (doe will not cannibalize healthy kits if well-fed, but dead kits attract flies and cause disease). Count live kits. If more than 8-10, consider fostering extras to a doe with a smaller litter (rub the fostered kit with the foster doe's fur to transfer scent).

**Nursing**:
- Does nurse once or twice daily (typically early morning and evening) — each nursing session lasts only 3-5 minutes. Rabbit milk is extremely concentrated (12-13% protein, 13-15% fat — one of the richest mammalian milks). Kits double their birth weight in 6 days.
- Kits begin nibbling solid food (pellets and hay) at 2-3 weeks. Wean at 4-6 weeks by separating from the doe. Weaning at 4 weeks allows the doe to be rebred sooner (intensive production), but 5-6 weeks produces more robust fryers.

**Production schedule**:
- 4-6 litters per year is sustainable without burning out the doe. Intensive operations (8+ litters/year) shorten doe lifespan to 1-2 years.
- Rest the doe for 2-4 weeks between weaning and rebreeding. A healthy doe produces well for 2-3 years.
- **Rebreeding**: Return the doe to the buck 2-4 weeks after kindling (or at weaning). Some producers "race breed" (rebreed 1-2 weeks post-kindling) for maximum output, but this taxes the doe.

### Meat Production

**Growth and processing**:
- Kits reach 2-2.5 kg dressed weight at 10-12 weeks (optimal slaughter age). Fryers older than 14 weeks produce tougher meat and lower feed efficiency.
- Dressing percentage: 55-60% of live weight (a 2.5 kg live fryer yields 1.4-1.5 kg dressed carcass).
- Processing: dispatch by cervical dislocation (manual) or captive bolt. Bleed immediately. Skin, eviscerate, and chill to 4°C within 1 hour. Rabbit meat is light-colored (similar to chicken breast) with a mild flavor.

**Feed conversion**:
- Rabbits convert feed to meat at 2.5-3.5:1 (kg feed per kg weight gain) — more efficient than chickens (1.8-2.2:1 for broilers, but rabbits can utilize forage that poultry cannot) and far more efficient than pigs (3-4:1) or cattle (6-10:1 on grain).
- The true advantage: rabbits can achieve near-poultry efficiency on forage and hay alone, without grain. This makes them the most resource-efficient meat animal in low-input systems.

**Annual production from a single doe**:
- 5 litters × 8 kits = 40 fryers per year
- 40 fryers × 1.5 kg dressed weight = 60 kg dressed meat per doe per year
- With 5 does and 1 buck: 300 kg dressed meat per year from a small rabbitry

### Angora Wool Production

Angora rabbit wool is one of the finest and warmest natural fibers — 12-16 microns in diameter (finer than cashmere at 14-19 microns) with exceptional warmth (7× warmer than sheep wool by weight due to hollow fibers that trap air).

**Harvesting methods**:
- **Plucking (hand-pulling)**: Harvest loose, mature fiber by gently pulling it from the rabbit's coat. The rabbit naturally releases mature fiber — pulling does not hurt when done correctly. Plucked fiber is longest and cleanest (4-8 cm staple length). Harvest every 90-100 days (3-4 times per year). Labor-intensive but produces the highest quality fiber. Most Angora producers use this method.
- **Shearing**: Cut fiber with scissors or electric clippers. Faster than plucking but produces shorter, more variable fiber length and includes more guard hairs. Shear every 90 days. Less stress on the rabbit if done skillfully.
- **Combing**: Use a fine-toothed comb to remove loose fiber. Intermediate between plucking and shearing. Useful for maintenance grooming between full harvests.

**Yield**:
- English Angora: 400-500 g/year
- French Angora: 500-800 g/year
- Giant Angora: 800-1200 g/year

**Fiber processing**:
- Angora fibers are smooth, slippery, and lack the scales that help sheep wool fibers grip each other. 100% Angora yarn is difficult to spin and very fragile — Angora is typically blended with 50-80% sheep wool or alpaca for structure and elasticity.
- Hand-spinning Angora: card with sheep wool or alpaca, then spin on a wheel or drop spindle. A single Angora rabbit's annual production yields enough fiber for 2-4 garments when blended.
- Angora yarn "halos" — the fluffy, fuzzy surface that Angora garments are known for develops after knitting as loose fibers work their way to the surface.

**Angora rabbit care**:
- Daily grooming essential for English Angora (prevents matting). French and Giant Angoras need grooming 2-3 times per week. Matted fleece invites parasites, skin infections, and heat stress.
- Keep Angoras on solid-bottom cages or provide resting boards — wire floors damage the long fiber on the feet ("wool block" on feet collects droppings and debris).
- **[Wool block](../glossary/wool-block.html)** (gastric stasis): Angoras ingest loose fiber during grooming. Unlike cats, rabbits cannot vomit. Fiber accumulates in the stomach, forming a blockage that prevents food passage. Symptoms: reduced appetite, small or no droppings, hunched posture. Prevention: daily grooming to remove loose fiber, unlimited grass hay (fiber keeps the gut moving), weekly papaya or pineapple enzyme treatment (bromelain/papain may help break down wool in the stomach). Wool block is the leading cause of death in Angora rabbits.

### Rabbit Manure

Rabbit manure is one of the most valuable organic fertilizers available, with a unique property: it is "cold" (low nitrogen release rate), meaning it can be applied directly to growing plants without composting first. This is unusual — fresh chicken, pig, or cattle manure burns plants due to high nitrogen and ammonia content.

**Nutrient content** (fresh weight basis):
- Nitrogen (N): 2.0-2.4%
- Phosphorus (P₂O₅): 1.0-1.4%
- Potassium (K₂O): 0.5-1.0%
- Calcium: 1.0-1.5%

**Application**:
- Direct application: spread fresh manure around growing plants at 1-2 kg per m². Will not burn seedlings or transplants. The slow nitrogen release feeds plants steadily over 4-6 weeks.
- Composting: rabbit manure composts readily with bedding straw and kitchen scraps. Turn pile weekly for finished compost in 6-8 weeks. Composted rabbit manure is an excellent potting mix component (20-30% by volume).
- Worm casting production: rabbit manure is an ideal feedstock for vermiculture (earthworm composting). Red wiggler worms (*Eisenia fetida*) thrive on rabbit droppings, producing worm castings that are even more nutrient-dense than the raw manure. A rabbit-worm integrated system produces premium fertilizer with no additional feed input.
- Annual manure production: 20-40 kg per adult rabbit per year. A 10-doe rabbitry produces 300-600 kg of manure annually — sufficient fertilizer for 300-600 m² of intensive vegetable production.

### Disease Prevention

Rabbits are susceptible to several diseases, but most are preventable with good management.

**Respiratory disease (Pasteurellosis / "Snuffles")**:
- Caused by *Pasteurella multocida*. Symptoms: white or yellow nasal discharge, sneezing, wet forepaws (from wiping nose), matted fur on inner forelegs. Highly contagious.
- Prevention: good ventilation (ammonia buildup irritates respiratory tract), avoid overcrowding, quarantine new animals for 30 days. Once established in a rabbitry, pasteurellosis is nearly impossible to eradicate — cull affected animals.
- Treatment: limited effectiveness. Antibiotics (enrofloxacin) may suppress symptoms but do not eliminate the bacteria. Prevention is the only real strategy.

**Enteritis (digestive disease)**:
- Group of conditions causing diarrhea, bloating, and death in young rabbits. Multiple causes: bacterial (*Clostridium*, *E. coli*), dietary (sudden feed changes, excess grain, insufficient fiber), and stress-related.
- Prevention: gradual feed changes (transition over 5-7 days), unlimited grass hay (fiber is the primary defense against enteritis), clean water, stress reduction. Mortality can be 50-80% in untreated outbreaks.
- **Mucoid enteritis**: severe form producing thick, gelatinous mucus in the droppings. Often fatal in 1-2 days. Triggered by stress and dietary disruption.

**Coccidiosis**:
- Protozoan parasite (*Eimeria* species). Hepatic (liver) and intestinal forms. Symptoms: weight loss, poor growth, diarrhea (sometimes bloody in intestinal form), pot-bellied appearance in young rabbits.
- Prevention: keep cages clean, prevent manure buildup, use wire floors (droppings fall away), avoid feeding from the floor. Treat with sulfa drugs (sulfadimethoxine) in water if outbreak occurs.

**Sore hocks (ulcerative pododermatitis)**:
- Pressure sores on the hock (ankle) from wire floors. More common in heavy breeds and Rex rabbits (thin foot pad fur). Prevention: provide resting boards, keep cages clean and dry, cull rabbits with chronic sore hocks (heritable tendency).

**Myxomatosis and RHD (Viral Hemorrhagic Disease)**:
- Fatal viral diseases in many regions. No treatment. Prevention: exclude wild rabbits from the rabbitry (wire mesh barriers, double-door entry), avoid contact with wild rabbit carcasses. Vaccines available in some regions.

### Minimal Space Requirements

Rabbits are the most space-efficient livestock:

- **10-doe rabbitry** (with 1 buck): approximately 10-15 m² of cage space, plus 5-10 m² for feed storage, processing area, and aisles. Total: 15-25 m².
- Produces 300 kg dressed meat per year from that space — roughly 12-20 kg meat per m² of housing per year. No other livestock comes close to this density.
- Stackable cages (2-3 tiers) further reduce floor space requirements. A 2 × 3 m area can house a complete 10-doe rabbitry in stacked cages with drop pans.
- Can be housed in existing structures (barns, sheds, garages) without modification — no pasture required, though access to outdoor hutches in mild weather improves welfare.

### Safety

**Handling rabbits**:
- Rabbits have powerful hind legs with sharp claws. An improperly restrained rabbit can kick hard enough to fracture its own spine. **Always support the hindquarters** when lifting — one hand under the chest, the other supporting the rump. Never pick up by the ears or scruff alone.
- Rabbits are prey animals and easily frightened. Approach calmly, speak quietly, and avoid sudden movements. A panicked rabbit thrashes violently and can injure itself or the handler.
- Scruffing (holding the loose skin at the back of the neck) is acceptable for brief restraint (examining, medicating) but always support the body weight with the other hand.

**Zoonotic diseases**:
- **Tularemia** ("rabbit fever"): bacterial disease (*Francisella tularensis*) transmitted by ticks and direct contact with infected rabbits. Wear gloves when processing rabbits. Cooking kills the bacteria. Symptoms in humans: fever, swollen lymph nodes, skin ulcer at infection site.
- **Ringworm**: fungal skin infection transmitted by contact. More common in Angora rabbits (dense fur retains moisture). Wear gloves when handling affected animals.
- General hygiene: wash hands after handling rabbits or cleaning cages. Disinfect equipment between uses.
