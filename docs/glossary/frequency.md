# Frequency

Frequency measures the rate at which a recurring event occurs, expressed in hertz (Hz) or cycles per second. In electrical engineering, alternating current frequency (50 or 60 Hz in power grids, up to GHz in radio and computing) determines the behavior of motors, transformers, and transmission systems. In mechanical systems, frequency describes rotational speed (revolutions per minute, RPM) and vibration characteristics that affect machine performance and fatigue life.

In measurement and metrology, frequency standards provide the basis for time measurement. Quartz crystal oscillators (32,768 Hz in wristwatches) offer stability of roughly 10 ppm, while atomic clocks using cesium-133 (9,192,631,770 Hz definition of the second) achieve stability better than 10⁻¹². Frequency measurement is fundamental to electrical instrumentation—frequency counters, spectrum analyzers, and phase-locked loops all depend on precise time-base references.

In silicon production, the frequency of alternating current supplied to electric arc furnaces affects electrode consumption, energy distribution, and furnace stirring. Lower frequencies produce deeper arc penetration and better mixing in the melt. Understanding frequency and its relationship to period (T = 1/f), wavelength (λ = v/f), and angular velocity (ω = 2πf) is essential across all electrical and mechanical engineering domains.
