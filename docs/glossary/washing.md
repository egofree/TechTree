# Washing

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: energy, polymers, silicon

Pass crushed coal through water trough. Coal (SG ~1.3) floats; rock (SG ~2.5) sinks. Simple but effective. Dense-media separation (magnetite-water slurry of controlled density 1.3-1.8 SG) gives sharper separation for finer sizes. A Baum jig washer processes 50-200 tonnes/hour.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Washing — the use of water or other fluids to remove impurities from solid materials — is a purification step in multiple industrial processes. In [Coal](../energy/coal.md) preparation, washing separates coal from mineral matter (rock, shale, clay) based on density differences. In [Gutta-Percha](../polymers/rubber/gutta-percha.md) processing, repeated washing in warm water removes plant matter, pigments, and water-soluble impurities. In [MG-Si Production](../silicon/mg-si-production.md), washing quartz raw material removes clay and surface contamination, especially iron from crushing equipment.

## Technical Details

Coal washing exploits the density difference between coal (~1.3 specific gravity) and rock (~2.5 SG). The simplest method — a water trough — relies on gravity separation: coal floats, rock sinks. Dense-media separation improves on this by using a magnetite-water slurry of precisely controlled density (1.3-1.8 SG). Material less dense than the medium floats (clean coal), denser material sinks (reject). The Baum jig washer, using a pulsating water column, processes 50-200 tonnes/hour and is the standard industrial washing method.

For gutta-percha, washing involves kneading crude material in warm water (50-60°C) for 5-6 cycles. Each cycle removes more plant debris, pigments, and soluble impurities. The material progressively darkens as residual tannins oxidize, but purity improves with each wash.

For quartz preparation in silicon production, water washing removes clay and surface iron contamination from crushing equipment — iron is a critical impurity in metallurgical-grade silicon that must be minimized.

Wash water is typically recycled through settling ponds where suspended solids settle out and are recovered or disposed.
