# Hull Construction & Shipbuilding

> **Node ID**: marine.shipbuilding
> **Domain**: Marine & Naval Engineering
> **Parent**: marine
> **Timeline**: Years 0-50+
> **Outputs**: vessels, wooden hulls, iron hulls, steel hulls

Shipbuilding evolves through four major construction methods, each enabled by advances in materials and tools. The progression from hollowed logs to iron-hulled steamships spans millennia and requires increasingly sophisticated metallurgy, woodworking, and eventually machine tools.

For operational aspects of water transport (navigation, cargo handling, canal engineering), see [Water Transport](../transport/shipping.md).

### Dugout Canoe Construction (Stone Age)

The earliest watercraft: a single tree trunk hollowed by fire and adze work.

**Timber selection**: Straight-trunked trees 3-12 m long, 0.4-1.0 m diameter. Oak, pine, cedar, basswood, or poplar. Select green (living) timber — easier to work than seasoned wood. Fell with stone axes or adzes.

**Hollowing process**:
1. Strip bark from the trunk section
2. Build controlled fires along the upper surface of the trunk, burning a channel 20-30 cm wide
3. Scrape charred wood with stone or shell tools after each burn cycle
4. Repeat burn-and-scrape cycles until interior depth reaches 30-50 cm
5. Shape interior with adzes to uniform 2-4 cm wall thickness
6. Fire-hardens the interior surface, improving rot resistance

**Hull shaping**: Exterior shaped with adzes. Round-bottom hulls (semi-circular cross-section) track straighter and are faster. Flat-bottom hulls resist rolling and offer more stability. Bow and stern tapered to knife-edges for minimum drag. Freeboard (distance from waterline to gunwale) 20-40 cm.

**Dimensions and performance**:
- Small (3-5 m): 1-2 paddlers, 0.3-0.5 tonnes cargo, 3-4 knots
- Medium (5-8 m): 2-4 paddlers, 0.5-1.5 tonnes cargo, 3-5 knots
- Large (8-12 m): 4-8 paddlers, 1-2 tonnes cargo, 3-5 knots

**Expanded dugout technique**: For wider vessels, soften the completed dugout with steam or boiling water, then spread the sides apart with wooden wedges or thwarts (cross-pieces). This increases beam by 30-50% without sacrificing hull integrity. The expanded hull is held in shape by fixed thwarts. This technique bridges the gap between dugouts and plank-built boats.

### Clinker-Built Construction (Overlapping Planks)

The dominant Northern European tradition from the Bronze Age through the Viking era.

**Keel and backbone**:
- Keel plank: oak or other hardwood, 10-20 cm square section, full length of vessel
- Stem (forward) and sternpost (aft): curved oak timbers joined to keel with scarph joints (overlapping joints secured by trenails or iron bolts)
- Keel provides structural backbone and lateral resistance to prevent sideslip

**Planking sequence**:
1. Lay the garboard strake (first plank) against the keel, fastened with iron nails or trenails
2. Each successive strake (plank row) overlaps the one below by 2-4 cm
3. Fasten overlaps with iron nails driven through from outside, clenched (bent over) over iron roves (washers) on the inside
4. Later tradition uses copper rivets instead of iron nails — copper doesn't rust, longer service life

**Plank specifications**:
- Thickness: 2-4 cm (oak or pine)
- Width: 20-35 cm per strake
- Length: Full length where possible; scarph joints (15-20 cm overlap) where joining shorter stock
- Edge-to-edge gap at overlaps: 0-2 mm (sealed by caulking)

**Frames (ribs)**:
- Inserted after planking is complete (shell-first construction)
- Naturally curved oak branches, steamed to shape where needed
- Fastened to planks with trenails (wooden dowels, typically oak or locust)
- Frame spacing: 30-50 cm center-to-center
- Frame cross-section: 8-15 cm molded (fore-aft) × 6-10 cm sided (port-starboard)

**Caulking and waterproofing**:
- Drive moss, animal hair, or tarred oakum into the overlap seams with a caulking iron and mallet
- Seal exterior with tar or pitch (distilled from pine resin or coal tar)
- Re-caulk every 3-5 years as wood works and caulking compresses

**Structural characteristics**:
- The overlapping construction produces a flexible hull that absorbs wave impact
- Flexibility is an advantage in rough seas — the hull "works" with the waves
- Maximum practical length: ~30 m (Viking longship) — beyond this, the flexible hull lacks sufficient longitudinal strength
- Weight: 2-10 tonnes empty, cargo capacity 5-20 tonnes for 15-25 m vessels

### Carvel-Built Construction (Edge-to-Edge Planks)

The Mediterranean tradition that enables larger, stiffer hulls. Used for all major sailing ship construction from the 15th century onward.

**Skeleton-first construction**:
1. Erect the keel on the building berth (slipway)
2. Set up frames (ribs) at regular spacing, fastened to the keel with floor timbers
3. Fix the planking edge-to-edge against the frames — smooth hull surface
4. Each plank butts flush against its neighbor, no overlap

**Frame specifications**:
- Heavy oak frames, 20-40 cm molded × 15-25 cm sided
- Frame spacing: 40-60 cm center-to-center
- Double-frame construction: paired frames (sandwich style) for maximum strength in large warships
- Floor timbers cross the keel at the bottom, connecting port and starboard frames
- Futtocks (frame segments) joined with scarf joints and bolts

**Plank specifications**:
- Thickness: 3-6 cm (oak for frames and planking; pine for decking and upper works)
- Width: 25-40 cm
- Fastening: iron nails or wooden trenails driven through plank and frame
- Gaps caulked with oakum (frayed hemp rope fibers) and sealed with pitch

**Advantages over clinker**:
- Smoother hull surface reduces drag 5-15% compared to overlapping planks
- Stiffer hull allows larger vessel size: 20-60+ m practical
- Interior space more usable (no plank overlaps intruding)
- Better suited for carrying heavy guns (warships)

**Watertight integrity**:
- Depends entirely on caulking quality between planks
- Re-caulk every 3-5 years: remove old oakum, drive in fresh, reseal with pitch
- Copper sheathing (0.5-1.0 mm sheets nailed below waterline) prevents worm damage and fouling
- Lead sheeting: cheaper alternative for hull protection, less effective against fouling

### Iron and Steel Hull Construction

The critical transition enabled by iron and steel production and rolling mills.

**Why iron/steel hulls**:
- Wood has a practical length limit of ~70 m (HMS Victoria, 1860, pushed to 80 m with iron reinforcement)
- Iron hulls allow 100-200+ m vessels
- Steel hulls (from the 1880s) allow 300+ m vessels
- Metal hulls are stronger per unit weight, don't rot, and don't suffer worm damage

**Iron hull construction (1840s-1870s)**:
- Wrought iron plates (6-12 mm thick) rolled in plate mills
- Plates fastened to iron frames with rivets (iron or steel, 19-25 mm diameter)
- Rivet pitch (spacing): 3-4 rivet diameters along the edge (57-100 mm)
- Frame spacing: 500-900 mm center-to-center
- Angle iron (L-shaped) frames, 100-200 mm leg length
- Plates overlap at edges by 2-3 rivet diameters in lap joints
- Butt straps (reinforcement plates) at plate ends

**Steel hull construction (1880s onward)**:
- Bessemer and open-hearth steel replaces wrought iron
- Steel is 20-30% stronger than wrought iron, allowing thinner plates for same strength
- Plate thickness: 6-20 mm depending on location (thickest at keel and bottom, thinner at topsides)
- Frame spacing: 600-900 mm
- Initially riveted; welding replaces riveting from the 1930s onward

**Riveting process**:
1. Heat rivet to bright red (900-1000°C) in a forge
2. Insert through pre-drilled holes in overlapping plates
3. Hold the manufactured head with a dolly (heavy steel bar) on one side
4. Form the second head by hammering or pneumatic riveting on the other side
5. As the rivet cools, it contracts, clamping the plates tightly together
6. A competent riveting crew drives 200-400 rivets per day

**Welding (later stage)**:
- Oxy-acetylene welding: 3100°C flame fuses steel plate edges directly
- Arc welding (SMAW): electric arc between flux-coated electrode and workpiece melts metal
- Welded hulls are 10-15% lighter than riveted (no overlap at joints, no rivet weight)
- Welded joints are inherently watertight (no rivet holes to leak)
- Quality control critical: X-ray or ultrasonic inspection of critical welds

### Watertight Bulkheads

Essential for safety on iron and steel vessels. Transverse bulkheads (walls) divide the hull into separate compartments.

**Bulkhead design**:
- Steel plate (6-10 mm) welded or riveted to frames
- Watertight doors (steel, rubber-gasketed) allow passage between compartments
- Minimum 4-8 bulkheads for a 100 m cargo vessel
- Each bulkhead must withstand the hydrostatic pressure of a flooded adjacent compartment
- Collision bulkhead (forwardmost): extends to the freeboard deck, 5-8% of ship length from bow

**Flooding calculations**:
- If one compartment floods, the ship must remain afloat with a positive metacentric height (GM)
- If two adjacent compartments flood (one-bulkhead-damage criterion), the ship must remain afloat
- Passenger vessels must survive flooding of any two adjacent compartments

### Ship Structure Stress Analysis

**Longitudinal strength**:
- A ship at sea is supported unevenly by buoyancy — wave crests support the ends while the midship sinks, or vice versa. This creates longitudinal bending stresses.
- Hogging: wave crest amidships, troughs at ends → hull bends convex upward → deck in tension, keel in compression
- Sagging: wave crests at ends, trough amidships → hull bends concave → deck in compression, keel in tension
- Maximum bending moment for a 100 m cargo vessel: approximately 50,000-100,000 tonne-meters
- Required section modulus (resistance to bending) depends on vessel length, beam, and displacement

**Frame stress**:
- Hydrostatic pressure on the hull plating transfers loads to the frames
- Frame loading: p × s × l, where p = water pressure (ρgh), s = frame spacing, l = frame span
- A frame at 3 m depth with 600 mm spacing and 2 m span carries: 1025 × 9.81 × 3 × 0.6 × 2 = 36,200 N (3,690 kgf)

### Shipyard Facilities

**Building berth (slipway)**:
- Inclined concrete or stone foundation (1:10 to 1:20 slope)
- Ground ways (heavy timber or steel runners) aligned on each side
- Cradle supports the hull during construction
- Launching: grease the ways with tallow or petroleum grease. Release the trigger. Gravity carries the hull into the water. Vessel must reach buoyancy before the end of the ways.

**Dry dock (graving dock)**:
- Excavated chamber with entrance gate (caisson or floating gate)
- Vessel enters at high water. Gate closes. Pumps empty the dock.
- Vessel settles on keel blocks (timber or concrete) and side shores (timber props)
- Allows inspection and repair of hull below waterline
- Dimensions: 100-400 m long, 20-60 m wide, 8-15 m deep
- Pumping capacity: 1,000-10,000 m³/hour to empty the dock in 2-4 hours

**Floating dry dock**:
- U-shaped pontoon that sinks by flooding ballast tanks, allowing vessel entry
- Then pumps out ballast to rise, lifting the vessel clear of the water
- Capacity: 1,000-100,000 tonnes lift
- Mobile — can be towed to where needed

### Sailing Rig Evolution (Summary)

For detailed sailing mechanics, see [Water Transport](../transport/shipping.md).

- **Square sail** (3000 BCE+): Rectangular canvas hung from horizontal yard. Efficient downwind only. 1-2 masts. Sail area: 2-4 m² per tonne displacement.
- **Lateen sail** (200 BCE+): Triangular sail on angled yard. Tacks upwind at 45-60°. Dominant Mediterranean and Indian Ocean.
- **Full-rigged ship** (1400 CE+): Three masts — fore (square), main (square), mizzen (lateen). Combines downwind power with upwind capability. Sail area 200-2000 m² for 100-1000 tonne vessels.
- **Sail area calculation**: SA/D ratio = sail area (m²) / (displacement in m³)^2/3. Target 15-20 for cargo, 20-30 for performance.

### Safety & Hazards

- **Shipyard injuries**: Falls from height (working on scaffolding up to 15 m), dropped tools/rivets, noise-induced hearing loss from riveting (110-120 dB). Safety nets, hard hats, and hearing protection mandatory.
- **Welding hazards**: UV radiation burns eyes (arc eye), toxic fumes from flux coatings, fire risk from sparks. Welding screens, ventilation, and fire watches required.
- **Confined space entry**: Double-bottom tanks, fuel tanks, and ballast tanks accumulate toxic gases. Ventilate and test atmosphere before entry. Never enter alone.
- **Launch hazards**: Hull accelerates rapidly during launch (0-15 knots in seconds). Clear the path. Standing water ahead of the ways creates a wave that can capsize small boats.
