# Calcination

> **Type**: equipment | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, chemistry, energy, metals

CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ at 900-1000°C. ΔH = +178 kJ/mol (strongly endothermic). This is the most energy-intensive step. In a shaft kiln: limestone (50-150 mm pieces) fed at the top descends through three zones. Preheating zone (200-800°C), calcination zone (900-1000°C), cooling zone (100-400°C). Residence time: 12-24 hours.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Calcination — heating a material to high temperature to drive off volatile components or induce thermal decomposition — is a fundamental process in chemical and metallurgical production. In [Alkali Production](../chemistry/alkalis.md), it decomposes sodium bicarbonate to soda ash. In [Lime Production](../ceramics/lime.md), it converts limestone to quicklime. In [Aluminum Production](../metals/aluminum.md), it calcines bauxite and alumina. In [Electrode Manufacturing](../energy/electrode-manufacturing.md), it carbonizes petroleum coke. Calcination appears wherever a raw material must be thermally upgraded before further processing.

## Technical Details

The calcination of limestone (CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂) is the canonical example. At 900-1000°C, the strongly endothermic reaction (ΔH = +178 kJ/mol) decomposes calcium carbonate into calcium oxide (quicklime) and carbon dioxide. A shaft kiln processes this in three zones: the preheating zone (200-800°C) where kiln exhaust gases heat incoming stone; the calcination zone (900-1000°C) where fuel burns and decomposes the stone; and the cooling zone (100-400°C) where rising combustion air cools the quicklime while preheating itself. Residence time is 12-24 hours.

The energy intensity of calcination makes kiln design critical. Simple pit kilns lose 60-80% of heat input. Shaft kilns recover heat through counter-current flow, achieving 50-70% thermal efficiency. Rotary kilns offer better control and throughput but require more complex construction. The fuel requirement is substantial: roughly 100-200 kg of coal per tonne of lime produced.

Beyond limestone, calcination processes include converting aluminum hydroxide to alumina (Al(OH)₃ → Al₂O₃ at 1000-1200°C), decomposing iron carbonate ores, and driving final carbonization in electrode manufacture. Each shares the pattern of high-temperature endothermic decomposition in a purpose-built kiln.
