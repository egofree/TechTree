# Evaporation

Evaporation is both a natural phase-change process and a controlled industrial technique for concentrating solutions or depositing thin films. In thin-film deposition for semiconductor manufacturing, evaporation is performed by resistive heating of a tungsten or molybdenum boat (thermal evaporation) or by electron-beam evaporation, where a focused e-beam heats source material in a water-cooled copper crucible. The process operates at chamber pressures of 10⁻⁶ Torr, where evaporated atoms travel in straight lines from source to substrate (line-of-sight deposition).

Deposition rates range from 5–100 nm/min depending on source material and power. Film purity depends on source purity and vacuum quality. A key limitation of evaporation is poor step coverage—shadowing at step edges produces non-uniform film thickness at surface topology changes, which limits its use for advanced multi-level metallization schemes.

In chemical processing, evaporation concentrates solutions by boiling off solvent (typically water), leaving behind dissolved solids. Multiple-effect evaporators recover steam from one stage to heat the next, reducing energy consumption. This technique is essential in alkali production, soap making, sugar refining, and pulp chemical recovery.
