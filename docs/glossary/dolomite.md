# Dolomite

> **Type**: equipment | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, chemistry, metals

(CaCO₃·MgCO₃): Calcined to CaO·MgO at 1600-1800°C. Less expensive than magnesite but hydrates in moist air (CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ — expansion destroys the brick). Used in AOD converters and cement kiln burning zones. Tar-bonded dolomite (impregnated with coal tar pitch) resists hydration — the tar carbon fills pores and forms a protective carbon barrier.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Dolomite is a double carbonate of calcium and magnesium, widely available as a sedimentary rock, that serves as a refractory raw material and a magnesium source. Calcined dolomite (doloma, CaO·MgO) withstands the basic slag environments in steelmaking vessels that would dissolve acidic silica-based refractories. The main limitation — hydration in moist air — requires either immediate use or tar-bonding treatment.

## Technical Details

Raw dolomite (CaCO₃·MgCO₃) is identified by its effervescence only in powdered form with weak acid (distinguishing it from calcite, which effervesces vigorously in acid even as solid chunks). It requires higher firing temperature than limestone: 1600-1800°C to achieve complete decomposition to CaO·MgO.

The calcined product, dolomitic lime or doloma, serves in several metallurgical roles: lining AOD (Argon-Oxygen Decarburization) converters for stainless steel production, lining cement kiln burning zones, and as a flux in non-ferrous smelting. Its refractory value comes from resistance to basic slags — the CaO·MgO composition is chemically compatible with the CaO-rich, CaF₂-containing slags used in secondary steelmaking.

The hydration vulnerability is the binding constraint: CaO in the calcined dolomite absorbs atmospheric moisture, converting to Ca(OH)₂ with a volume expansion that physically destroys the refractory brick. Tar-bonded dolomite addresses this by impregnating the brick with coal tar pitch, which carbonizes during preheating to fill pores with a protective carbon barrier. This adds a processing step and introduces volatile organic emissions during initial heating.

In magnesium production, dolomite is calcined and then mixed with ferrosilicon as a reducing agent in the Pidgeon process (silicothermic reduction under vacuum at 1200°C) to produce magnesium vapor, which condenses as crowns (pure Mg). The reaction: 2(CaO·MgO) + Si(Fe) → 2Mg(gas) + 2CaO·SiO₂.
