# Carbon Monoxide Poisoning

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, foundations, metals

Indoor fires (hearths, fire-banked coals in shelters) produce carbon monoxide. CO is odorless and causes headache, drowsiness, confusion, and death at sustained high concentration. Never burn charcoal or use a Dakota fire pit inside an enclosed shelter. Ensure ventilation — at minimum a high vent and a low vent for airflow.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Carbon monoxide poisoning is the most lethal acute hazard in the entire tech tree, present from the earliest [Fire-Making](../foundations/fire.md) through [Kilns](../ceramics/kilns.md) and [Copper & Bronze](../metals/copper-bronze.md) smelting. Every stage that involves combustion in or near enclosed spaces carries a CO poisoning risk. The danger is insidious: CO produces no warning irritation, and victims may not realize they are being poisoned until they lose consciousness.

## Technical Details

CO poisoning occurs when carbon monoxide displaces oxygen on hemoglobin molecules in red blood cells. CO's binding affinity is approximately 200× that of oxygen, meaning even low atmospheric concentrations produce dangerous carboxyhemoglobin levels over time. At 100 ppm, headache and mild nausea develop within hours. At 400 ppm, symptoms escalate to severe headache, dizziness, and visual disturbances. Above 1000 ppm (0.1%), unconsciousness occurs within minutes and death follows.

The most dangerous scenarios involve fire banking (coals buried in ash at 300-500°C for 8-12 hours overnight) inside shelters. The smoldering coals produce CO continuously, and the enclosed space allows concentration to build to lethal levels while occupants sleep. Charcoal burning indoors is equally deadly — charcoal produces more CO than wood per unit of combustion.

Prevention is straightforward: ventilation. Every enclosed space with any combustion source must have at minimum a high vent (for CO to escape, since warm CO rises) and a low vent (for fresh air to enter). The rule is absolute: never burn charcoal, operate a gasifier, or use a Dakota fire pit inside an enclosed shelter. If occupants wake with headache, nausea, or confusion, evacuate immediately — these are early CO poisoning symptoms.
