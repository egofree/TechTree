# Bearings

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, energy, measurement, textiles

Press brass bushings or jeweled bearings (agate or synthetic ruby) into the gauge housing for the sector pivot and pointer shaft. Friction at these pivots is the dominant error source — even tiny friction causes hysteresis (different readings on rising vs. falling pressure).

## Context in the Tech Tree

Bearings are mechanical elements that constrain relative motion to a single degree of freedom while minimizing friction. In [Temperature & Pressure Measurement](../measurement/temperature-pressure.md), jeweled bearings provide the low-friction pivots essential for accurate gauge readings. In [Heat Engines](../energy/engine.md), crankshaft and connecting rod bearings sustain thousands of RPM under high load. In [Spinning](../textiles/spinning.md), spindle bearings determine yarn quality by controlling wobble. Bearings are a cross-cutting capability that appears wherever rotating or sliding motion must be controlled.

## Technical Details

Bearing selection depends on load, speed, precision, and available materials. Plain (journal) bearings — a shaft rotating in a lubricated sleeve — are the simplest: brass or bronze bushings pressed into a housing, lubricated by oil or grease. Babbitt bearings (tin or lead alloy with antimony and copper) provide an embeddable surface that traps contaminants, protecting the shaft. These are used for engine crankshafts and large machinery.

Ball and roller bearings reduce friction further by replacing sliding contact with rolling contact. Precision ball bearings (steel balls between hardened inner and outer races) achieve very low friction and high speed capability. Manufacturing them requires hard, round balls (tolerance <0.001 mm) and equally precise races — a capability that emerges from the Machine Tools stage.

Jeweled bearings (agate, synthetic ruby, or sapphire) are used in instruments where friction must be minimized to the greatest possible extent. Pressure gauges, clocks, and compasses use jeweled pivots because even microscale friction causes hysteresis errors. The hardness and smoothness of the jewel material minimizes stick-slip behavior, enabling repeatable measurements.
