## Overview

Light aircraft — single-engine propeller planes of wood-and-fabric or aluminum construction — enable rapid long-distance transport, aerial survey, and emergency medical evacuation. They require internal combustion engines (100-300 hp), gasoline fuel, aluminum or composite airframes, and basic avionics (magnetic compass, altimeter, airspeed indicator).

## Aerodynamic Fundamentals

**Lift generation**: A wing generates lift proportional to airspeed squared, wing area, and the lift coefficient (which depends on angle of attack and airfoil shape). Lift equation: L = ½ρV²SCL, where ρ = air density (1.225 kg/m³ at sea level, ISA), V = airspeed (m/s), S = wing area (m²), CL = lift coefficient (typically 0.2-1.6 for light aircraft airfoils). For a 1000 kg aircraft in level flight, lift = weight = 9810 N. At 50 m/s (180 km/h) with 16 m² wing area: CL = 2 × 9810 / (1.225 × 50² × 16) = 0.40 — comfortably below the stall CL of ~1.4-1.6.

**Stall**: When angle of attack exceeds the critical angle (~15-18° for most airfoils), airflow separates from the upper wing surface, lift drops sharply, and drag increases dramatically. Stall speed: Vs = √(2W / ρSCLmax). For a 1000 kg aircraft with 16 m² wing and CLmax = 1.5: Vs = √(2 × 9810 / (1.225 × 16 × 1.5)) = 26 m/s (94 km/h). Flaps increase CLmax to 1.8-2.2, reducing stall speed by 10-20%. Deploying flaps 10° for takeoff (increases lift with moderate drag); 20-40° for landing (increases lift and drag, allows steeper approach at lower speed).

**Drag and power**: Total drag = parasite drag + induced drag. Parasite drag: Dp = ½ρV²CDpS, where CDp = 0.02-0.04 for clean light aircraft (Cessna 172: CDp ≈ 0.027). Induced drag: Di = 2L²/(ρV²πb²e), where b = wingspan, e = Oswald efficiency (0.7-0.85). At cruise (50 m/s, 1000 kg, 11 m span): Dp = ½ × 1.225 × 50² × 0.027 × 16 = 662 N. Di = 2 × 9810² / (1.225 × 50² × π × 11² × 0.8) = 413 N. Total drag = 1075 N. Power required = Drag × V = 1075 × 50 = 53.8 kW (72 hp). A 160 hp engine at 75% power delivers 120 hp (89 kW) — ample for cruise with ~35% power margin for climb.

**Range and endurance**: Breguet range equation: R = (η/c) × (L/D) × ln(Wi/Wf), where η = propeller efficiency (0.75-0.85), c = specific fuel consumption (0.20-0.30 kg/kW·hour for aviation gasoline engines), L/D = lift-to-drag ratio (8-12 for light aircraft), Wi = initial weight, Wf = final weight (after fuel burn). Example: η = 0.80, c = 0.25 kg/kWh, L/D = 10, Wi = 1000 kg, Wf = 930 kg (70 kg fuel burn). R = (0.80/0.25) × 10 × ln(1000/930) = 3.2 × 10 × 0.0726 = 2.32 kW·hour/kg × 10 × 0.0726 × (conversion) = 930 km. Cessna 172 actual range: ~1100 km with 210 L fuel (150 kg) at 75% power, 185 km/h cruise.

## Airframe Design

**Cantilever high-wing configuration** (Cessna-style): Wing attached to the top of the fuselage without external struts (cantilever) or with lift struts (strut-braced — lighter but more drag). High wing provides ground clearance for the propeller, good downward visibility (important for aerial survey and navigation), and natural shade for the cabin. Wing dihedral: 2-4° (upward angle from root to tip — provides roll stability; the low-wing aircraft needs more dihedral, 5-7°, for equivalent stability). Wing incidence: +1° to +3° (angle between wing chord and fuselage reference line — allows level fuselage attitude in cruise while the wing flies at positive angle of attack).

**Fuselage construction — aluminum**: Semi-monocoque structure: aluminum skin (2024-T3 or 6061-T6, 0.5-1.5 mm thick) carries shear and torsion loads, reinforced by longitudinal stringers (L-section or hat-section, 1.0-2.0 mm thick, spaced 150-300 mm) and transverse frames/bulkheads (1.5-3.0 mm sheet, spaced 300-600 mm). Riveted construction: 2017-T4 or 2117-T4 solid rivets (3.2-4.8 mm diameter, driven by pneumatic rivet gun against a bucking bar). Rivet spacing: 4-6 rivet diameters along the seam. A typical light aircraft fuselage has 5000-15,000 rivets. See [Aluminum Production](../metals/aluminum.md) and [Riveting](../machine-tools/joining.md).

**Wing construction — aluminum**: Front spar and rear spar (C-channel or I-section extrusion, 6061-T6, 50-80 mm deep, 2-4 mm wall) run spanwise, carry bending loads (the front spar typically carries 60-70% of the bending moment). Ribs (1.0-2.0 mm sheet, stamped to airfoil shape, spaced 300-500 mm along the span) maintain the airfoil profile and transfer air loads to the spars. Skin (0.5-1.0 mm 2024-T3, wrapped over the rib-spar structure and riveted) carries torsion and shear. Fuel tanks: integral tanks in the wing (seal the bays between ribs and spars with two-part polysulfide sealant, 2-4 coats, tested to 0.35 bar pressure for leaks) or separate fiberglass/aluminum tanks in the wing root. Fuel capacity: 80-210 L total.

**Wood and fabric construction**: Wing spars: sitka spruce (Picea sitchensis, density 0.43 g/cm³, bending strength 70-85 MPa, modulus 10-12 GPa) — the traditional aircraft wood due to excellent strength-to-weight ratio and straight, uniform grain. Spar dimensions: 50-100 mm deep × 20-40 mm thick (solid) or box spar (two spar caps joined by plywood web, 15-25% lighter for the same strength). Ribs: 6-10 mm plywood or laminated spruce strips, glued with casein or resorcinol adhesive. Fabric covering: Grade A cotton or Dacron polyester fabric (1.0-2.0 oz/yd², ~70-140 g/m²), shrunk drum-tight with a household iron at 150-200°C (Dacron shrinks ~10% at this temperature), then coated with nitrocellulose or polyurethane dope (4-6 coats, fills weave, provides tautness, UV protection, and waterproofing). Finished fabric weight: 300-500 g/m² (fabric + dope). Fabric service life: 10-20 years before recover required. Wood/fabric aircraft are 15-25% lighter than aluminum equivalents but more maintenance-intensive and slower (fabric drag is higher than smooth aluminum skin).

## Propeller

**Fixed-pitch propeller**: Single-piece wood or aluminum alloy propeller with fixed blade angle (pitch). Typical diameter: 1.5-2.0 m (for 100-300 hp engines). Number of blades: 2 (most efficient for light aircraft — each additional blade adds weight and cost but reduces diameter for the same power absorption). Blade airfoil: Clark Y or similar flat-bottom section, twist varying from ~35° at the hub to ~15° at the tip (twist compensates for the velocity gradient along the blade — the tip travels faster and needs less angle of attack). Propeller efficiency at cruise: 75-85% (fixed pitch is a compromise — optimized for either takeoff climb (fine pitch, more RPM) or cruise (coarse pitch, lower RPM). A climb prop has 100-200 RPM higher static RPM than a cruise prop).

**Constant-speed (variable-pitch) propeller**: Hydraulically or electrically actuated blade pitch change mechanism allows the pilot to select a constant RPM regardless of airspeed or power setting. At takeoff: fine pitch (low blade angle), engine reaches maximum RPM (2300-2700 RPM), maximum power and thrust. At cruise: coarse pitch (high blade angle), engine holds selected RPM (2000-2400 RPM), efficient conversion of engine power to thrust. Propeller governor: oil pressure (engine oil at 3-5 bar) drives a piston that rotates the blades through a cam and linkage. The governor flyweights sense RPM and modulate oil pressure to maintain the pilot-selected RPM. A constant-speed prop improves takeoff climb rate by 10-20% and cruise speed by 5-10% compared to fixed pitch. Cost: $8,000-25,000 (2-5× the cost of a fixed-pitch propeller).

**Propeller balance and vibration**: Static balance: the propeller must remain at rest in any rotational position — unbalanced centrifugal force causes vibration at engine RPM. Dynamic balance: add or remove weight at specific blade stations to minimize 1/rev vibration (the once-per-revolution frequency caused by mass or aerodynamic imbalance). Vibration limits: <0.5 IPS (inches per second) at the engine mount for acceptable comfort and structural life. Excessive vibration causes crankshaft fatigue, engine mount cracking, and instrument failure. Field balancing: attach an accelerometer to the engine cowling, run at cruise RPM, measure vibration amplitude and phase, add trim weights to the spinner backplate at the calculated angular position.

## Engine

**Horizontally opposed four-cylinder** (Lycoming O-320 / O-360, Continental O-200): Air-cooled, four-stroke, spark-ignition gasoline engine. Displacement: 3.2-5.9 L (O-320: 5.24 L, 150 hp; O-360: 5.89 L, 180 hp). Compression ratio: 7.0-8.7:1 (designed for 100LL avgas — 100 octane, low lead, 0.56 g/L tetraethyl lead). Bore × stroke: 130 × 140 mm (oversquare). Maximum RPM: 2700 (direct drive — no reduction gearbox, propeller bolted directly to the crankshaft). Specific fuel consumption: 0.22-0.28 kg/kWh at cruise power (75%). Oil capacity: 6-8 L (SAE 50 aviation oil, changed every 25-50 hours). Weight: 110-140 kg (dry). Power-to-weight: 1.0-1.4 hp/kg.

**Engine cooling**: Air-cooled by ram air directed over aluminum alloy cooling fins (cast integrally with the cylinder heads and barrels, 2-3 mm thick, 10-20 mm deep, 30-40 fins per cylinder). Cooling airflow: 5-15 kg/s of air per 100 hp. Cylinder head temperature (CHT): 150-250°C normal, 300°C maximum continuous (above this, detonation risk increases sharply and aluminum alloy loses strength). Exhaust gas temperature (EGT): 600-750°C normal, used to monitor fuel-air mixture (peak EGT = stoichiometric mixture; rich-of-peak for cooling margin, lean-of-peak for fuel economy). Cowl flaps: adjustable openings at the rear of the engine cowling — open for increased cooling during climb (low airspeed, high power), closed for reduced drag during cruise.

**Fuel system**: Aviation gasoline (avgas) 100LL: octane rating 100 (motor method), tetraethyl lead (TEL) anti-knock additive, density 0.72 kg/L, energy content 44.7 MJ/kg (12.4 kWh/kg). Fuel system: tanks → fuel selector valve → electric boost pump (0.5-1.5 bar) → engine-driven mechanical pump → carburetor or fuel injection. Carburetor icing risk: fuel vaporization cools the carburetor throat by 15-25°C; at 60-100% humidity and OAT 0-25°C, ice can form on the throttle plate, blocking airflow and causing power loss or engine failure. Carburetor heat: duct warm air from around the exhaust muffler into the carburetor intake — melts ice but reduces power (warm air is less dense). Fuel injection (more reliable, no icing risk): continuous-flow system injects fuel upstream of the intake valve at 1-2 bar. Fuel consumption at 75% power: 25-35 L/hour.

**Time Between Overhaul (TBO)**: Aircraft engines are overhauled at a factory-specified interval regardless of condition (typically 1500-2000 hours for Lycoming/Continental four-cylinder engines). Overhaul: disassemble, inspect all parts to dimensional limits, replace pistons, rings, bearings, seals, and any parts beyond tolerance, reassemble and test. Cost: $15,000-30,000. Engine life can be extended by careful operation: avoid sudden throttle changes (shock cooling cracks cylinder heads), monitor CHT and oil temperature (keep below limits), use correct fuel-air mixture, and change oil at recommended intervals.

## Flight Instruments

**Six-pack basic instrument panel**: (1) Airspeed indicator (ASI): pitot-static system measures dynamic pressure (pitot tube total pressure minus static port pressure) and displays as airspeed via an aneroid capsule. Range: 40-200 knots (80-370 km/h). Color-coded arcs: white (flap operating range), green (normal operating), yellow (caution — smooth air only), red line (never exceed, Vne). (2) Attitude indicator (artificial horizon): gyroscope (electric or vacuum-driven at 10,000-20,000 RPM) maintains a reference horizon, showing pitch and roll attitude. Tumble limit: ±60° pitch, ±90° roll (exceeding these limits causes the gyro to tumble and provide erroneous readings until it erects, ~1-5 minutes). (3) Altimeter: aneroid barometer calibrated in feet or meters. Sensitive altimeter: adjustable barometric pressure setting (Kollsman window) compensates for local atmospheric pressure. At 1013.25 hPa (standard sea level pressure), altimeter reads pressure altitude. Accuracy: ±30 m at sea level. (4) Turn coordinator: electric gyro shows rate of turn (standard rate = 3°/s, completing a 360° turn in 2 minutes) and coordination (slip/skid ball — ball centered = coordinated flight). (5) Heading indicator (directional gyro): gyro-stabilized compass, not affected by magnetic dip or acceleration errors like a magnetic compass. Must be reset to magnetic compass every 10-15 minutes (gyro precession causes 2-5°/hour drift). (6) Vertical speed indicator (VSI): rate of climb/descent in feet per minute (range ±2000 fpm, ±10 m/s). Lag: 3-9 seconds to stabilize at the correct rate after a change in vertical speed.

**Pitot-static system**: Pitot tube (stainless steel, 6-10 mm diameter, mounted on wing leading edge or nose) captures total (ram) air pressure. Static ports (flush holes in fuselage side, 2-4 mm diameter, one or two on each side) capture ambient static pressure. Pitot heat (electric, 50-100 W) prevents icing. Blocked pitot tube causes ASI to read zero (or erratic readings); blocked static ports affect ASI, altimeter, and VSI. Alternate static source (inside cabin) available as backup.

## Performance

**Takeoff performance**: Ground roll distance to clear a 15 m obstacle (Cessna 172, 160 hp, 1000 kg, sea level, ISA): ~400 m at max weight. Factors: headwind reduces ground roll by ~10% per 5 knots; hot/high conditions increase roll by ~10% per 1000 ft density altitude above sea level; grass runway adds 15-25%; wet grass adds 40-60%. Rotation speed (Vr): 50-55 knots (93-102 km/h). Best angle of climb speed (Vx): 56 knots (maximum altitude gain per unit distance — for clearing obstacles). Best rate of climb speed (Vy): 73 knots (maximum altitude gain per unit time — for reaching cruise altitude quickly). Initial climb rate: 3-5 m/s (600-1000 fpm) at max weight, sea level.

**Cruise performance**: Cessna 172 at 75% power: 110-120 knots (205-222 km/h) true airspeed at 2500-3000 m altitude. Fuel consumption: 28-35 L/hour. Endurance: 4-5 hours (with 45 min reserve). Range: 800-1100 km. At 65% power (lean-of-peak mixture): 105-115 knots, 22-28 L/hour — range increases to 1000-1300 km with better fuel economy. Service ceiling: 4000-5000 m (13,000-15,000 ft) — limited by naturally aspirated engine power loss with altitude (power drops ~3% per 300 m / 1000 ft above sea level).

**Landing performance**: Approach speed: 60-70 knots (110-130 km/h) with flaps 30-40°. Landing roll: 200-400 m (no brakes) to 100-250 m (hard braking). Stall speed with full flaps: 40-45 knots (74-83 km/h). Crosswind limit: 15-20 knots demonstrated, 10-12 knots practical for safe operations. Short-field technique: approach at Vref (1.3 × Vs) = 52-58 knots, touch down on main wheels first, apply brakes firmly, retract flaps for maximum braking effectiveness.

## Construction Methods

**Riveted aluminum assembly**: Cut and form skin panels to shape (brake press for bending, English wheel for compound curves). Drill all rivet holes with a pneumatic drill (#30 or #40 drill bit, 3.3-2.8 mm diameter for 3.2 mm rivets). Deburr holes. Apply sealant (chromate primer + polysulfide sealant in lap joints for corrosion protection and fuel tank integrity). Insert rivets from the outside, buck from inside with a bucking bar (hardened steel, 150-400 g). Drive with pneumatic riveting gun (2-3 Hz cycle rate). Inspect: rivet head height above skin surface 0.8-1.5 mm (flush rivets: countersunk, head within +0.00/-0.10 mm of skin surface). Typical build time: 2000-4000 hours for a four-seat aluminum aircraft (kit-built). Factory production: 800-2000 hours with jigs and fixtures.

**Composite construction** (fiberglass/carbon fiber): Lay up epoxy resin + fiberglass cloth (E-glass or S-glass, 200-600 g/m²) or carbon fiber cloth (200-400 g/m²) in female molds. Laminate schedule: 2-4 layers of cloth for skins, with foam or honeycomb core (5-25 mm Divinycell PVC foam or Nomex aramid honeycomb) in a sandwich construction for stiffness (sandwich panel bending stiffness is proportional to core thickness cubed — doubling core thickness increases stiffness 8×). Cure at room temperature for 24 hours or at 60-80°C for 4-8 hours (elevated temperature cure increases Tg and mechanical properties). Post-cure at 80-100°C for 4-8 hours. Weight savings vs. aluminum: 15-25% for equivalent strength. Composite aircraft (e.g., Cirrus SR20/SR22) have smoother surfaces (no rivets, 5-10% less drag), better fatigue resistance, and unlimited airframe life (no metal fatigue or corrosion), but require temperature-controlled storage (epoxy softens above Tg of 60-80°C — a hot hangar can damage the structure) and are harder to repair after damage.

## Avionics and Navigation

**Magnetic compass**: Direct-reading magnetic compass (liquid-filled, 100 cSt silicone oil dampens oscillation). Errors: magnetic variation (difference between magnetic north and true north, varies 0-25° by location, marked on aviation charts), magnetic dip (in northern/southern latitudes, compass needle dips toward the magnetic pole — causes acceleration and turning errors: on east/west headings, the compass leads the turn in the northern hemisphere). Compass deviation card: calibrated by swinging the compass on the ground (rotate aircraft through 8 cardinal headings, record deviation at each). Residual deviation after compensation: ±2-5°.

**VHF radio**: 118.000-136.975 MHz, 25 kHz channel spacing (760 channels). Transmitter power: 5-10 W. Range: line-of-sight (radio horizon at 1500 m altitude: ~140 km). Required for communication with air traffic control, flight service stations, and other aircraft. Emergency frequency: 121.500 MHz (monitored by ATC and rescue services). Antenna: quarter-wave whip (0.5-0.6 m length) on fuselage top.

**Transponder**: Mode C (altitude reporting) or Mode S transponder. Replies to ATC radar interrogation with aircraft identification (4-digit squawk code, 0000-7777 octal) and pressure altitude (in 100 ft increments). ATC assigns squawk codes to identify each aircraft on the radar display. Special codes: 7700 (emergency), 7600 (radio failure), 7500 (hijack). Mode S provides discrete addressing (prevents garble in dense airspace) and extended squitter (ADS-B position broadcast at 1090 MHz, enabling aircraft-to-aircraft traffic awareness without ground radar).

## Operational Considerations

**Fuel and range planning**: Minimum fuel = trip fuel + contingency (5-10% of trip fuel) + alternate fuel (fuel to reach divert airport + 30 min hold) + final reserve (45 min at economy cruise). Example: 300 nm trip, 28 L/hr burn, 100 kt cruise = 3 hours + 0.3 hr contingency + 0.5 hr alternate + 0.75 hr reserve = 4.55 hours × 28 = 127 L required. With 210 L tanks: 83 L margin (60 min additional endurance).

**Weight and balance**: Maximum takeoff weight (MTOW): 1000-1300 kg for typical four-seat aircraft. Useful load: MTOW minus empty weight = 350-500 kg (passengers + fuel + baggage). Center of gravity (CG) must remain within forward and aft limits (typically 15-35% of mean aerodynamic chord, MAC). Forward CG limit: set by elevator authority (too far forward, elevator cannot raise the nose for landing flare). Aft CG limit: set by stability (too far aft, aircraft becomes unstable in pitch and may be uncontrollable). Loading computation: moment = weight × arm (distance from datum, typically the firewall). Sum all moments, divide by total weight to get CG position. Verify CG is within the envelope graph at takeoff and landing weights.

**Maintenance schedule**: Daily/pre-flight: check oil level (6-8 L), fuel quantity and contamination (water in sump drains), tire pressure (24-30 psi), control surface free movement, pitot tube and static ports clear. 50-hour: oil change (8 L, replace filter), inspect spark plugs (gap 0.5-0.6 mm, rotate or replace), check magneto timing (20-25° BTC), inspect air filter, check brakes and hydraulic fluid level. 100-hour / Annual inspection: comprehensive — remove inspection panels, check all structural attach points, control cables (tension 30-70 N, replace if worn >50%), pulleys, hinges, engine compression test (60-80 psi per cylinder, minimum 60/80), check exhaust system for cracks (CO poisoning risk), verify instrument calibrations. Cost: $1000-3000 for annual inspection, $200-500 for 50-hour oil change.

## Airport and Airfield Requirements

**Grass airstrip**: Minimum dimensions: 400-600 m long × 20-30 m wide for light single-engine aircraft. Surface: flat, well-drained grass (75-150 mm height, mowed regularly). Bearing strength: California Bearing Ratio (CBR) >5-8 (firm turf — a Cessna 172 at 1000 kg exerts ~0.5 MPa tire pressure on the surface). Grading: longitudinal slope <2%, transverse slope 1-2% for drainage. Obstacle clearance: no obstacles above a 1:20 approach slope (3° glide path) within 200 m of each runway end. Marking: white edge markers (cones or painted tires) at 50 m intervals. Wind sock at each end. Drainage: crowned cross-section with ditches on both sides, 1-2 m from runway edge. In wet climates, grass airstrips become unusable for 20-60 days per year (waterlogging softens the surface below CBR 5 — aircraft wheels sink and rut the runway). All-weather alternative: compacted gravel strip (100-200 mm crushed gravel on geotextile fabric sub-base, crowned for drainage).

**Paved runway for light aircraft**: 600-1000 m long × 18-23 m wide. Pavement: 75-150 mm asphalt on 150-300 mm compacted gravel base. Asphalt: hot-mix (150-170°C), 5-7% bitumen binder by weight, laid with a paver at 30-50 mm lifts, compacted with a 5-10 tonne vibratory roller to 95-98% Proctor density. Bearing strength: designed for 5-15 tonnes aircraft (PCN 15-30 on the ACN-PCN scale). Marking: white threshold bars, centerline dashes (30 m long, 15 m gap), edge stripes. Edge lighting: omnidirectional yellow lights at 60 m intervals (solar-powered LED, 10-25 cd intensity, acceptable for visual flight rules (VFR) operations). For instrument flight rules (IFR): approach lighting system (ALS), precision approach path indicator (PAPI), and runway edge lights (white, medium-intensity, 100-500 cd). See [Electrical Systems](../electronics/electrical-systems.md).

**Wind and weather considerations**: Aircraft take off and land into the wind (headwind reduces ground speed, shortens ground roll — every 10 knots of headwind reduces takeoff roll by ~10%). Crosswind component: maximum demonstrated crosswind for a Cessna 172 is 15-20 knots; for a Piper Cub, 10-12 knots. Tailwind: each knot of tailwind adds ~10% to landing roll — tailwind landings are avoided whenever possible. Density altitude: on hot days at high elevation, air density drops, reducing engine power and aerodynamic lift. At 2000 m elevation on a 35°C day, density altitude is ~3500 m — a normally aspirated engine produces only ~65% of rated power, and takeoff roll increases by 60-80%. This is a critical safety consideration for mountain airports.

## Emergency Procedures

**Engine failure after takeoff**: At low altitude (below 150 m / 500 ft), there is no time to troubleshoot. Immediate actions: (1) Lower the nose to maintain best glide speed (65-75 knots) — the aircraft descends at 3-5 m/s, giving 30-45 seconds of glide time from 150 m. (2) Select a landing area ahead and slightly below — do not attempt to turn back to the runway (the bank angle and altitude loss in a 180° turn will result in a stall/spin, which is fatal at low altitude). (3) Fuel selector OFF, mixture IDLE CUTOFF, ignition OFF, master OFF — prevents post-crash fire. (4) Flaps as needed for the selected landing area. (5) Mayday call on 121.5 MHz (if time permits). Glide ratio: 8:1 to 10:1 for most light aircraft (a Cessna 172 at 300 m altitude can glide 2.4-3.0 km). See [Medicine](../health/medicine.md) for post-crash medical response.

**Forced landing (engine failure in cruise)**: At cruise altitude (1500-3000 m), the pilot has 5-15 minutes of glide time. Actions: (1) Best glide speed (maximizes range — typically 65-75 knots, slightly faster than best L/D speed due to propeller drag). (2) Identify a suitable field: >300 m long, flat, free of obstacles (power lines, trees, ditches), within gliding distance. (3) Fly a standard traffic pattern to the field (upwind leg, crosswind, downwind, base, final — starting at ~600 m above ground level). (4) Attempt engine restart: fuel selector to fullest tank, boost pump ON, mixture RICH, primer as needed, check magnetos. (5) If restart fails, secure the aircraft (fuel OFF, master OFF, door unlatched for emergency egress after landing). (6) Touch down at the slowest safe speed, full flaps, on the main wheels. (7) Apply heavy braking.

**Fire in flight**: Engine fire: fuel selector OFF, mixture IDLE CUTOFF, full throttle (draws remaining fuel through the engine and exhausts it), cabin heat OFF (prevents smoke from entering cabin through the heater muff), sideslip (bank 10-15° into the wind) to blow flames away from the cabin. If fire persists: immediate landing at the nearest suitable field. Electrical fire: master switch OFF (kills all electrical power, stops the fire source), close all vents, use a handheld fire extinguisher (Halon 1211, 1-2 kg, BCF type — the standard aviation fire extinguisher, effective on electrical and fuel fires with minimal residue). Land as soon as possible. Cabin fire: extinguish, ventilate (open fresh air vents), land immediately.

## Parachute Systems

**Ballistic Recovery System (BRS)**: Whole-aircraft parachute systems (Cirrus Aircraft standard equipment, retrofit available for Cessna 172 and other types). A 15-25 m² ballistic-deployed parachute (rocket-propelled deployment at 50-100 m/s extraction speed, pulling the parachute from its canister mounted behind the cabin or in the wing root) lowers the entire aircraft and occupants to the ground. Rocket motor: solid propellant, 1-3 second burn, 2-5 kN thrust. Deployment altitude: minimum 150 m (500 ft) AGL for full inflation; higher is better (deployment at 600 m gives 2-3 minutes of descent). Descent rate under canopy: 6-9 m/s (1200-1800 fpm). Landing impact: equivalent to a 5-8 m vertical drop — severe but usually survivable with proper occupant restraint (shoulder harness + lap belt, 25-30 g deceleration). Activation: pilot pulls a handle in the cockpit, which fires the rocket. The parachute risers attach to the airframe at reinforced hard points (4-6 attachment points on the fuselage/wing carry-through structure, each rated to 15-25 kN). System weight: 10-25 kg. Cost: $10,000-20,000 (factory installed). Over 1,000 lives saved by BRS deployments as of 2023.

## Medical Evacuation (MEDEVAC)

**Patient transport configuration**: Remove the right front or rear seat and install a patient stretcher (Ferno model 30 or equivalent, aluminum frame, 190 cm long, 48 cm wide, rated to 180 kg). Mount a Stokes litter (rigid basket stretcher) for hoist operations. Medical equipment: portable oxygen cylinder (D-size, 400 L capacity, 2-4 L/min flow rate), suction apparatus (manual or battery-powered, -300 to -500 mmHg vacuum), blood pressure monitor, pulse oximeter, IV pole and fluid bags. A single-engine aircraft can transport one stretcher patient + one medical attendant + pilot. Cruising speed 180-220 km/h reduces transport time by 5-10× compared to ground ambulance over rough roads (a 200 km flight takes 1 hour vs. 4-6 hours by road). Critical for trauma, obstetric emergencies, and cardiac events in remote areas.

**Hoist rescue**: Some light helicopters (Bell 206, Robinson R44) or fixed-wing amphibious aircraft can be equipped with a rescue hoist (hydraulic or electric, 100-250 kg capacity, cable length 30-90 m, speed 0-1.0 m/s). Hoist allows extraction of injured persons from terrain where landing is impossible (dense forest, mountain slopes, water). Fixed-wing aircraft cannot hover — they must fly low and slow (60-80 knots, 10-30 m altitude) while the hoist is deployed, making this a high-risk operation requiring specialized training. In a bootstrap scenario, ground-based litter carries and horse/mule transport are the practical alternatives for mountain rescue.

## Aircraft Structures and Load Paths

**Wing loads and structural design**: In flight, the wing generates lift upward while the fuselage (with passengers, fuel, and cargo) pulls downward by gravity. The wing root bending moment M = L × (span/4) for an elliptical lift distribution, where L = total lift force. For a 1000 kg aircraft at 4g maneuvering load: L = 39,240 N. Wing root bending moment: 39,240 × (11/4) = 107,910 N·m (11 m span). The front spar carries ~65% of this: 70,142 N·m. If the front spar cap is 80 mm² cross-section at 150 mm from the neutral axis: bending stress = 70,142 / (80 × 10⁻⁶ × 150 × 10⁻³) = 5.8 GPa — far exceeding aluminum's yield strength of 276 MPa (2024-T3). In practice, the spar caps are much larger (500-1500 mm²) and the calculation includes the entire wing box (skin + spar caps + spar webs acting as a beam). Design load factors: +3.8g / -1.5g for normal category (FAR Part 23), +4.4g / -1.8g for utility category, +6.0g / -3.0g for aerobatic category. Ultimate load = 1.5 × limit load (the structure must survive 150% of the design load without failure).

**Fuselage load paths**: The fuselage is a pressurized cylinder (if cabin pressurized — unpressurized light aircraft do not need this) and a beam spanning from the wing attachment to the tail. Tail loads: the horizontal tail generates a downward force of 1000-5000 N in flight to balance the wing's pitching moment. At the tail attachment, this creates a bending moment of 1000-5000 N × 4-6 m (tail arm) = 4000-30,000 N·m, plus torsion from the vertical tail (side force of 500-3000 N × 1-2 m arm). The fuselage structure resists these loads with skin shear (the skin acts as a shear web) and stringer/frame bending resistance. Floor beams (50-100 mm deep aluminum channels at 250-400 mm spacing) carry floor loads (passenger seats at 75-100 kg per seat + 4-9g forward crash loads per FAR 23.561).

**Control surfaces**: Ailerons: on the outboard trailing edge of each wing, span ~2-4 m, chord ~200-300 mm. Maximum deflection: ±20-30°. Control force: 20-80 N at the control wheel for a 60° bank (Cessna 172 — harmonized controls should require ~25 N for a standard-rate turn). Elevator: on the horizontal tail, span ~3 m, chord ~250-400 mm. Deflection: ±25-30°. Full-aft elevator at Vs generates CLmax for the tail, enabling the wing to reach its maximum lift coefficient before the tail stalls. Rudder: on the vertical tail, span ~1.5-2.0 m, chord ~250-350 mm. Maximum deflection: ±25-30°. Rudder authority must be sufficient to maintain directional control with one engine inoperative (multi-engine aircraft) or to counteract adverse yaw from aileron deflection. Control surface balance: mass balance weights (100-500 g lead or steel) on the control surface leading edge to move the center of gravity forward of the hinge line — prevents flutter (divergent oscillation that can destroy the control surface and wing in seconds at high speed). Flutter speed must be at least 1.2 × Vne (never-exceed speed).

## Flight Planning and Navigation

**Dead reckoning navigation**: The fundamental navigation method for light aircraft (supplementing pilotage — visual reference to ground features). Plot the intended course on an aeronautical chart (1:500,000 sectional chart). Measure true course (TC) with a protractor. Apply magnetic variation (VAR: east is least/subtract, west is best/add — obtained from isogonic lines on the chart). Apply wind correction angle (WCA) computed from the E-6B flight computer (a circular slide rule): given true airspeed (TAS), wind direction/speed, and true course, calculate heading (HDG) and ground speed (GS). Example: TC 090°, TAS 110 kt, wind from 180° at 20 kt → WCA -10° (crab 10° into wind), HDG 080°M, GS 107 kt. Fuel required = (distance / GS) × fuel flow + reserve. For a 200 NM leg at 107 kt GS with 12 gal/hr flow: 200/107 = 1.87 hr × 12 = 22.4 gal + 8 gal reserve = 30.4 gal total (check: 42 gal usable → 11.6 gal margin).

**Pilotage (visual navigation)**: Identify ground features visible from the air and match them to the sectional chart. Key references: rivers (sinuous, reflective), highways (straight gray lines), railroads (straight with gentle curves, dark line), towns (clusters of buildings), towers (tall structures with flashing lights, marked on chart with height MSL). Checkpoints every 10-20 NM along the planned route. At each checkpoint: record time, compare actual vs. planned ground speed (if slower than planned, recalculate fuel remaining and arrival time). Off-course correction: double the angular drift for a reciprocal heading (if 5° right of course after 50 NM, turn left 10° to parallel the course, then 5° more to intercept). See [Navigation](../foundations/navigation.md).

**Radio navigation (VOR)**: VHF Omnidirectional Range — a ground-based transmitter (108.0-117.95 MHz) that broadcasts a directional signal allowing the aircraft receiver to determine the magnetic bearing (radial) from the station. Accuracy: ±1-2°. Range: 50-200 NM depending on altitude and terrain. The pilot selects a VOR frequency on the NAV receiver and observes the Course Deviation Indicator (CDI) needle — centered when on the selected radial, deflecting 2° per dot (5 dots full scale = 10° off course). To fly to a VOR station: tune the frequency, identify the station (Morse code identifier), rotate the OBS (Omnibearing Selector) to center the CDI needle with a TO indication, and fly the heading shown on the OBS. Distance Measuring Equipment (DME): co-located with VOR, provides slant range distance to the station (accuracy ±0.5 NM). DME operates at 962-1213 MHz, transponder-based (aircraft interrogator → ground transponder → aircraft receiver, round-trip time × speed of light / 2 = distance).

## Cross-Country Flight Operations

**Flight planning workflow**: (1) Select route: direct (great circle or rhumb line) or via VOR airways (Victor Airways — 8 NM wide corridors between VOR stations, at altitudes 1200-18,000 ft MSL). (2) Check NOTAMs (Notices to Airmen) for airspace restrictions, runway closures, and navigational aid outages. (3) Check weather: ceiling and visibility at departure, en route, and destination. VFR minimum: 3 SM visibility, 1000 ft below clouds, 500 ft above clouds, 2000 ft horizontal from clouds (Class G airspace below 10,000 ft MSL). (4) File a VFR flight plan with Flight Service Station (FSS): aircraft type, departure point, route, altitude, destination, estimated time en route, number of persons on board. If the flight plan is not closed within 30 minutes of the estimated arrival time, search and rescue is initiated. (5) Weight and balance: verify CG within limits at takeoff and landing. (6) Preflight inspection: walk around the aircraft checking tires, brakes, control surfaces, engine oil, fuel contamination (sump fuel from each tank — check for water droplets, which settle to the bottom since water is denser than avgas), and pitot-static system.

**Traffic pattern operations**: Standard traffic pattern: rectangular path around the runway. Downwind leg (parallel to runway, opposite direction of landing, at pattern altitude 300-500 m AGL), base leg (perpendicular to runway, descending), and final approach (aligned with runway centerline, descending to landing). Entry: 45° to downwind at pattern altitude. Radio calls at each position: "Cessna 172, downwind runway 27, full stop" — announces position and intentions to other aircraft in the pattern. Spacing: maintain 0.5-1.0 NM behind preceding aircraft (watch for wake turbulence from heavier aircraft — avoid flying below another aircraft's flight path). Pattern speed: 70-80 knots (Cessna 172). Power reduction to 1500-1800 RPM on downwind abeam the numbers (touchdown point), deploy 10° flaps, descend at 500 fpm.

## Aircraft Icing

**Icing conditions**: Structural ice accumulates on aircraft surfaces when flying through visible moisture (clouds, rain, drizzle) at temperatures between 0°C and -20°C (peak accumulation at -5°C to -15°C where supercooled water droplet concentration is highest). Three types: (1) Clear ice (glaze ice): forms at 0°C to -5°C from large supercooled droplets — freezes slowly, spreads along the surface, forms a dense, transparent, heavy ice layer (density 0.9 g/cm³). Most dangerous — heavy (adds 2-5 kg/m² per minute of accumulation) and alters airfoil shape dramatically. (2) Rime ice: forms at -10°C to -20°C from small droplets — freezes instantly on impact, forming a rough, opaque, milky-white deposit (density 0.3-0.6 g/cm³). Lighter than clear ice but rougher — significantly increases drag. (3) Mixed ice: combination of clear and rime — most common in the -5°C to -15°C range.

**Performance degradation**: Ice accumulation on the wing leading edge (even 6-12 mm of rough ice) increases drag by 50-100% and reduces lift by 20-30%. Stall speed increases by 10-20%. Ice on the propeller blades reduces thrust by 10-30% (rough blade surface disrupts the laminar airflow). Ice on the pitot tube blocks the airspeed indicator (blocked pitot = ASI reads zero or erratic — potentially fatal if the pilot does not recognize the failure). Ice on the windscreen obscures forward visibility. Ice on the radio antenna reduces communication range or severs the antenna entirely. Carburetor ice (discussed in Engine section) can form at outside air temperatures as high as 20°C if humidity is above 50%.

**Deicing and anti-icing systems**: (1) Pneumatic deicing boots: rubber boots on wing and tail leading edges inflate periodically (pressure 1.5-2.5 bar, cycle time 3-6 seconds) to crack and shed accumulated ice. Boots are 1-3 mm thick neoprene/rubber, bonded to the wing leading edge. Operate after ice accumulates 6-12 mm (if operated too early, the ice forms a smooth bridge over the inflated boot and cannot be removed). Weight penalty: 5-15 kg. Cost: $5,000-15,000. (2) Heated surfaces: electrothermal heating elements (Nichrome wire, 200-500 W/m²) embedded in the propeller blades, windscreen, and pitot tube. Propeller deice: cyclic heating (30 seconds on / 30 seconds off per blade) prevents ice buildup. (3) Fluid deicing: TKS fluid (ethylene glycol + isopropyl alcohol) stored in a 20-40 L tank, pumped through porous titanium panels on the wing leading edge at 3-8 L/hour. The fluid weeps from the panel surface, forming a film that prevents ice adhesion. Duration: 1-2.5 hours per tank. Used on general aviation aircraft (Cirrus, Beechcraft). (4) Inadvertent icing encounter: if caught in icing without protection, descend immediately to an altitude below the freezing level (temperature increases ~2°C per 1000 ft descent in a standard atmosphere). Turn to exit icing conditions (often only 10-20 NM wide). Declare an emergency (ATC provides priority handling and vectors to nearest VFR conditions or suitable airport).

## Floatplanes and Amphibious Operations

**Float installation**: Replace the wheeled landing gear with two aluminum floats (each 5-8 m long, 0.5-0.7 m beam, 0.4-0.6 m depth, displacement 300-600 kg per float). Each float has 5-8 watertight compartments (separated by bulkheads with water-tight hatches — if one compartment floods, the float retains 80-90% buoyancy). Hydrodynamic steps (transverse notches 30-50% of the way along the float) break water suction during takeoff — the step allows the float to transition from displacement mode (plowing through water at low speed) to planing mode (skimming on the surface at high speed). Takeoff run on water: 400-800 m depending on load, temperature, and water conditions. Speed at liftoff: 50-60 knots. Weight penalty: 50-100 kg for floats vs. wheels (reduces useful load by 8-15%). Drag penalty: 10-20 knots cruise speed loss due to float drag. Amphibious floats add retractable wheels (main + nosewheel) inside the floats — an additional 30-50 kg but allows operation from both water and paved runways.

**Water handling**: Taxi on water at 5-10 knots (idle power, no wake). Turn by applying differential braking on the water rudders (small rudders on the back of each float, retractable for flight) or by varying engine power (a single-engine floatplane turns toward idle side). Sail effect: the aircraft weathervanes into the wind on the water — the pilot must plan all maneuvers considering wind direction. "Step taxi": accelerate to 25-35 knots on the step (the float planes on the water surface, reducing drag) for fast taxi and positioning. Glassy water landing: no visual cues on a mirror-calm water surface — depth perception is lost. Technique: establish a constant descent rate (100-200 fpm) at a constant airspeed (5-10 knots above stall) and hold the attitude until the floats touch the water. Do not flare (there is no visual horizon reference — flaring too high results in a hard drop onto the water).

## Seaplane Operations in Remote Areas

**Bush flying**: Light aircraft on floats or skis provide the primary (and often only) transportation to remote communities in Alaska, northern Canada, Siberia, and Papua New Guinea. A float-equipped Cessna 185 (300 hp, 6 seats, 1500 kg MTOW) can transport 400-500 kg of cargo (food, fuel, medicine, building materials) to lakes and rivers with no road access. Operating cost: $300-600/hour (fuel $50-80/hr, maintenance $80-150/hr, engine reserve $30-50/hr, insurance $20-40/hr, pilot $50-100/hr). Cost per tonne-km: $2-5 (expensive but the only option — road construction in these areas costs $1-5 million per km). Seasonal variation: float operations in summer (June-September in Alaska), ski operations in winter (December-March, landing on frozen lakes with 3-5 mm steel skis bolted to the float attachment points). Spring and fall freeze-up and breakup periods (2-6 weeks each) ground all float/ski aircraft — communities stockpile supplies before these transitions.

## Regulatory Framework

**Airworthiness certification**: In the US, light aircraft are certified under FAR Part 23 (small airplanes, <19,000 lb / 8,600 kg MTOW, <19 passengers). The manufacturer must demonstrate compliance with ~500+ regulations covering structural strength (limit loads × 1.5 = ultimate loads, no failure), flight characteristics (stall warning, spin recovery for single-engine aircraft, controllability at VMC), engine and systems (fuel system integrity, electrical fire protection, oxygen system for flight above 12,500 ft), and crashworthiness (shoulder harness loads of 9g forward, seat/restraint system must protect occupants in a 26g vertical impact per FAR 23.562). Certification flight test program: 200-500 flight hours over 1-3 years, demonstrating safe operation across the full envelope (altitude, temperature, loading, CG positions). Cost: $10-50 million for a new type certificate. Once certified, each individual aircraft receives an airworthiness certificate after a final inspection. The airworthiness certificate remains valid as long as the aircraft is maintained per FAR Part 43 (annual inspections, airworthiness directives compliance) and operated per FAR Part 91 (general operating and flight rules).

**Pilot certification**: Private pilot license (PPL) minimum requirements (ICAO / FAA): 40 hours total flight time (national average: 55-70 hours), including 20 hours dual instruction, 10 hours solo, 5 hours solo cross-country, 3 hours instrument training, 3 hours night flight, 3 hours test preparation. Written exam: 60 questions, 70% pass mark, covering aerodynamics, weather, navigation, regulations, aircraft systems, and flight planning. Practical test (checkride): oral exam (1-2 hours) + flight test (1.5-2 hours) covering preflight inspection, takeoff/landing, ground reference maneuvers, navigation, stall recovery, emergency procedures, and night operations. Medical certificate: Class 3 (valid 24 months for pilots under 40, 60 months over 40) — requires vision 20/40 correctable, no epilepsy, no insulin-dependent diabetes, no psychosis. Cost to obtain PPL: $8,000-15,000 (aircraft rental $120-180/hr × 55-70 hours + instructor $50-80/hr × 30-40 hours + written exam $150 + checkride $600-800 + medical $100-200). See [Education](../foundations/education.md).

## Maintenance and Inspection

**Annual inspection**: Required by FAR Part 91.409 for all aircraft (every 12 calendar months). Performed by an A&P (Airframe and Powerplant) mechanic with Inspection Authorization (IA). Scope: complete inspection of the airframe, engine, propeller, and all systems. Structural: check all skin panels for cracks, corrosion, and loose rivets (tap-test with a 50 g inspection hammer — a tight rivet rings, a loose rivet thuds). Check control surface hinges for wear (maximum play: 0.05-0.15 mm at hinge points), control cables for broken strands (replace cable if >3 broken strands in one lay length), and pulleys for free rotation and bearing wear. Engine: compression test (differential compression: apply 80 psi regulated air pressure to each cylinder through the spark plug hole at TDC compression stroke; acceptable: >60/80 psi, with <25% leakage rate and leakage past rings, not valves). Borescope inspection of cylinder walls and valves (look for burning, pitting, cracks). Oil analysis: send a 50 mL oil sample to a laboratory for spectrometric analysis — trend the concentrations of wear metals (Fe, Al, Cu, Cr, Ni, Pb, Ag) in ppm over successive oil changes. A sudden increase (>2× baseline) in any wear metal indicates impending component failure (e.g., rising Cu indicates bearing wear, rising Fe indicates cylinder or gear wear, rising Al indicates piston wear). Cost: $1,500-3,000 for a single-engine aircraft annual inspection (labor 20-40 hours at $60-100/hour + parts and consumables).

**Corrosion prevention**: Aluminum aircraft are susceptible to galvanic corrosion (dissimilar metals in contact in the presence of moisture) and poultice corrosion (moisture trapped under installed components, upholstery, or soundproofing). Prevention: apply chromate conversion coating (Alodine 1200, a hexavalent chromium solution that forms a protective Cr₂O₃ layer on aluminum surfaces) or non-chromate alternative ( Henkel Bonderite M-NT 1, trivalent chromium or zirconium-based) to all interior aluminum surfaces. Prime with zinc chromate or epoxy primer (2-component, MIL-PRF-23377). Apply corrosion-preventive compound (CPC) to enclosed cavities: ACF-50 or Corrosion X (thin oil-based film, applied by spray nozzle into enclosed spaces — wing interiors, fuselage bays, empennage). Reapply every 1-2 years. Inspect for corrosion annually: look for white powder (aluminum oxide) on skin surfaces and joints, particularly around the battery compartment (battery acid fumes accelerate corrosion), wing root fittings, landing gear attach points, and control surface hinges. Treat corrosion by: removing all oxide (Scotch-Brite pad or glass bead blasting), treating the bare metal with Alodine, priming, and topcoating. If corrosion has penetrated >10% of the material thickness in a structural member, the member must be repaired (doubler plate riveted over the affected area, extending 2-3 rivet rows beyond the corrosion) or replaced.

**100-hour inspection**: Required for aircraft used for flight instruction or hire (FAR 91.409). Same scope as annual inspection but can be performed by any A&P mechanic (no IA required). The 100-hour clock resets each time the inspection is completed (not calendar-based — based on tachometer time). If the aircraft reaches 100 hours during a flight, it may continue to the next point of landing but cannot depart for further flight until the inspection is completed. Operating an aircraft beyond the 100-hour limit (for hire) is a violation carrying civil penalty up to $27,500 per occurrence. Many owners proactively combine the 100-hour and annual inspections when the intervals overlap to reduce downtime. See [Machine Tools](../machine-tools/index.md) for related mechanical inspection techniques.

**Phase inspections**: For aircraft on a progressive inspection program (FAR 91.409(d)), the annual inspection is divided into 4 phases, each performed every 200-300 hours or 3 calendar months (whichever comes first). Phase 1: engine and accessories (compressions, magneto check, oil change, filter inspection, spark plugs, ignition harness, fuel system). Phase 2: landing gear, brakes, tires, and hydraulic system (retractable gear aircraft: cycle gear, check up-lock and down-lock engagement, inspect actuator seals for leakage, measure brake disc thickness — minimum 50% of new thickness). Phase 3: flight controls and rigging (check cable tensions with a tensiometer, inspect bellcranks and pushrods for wear, verify control surface travel within ±2° of specification). Phase 4: fuselage, wing, and empennage structure (inspect all accessible structure for cracks, corrosion, and loose fasteners, check wing attachment bolts for proper torque). Each phase takes 4-8 hours. Total across all 4 phases: equivalent to one annual inspection but spread throughout the year with less downtime per event.

**Airworthiness Directives (ADs)**: Mandatory safety actions issued by the aviation authority (FAA in the US, EASA in Europe) when an unsafe condition is discovered in a type-certificated aircraft. ADs may require inspection (e.g., inspect wing spar for cracks within the next 100 hours), modification (e.g., replace aluminum fuel lines with fire-resistant Teflon hose within 50 hours), or operational limitation (e.g., do not operate above 15,000 ft until a seal is replaced). Non-compliance with an AD makes the aircraft unairworthy — illegal to fly. ADs are tracked in the aircraft maintenance logbook and compliance recorded with date, hours, and mechanic signature. A typical Cessna 172 has 30-50 active ADs accumulated over 50+ years of production; a new aircraft type has 0-5 ADs in the first 5 years, increasing as the fleet ages and service experience accumulates. AD compliance cost: $50 (simple visual inspection) to $50,000+ (wing spar replacement). See [Machine Tools](../machine-tools/index.md) for related mechanical inspection techniques.

**Aircraft registration and documentation**: Every aircraft must carry a Certificate of Registration (establishes ownership and nationality), Certificate of Airworthiness (confirms the aircraft meets its type design and is in a condition for safe operation), radio station license (if equipped with VHF radio), weight and balance report (current empty weight and CG from the most recent weighing — required after major repairs, paint, or equipment changes that alter the empty weight by >1%), and aircraft operating handbook (AOH or POH — Pilot's Operating Handbook, the manufacturer's approved flight manual specific to the aircraft serial number, containing performance tables, limitations, emergency procedures, and systems descriptions). The POH is the authoritative reference — in any conflict between the POH and general guidance, the POH prevails. Aircraft logbooks: airframe log (all maintenance, repairs, modifications, and inspections recorded chronologically with date, tachometer time, description of work, mechanic signature, and certificate number), engine log (same format, tracking all engine maintenance including oil changes, cylinder replacements, and overhaul history), and propeller log (tracking propeller hours, overhaul, and any blade repairs).
