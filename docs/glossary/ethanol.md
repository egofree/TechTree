# Ethanol

Ethanol (C₂H₅OH) is a two-carbon alcohol produced by fermentation of sugars or synthesized from ethylene. As a fuel, ethanol has an octane rating of approximately 100, making it an excellent anti-knock agent. Its energy density is roughly 60% of gasoline by volume, meaning larger fuel tanks are needed for equivalent range. Ethanol is hygroscopic (absorbs water from the atmosphere) and attacks some rubber compounds and metals, requiring compatible materials in fuel systems.

When used as an engine fuel, carburetor jets must be enlarged approximately 30% compared to gasoline, because ethanol requires a richer mixture—its stoichiometric air-fuel ratio is 9:1 versus 14.7:1 for gasoline. Beyond fuel use, ethanol serves as a solvent, a feedstock for chemical synthesis (acetic acid, ethyl esters), and a disinfectant in medical applications.

In a bootstrap context, ethanol from fermentation of agricultural products offers a renewable liquid fuel that can be produced without petroleum infrastructure. However, the land area required for feedstock cultivation and the energy input for distillation (separating ethanol from water requires substantial heat) must be factored into feasibility assessments.
