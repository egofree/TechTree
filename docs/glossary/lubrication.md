# Lubrication

> **Type**: material | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: energy, machine-tools, metals, textiles

The application of a friction-reducing substance between moving surfaces to prevent wear, reduce energy loss, and dissipate heat. In the tech tree, lubrication progresses from animal fat and plant oils (Year 0) through refined mineral oils (petroleum distillation) to synthetic lubricants. Wire drawing uses tallow or soap solution on wire entering dies. Bearings require oil or grease. Steam engine cylinders use oil injection. Aircraft engines use total-loss splash systems with oil reservoir in the crankcase.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Lubrication is essential wherever mechanical motion occurs — which is everywhere in the tech tree beyond the most primitive tools. The potter's wheel bearing needs grease. Wire drawing for electrical conductors requires lubrication to prevent galling (wire sticking to the die and snapping). Machine tool slideways need oil films. Steam engine pistons require continuous cylinder oil injection. Generators and motors need bearing lubrication for long service life.

The energy domain covers lubrication of prime movers (steam engines, internal combustion engines, water turbines) and power transmission (gearboxes, bearings, chains). The machine-tools domain addresses lubrication of cutting tools, slideways, and spindle bearings where precision depends on smooth, stick-slip-free motion.

## Technical Details

Lubricants are classified by viscosity (resistance to flow, measured in centistokes), viscosity index (change in viscosity with temperature), and load-carrying capacity. Early lubricants — tallow (rendered beef/mutton fat), lard oil, neatsfoot oil, olive oil, castor oil — provide adequate lubrication at moderate temperatures and loads. They oxidize and thicken over time, requiring frequent replacement.

Mineral oils (distilled from petroleum) offer more stable viscosity and longer service life. They are graded by SAE viscosity number (SAE 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 90). Grease — mineral oil thickened with lithium or calcium soap — stays in place on bearings and gears where liquid oil would drain away. For high-temperature applications (steam cylinders, internal combustion engines), compounded oils with fatty acid additives resist thermal breakdown.

The fundamental lubrication regime matters: boundary lubrication (thin molecular film, surfaces in partial contact), hydrodynamic lubrication (full fluid film separates surfaces — journal bearings at operating speed), and elastohydrodynamic lubrication (elastic deformation of surfaces creates a film — rolling element bearings). Getting the right lubricant in the right regime prevents premature failure.
