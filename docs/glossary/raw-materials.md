# Raw Materials

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: foundational | **Domains**: energy, silicon, ceramics, metals

Petroleum coke (60-65% of anode mass, calcined at 1200-1300°C to remove volatiles and develop crystalline structure), coal tar pitch (13-16% as binder), recycled anode butts (20-25%, remaining carbon from consumed anodes, crushed and re-used).

## Context in the Tech Tree

Raw materials are the natural or minimally processed inputs to industrial processes. Understanding raw material requirements is the first step in establishing any production capability, because material availability constrains process design and siting decisions. In [Electrode Manufacturing](../energy/electrode-manufacturing.md), anode raw materials are petroleum coke, coal tar pitch, and recycled anode butts. In [Metallurgical-Grade Silicon Production](../silicon/mg-si-production.md), raw materials are quartz (SiO₂), carbon reductant (coke, charcoal, or coal), and wood chips (porosity agent). In [Lime Production](../ceramics/lime.md), the raw material is simply limestone (CaCO₃).

## Technical Details

Raw material specifications directly affect product quality and process efficiency. For aluminum smelting anodes, petroleum coke must be calcined at 1200-1300°C before use — this step removes volatile matter (hydrocarbons, moisture) and develops the graphitic crystalline structure that provides electrical conductivity and mechanical strength. Uncalcined coke would outgas and disintegrate in the electrolytic cell.

Coal tar pitch serves as the binder that holds coke particles together. When the anode is baked (at 1000-1200°C), the pitch carbonizes and forms a carbonaceous matrix bonding the coke aggregate into a monolithic block. Pitch quality is characterized by softening point (typically 100-120°C), coking value (50-60% — the fraction that converts to solid carbon during baking), and quinoline-insoluble content (affects binder properties).

Recycled anode butts (the 20-25% of the anode that remains after consumption in the cell) are crushed and blended back into new anode production. This is both economically necessary (pitch and coke are expensive) and technically sound (butt carbon has already been baked to high temperature and has good crystalline structure).

For silicon production, quartz purity is critical: iron and aluminum impurities in the quartz directly contaminate the silicon product. Wood chips added to the charge create porosity that allows gas flow through the furnace bed.
