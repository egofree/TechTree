# Fire Hazard

Fire hazard identifies conditions where combustible materials, ignition sources, and oxygen converge to create risk of uncontrolled fire or explosion. In spray booth operations with solvent-borne paints, vapor concentration exceeding the Lower Explosive Limit (LEL) creates an explosion risk. Fire suppression systems (water spray or dry chemical) are mandatory in spray booths, along with non-sparking tools and explosion-proof electrical equipment.

In welding and metalworking, fire hazards arise from sparks, hot slag, and open flames igniting nearby combustible materials. A 10-meter clear radius free of flammable materials should surround any hot work area. Fire watches must continue for 30 minutes after hot work ceases, as smoldering material can ignite well after the work appears finished.

Fire hazard assessment considers three components: fuel (what can burn), ignition source (what can start the fire), and oxygen (typically ambient air). Removing any one of these three breaks the fire triangle. In practice, this means controlling combustible storage, eliminating unnecessary ignition sources, and in enclosed spaces, monitoring oxygen levels. Every bootstrap industrial site needs fire extinguishers rated for the specific hazards present—Class A for ordinary combustibles, Class B for flammable liquids, Class C for electrical fires, and Class D for metal fires.
