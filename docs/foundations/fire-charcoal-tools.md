### Fire-Making

**Friction fire (bow drill method)**:
- **Materials**: Straight hardwood stick (hearth board: cedar, cottonwood, or willow; drill: harder wood like oak or maple), bow (curved stick with cordage from animal sinew or plant fiber), bearing block (stone or hardwood with grease-filled depression)
- **Process**: Cut V-notch in hearth board, place tinder bundle (dry grass, bark shavings, bird nest material) underneath. Wrap bowstring once around drill. Press bearing block on top, bow back and forth. Ember forms in 30-90 seconds with practice. Transfer ember to tinder, blow into flame.
- **Critical detail**: Hearth board must be dry. Drill diameter ~1-2 cm. Bow length ~60-80 cm. Cordage tension must grip drill without stalling.

**Flint-and-steel (faster, requires preparation)**:
- **Materials**: High-carbon steel striker (wrought iron works poorly; needs ~0.5-1% carbon), flint or chert, char cloth (cotton linen heated in sealed tin until carbonized), tinder
- **Process**: Strike steel edge against flint at glancing angle. Sparks (~800°C) land on char cloth, which glows. Fold into tinder bundle and blow to flame.
- **Char cloth production**: Place 5×5 cm cotton squares in sealed tin with small vent hole. Heat tin in fire for 5-10 minutes until smoke stops. Cloth turns black but retains fiber structure. Store dry.

### Charcoal Production

Charcoal is the primary fuel for all metallurgy until coke arrives in the Energy stage. Every kg of iron requires roughly 1-2 kg of charcoal.

**Earth-covered pit method (simplest)**:
- **Site selection**: Level ground, away from structures, downwind. Clay soil preferred (better seal).
- **Wood preparation**: Hardwood (oak, hickory, maple) gives denser, hotter charcoal. Cut to uniform lengths (~30-60 cm), split to ~5-10 cm diameter. Season 2-6 months (green wood produces more smoke, less charcoal). Stack ~50-200 kg per burn.
- **Pit construction**: Dig shallow pit 60-100 cm deep, 1-2 m diameter, sloping sides. Or use flat ground with earth mound.
- **Stacking**: Stand wood vertically in tight formation around central firing pole (5 cm diameter dry stick). Thickest pieces in center, thinner pieces outside. Leave air channels.
- **Firing**: Remove central pole, drop burning tinder into the hole. Wood ignites from center outward.
- **Controlled burn (CRITICAL)**: Cover partially with earth/leaves/sod once well lit. Leave small vent holes. The goal is *partial* combustion — enough heat to drive off water and volatiles (pyrolysis) but not so much that the wood burns to ash. Smoke transitions from white (steam) to yellow-brown (tar) to thin blue (nearly done).
- **Timing**: 12-48 hours depending on size. Small pits: ~12-24 hours. Large mound kilns: 24-48+ hours.
- **Cooling**: Seal all vents with earth. Let cool 24-48 hours. Opening too early causes reignition and loss.
- **Yield**: ~20-35% by weight (softwood less, hardwood more). ~100 kg wood → ~25 kg charcoal.
- **Quality indicators**: Charcoal should ring when struck, be lightweight, black throughout with retained wood grain structure. Brittle, easily crushed = underburned. White ash = overburned.

**Simple kiln method (higher yield, reusable)**:
- Build cylindrical stone or clay structure (~1 m tall, ~80 cm diameter) with grate at bottom and opening at top.
- Load wood from top, ignite from bottom through grate.
- Control air intake with adjustable door at base.
- Cover top with iron sheet or stone slab when smoking properly.
- Yield improves to ~30-40% by weight. Can be reused hundreds of times.

### Stone & Wood Tools

**Knapping (flint/obsidian/chert)**:
- **Hard hammer percussion**: Round cobble hammerstone (quartzite, granite, 200-500 g) struck against core to remove large flakes. Produces crude blanks.
- **Soft hammer percussion**: Antler billet or hardwood club for finer flake removal. Thinner, more controlled flakes.
- **Pressure flaking**: Antler tine or copper-tipped tool pressed against edge to remove tiny flakes for final shaping and sharpening. Produces edges sharper than surgical steel scalpels (obsidian ~3 nm edge width vs steel ~500 nm).
- **Tool types**: Hand axes (bifacial), scrapers (hide processing, wood shaping), projectile points (hunting), drill bits (rotating between hands or with bow drill attachment), burins (engraving, scoring).

**Ground stone tools**:
- **Pecking**: Repeatedly striking granite, basalt, or greenstone with hammerstone to rough out shape.
- **Grinding**: Rub against flat abrasive stone (sandstone) with water and sand slurry. Hours of grinding produce polished, sharp edges.
- **Tool types**: Axes (grooved for handle attachment), adzes (woodworking), mortars and pestles (grain processing), manos and metates (grain grinding).

**Wooden tools and implements**:
- **Hafting**: Attach stone heads to wooden handles using sinew, rawhide, or plant-fiber cordage, secured with pine pitch or birch tar adhesive.
- **Birch tar adhesive**: Heat birch bark in sealed container (similar to char cloth method) at 300-400°C. Black, sticky tar forms. Roll into balls. Soften with heat to apply. Strongest natural adhesive available.
- **Handles**: Split green wood, shape with stone tools, dry slowly to prevent checking.
- **Digging sticks**: Fire-harden tip of hardwood stick (heat until brown, not black) for soil penetration.
