# Crucible

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: ceramics, machine-tools, silicon

Fused silica (SiO₂), 200-450 mm diameter, 200-300 mm tall. Supported by graphite susceptor (cylindrical sleeve). Crucible is CONSUMABLE — slowly dissolves into silicon melt (oxygen incorporation into crystal, ~10-20 ppma O). Rotates at 2-15 RPM.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Crucibles are the vessels that contain molten material at extreme temperatures — the intersection of ceramics, metallurgy, and semiconductor crystal growth. From small clay-graphite crucibles for aluminum casting (5-15 kg capacity) to the fused silica crucibles used in Czochralski silicon crystal growth (holding hundreds of kilograms of molten silicon), the crucible must withstand both the chemical attack of the melt and the thermal shock of heating and cooling cycles.

## Technical Details

For non-ferrous metal casting (aluminum, brass, bronze), clay-graphite crucibles are the workhorse. They withstand temperatures to 1600°C, resist thermal shock, and are chemically inert to most non-ferrous melts. Steel pipe crucibles with welded bottom plates serve for aluminum at startup (melting point 660°C), but steel is NOT suitable for cast iron — steel's melting point (1370-1510°C) is dangerously close to iron's pouring temperature.

Silicon carbide crucibles offer longer life and higher temperature capability than clay-graphite, used in demanding foundry applications.

For silicon crystal growth (Czochralski process), fused silica crucibles are consumable items. The high-purity SiO₂ slowly dissolves into the silicon melt, introducing oxygen as a dopant at 10-20 ppma. Crucible rotation at 2-15 RPM promotes uniform melt temperature and oxygen distribution. The crucible sits inside a graphite susceptor heated by radio-frequency induction. Crucible cost is significant — a single CZ pull consumes one crucible, and fused silica of this purity requires advanced ceramics manufacturing.

Fire assay for precious metals uses smaller crucibles (15-30 g sample capacity) heated to 1000-1100°C with flux mixtures to separate gold and silver from gangue minerals.
