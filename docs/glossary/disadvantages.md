# Disadvantages

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: chemistry, computing, energy, gas-handling, machine-tools, metals, silicon

Lower power density and higher weight per kW than IC engines. Dynamic sealing at high temperature is difficult — leakage reduces efficiency over time. Slow thermal response to load changes (heat exchangers have thermal inertia). Expensive heat exchangers required on both hot and cold sides. Helium and hydrogen working fluids require high-pressure sealing (hydrogen permeates many materials).

## Context in the Tech Tree

Every technology in the tech tree carries disadvantages that constrain its application or require compensating capabilities. Stirling engines offer external combustion flexibility but suffer from low power density and sealing challenges. Vacuum systems enable semiconductor processing but require expensive materials and are vulnerable to implosion. Aluminum smelting is energy-intensive. Understanding disadvantages is essential for choosing between alternative approaches and for identifying which limitations must be overcome to advance to the next capability level.

## Technical Details

The Stirling engine's disadvantages illustrate the cascade effect: low power density means larger, heavier engines for equivalent output; high-temperature dynamic sealing is a materials science challenge (hydrogen permeates most metals at elevated temperature); and thermal inertia of heat exchangers causes slow load response, making Stirling engines unsuitable for variable-load applications despite their theoretical Carnot-limit efficiency.

In vacuum technology, disadvantages include the fragility of glass bell jars (catastrophic implosion risk from scratches or flaws under atmospheric external pressure), oil backstreaming from diffusion pumps contaminating vacuum chambers, and the high cost of precision-machined CF flanges with copper gaskets. Virtual leaks — trapped gas volumes inside the system — cause frustrating slow pressure rises that helium leak detectors cannot locate because the gas is already inside the chamber.

In electronics, vacuum tubes consume significant heater power (typically 1-2 W per tube just for the filament), generate substantial heat requiring forced-air cooling, and have limited operational life (filament burnout). Their physical size compared to transistors limits circuit density.

In aluminum smelting, the primary disadvantage is energy intensity: 13-15 kWh per kg of aluminum, versus 1-2 kWh/kg for steel. Carbon anodes are consumed at 0.4-0.5 kg per kg Al. The process requires continuous operation — the electrolyte must remain molten.
