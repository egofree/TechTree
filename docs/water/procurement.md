# Water Procurement

> **Dependencies**: None (root capability)
> **Enables**: None (leaf capability)
> **Node**: `water.procurement`
> **Parent**: [Water](index.md)
> **Timeline**: Years 0-5
> **Outputs**: raw_water, groundwater, rainwater

Locating and extracting water from natural sources. Before any
distribution network or treatment plant can function, a reliable source
must be found, developed, and maintained. This capability covers the
full range of procurement methods available from stone-age conditions
through early industrial development.

## Water Source Identification

Finding water is the first challenge. Several natural indicators reveal
subsurface water:

- **Vegetation indicators**: Lush green patches in arid terrain, willow
  and cottonwood trees, reeds and rushes all signal shallow water tables.
- **Geological indicators**: Fault lines, valley floors, alluvial fans,
  and limestone karst regions often bear water. Dry riverbeds frequently
  conceal subsurface flow.
- **Animal behavior**: Game trails converging on a point, bird flight
  patterns at dawn and dusk, and insect swarms near moist ground.
- **Topographic survey**: Contour mapping identifies natural catchments
  and potential reservoir sites.

## Well Digging

Wells are the most universal procurement method across civilizations.

### Hand-Dug Wells

- Diameter: 1-2 meters for human access
- Depth: typically 5-30 meters to water table
- Lining: stone, brick, or timber to prevent collapse
- Construction: dig during dry season when water table is lowest
- Safety: shoring as you descend, ventilation for deep wells, rope and
  bucket for debris removal
- Yield: depends on aquifer; typical hand-dug well serves 50-200 people

### Driven Wells (with metal tools)

- Pipe diameter: 3-8 cm steel or iron pipe
- Depth: 5-15 meters in sandy or gravelly soil
- Method: drive a screened pipe into the ground using a heavy driving cap
- Advantage: faster than digging, smaller footprint
- Limitation: only works in unconsolidated soils

### Bored Wells (later development)

- Auger-based drilling for deeper aquifers (15-50 meters)
- Requires metal tools and mechanical advantage (windlass or capstan)
- Casing with clay or concrete to seal surface contamination

## Spring Development

Springs are ideal water sources — naturally filtered groundwater
emerging at the surface.

- **Collection box**: Build a concrete, stone, or timber collection basin
  at the spring orifice. Size: 0.5-2 m³ depending on flow rate.
- **Sealing**: Clay or concrete seal around the orifice prevents surface
  water contamination. Backfill with gravel and sand.
- **Overflow pipe**: Direct excess flow to irrigation or drainage.
- **Delivery**: Gravity-fed pipe from collection box to storage tank or
  distribution point.
- **Protection**: Fence the spring area to exclude livestock. Plant
  vegetation uphill to reduce erosion.

Typical spring yield: 0.1-10 liters/second. A spring yielding 1 L/s
provides 86,400 liters per day — sufficient for roughly 1,700 people at
50 L/person/day.

## Rainwater Harvesting

Rainwater is among the purest natural water sources if collected
correctly.

### Roof Catchment Systems

- **Catchment area**: Corrugated iron, tile, or thatch roofs. Metal and
  tile preferred for cleanliness.
- **Gutters and downspouts**: Timber, galvanized iron, or bamboo.
  Minimum slope 0.5% for drainage.
- **First-flush diverter**: Discard the first 1-2 mm of rainfall (washes
  dust and bird droppings off roof). Simple standpipe with drain valve.
- **Storage tanks**: Ferrocement, stone, or clay cisterns. Size
  calculation: annual rainfall × roof area × runoff coefficient (0.8 for
  metal, 0.6 for tile).

### Yield Calculation

A 50 m² roof in a region with 800 mm annual rainfall collects:
50 × 0.8 × 800 = 32,000 liters/year = 88 L/day
Sufficient for roughly 1.5 people at 50 L/person/day.

### Surface Catchments

In arid regions without roofs, impervious surfaces collect rainwater:

- **Micro-catchments**: Cleared, compacted earth or stone-paved areas
  sloping toward a collection point. Typical size: 10-100 m².
- **Check dams**: Small stone or earth dams across seasonal watercourses
  to slow runoff and allow infiltration into the soil.
- **Tanks**: Underground cisterns (similar to Roman impluvium systems)
  for seasonal storage.

## Surface Water Collection

Rivers, lakes, and streams provide the highest-volume sources but
require more treatment than groundwater.

- **Intake design**: Locate upstream of settlement and sewage outfalls.
  Submerged intake with coarse screen (gravel filter basket).
- **Settling basins**: Allow suspended sediment to settle before use.
  Simple excavated ponds with 1-4 hour residence time.
- **Infiltration galleries**: Horizontal porous pipes buried in riverbank
  gravels. Natural sand filtration yields cleaner water than direct
  abstraction.

## Safety Considerations

- Never drink untreated surface water — waterborne pathogens (cholera,
  typhoid, dysentery) are major killers in all pre-industrial settings
- Well contamination from surface runoff is the most common failure mode
  — seal wellheads and maintain drainage away from the opening
- Deep well gases (carbon dioxide, hydrogen sulfide) can be lethal in
  confined spaces — always lower a lit candle before descending; if it
  extinguishes, do not enter
