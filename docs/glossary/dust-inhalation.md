# Dust Inhalation

> **Type**: noun | **Tier**: critical | **Domains**: plants, polymers, silicon, transport

Sawing, filing, or grinding hardened gutta-percha produces fine dust. Use dust extraction or wear respirator. Gutta-percha dust is not known to be toxic (it is biocompatible and used in dentistry) but any fine particulate (<10 μm) is a respiratory hazard with chronic exposure — the body cannot clear particles that penetrate deep into the lungs, and long-term exposure can cause pneumoconiosis-like conditions.

## Context in the Tech Tree

Dust inhalation is an occupational health hazard that accompanies grinding, crushing, sawing, and dry processing across the tech tree. Silica dust from quartz crushing causes silicosis. Cotton fiber dust causes byssinosis ("brown lung"). Silicon powder finer than 100 μm is a combustible dust explosion hazard as well as a respiratory hazard. Road construction generates dust from aggregate handling. Respirators, wet processing methods, and local exhaust ventilation are the standard mitigations.

## Technical Details

The threshold for respirable dust is particles below 10 μm in aerodynamic diameter — these penetrate deep into the alveolar regions of the lungs where the body's clearance mechanisms (macrophage engulfment and mucociliary transport) cannot remove them efficiently. Chronic exposure leads to fibrotic lung disease (pneumoconiosis), with specific forms depending on the dust composition.

Silica dust (crystalline SiO₂ from quartz crushing and silicon processing) causes silicosis — irreversible lung scarring with progressive loss of function. The risk is present at every stage of silicon production: quartz quarrying, crushing (jaw crusher, ball mill), and furnace loading. Wet crushing methods suppress dust at the source. Enclosed conveyor systems and local exhaust ventilation capture dust that is generated. P100 respirators are required in dusty areas.

Cotton dust causes byssinosis, characterized by chest tightness and reduced lung function, particularly on the first day of the work week ("Monday fever"). Cotton processing areas — ginning, carding, spinning — require ventilation and respiratory protection.

Silicon powder presents a dual hazard: respiratory (fine particulate) and explosion (combustible dust at concentrations above ~100 g/m³, minimum ignition energy ~100 mJ — low enough for static discharge). Prevention requires enclosing crushing and milling equipment, collecting dust at source, using explosion-proof electrical equipment, and grounding all equipment to prevent static buildup.
